LO 1 · Signaling Lecture Notes · In House Anki
Define and contrast the four modes of intercellular communication: endocrine, paracrine, autocrine, and neural.
tap to flip
Answer
Endocrine: hormone → BLOOD → distant target (e.g., insulin, TSH)
Paracrine: acts on NEIGHBORING cells (e.g., somatostatin → α/β islet cells)
Autocrine: acts on the SECRETING cell itself (e.g., IGF-1 on some tumors)
Neural: rapid, short, via synapse (e.g., ACh at NMJ)

Neurocrine (hybrid) = neuron releases hormone into blood (e.g., ADH, oxytocin).
LO 2 · Classes Lecture Notes · In House Anki
Name the three chemical classes of hormones used in lecture, with an example of each and a note on hydrophilic vs hydrophobic.
tap to flip
Answer
1. Amino-acid derivatives (small molecules built from single AAs) — generally HYDROPHILIC: catecholamines (E, NE, DA), melatonin; the EXCEPTION is thyroid hormone which is hydrophobic.

2. Peptides / proteins (chains of AAs) — HYDROPHILIC: insulin, GH, ACTH, TSH, PTH, ADH.

3. Lipid derivatives — HYDROPHOBIC:
• Steroids (built from cholesterol): cortisol, aldosterone, sex steroids, vitamin D.
• Eicosanoids (built from arachidonic acid): prostaglandins, thromboxanes, leukotrienes, prostacyclins.
LO 2 · Solubility / Signaling Lecture Notes · In House Anki
How does solubility predict storage, circulation, receptor location, AND the signaling mode (direct vs indirect)?
tap to flip
Answer
HYDROPHILIC (peptides, catecholamines) → STORED in vesicles → travel FREE in plasma → short ½-life (min) → cannot cross membrane → MEMBRANE receptors → INDIRECT signaling (transduction via 2nd messengers / ion channels / enzymes) with amplification.

HYDROPHOBIC (steroids, thyroid hormone, vitamin D) → NOT stored in most cells → travel BOUND to carriers → long ½-life (hr–days) → CROSS plasma membrane → INTRACELLULAR receptors (cytoplasm/organelle/nucleus, often transcription factors) → DIRECT signaling with slower gene-expression effects.
LO 2 · Glycoproteins Lecture Notes · In House Anki
What are the four glycoprotein hormones and what is the structural feature they share?
tap to flip
Answer
TSH, LH, FSH, hCG

Shared α-subunit (identical across all four).
Unique β-subunit confers specificity.

Clinical: hCG β-subunit similarity to TSH-β → molar pregnancy / choriocarcinoma can cause hyperthyroidism via TSH receptor cross-reactivity.
LO 3 · Catecholamines Lecture Notes · In House Anki
Write out the catecholamine synthesis pathway from precursor to epinephrine, naming each enzyme.
tap to flip
Answer
Tyrosine → (tyrosine hydroxylase — RATE-LIMITING) → L-DOPA → (DOPA decarboxylase, B6-dependent) → Dopamine → (dopamine β-hydroxylase) → Norepinephrine → (PNMT, cortisol-induced) → Epinephrine

PNMT = Phenylethanolamine-N-methyltransferase; only in adrenal medulla because local cortisol from cortex is required to induce it.
LO 3 · Rate-Limiting Step Lecture Notes · In House Anki
Which enzyme is rate-limiting for catecholamine synthesis, and how is it regulated?
tap to flip
Answer
Tyrosine hydroxylase (Tyr → L-DOPA)

Requires BH4 (tetrahydrobiopterin) as cofactor.
Feedback inhibited by end product (NE/DA).
Upregulated by sympathetic activity / phosphorylation.

Parkinson treatment = L-DOPA (bypasses this enzyme; crosses BBB).
LO 4 · Steroid Synthesis Lecture Notes · In House Anki
What is the initial (rate-limiting) step of steroid biosynthesis? Which enzyme catalyzes it and what regulates it?
tap to flip
Answer
Cholesterol → Pregnenolone
Enzyme: Desmolase (CYP11A1 / side-chain cleavage enzyme)
Location: inner mitochondrial membrane

Rate-limiting actually = StAR protein (transports cholesterol from outer → inner mito membrane). Without StAR, desmolase has no substrate.

Stimulated by: ACTH (adrenal), LH (gonads) via cAMP/PKA.
LO 4 · Steroid Classes (C21/C19/C18) Lecture Notes · In House Anki
How are steroids classified by carbon count, and which classes are C21, C19, and C18?
tap to flip
Answer
C21 steroids — progestins (progesterone) + corticoids (cortisol, aldosterone). Retain the cholesterol side chain largely intact.

C19 steroids — androgens (DHEA, androstenedione, testosterone). Side chain removed.

C18 steroids — estrogens (estradiol, estrone). Androgens further modified — aromatization of the A-ring by aromatase removes one more carbon → C18.

Vitamin D (secosteroid; calcitriol) is derived from cholesterol via UV photolysis → has a broken B-ring. Made in skin → liver → kidney.

LO 5 · Free Hormone Lecture Notes · In House Anki
State the free hormone hypothesis and give one clinical example of why it matters.
tap to flip
Answer
Only the FREE (unbound) fraction is biologically active and available to receptors. Carrier-bound hormone = inactive reservoir.

Pregnancy: estrogen ↑↑ hepatic TBG → ↑total T4 — but free T4 unchanged → patient EUTHYROID despite high total T4.

Measure FREE T4, not total, to assess thyroid function in pregnancy or with OCPs.
LO 5 · Half-Life Lecture Notes · In House Anki
Why do lipid-soluble hormones (cortisol, T4) have long half-lives while water-soluble hormones (insulin, ACTH) have short ones?
tap to flip
Answer
Carrier-bound hormones are protected from renal filtration and enzymatic degradation → slow release of free form → long ½-life (T4 ≈ 7 days; cortisol ≈ 60–90 min).

Water-soluble peptides circulate FREE → rapidly filtered/degraded by proteases → short ½-life (insulin ≈ 5 min; ACTH ≈ 10 min).
LO 6 · Receptor Types Lecture Notes · In House Anki
Classify hormone receptors by location + signaling, and match hormone class to receptor type.
tap to flip
Answer
Membrane receptors (water-soluble hormones):
• GPCR → cAMP (Gs/Gi), IP3/DAG/Ca²⁺ (Gq)
• RTK → MAPK/PI3K (insulin, IGF, GF)
• JAK-STAT → cytokine receptors (GH, prolactin, leptin, EPO)

Nuclear receptors (lipid-soluble hormones):
• Cytoplasmic → hormone binding → translocate to nucleus → bind HRE (steroids)
• Nuclear (pre-bound to DNA with RXR) → thyroid, vitamin D, retinoic acid
LO 6 · GPCR Pathways Lecture Notes · In House Anki
Name the three main G-protein subtypes and the second-messenger pathway each activates. Give one hormone that uses each.
tap to flip
Answer
Gs: ↑adenylyl cyclase → ↑cAMP → PKA — glucagon, ACTH, LH, FSH, TSH, PTH, β-adrenergic
Gi: ↓adenylyl cyclase → ↓cAMP — somatostatin, α₂-adrenergic, opioids
Gq: ↑PLC → IP3/DAG → Ca²⁺ + PKC — GnRH, TRH, ADH (V1), angiotensin II, α₁-adrenergic

Mnemonic: "FLAT ChAMP" (Gs) · "HAVe 1 M&M" (Gq)
LO 7 · Down-regulation Lecture Notes · In House Anki
Define receptor down-regulation and give a major clinical example.
tap to flip
Answer
Prolonged high hormone levels → cell reduces receptor number (internalization/degradation) + reduces post-receptor signaling → decreased sensitivity to that hormone.

Classic example: Type 2 diabetes — chronic hyperinsulinemia → insulin receptor down-regulation → insulin resistance.

Opposite = up-regulation (low hormone → more receptors). E.g., denervation hypersensitivity.
LO 7 · Permissiveness Lecture Notes · In House Anki
Define hormone permissiveness and give a high-yield example.
tap to flip
Answer
Permissiveness: one hormone is REQUIRED for another hormone's full effect, even though hormone A has no direct effect itself.

Classic example: Cortisol permits catecholamine vascular effects → addisonian crisis presents with hypotension refractory to pressors because catecholamines can't vasoconstrict without permissive cortisol.

Also: T3 permits β-adrenergic sensitivity → tachycardia in hyperthyroidism.
LO 7 · Synthesis vs. Storage Lecture Notes · In House Anki
Contrast hormones released by synthesis-on-demand vs. stored-and-released. Give one example of each.
tap to flip
Answer
Stored & released on demand (FAST, seconds): peptides (insulin, glucagon, GH, ACTH) stored in vesicles; catecholamines in chromaffin granules; thyroid hormone in colloid.

Synthesis-on-demand (SLOW, min–hr): steroid hormones — not stored; substrate + enzyme expression up-regulated when needed (e.g., ACTH → StAR induction → cortisol within minutes, peak hours).
LO 2 · Eicosanoids Lecture Notes · In House Anki
What are the eicosanoids, what single precursor molecule are they all built from, and name the four subclasses.
tap to flip
Answer
Eicosanoids = lipid-derivative autacoids (act locally as hormones / paracrine / autocrine signals). All derived from arachidonic acid (20-carbon PUFA released from membrane phospholipids by phospholipase A2).

Four subclasses:
• Prostaglandins (PGE₂, PGF₂α, PGD₂) — via COX
• Thromboxanes (TXA₂) — via COX (platelets)
• Prostacyclin (PGI₂) — via COX (endothelium)
• Leukotrienes (LTB₄, LTC₄/D₄/E₄) — via lipoxygenase

Clinical: NSAIDs block COX → blunt prostaglandin / thromboxane signaling.
LO 6 · Direct vs Indirect Lecture Notes · In House Anki
Define "direct" and "indirect" hormone signaling as used in the lecture. Which hormone classes use each, and how does the lag time differ?
tap to flip
Answer
Indirect signaling — hydrophilic hormone binds a MEMBRANE receptor and transduces a signal across the bilayer (via 2nd messenger, ion channel, or enzyme). Amplification + fast onset (seconds–minutes). Used by peptides + catecholamines.

Direct signaling — hydrophobic hormone CROSSES the membrane and directly binds an INTRACELLULAR receptor (often a transcription factor in cytoplasm or organelle). No transduction step needed. Slower onset (hours) because it must change gene transcription. Used by steroids + thyroid hormone.

Caveat: some hydrophobic hormones also have rapid membrane-bound receptor effects — it's receptor-dependent.
LO 1 · Target Cells & Specificity Lecture Notes · In House Anki
Hormones reach nearly every cell in the body via the bloodstream — so what determines which cells respond?
tap to flip
Answer
Receptors create specificity. A "target cell" is simply a cell that expresses the receptor for a given hormone. Cells without the receptor are effectively deaf to the hormone's signal, even though the hormone physically reaches them.

Consequences:
• A tissue's sensitivity profile depends entirely on which receptors it expresses.
• Pharmacologic blockers (e.g., spironolactone at the mineralocorticoid receptor) work by occupying the target receptor.
• Receptor cross-reactivity (hCG at TSH-R in molar pregnancy; cortisol at MR when 11β-HSD2 is overwhelmed) explains unexpected clinical phenotypes.
LO 2 · Regulated vs Constitutive Lecture Notes · In House Anki
Define "regulated" vs "constitutive" hormone secretion. Which hormone classes use each pathway?
tap to flip
Answer
REGULATED secretion: hormone is pre-synthesized, packaged into secretory granules/vesicles, and released by Ca²⁺-triggered exocytosis ONLY upon stimulus. Fast (seconds).
• Peptides (insulin, ACTH, GH, PTH) — in secretory granules
• Catecholamines — in chromaffin granules
• Thyroid hormone — stored bound to thyroglobulin in colloid (unique: intra-lumenal store)

CONSTITUTIVE secretion: hormone is synthesized de novo on demand, cannot be stored, diffuses out immediately as it is made. Slow (min–hr).
• Steroids — cholesterol → hormone via StAR + enzymes
• Eicosanoids — arachidonic acid → PGs/LTs, locally

Rule of thumb: Lipid-soluble = can't be stored in vesicles → constitutive.

Anki #59 · LO 4 · Vitamin D Activation In House Anki
Vitamin D is activated in two _____ reactions: _____ (liver) and _____ (kidney).
tap to flip
Answer
Two hydroxylation reactions: 25-hydroxylation in liver → 25-OH-D (calcidiol; storage form) then 1α-hydroxylation in kidney PCT → 1,25-(OH)₂-D (calcitriol; active form). Kidney step is rate-limiting — ↑ by PTH, ↓ Pi, ↓ Ca²⁺; ↓ by FGF23.
Anki #104 · LO 5 · TBG & Estrogen In House Anki
Estrogen increases TBG → _____ T4 but _____ free T4.

Estrogen (pregnancy, OCPs) stimulates hepatic TBG synthesis. ↑ Total T4 (more carrier-bound) but normal free T4 (biologically active fraction unchanged). Patient is EUTHYROID. Always measure FREE T4 — not total — to judge thyroid status.

Cascade 4 · LO 3 — Enzyme Affected → Downstream Effect: Carbidopa + L-DOPA in Parkinson's Lecture Notes
Trigger Parkinson's patient started on oral L-DOPA combined with carbidopa. Why the combination?
↓
Step 1 Enzyme block
Carbidopa inhibits DOPA decarboxylase (AADC) — the enzyme that converts L-DOPA → dopamine. Critically, carbidopa does NOT cross the blood-brain barrier, so it inhibits AADC in the periphery but NOT in the CNS.
↓
Step 2 Drug kinetics
Without carbidopa, ~95% of oral L-DOPA is converted to dopamine peripherally (gut, liver, vascular tissue) and never reaches the CNS. With carbidopa, peripheral AADC is blocked → much more L-DOPA survives to cross the BBB → central AADC (in surviving nigrostriatal neurons) converts it to dopamine where it's needed.
↓
Step 3 CNS outcome
More central dopamine → improved nigrostriatal dopaminergic tone → reduction in bradykinesia, rigidity, tremor. Effective L-DOPA dose is lowered (because more reaches the brain), and the ratio of L-DOPA to peripheral dopamine shifts favorably.
↓
Step 4 Side-effect profile
Peripheral dopamine formation is the main source of the bad side effects: nausea and vomiting (area postrema — outside the BBB!), orthostatic hypotension, and arrhythmias. By blocking peripheral AADC, carbidopa dramatically reduces these side effects. This is a pure application of the "block the enzyme upstream where you DON'T want the product" principle from the catecholamine enzyme map.

Cascade 7 · LO 3 — Enzyme Affected → Downstream Effect: Metyrosine Pre-Op Pheochromocytoma Lecture Notes · Study Guide
Trigger Patient with a functional pheochromocytoma being prepared for surgery. Metyrosine (α-methyl-para-tyrosine) is started 1–3 weeks preoperatively.
↓
Step 1 Enzyme block
Metyrosine is a competitive inhibitor of tyrosine hydroxylase (TH) — the rate-limiting step of catecholamine biosynthesis (tyrosine → L-DOPA). No other enzyme can compensate.
↓
Step 2 Pathway effect
Block at TH → ALL downstream catecholamines are depleted: ↓ L-DOPA, ↓ dopamine, ↓ norepinephrine, ↓ epinephrine. The tumor (and the rest of the body) gradually exhausts its stored catecholamine pool.
↓
Step 3 Labs / physiology
↓ Urinary metanephrines + VMA (catecholamine metabolites). Blood pressure falls; heart rate may rebound ↑ transiently (baroreflex), but is typically blunted too. Always combined with α-blockade (e.g., phenoxybenzamine) THEN β-blockade — never β-blockade alone (unopposed α → crisis).
↓
Step 4 Clinical
During tumor handling, intraoperative catecholamine release is much smaller → safer hemodynamics, reduced risk of hypertensive crisis, arrhythmia, and stroke. Side effects of metyrosine mirror the block: sedation, depression, Parkinsonism (↓ CNS dopamine), galactorrhea (↓ dopamine → disinhibited prolactin).
Cascade 8 · LO 3/7 — Enzyme Affected + Loss of Permissiveness: Primary Adrenal Insufficiency (Addison) Lecture Notes · Study Guide
Trigger Patient with autoimmune primary adrenal insufficiency (Addison's disease) — adrenal cortex destroyed; cortex-derived cortisol falls to near zero. Adrenal medulla is anatomically present.
↓
Step 1 PNMT induction fails
PNMT (phenylethanolamine-N-methyltransferase) in the adrenal medulla requires high local cortisol delivered by the intra-adrenal portal venous system. Without cortisol, PNMT gene expression is NOT induced → ↓ NE→Epi conversion → ↓ medullary epinephrine output.
↓
Step 2 Loss of permissiveness
Cortisol is permissive for catecholamine vasoconstriction — it up-regulates α₁-adrenergic receptors on vascular smooth muscle. Without cortisol, α₁ receptor expression falls → catecholamines can't effectively constrict vessels even if they are present.
↓
Step 3 Hemodynamics
Double hit: less circulating Epi (PNMT failure) + blunted vascular response to whatever catecholamines exist (loss of permissiveness) → refractory hypotension. Mineralocorticoid failure compounds this by causing volume depletion (↓ Na⁺ reabsorption, ↑ K⁺ retention).
↓
Step 4 Labs + Clinical
Labs: ↓ cortisol, ↑ ACTH (loss of feedback) → hyperpigmentation (ACTH shares precursor POMC with α-MSH); ↓ aldosterone, ↑ renin, ↓ Na⁺, ↑ K⁺; ↓ urinary metanephrines over time.
Clinical: fatigue, weight loss, salt craving, abdominal pain, hyperpigmentation of palmar creases + buccal mucosa, postural dizziness. Crisis trigger: any stressor (illness, surgery, trauma) → refractory shock despite IV fluids + pressors until IV hydrocortisone is given.
Cascade 9 · LO 7 — Receptor Sensitivity → Dose-Response Curve: Hyperinsulinemia → T2DM Evolution Lecture Notes · Study Guide
Trigger Obese patient with chronic hyperinsulinemia (fasting insulin persistently elevated over months to years).
↓
Step 1 Receptor
Prolonged HIGH ligand exposure → insulin receptors are internalized (endocytosed), and those remaining are desensitized (ser/thr phosphorylation of IRS). Result: ↓ receptor NUMBER + ↓ receptor AFFINITY.
↓
Step 2 Dose-response curve
Curve shifts RIGHTWARD (↑ EC50 — more insulin needed for half-max response) AND Vmax decreases (plateau lowered — maximum response is blunted). This combination is the classic "insulin resistance" curve.
↓
Step 3 Compensation
Pancreatic β-cells sense hyperglycemia and secrete MORE insulin to overcome resistance → serum insulin rises further → receptors down-regulate further (positive-feedback to pathology). Euglycemia is maintained briefly through hyperinsulinemia.
↓
Step 4 β-cell exhaustion
Over years, chronic hypersecretion + glucotoxicity + lipotoxicity exhaust β-cells (amyloid deposition, apoptosis). Insulin secretion falls → euglycemia can no longer be maintained → overt T2DM (fasting glucose > 126 mg/dL, A1c > 6.5%).
↓
Step 5 Clinical evolution
Progression: metabolic syndrome (central obesity + dyslipidemia + HTN + hyperinsulinemia, acanthosis nigricans) → impaired fasting glucose / prediabetes → early T2DM (high insulin, high glucose) → late T2DM (low insulin, very high glucose — insulin-requiring). Reversibility is greatest early; β-cell mass loss is increasingly irreversible. Key targets: weight loss, exercise (restore receptor sensitivity), metformin (↑ receptor sensitivity peripherally).
Cascade 10 · LO 2/4 — Enzyme Affected → Downstream Effect: NSAIDs Block COX → Eicosanoid Loss Lecture Notes · Study Guide
Trigger Patient started on a non-selective NSAID (ibuprofen, naproxen) for chronic pain.
↓
Step 1 Enzyme block
Arachidonic acid is released from membrane phospholipids by phospholipase A2, then metabolized by either COX → prostaglandins / thromboxanes / prostacyclin OR 5-LOX → leukotrienes. NSAIDs inhibit COX-1 and COX-2, leaving the LOX arm intact.
↓
Step 2 Loss of prostanoids
↓ PGE2 (inflammation, pain, fever, protective GI mucus, renal vasodilation)
↓ PGI2 / prostacyclin (vasodilation, anti-platelet)
↓ TXA2 (platelet aggregation, vasoconstriction) — especially after COX-1 inhibition in platelets
LOX arm is NOT blocked → leukotrienes can rise (substrate shunt) → can precipitate aspirin-sensitive asthma.
↓
Step 3 Therapeutic effects
Anti-inflammatory (↓ PGE2 in tissue) · Analgesic (↓ peripheral sensitization of nociceptors) · Antipyretic (↓ PGE2 at hypothalamic set-point) · Anti-platelet (↓ TXA2; aspirin is irreversible and potent on platelet COX-1).
↓
Step 4 Side effects
GI: ↓ PGE2 → ↓ mucus + ↓ bicarbonate → peptic ulceration, bleeding.
Renal: ↓ PGE2/PGI2 → loss of afferent arteriolar vasodilation → ↓ GFR, especially in hypovolemia → AKI.
Cardiovascular: ↓ PGI2 + ↑ Na/H₂O retention → ↑ BP; COX-2-selective agents shift the PGI2 / TXA2 balance toward pro-thrombotic (↑ MI, stroke risk).
Asthma: shunted leukotrienes can trigger bronchospasm in sensitive patients.

Trap 09 · LO 2 Lecture Notes
"Prostaglandins, thromboxanes, and leukotrienes are peptide hormones derived from precursor amino acids in the liver."

Trap 10 · LO 5 Lecture Notes · Study Guide
"The protein-bound fraction of a circulating hormone is the biologically active fraction, because it is protected from degradation."
FALSE. Per the free hormone hypothesis, only the free (unbound) fraction is biologically active — it is what binds receptors and senses feedback. Carrier-bound hormone is INACTIVE but serves as a reservoir that extends half-life and protects from renal/hepatic clearance. Changes in carrier levels (pregnancy → ↑TBG, ↑CBG) shift TOTAL hormone but NOT free hormone → patient remains clinically euthyroid / eucortisolic.
Trap 11 · LO 6 Lecture Notes · Study Guide
"The thyroid hormone receptor (TR) sits in the cytoplasm and only enters the nucleus AFTER binding T3, like the glucocorticoid receptor."
FALSE — classic board trap. TR (and RAR, VDR) are already bound to DNA (HRE) in the absence of hormone, actively REPRESSING transcription. When T3 arrives it DE-REPRESSES and activates transcription. Glucocorticoid receptor (GR) IS cytoplasmic until cortisol binds → then translocates. Know the distinction: steroid receptors (GR/MR/AR/PR/ER) = cytoplasmic→nuclear; thyroid/retinoid receptors (TR/RAR/VDR) = DNA-bound repressors.
Trap 12 · LO 7 Lecture Notes · Study Guide
"Permissiveness means one hormone amplifies the effect of another; in the absence of the permissive hormone, the primary hormone has zero effect."
TRUE. Permissiveness = one hormone is REQUIRED for another to exert its full effect. Classic example: cortisol is permissive for catecholamine vasoconstriction — without cortisol, α₁ receptors fail to up-regulate and catecholamines cannot maintain vascular tone → addisonian shock. Another example: thyroid hormone is permissive for normal growth via upregulation of GH receptors.
Trap 13 · LO 3 Lecture Notes
"Post-ganglionic sympathetic neurons and the adrenal medulla both release epinephrine as their primary catecholamine, because both express PNMT."
FALSE. Post-ganglionic sympathetic neurons release NOREPINEPHRINE (they lack PNMT). Only the adrenal medulla expresses PNMT, because PNMT is induced by local cortisol delivered via the intra-adrenal portal venous system from the adrenal cortex. This is why adrenal medulla output is ~80% epinephrine while sympathetic nerves release NE at target organs.
Trap 14 · LO 1 Lecture Notes
"Neural signaling acts over seconds to minutes, while endocrine signaling is essentially instantaneous because hormones reach target organs in milliseconds."
FALSE — reversed. Neural signaling is FAST (milliseconds, via synapse); endocrine signaling is SLOW (seconds → hours, because hormones must travel through the bloodstream to reach distant targets and then trigger intracellular cascades). This trade-off is exactly why the body has both systems: nervous for rapid discrete responses, endocrine for sustained coordinated responses.
Trap 15 · LO 4 Lecture Notes · Study Guide
"The zona reticularis is the dominant source of cortisol in the adrenal cortex, while the zona fasciculata produces DHEA and other androgen precursors."
FALSE — reversed. The zones go outer → inner as G-F-R: zona Glomerulosa → ALDOSTERONE (regulated by Ang II / K⁺); zona Fasciculata → CORTISOL (regulated by ACTH); zona Reticularis → DHEA / androgens. A useful mnemonic is "Salt, Sugar, Sex" for glomerulosa/fasciculata/reticularis products. Reticularis lacks 21-hydroxylase and aldosterone synthase, so it CAN'T make cortisol or aldosterone.
Trap 16 · LO 4 Lecture Notes · Study Guide
"In classic 21-hydroxylase deficiency (the most common CAH), substrate backs up and is shunted through the intact 17α-hydroxylase pathway — leading to ↑ DHEA/androgens, virilization, and ↓ cortisol AND ↓ aldosterone."
TRUE. 21-hydroxylase (CYP21) deficiency accounts for ~95% of CAH. Blocking the 21-OH step prevents conversion to both 11-deoxycorticosterone (aldo arm) AND 11-deoxycortisol (cortisol arm) → ↓ aldo (salt-wasting, hyperkalemia, hypotension) + ↓ cortisol (loss of negative feedback → ↑ACTH). Upstream substrate (progesterone / 17-OH-progesterone — the classic screening marker) shunts through the intact 17,20-lyase pathway to DHEA → VIRILIZATION. This is the "block upstream → substrate shunt downstream" principle used throughout steroid biochemistry.

LO 1 — Signaling
Define and contrast the four modes of intercellular communication: endocrine, paracrine, autocrine, and neural.
tap to flip
Answer
Endocrine: hormone → BLOOD → distant target (e.g., insulin, TSH)
Paracrine: acts on NEIGHBORING cells (e.g., somatostatin → α/β islet cells)
Autocrine: acts on the SECRETING cell itself (e.g., IGF-1 on some tumors)
Neural: rapid, short, via synapse (e.g., ACh at NMJ)

Neurocrine (hybrid) = neuron releases hormone into blood (e.g., ADH, oxytocin).
LO 2 — Classes
Name the three chemical classes of hormones used in lecture, with an example of each and a note on hydrophilic vs hydrophobic.
tap to flip
Answer
1. Amino-acid derivatives (small molecules built from single AAs) — generally HYDROPHILIC: catecholamines (E, NE, DA), melatonin; the EXCEPTION is thyroid hormone which is hydrophobic.

2. Peptides / proteins (chains of AAs) — HYDROPHILIC: insulin, GH, ACTH, TSH, PTH, ADH.

3. Lipid derivatives — HYDROPHOBIC:
• Steroids (built from cholesterol): cortisol, aldosterone, sex steroids, vitamin D.
• Eicosanoids (built from arachidonic acid): prostaglandins, thromboxanes, leukotrienes, prostacyclins.
LO 2 — Solubility / Signaling
How does solubility predict storage, circulation, receptor location, AND the signaling mode (direct vs indirect)?
tap to flip
Answer
HYDROPHILIC (peptides, catecholamines) → STORED in vesicles → travel FREE in plasma → short ½-life (min) → cannot cross membrane → MEMBRANE receptors → INDIRECT signaling (transduction via 2nd messengers / ion channels / enzymes) with amplification.

HYDROPHOBIC (steroids, thyroid hormone, vitamin D) → NOT stored in most cells → travel BOUND to carriers → long ½-life (hr–days) → CROSS plasma membrane → INTRACELLULAR receptors (cytoplasm/organelle/nucleus, often transcription factors) → DIRECT signaling with slower gene-expression effects.
LO 2 — Glycoproteins
What are the four glycoprotein hormones and what is the structural feature they share?
tap to flip
Answer
TSH, LH, FSH, hCG

Shared α-subunit (identical across all four).
Unique β-subunit confers specificity.

Clinical: hCG β-subunit similarity to TSH-β → molar pregnancy / choriocarcinoma can cause hyperthyroidism via TSH receptor cross-reactivity.
LO 3 — Catecholamines
Write out the catecholamine synthesis pathway from precursor to epinephrine, naming each enzyme.
tap to flip
Answer
Tyrosine → (tyrosine hydroxylase — RATE-LIMITING) → L-DOPA → (DOPA decarboxylase, B6-dependent) → Dopamine → (dopamine β-hydroxylase) → Norepinephrine → (PNMT, cortisol-induced) → Epinephrine

PNMT = Phenylethanolamine-N-methyltransferase; only in adrenal medulla because local cortisol from cortex is required to induce it.
LO 3 — Rate-Limiting Step
Which enzyme is rate-limiting for catecholamine synthesis, and how is it regulated?
tap to flip
Answer
Tyrosine hydroxylase (Tyr → L-DOPA)

Requires BH4 (tetrahydrobiopterin) as cofactor.
Feedback inhibited by end product (NE/DA).
Upregulated by sympathetic activity / phosphorylation.

Parkinson treatment = L-DOPA (bypasses this enzyme; crosses BBB).
LO 4 — Steroid Synthesis
What is the initial (rate-limiting) step of steroid biosynthesis? Which enzyme catalyzes it and what regulates it?
tap to flip
Answer
Cholesterol → Pregnenolone
Enzyme: Desmolase (CYP11A1 / side-chain cleavage enzyme)
Location: inner mitochondrial membrane

Rate-limiting actually = StAR protein (transports cholesterol from outer → inner mito membrane). Without StAR, desmolase has no substrate.

Stimulated by: ACTH (adrenal), LH (gonads) via cAMP/PKA.
LO 4 — Steroid Classes (C21/C19/C18)
How are steroids classified by carbon count, and which classes are C21, C19, and C18?
tap to flip
Answer
C21 steroids — progestins (progesterone) + corticoids (cortisol, aldosterone). Retain the cholesterol side chain largely intact.

C19 steroids — androgens (DHEA, androstenedione, testosterone). Side chain removed.

C18 steroids — estrogens (estradiol, estrone). Androgens further modified — aromatization of the A-ring by aromatase removes one more carbon → C18.

Vitamin D (secosteroid; calcitriol) is derived from cholesterol via UV photolysis → has a broken B-ring. Made in skin → liver → kidney.
LO 5 — Carriers
Match each carrier protein to its hormone: CBG, TBG, SHBG, DBP, albumin.
tap to flip
Answer
CBG (transcortin) → cortisol (also binds progesterone)
TBG → T3 & T4
SHBG → testosterone, estradiol, DHT
DBP → vitamin D / 25-OH-D
Albumin → non-specific, low affinity, high capacity (all steroids, thyroid hormone)
LO 5 — Free Hormone
State the free hormone hypothesis and give one clinical example of why it matters.
tap to flip
Answer
Only the FREE (unbound) fraction is biologically active and available to receptors. Carrier-bound hormone = inactive reservoir.

Pregnancy: estrogen ↑↑ hepatic TBG → ↑total T4 — but free T4 unchanged → patient EUTHYROID despite high total T4.

Measure FREE T4, not total, to assess thyroid function in pregnancy or with OCPs.
LO 5 — Half-Life
Why do lipid-soluble hormones (cortisol, T4) have long half-lives while water-soluble hormones (insulin, ACTH) have short ones?
tap to flip
Answer
Carrier-bound hormones are protected from renal filtration and enzymatic degradation → slow release of free form → long ½-life (T4 ≈ 7 days; cortisol ≈ 60–90 min).

Water-soluble peptides circulate FREE → rapidly filtered/degraded by proteases → short ½-life (insulin ≈ 5 min; ACTH ≈ 10 min).
LO 6 — Receptor Types
Classify hormone receptors by location + signaling, and match hormone class to receptor type.
tap to flip
Answer
Membrane receptors (water-soluble hormones):
• GPCR → cAMP (Gs/Gi), IP3/DAG/Ca²⁺ (Gq)
• RTK → MAPK/PI3K (insulin, IGF, GF)
• JAK-STAT → cytokine receptors (GH, prolactin, leptin, EPO)

Nuclear receptors (lipid-soluble hormones):
• Cytoplasmic → hormone binding → translocate to nucleus → bind HRE (steroids)
• Nuclear (pre-bound to DNA with RXR) → thyroid, vitamin D, retinoic acid
LO 6 — GPCR Pathways
Name the three main G-protein subtypes and the second-messenger pathway each activates. Give one hormone that uses each.
tap to flip
Answer
Gs: ↑adenylyl cyclase → ↑cAMP → PKA — glucagon, ACTH, LH, FSH, TSH, PTH, β-adrenergic
Gi: ↓adenylyl cyclase → ↓cAMP — somatostatin, α₂-adrenergic, opioids
Gq: ↑PLC → IP3/DAG → Ca²⁺ + PKC — GnRH, TRH, ADH (V1), angiotensin II, α₁-adrenergic

Mnemonic: "FLAT ChAMP" (Gs) · "HAVe 1 M&M" (Gq)
LO 7 — Down-regulation
Define receptor down-regulation and give a major clinical example.
tap to flip
Answer
Prolonged high hormone levels → cell reduces receptor number (internalization/degradation) + reduces post-receptor signaling → decreased sensitivity to that hormone.

Classic example: Type 2 diabetes — chronic hyperinsulinemia → insulin receptor down-regulation → insulin resistance.

Opposite = up-regulation (low hormone → more receptors). E.g., denervation hypersensitivity.
LO 7 — Permissiveness
Define hormone permissiveness and give a high-yield example.
tap to flip
Answer
Permissiveness: one hormone is REQUIRED for another hormone's full effect, even though hormone A has no direct effect itself.

Classic example: Cortisol permits catecholamine vascular effects → addisonian crisis presents with hypotension refractory to pressors because catecholamines can't vasoconstrict without permissive cortisol.

Also: T3 permits β-adrenergic sensitivity → tachycardia in hyperthyroidism.
LO 7 — Synthesis vs. Storage
Contrast hormones released by synthesis-on-demand vs. stored-and-released. Give one example of each.
tap to flip
Answer
Stored & released on demand (FAST, seconds): peptides (insulin, glucagon, GH, ACTH) stored in vesicles; catecholamines in chromaffin granules; thyroid hormone in colloid.

Synthesis-on-demand (SLOW, min–hr): steroid hormones — not stored; substrate + enzyme expression up-regulated when needed (e.g., ACTH → StAR induction → cortisol within minutes, peak hours).
LO 2 — Eicosanoids
What are the eicosanoids, what single precursor molecule are they all built from, and name the four subclasses.
tap to flip
Answer
Eicosanoids = lipid-derivative autacoids (act locally as hormones / paracrine / autocrine signals). All derived from arachidonic acid (20-carbon PUFA released from membrane phospholipids by phospholipase A2).

Four subclasses:
• Prostaglandins (PGE₂, PGF₂α, PGD₂) — via COX
• Thromboxanes (TXA₂) — via COX (platelets)
• Prostacyclin (PGI₂) — via COX (endothelium)
• Leukotrienes (LTB₄, LTC₄/D₄/E₄) — via lipoxygenase

Clinical: NSAIDs block COX → blunt prostaglandin / thromboxane signaling.
LO 6 — Direct vs Indirect
Define "direct" and "indirect" hormone signaling as used in the lecture. Which hormone classes use each, and how does the lag time differ?
tap to flip
Answer
Indirect signaling — hydrophilic hormone binds a MEMBRANE receptor and transduces a signal across the bilayer (via 2nd messenger, ion channel, or enzyme). Amplification + fast onset (seconds–minutes). Used by peptides + catecholamines.

Direct signaling — hydrophobic hormone CROSSES the membrane and directly binds an INTRACELLULAR receptor (often a transcription factor in cytoplasm or organelle). No transduction step needed. Slower onset (hours) because it must change gene transcription. Used by steroids + thyroid hormone.

Caveat: some hydrophobic hormones also have rapid membrane-bound receptor effects — it's receptor-dependent.
LO 7 — EC50 / Response Curves
What is EC50 on a hormone dose-response curve, and what three mechanisms can shift cellular sensitivity?
tap to flip
Answer
EC50 = concentration of hormone that produces HALF the maximal cellular response — an index of cellular sensitivity. Lower EC50 = more sensitive.

Three ways to shift sensitivity:
1. Different receptor isoforms with different affinities for the same hormone (e.g., IGF-1R vs insulin receptor for insulin/IGF-1 cross-talk).
2. Post-translational modification of the receptor — phosphorylation, ubiquitination, glycosylation alter affinity or turnover.
3. Receptor number — up-regulation (more receptors, lower EC50) or down-regulation (fewer, higher EC50).
LO 7 — Insulin Resistance Curve
On a glucose-lowering response curve, how does insulin resistance (as in T2DM) appear, and what does the shift mean mechanistically?
tap to flip
Answer
Insulin resistance causes a RIGHTWARD SHIFT of the insulin dose-response curve — higher EC50, the same maximal response (eventually) requires much more insulin. In severe disease, the maximal response itself falls (lower plateau).

Mechanism: chronic hyperinsulinemia → insulin receptor down-regulation + post-receptor signaling defects (SOCS proteins, IRS-1 serine phosphorylation) → ↑ EC50.

Clinically: early T2DM compensates with hyperinsulinemia (moves along the shifted curve); late T2DM β-cells exhaust, glucose rises sharply.
LO 1 — Target Cells & Specificity
Hormones reach nearly every cell in the body via the bloodstream — so what determines which cells respond?
tap to flip
Answer
Receptors create specificity. A "target cell" is simply a cell that expresses the receptor for a given hormone. Cells without the receptor are effectively deaf to the hormone's signal, even though the hormone physically reaches them.

Consequences:
• A tissue's sensitivity profile depends entirely on which receptors it expresses.
• Pharmacologic blockers (e.g., spironolactone at the mineralocorticoid receptor) work by occupying the target receptor.
• Receptor cross-reactivity (hCG at TSH-R in molar pregnancy; cortisol at MR when 11β-HSD2 is overwhelmed) explains unexpected clinical phenotypes.
LO 2 — Regulated vs Constitutive
Define "regulated" vs "constitutive" hormone secretion. Which hormone classes use each pathway?
tap to flip
Answer
REGULATED secretion: hormone is pre-synthesized, packaged into secretory granules/vesicles, and released by Ca²⁺-triggered exocytosis ONLY upon stimulus. Fast (seconds).
• Peptides (insulin, ACTH, GH, PTH) — in secretory granules
• Catecholamines — in chromaffin granules
• Thyroid hormone — stored bound to thyroglobulin in colloid (unique: intra-lumenal store)

CONSTITUTIVE secretion: hormone is synthesized de novo on demand, cannot be stored, diffuses out immediately as it is made. Slow (min–hr).
• Steroids — cholesterol → hormone via StAR + enzymes
• Eicosanoids — arachidonic acid → PGs/LTs, locally

Rule of thumb: Lipid-soluble = can't be stored in vesicles → constitutive.
LO 2 — Storage Forms by Class
Name the SPECIFIC storage form/location for each major hormone class.
tap to flip
Answer
Peptide/protein hormones → stored as pre-formed hormone in secretory granules (RER → Golgi → granule). Example: insulin in β-cell granules with C-peptide.

Catecholamines → stored in chromaffin granules / synaptic vesicles. Dopamine β-hydroxylase works INSIDE the granule to make NE.

Thyroid hormone → stored extracellularly (unique!) as iodinated thyroglobulin in follicular colloid. Released by proteolysis of thyroglobulin. Largest hormone store in the body — months of supply.

Steroid hormones → NOT STORED. Synthesized de novo from cholesterol when stimulated (StAR → desmolase). Cholesterol droplets are the substrate store, not a hormone store.

Eicosanoids → NOT stored. Made locally on demand from membrane arachidonic acid.
Anki #25 · LO 3 — Catecholamine Control
Catecholamine production by neuroendocrine cells in the adrenal medulla is controlled by _____ ganglionic _____ fibers.
tap to flip
Answer
Preganglionic sympathetic fibers. Adrenal chromaffin cells are modified postganglionic neurons — they receive direct preganglionic cholinergic input (ACh via nicotinic receptors) and dump Epi/NE straight into the bloodstream, bypassing the usual two-neuron chain.
Anki #32 · LO 3 — Rate-Limiting Step
The rate-limiting step of catecholamine synthesis is _____.
tap to flip
Answer
Tyrosine hydroxylase (Tyr → L-DOPA). Most regulated, BH4-dependent, feedback-inhibited by catecholamines. α-methyltyrosine (metyrosine) blocks this enzyme (pre-op pheo).
Anki #33 · LO 2 — Peptide Types
Peptide hormones can be categorized into _____ and _____.
tap to flip
Answer
Glycoproteins (TSH, LH, FSH, hCG — carbohydrate-modified) and short polypeptides / small proteins (insulin, GH, PTH, ADH, oxytocin, ACTH).
Anki #35 · LO 2/6 — Class → Receptor
Hormones classified as _____ determine whether they bind to membrane or intracellular receptors.
tap to flip
Answer
Hormone class / chemical type determines receptor location. Water-soluble (peptides, catecholamines) → membrane. Lipid-soluble (steroids, thyroid, vitamin D) → intracellular/nuclear.
Anki #37 · LO 1 — Juxtacrine
_____ signaling involves direct cell-to-cell contact via growth factors and cytokines.
tap to flip
Answer
Juxtacrine signaling. Requires membrane-bound ligand on one cell binding a receptor on an adjacent cell. Examples: Notch–Delta in development; membrane-bound TNF-α; EGF family members tethered to donor cell surface.
Anki #39 · LO 2 — Glycoprotein Subunits
Glycoprotein hormones (FSH, LH, TSH, hCG) share a common _____ subunit and differ in _____ subunit.
tap to flip
Answer
Shared alpha (α) subunit; unique beta (β) subunit confers specificity. Clinical: hCG-β is what pregnancy tests detect; hCG cross-reacts with TSH-R in molar pregnancy (high hCG → thyrotoxicosis).
Anki #40 · LO 6 — Receptor Location
Steroid hormones act via _____ receptors; peptide hormones act via _____ receptors.
tap to flip
Answer
Intracellular / nuclear receptors (steroids) → alter gene transcription (slow, durable).
Membrane receptors (peptides) → GPCR, RTK, JAK-STAT → second messengers (fast).
Anki #42 · LO 6 — Nuclear Receptor Superfamily
Nuclear receptors belong to the _____ superfamily.
tap to flip
Answer
Ligand-gated transcription factor superfamily. Members: steroid receptors (GR, MR, AR, ER, PR), thyroid hormone receptor (TR), vitamin D receptor (VDR), retinoic acid receptor (RAR/RXR), PPAR. All have DBD, LBD, TAD domains.
Anki #52 · LO 3 — Catecholamine Pathway
Tyrosine → _____ → _____ → _____ → _____.
tap to flip
Answer
Tyrosine → L-DOPA → Dopamine → Norepinephrine → Epinephrine
Enzymes: TH (rate-limiting, BH4) → DOPA decarboxylase (B6) → Dopamine β-hydroxylase (in vesicle, Vit C, Cu²⁺) → PNMT (adrenal medulla, cortisol-induced).
Anki #59 · LO 4 — Vitamin D Activation
Vitamin D is activated in two _____ reactions: _____ (liver) and _____ (kidney).
tap to flip
Answer
Two hydroxylation reactions: 25-hydroxylation in liver → 25-OH-D (calcidiol; storage form) then 1α-hydroxylation in kidney PCT → 1,25-(OH)₂-D (calcitriol; active form). Kidney step is rate-limiting — ↑ by PTH, ↓ Pi, ↓ Ca²⁺; ↓ by FGF23.
Anki #60 · LO 6 — Vitamin D Receptor
1,25-diOH-D acts via a _____ receptor.
tap to flip
Answer
Nuclear (intracellular) receptor — VDR. Heterodimerizes with RXR on vitamin-D-response elements (VDRE). Drives transcription of Ca²⁺-binding protein (calbindin), TRPV6, osteocalcin, etc.
Anki #61 · LO 2 — Steroid Storage
Steroid hormones are synthesized _____ and stored in _____.
tap to flip
Answer
De novo (synthesized on demand; cannot be stored as hormone). Substrate cholesterol is stored in lipid droplets inside steroidogenic cells. Stimulated by ACTH (adrenal), LH (gonads) via cAMP → PKA → StAR phosphorylation.
Anki #62 · LO 2 — Peptide Storage
Peptide hormones stored in _____ are released by _____ signaling.
tap to flip
Answer
Stored in secretory granules (vesicles); released by exocytosis (Ca²⁺-triggered, regulated secretion). Trigger → intracellular Ca²⁺ ↑ → granules fuse with membrane → contents dumped into blood.
Anki #63 · LO 2 — Catecholamine Storage
Catecholamines are stored preformed in _____ within chromaffin cells.
tap to flip
Answer
Vesicles / granules (chromaffin granules). Stored bound to chromogranin proteins + ATP. DβH inside the granule converts dopamine → NE. PNMT acts in cytosol to make Epi → re-enters granule.
Anki #64 · LO 2 — Thyroid Hormone Storage
Thyroid hormones are stored as _____ and released by _____.
tap to flip
Answer
Stored as iodinated thyroglobulin in follicular colloid (extracellular!). Released by proteolysis — colloid is endocytosed back into the follicular cell, lysosomes cleave Tg, free T3/T4 exit across basal membrane. Unique among hormones: the only extracellular hormone store.
Anki #65 · LO 5 — Deiodinase
Hormone _____ (T4 to T3) is catalyzed by tissue-specific _____ enzymes.
tap to flip
Answer
Metabolism / conversion catalyzed by deiodinases.
• D1 — liver, kidney: makes plasma T3 (5′-deiodination)
• D2 — brain, pituitary, BAT: local T3 (intracellular feedback)
• D3 — placenta, fetal: inactivates to reverse T3 (rT3)
Starvation and illness increase D3 → euthyroid sick syndrome (↓ T3, ↑ rT3).
Anki #66 · LO 2/5 — Transport
_____ circulate free; _____ require carrier proteins.
tap to flip
Answer
Peptide hormones + catecholamines circulate FREE (hydrophilic, dissolve directly in plasma).
Steroid hormones + thyroid hormone require CARRIER proteins (hydrophobic, would aggregate in plasma). Carriers: CBG, TBG, SHBG, DBP, albumin.
Anki #67 · LO 5 — Half-Life
_____ is the plasma half-life (t½) of a hormone.
tap to flip
Answer
Terminal elimination half-life — time for plasma hormone concentration to fall by 50%. Determined by metabolism + excretion + binding-protein protection. Peptides: minutes; catecholamines: seconds; cortisol: ~60–90 min; T4: ~7 days.
Anki #68 · LO 6 — Kd
_____ is the dissociation constant (lower = higher affinity).
tap to flip
Answer
Kd (dissociation constant) = concentration of hormone at which 50% of receptors are bound. Lower Kd = HIGHER affinity (tighter binding, less hormone needed). Insulin receptor Kd ≈ 10⁻⁹ M (nanomolar-range affinity).
Anki #75 · LO 6 — Cross-Reactivity
Cross-reactivity occurs when _____ bind the same antibody.
tap to flip
Answer
Structurally similar molecules bind the same antibody. Relevant in immunoassays (can give false results). Clinical: hCG-β at TSH receptor → molar pregnancy thyrotoxicosis. hCG cross-reacts with LH receptor (shared α).
Anki #100 · LO 6 — Nuclear Receptor Domains
Nuclear receptors contain _____, _____, and _____ domains.
tap to flip
Answer
DBD (DNA-binding domain — zinc fingers, binds HRE)
LBD (ligand-binding domain — binds hormone)
TAD (transactivation domain — recruits coactivators)
HSP90 holds receptor in cytoplasm until hormone binds; hormone binding releases HSP90 → dimerization → nuclear import → HRE binding → transcription.
Anki #101 · LO 1 — Endocrine
_____ signaling: hormones in bloodstream to distant tissues.
tap to flip
Answer
Endocrine signaling. Hormone secreted into blood → reaches distant target cells with appropriate receptors. Examples: insulin (pancreas → muscle/liver); cortisol (adrenal → whole body).
Anki #102 · LO 1 — Paracrine
_____ signaling: hormones act on nearby cells.
tap to flip
Answer
Paracrine signaling. Local diffusion to adjacent cells (does NOT enter blood). Example: somatostatin from δ-cells → α/β islet cells; histamine from ECL → parietal cells.
Anki #103 · LO 1 — Autocrine
_____ signaling: cell responds to its own signals.
tap to flip
Answer
Autocrine signaling. Cell secretes a hormone/factor that binds back to its own surface receptors. Examples: IGF-1 on some tumor cells (growth loop); IL-2 on T cells after activation.
Anki #104 · LO 5 — TBG & Estrogen
Estrogen increases TBG → _____ T4 but _____ free T4.
tap to flip
Answer
Estrogen (pregnancy, OCPs) stimulates hepatic TBG synthesis. ↑ Total T4 (more carrier-bound) but normal free T4 (biologically active fraction unchanged). Patient is EUTHYROID. Always measure FREE T4 — not total — to judge thyroid status.

Test Trap Mode: Each statement is either TRUE or FALSE as written. Many are designed to catch common exam errors. Click T or F — then read the explanation carefully.
Trap 01 — LO 1
"A paracrine signal is characterized by a hormone being secreted into the bloodstream and acting on nearby cells."
FALSE. Paracrine signaling is defined by LOCAL DIFFUSION (no bloodstream entry) to NEIGHBORING cells. Endocrine = enters blood, acts distantly. The word "paracrine" literally means "alongside" — no circulation.
Trap 02 — LO 2
"Steroid hormones, unlike peptide hormones, are stored in secretory vesicles and released on demand."
FALSE. Reversed! Peptides are stored preformed in vesicles → released within seconds of stimulus. Steroids are lipid-soluble and CANNOT be stored in vesicles; they must be SYNTHESIZED de novo from cholesterol after stimulus (delay of minutes to hours).
Trap 03 — LO 3
"The rate-limiting enzyme in catecholamine biosynthesis is dopamine β-hydroxylase."
FALSE. The rate-limiting enzyme is tyrosine hydroxylase (Tyr → L-DOPA), which requires BH4 as a cofactor. Dopamine β-hydroxylase is a downstream step (DA → NE). Remember: rate-limiting = FIRST committed step in most biosynthetic pathways.
Trap 04 — LO 3
"PNMT converts norepinephrine to epinephrine and is found throughout the sympathetic nervous system."
FALSE. PNMT is found only in the adrenal medulla, not in peripheral sympathetic neurons. This is because PNMT expression requires high local cortisol from adrenal cortex (delivered via portal-like vasculature). That's why sympathetic neurons release mostly NE, while the adrenal medulla releases ~80% Epi.
Trap 05 — LO 4
"Cortisol, aldosterone, estrogen, and thyroid hormone all derive from cholesterol and share the same first enzymatic step."
FALSE. Thyroid hormone is NOT a steroid — it's an amine derived from tyrosine + iodine (on thyroglobulin). Cortisol, aldosterone, and estrogen ARE steroids derived from cholesterol via desmolase (CYP11A1). Classic trap: thyroid hormone behaves LIKE a steroid (lipid-soluble, carrier-bound, nuclear receptor) but is synthesized completely differently.
Trap 06 — LO 5
"A pregnant woman with elevated total T4 but normal free T4 is hyperthyroid and should be treated with methimazole."
FALSE. This is a classic free-hormone-hypothesis trap. Estrogen in pregnancy ↑hepatic TBG → more T4 binds carrier → total T4 rises, but free T4 is unchanged. Because only free hormone is bioactive, the patient is EUTHYROID. No treatment needed. Always assess thyroid status with FREE T4, not total.
Trap 07 — LO 6
"Nuclear receptors (like the glucocorticoid receptor) produce rapid effects within seconds because they act directly on ion channels."
FALSE. Nuclear receptors act by altering gene transcription → mRNA → new protein → physiologic effect. This is a SLOW process (minutes to hours to days), not seconds. Rapid effects are characteristic of water-soluble hormones binding MEMBRANE receptors (GPCRs / RTKs) using second messengers.
Trap 08 — LO 7
"In insulin resistance, pancreatic β-cells respond by up-regulating insulin receptors on hepatocytes to compensate."
FALSE. Chronic hyperinsulinemia DOWN-regulates insulin receptors (the problem, not the response). β-cells compensate by SECRETING MORE INSULIN, not by regulating peripheral receptors. Pancreatic β-cells cannot control hepatocyte receptor number — that's a local target-cell phenomenon driven by the high ambient hormone level. On the dose-response curve this appears as a rightward shift (higher EC50).
Trap 09 — LO 2
"Prostaglandins, thromboxanes, and leukotrienes are peptide hormones derived from precursor amino acids in the liver."
FALSE. These are EICOSANOIDS — lipid-derived autacoids built from arachidonic acid (a 20-carbon fatty acid released from membrane phospholipids by phospholipase A2). They are hydrophobic lipid hormones — NOT peptides. Made LOCALLY by most cells, they act mostly paracrine/autocrine via membrane GPCRs. NSAIDs (COX inhibitors) and zileuton (lipoxygenase inhibitor) target these pathways.
Trap 10 — LO 2 (Solubility)
"Thyroid hormones (T3 and T4) act through membrane G-protein coupled receptors using cAMP as a second messenger."
FALSE. Thyroid hormone is an amine structurally (derived from tyrosine + iodine), but it is HYDROPHOBIC — so it CROSSES the cell membrane and acts through NUCLEAR (intracellular) receptors, altering gene transcription. Thyroid-response elements pair with RXR on DNA. This is why thyroid hormone has a slow (hours–days) but durable effect. Classic trap: T3/T4 are amines but BEHAVE like steroids (lipid-soluble, carrier-bound to TBG, long half-life, nuclear receptor). The ONLY amine-derived hormone that uses nuclear receptors.
Trap 11 — LO 2 (Solubility)
"Catecholamines are lipid-soluble amines that circulate bound to a carrier protein and bind intracellular receptors."
FALSE. Catecholamines (E, NE, DA) are HYDROPHILIC amines — despite being derived from tyrosine. They circulate FREE in plasma (no carrier protein), have a very short half-life (seconds–minutes), and bind membrane GPCRs (α and β adrenergic receptors → G-protein / cAMP / IP3 signaling). Thyroid hormone is the ONE amine that breaks this rule (hydrophobic, carrier-bound, nuclear receptor).
Trap 12 — LO 6 (Nuclear Receptors)
"All steroid hormones act via nuclear receptors that function as ligand-gated transcription factors."
TRUE. Steroid hormones (cortisol, aldosterone, estrogen, progesterone, testosterone) plus vitamin D, thyroid hormone, and retinoic acid all bind members of the nuclear receptor superfamily — these are ligand-activated transcription factors with three domains: DBD (zinc-finger), LBD, TAD. Some steroid receptors reside in cytoplasm bound to HSP90 until ligand binds; others (T3R, VDR) sit pre-bound to DNA as heterodimers with RXR. Rapid non-genomic effects of steroids exist too, but the classical mechanism is transcription.
Trap 13 — LO 6 (Kd / Affinity)
"A lower Kd indicates stronger hormone–receptor affinity."
TRUE. Kd is the dissociation constant — the hormone concentration at which 50% of receptors are occupied. A low Kd means very little hormone is required to saturate receptors, i.e., HIGH affinity. Insulin receptor Kd ≈ 10⁻⁹ M (nanomolar affinity). Kd is INVERSELY related to affinity — easy to mix up with EC50 (which can be shifted by receptor number, not just intrinsic affinity).
Trap 14 — LO 5 (Carriers)
"TBG binds T3 and T4 specifically; CBG is the main carrier for cortisol; DBP is the main carrier for vitamin D."
TRUE. Each hydrophobic hormone has a preferred carrier: TBG → T3/T4; CBG (transcortin) → cortisol (also progesterone); DBP → 25-OH vitamin D; SHBG → testosterone, DHT, estradiol. Albumin binds ALL hydrophobic hormones loosely (low affinity, high capacity). Classical exam trap: measure FREE hormone when carrier protein shifts (pregnancy ↑TBG → ↑total T4 but normal free T4).
Trap 15 — LO 2 (Vitamin D)
"Vitamin D (calcitriol) acts as a hormone through an intracellular / nuclear receptor similar to steroid hormones."
TRUE. Calcitriol (1,25-(OH)₂-D₃) is a secosteroid — it is structurally a steroid with a broken B-ring, made from cholesterol via UV photolysis in skin. It binds the VDR (vitamin D receptor), a nuclear receptor that heterodimerizes with RXR on VDR-response elements. Like cortisol/estrogen it exerts delayed transcriptional effects (Ca²⁺-binding protein, TRPV6, osteocalcin).
Trap 16 — LO 6 (RXR Partners)
"RXR heterodimerizes with RAR, VDR, TR, and PPAR receptors."
TRUE. RXR (retinoid X receptor) is a "promiscuous" heterodimer partner for several nuclear receptors: RAR (retinoic acid), VDR (vitamin D), TR (thyroid hormone), PPAR (fatty acids / fibrates / TZDs), LXR (oxysterols). These heterodimers sit on DNA response elements awaiting hormone. Clinical: fibrates (PPAR-α), pioglitazone (PPAR-γ), levothyroxine (TR), isotretinoin (RAR) all converge on RXR-heterodimer signaling.
Trap 17 — LO 7 (Concentration vs Effect)
"Higher plasma hormone concentrations always indicate stronger biological effect at the target tissue."
FALSE. Biological effect depends on the receptor, not just hormone level. Hormone resistance (Type 2 DM, androgen insensitivity, Laron dwarfism) has HIGH hormone and LOW effect because receptors are defective or downregulated. Only the free fraction is active (carrier-bound is inactive). Also: extreme receptor down-regulation can flatten the dose-response curve (lower plateau). Always interpret hormone levels in context of clinical effect + TSH/feedback axis.
Trap 18 — LO 5 (Immunoassay)
"In a radioimmunoassay (RIA), radio-labeled hormone competes with the patient's hormone for a fixed amount of antibody."
TRUE. RIA is a competitive binding assay — more patient hormone means less labeled hormone bound to antibody → signal inversely correlates with hormone concentration. Invented by Rosalyn Yalow (Nobel, 1977). Modern descendent = ELISA (enzyme/color detection instead of radioisotope). IRMA (immunoradiometric) uses a sandwich format — unlabeled capture Ab + radio-labeled detector Ab; signal correlates DIRECTLY.
Trap 19 — LO 2 (Glycoprotein Subunits)
"Glycoprotein hormones share a common alpha subunit and differ in their beta subunit, which confers specificity."
TRUE. The four glycoprotein hormones (TSH, LH, FSH, hCG) share an identical α-subunit. Each has a unique β-subunit that gives it biological specificity. This is why pregnancy tests measure β-hCG. Clinical implication: at extremely high hCG (molar pregnancy, choriocarcinoma), partial similarity between hCG-β and TSH-β causes TSH-receptor cross-reactivity → thyrotoxicosis without thyroid disease.

Big Picture: Solubility predicts almost everything — where the hormone is stored, how it travels, how fast it acts, where its receptor lives. Hydrophilic ⇒ stored in vesicles, free in plasma, membrane receptor, INDIRECT signaling (fast). Hydrophobic ⇒ made-on-demand (or stored bound), carrier-bound, intracellular receptor, DIRECT signaling (slow + durable). Eicosanoids are the exception — lipid-derived but act locally and fast.

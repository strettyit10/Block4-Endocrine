Prolactinoma
Most common pituitary tumor, typical presentation (F/M), lab hallmark, treatment of choice, and why surgery is NOT first-line?
Answer
Most common pituitary adenoma.
Females: amenorrhea, galactorrhea, infertility (smaller, diagnosed earlier)
Males/post-menopausal: mass effect (bitemporal hemianopia, headache), hypogonadism/erectile dysfunction

Lab: prolactin >200 ng/mL highly suggestive
Tx: cabergoline (or bromocriptine) — dopamine D2 AGONIST shrinks tumor AND lowers PRL. Surgery reserved for drug-resistant cases.
Stalk Effect vs Prolactinoma
How do you distinguish a prolactinoma from the "stalk effect" of a non-prolactin adenoma by PRL level?
Answer
Prolactinoma: PRL usually >200 ng/mL (often >500–1000)
Stalk effect (macro-adenoma compresses stalk → ↓dopamine delivery → disinhibited lactotropes): PRL typically 25–150 ng/mL

Same mechanism underlies PRL rise with antipsychotics, methyldopa, metoclopramide — all block D2 signaling.

Clinical pearl: massive PRL "disappears" on immunoassay = HOOK EFFECT — dilute the sample.
Acromegaly / Gigantism
Mechanism, age-dependent difference, best screening test, and pharmacologic options?
Answer
Mechanism: Somatotrope adenoma → ↑GH → ↑IGF-1
Before epiphyseal closure → gigantism; after → acromegaly (jaw enlargement, frontal bossing, hand/foot growth, organomegaly, glucose intolerance)

Best screen: serum IGF-1 (GH is pulsatile, IGF-1 is stable)
Confirm: oral glucose tolerance test → failure to suppress GH
Drugs: octreotide (somatostatin analog); pegvisomant (GH receptor antagonist); cabergoline (dopamine agonist — works on some somatotropes paradoxically)
Cushing Disease
Definition (Disease vs Syndrome), high-vs-low-dose dex suppression pattern, and why?
Answer
Cushing DISEASE = pituitary ACTH-producing adenoma (corticotrope). Cushing SYNDROME = any cause of hypercortisolism.

Low-dose dex: fails to suppress cortisol (both Cushing disease AND ectopic ACTH)
High-dose dex: SUPPRESSES cortisol in pituitary disease (retains negative feedback) but DOESN'T suppress in ectopic (e.g., small cell lung cancer — tumor doesn't care about feedback)

Pituitary source → ↑ACTH modestly. Ectopic → ↑↑ACTH + hypokalemia/alkalosis (cortisol spills to MR).
Sheehan Syndrome
Setting, mechanism, first clinical clue, and which hormones are affected first?
Answer
Setting: Postpartum woman with severe obstetric hemorrhage
Mechanism: pregnancy-induced pituitary hyperplasia (especially lactotropes) + hemorrhage → hypotension → ischemic necrosis of anterior pituitary

First clinical clue: failure to lactate postpartum (loss of prolactin)
Order of loss: GH & PRL → FSH/LH → TSH → ACTH
Posterior pituitary SPARED (different blood supply — not portal).
Pituitary Apoplexy
Trigger, presentation, most urgent hormone to replace, and imaging findings?
Answer
Sudden hemorrhage or infarction of a preexisting pituitary adenoma.
Presentation: thunderclap headache, bitemporal hemianopia, ophthalmoplegia (CN III/IV/VI through cavernous sinus), altered consciousness.

Most urgent: IV hydrocortisone FIRST (loss of ACTH → adrenal crisis — can kill within hours). Then thyroid replacement.
Imaging: MRI shows hemorrhagic pituitary mass, often with sellar expansion. Surgical decompression may be needed for visual loss.
Kallmann Syndrome
Genetic defect, triad, and expected hormone pattern?
Answer
Defect: failure of GnRH neurons + olfactory neurons to migrate from olfactory placode to hypothalamus (KAL1 / ANOS1 gene most common; X-linked)

Triad: 1. Anosmia / hyposmia 2. Delayed or absent puberty 3. Midline defects (cleft lip/palate)

Hormones: ↓GnRH → ↓LH + ↓FSH → ↓sex steroids (hypogonadotropic hypogonadism)
Tx: pulsatile GnRH (for fertility), testosterone/estrogen replacement.
Craniopharyngioma
Embryology, two age peaks, imaging hallmark, and presenting symptoms?
Answer
Origin: remnants of Rathke's pouch (oral ectoderm)
Bimodal age: children (5–14y) and older adults (50–70y)

Imaging: calcified suprasellar mass — "motor oil" fluid with cholesterol crystals on biopsy

Presenting features:
• Bitemporal hemianopia (optic chiasm compression)
• Growth failure (↓GH)
• Central DI (stalk)
• Panhypopituitarism often develops
Bitemporal Hemianopia
Anatomic basis, most common causes, why inferior retinal fibers fall first?
Answer
Anatomy: nasal retinal fibers CROSS at optic chiasm — they carry TEMPORAL visual fields. A sellar mass compressing the chiasm from below takes out these crossing fibers → loss of both temporal fields.

Most common causes: pituitary adenoma (macro), craniopharyngioma, meningioma.
Superior temporal defects first: inferior nasal fibers (which carry SUPERIOR field) loop lowest and get hit first as a mass grows upward.
Central vs Nephrogenic DI
Two mechanisms, water deprivation test findings, and desmopressin response?
Answer
Central DI: inadequate ADH synthesis/release (trauma, stalk section, craniopharyngioma, idiopathic, pituitary surgery)
Nephrogenic DI: kidney unresponsive to ADH (lithium, hypercalcemia, V2-receptor or AQP2 mutation, hereditary)

Water deprivation: both fail to concentrate urine
After desmopressin:
• Central → urine osm ↑ >50% (kidney responds)
• Nephrogenic → no response (<10%)

Tx: Central → desmopressin. Nephrogenic → HCTZ/amiloride + low-salt.
SIADH
Lab triad, most common causes, and treatment hierarchy?
Answer
Triad: hyponatremia + INAPPROPRIATELY concentrated urine (osm >100) + clinical euvolemia

Causes:
• Ectopic ADH (small-cell lung cancer — classic)
• CNS disease (stroke, tumor, bleed)
• Pulmonary (pneumonia, TB)
• Drugs: SSRIs, carbamazepine, cyclophosphamide

Tx: Mild → fluid restriction. Moderate → demeclocycline (old) or vaptans (V2 antagonists). Severe/symptomatic → hypertonic saline CAUTIOUSLY (risk osmotic demyelination).
Octreotide
Mechanism, three major uses, and side effects?
Answer
Mechanism: synthetic somatostatin analog — binds SSTR-2/5 receptors (Gi) → ↓cAMP → ↓hormone secretion

Uses:
• Acromegaly (↓GH)
• Carcinoid / VIPoma / glucagonoma symptom control
• Variceal bleeding (splanchnic vasoconstriction)

Side effects: cholelithiasis (↓gallbladder motility), steatorrhea, hyperglycemia or hypoglycemia, bradycardia, injection-site pain.
Leuprolide Paradox
Why does the same drug (leuprolide) both stimulate AND suppress sex hormones depending on dosing?
Answer
Leuprolide = GnRH AGONIST.

Pulsatile (physiologic): mimics natural GnRH → stimulates LH/FSH → fertility restoration
Continuous (pharmacologic): constant stimulation DOWN-REGULATES GnRH receptors on gonadotropes → shuts down LH/FSH → medical castration

Clinical uses (continuous): prostate cancer, endometriosis, central precocious puberty, IVF ovarian suppression. Initial "tumor flare" from transient stimulation before receptor down-regulation.
Empty Sella Syndrome
Definition, causes, and typical hormone pattern?
Answer
Herniation of subarachnoid space INTO sella → flattens pituitary against sellar floor → MRI looks like "empty."

Primary: congenital defect of diaphragma sellae (common incidental finding; obese women)
Secondary: prior tumor infarct/surgery/radiation

Hormones: usually intact (pituitary tissue still functions at the rim) but can evolve to hypopituitarism over time. Hyperprolactinemia from stalk stretch possible. Most are asymptomatic incidental MRI findings.
Adipsic DI / OVLT Dysfunction
What is adipsic diabetes insipidus, where is the lesion, and why does it matter clinically?
Answer
Adipsic (or essential) hypernatremia with DI — occurs when damage involves the OVLT and other osmoreceptor CVOs along the anterior wall of the third ventricle.

Result: patient LOSES the normal osmoreceptor-driven ADH release AND the thirst response → severe hypernatremia without protective thirst.

Causes: craniopharyngioma, germinomas, anterior communicating artery aneurysms, surgery/trauma near the anterior hypothalamus.

Clinical pearl: patients cannot be managed with desmopressin alone — they also need scheduled (not thirst-driven) fluid intake. Highlights why the OVLT is functionally critical for water homeostasis.
Oxytocin — Beyond Labor
Newer-recognized CNS actions of oxytocin and what disorders are being studied for oxytocin-based therapy?
Answer
Classic peripheral: uterine contraction (Ferguson reflex — positive feedback with cervical stretch); milk let-down reflex in breastfeeding.

Neuropeptide / CNS:
• Pair bonding, maternal behavior
• Social recognition / trust
• Memory (especially social)
• Anxiolytic effect
• Modulates appetite / metabolism
• Mild ↓ blood pressure

Therapeutic interest: autism spectrum disorder (social cognition), social anxiety, postpartum depression, PTSD. Trials use intranasal oxytocin (can reach the brain via the olfactory route). Efficacy is still uncertain — compelling mechanism but inconsistent clinical results.
LO 4 — McCune-Albright Syndrome
Mutation type, affected protein, and classic triad?
Answer
Somatic (post-zygotic, mosaic) activating mutation in GNAS → Gsα locked ON → uncontrolled cAMP in affected tissues.
Triad: precocious puberty + "coast of Maine" café-au-lait macules + polyostotic fibrous dysplasia.

Germline GNAS activation is embryonic lethal → disease survives only as a mosaic (patchy features). Constitutive cAMP in gonads → sex steroid production WITHOUT gonadotropins (peripheral precocious puberty — LH/FSH are LOW).
LO 4 — Cholera Toxin
What does cholera toxin ADP-ribosylate, and what's the clinical result?
Answer
ADP-ribosylates Gsα → locks Gs permanently in the GTP-bound active state → adenylyl cyclase constitutively ON → uncontrolled ↑cAMP → PKA phosphorylates CFTR → intestinal Cl⁻ secretion (Na⁺ + H₂O follow) → rice-water diarrhea, severe dehydration, hypokalemia, metabolic acidosis. Treatment: fluid/electrolyte replacement; doxycycline reduces bacterial load but doesn't reverse toxin.
LO 4 — Pertussis Toxin
What does pertussis toxin ADP-ribosylate, and what's the clinical result?
Answer
ADP-ribosylates Giα → locks Gi OFF → loss of Gi inhibition of adenylyl cyclase → relative ↑cAMP in immune cells + respiratory epithelium → whooping cough, paroxysmal coughing fits, inspiratory "whoop," lymphocytosis (Gi normally mediates immune cell trafficking). DTaP vaccine prevents.
LO 4 — Achondroplasia
Mutation, inheritance, and molecular mechanism?
Answer
Activating (gain-of-function) mutation in FGFR3 (a receptor tyrosine kinase) → constitutive FGF signaling → excessive inhibition of growth-plate chondrocyte proliferation → shortened long bones. Autosomal dominant; ~80% de novo (paternal germline). Heterozygous = classic phenotype (short limbs, rhizomelic, normal trunk, frontal bossing, midface hypoplasia, normal intelligence). Homozygous = embryonic lethal.
LO 5 — Receptor Downregulation / Leuprolide
Why does CONTINUOUS leuprolide suppress LH/FSH while PULSATILE GnRH stimulates them? Clinical applications?
Answer
Pulsatile GnRH (every ~90 min) maintains receptor sensitivity → normal LH/FSH.
Continuous GnRH agonism → receptor desensitization + internalization → ↓LH/FSH → medical castration.

Initial "flare": first 1–2 weeks produce a surge of LH/FSH → testosterone spike → pain in bone mets, urinary obstruction in prostate cancer. Give anti-androgen (bicalutamide, flutamide) during the flare window.

Uses: prostate cancer, endometriosis, uterine fibroids, central precocious puberty, IVF pretreatment. GnRH ANTAGONISTS (degarelix, cetrorelix) block receptor directly → NO flare.
LO 4 — Tamoxifen (SERM)
What is a SERM? Tamoxifen's tissue-specific actions?
Answer
SERM = selective estrogen receptor modulator — tissue-specific agonist or antagonist depending on local coactivator/corepressor availability.

Tamoxifen:
• BREAST — ANTAGONIST (used for ER+ breast cancer)
• BONE — AGONIST (preserves density, anti-osteoporotic)
• UTERUS — AGONIST → ↑ endometrial cancer risk (~2–3× after 5 yr)
• Also ↑ VTE risk, hot flashes

Typically used for 5 years in pre- or post-menopausal ER+ breast cancer. Metabolized to active endoxifen by CYP2D6 (poor metabolizers = reduced efficacy).
LO 4 — Raloxifene (SERM)
How does raloxifene differ from tamoxifen?
Answer
Raloxifene:
• BREAST — ANTAGONIST (like tamoxifen)
• BONE — AGONIST (prevents osteoporosis)
• UTERUS — ANTAGONIST ← KEY DIFFERENCE

No endometrial cancer risk → preferred for long-term post-menopausal osteoporosis. Still ↑ VTE risk and hot flashes. Also reduces invasive ER+ breast cancer risk in high-risk women.
LO 4 — Mifepristone (RU-486)
Receptor target, mechanism, clinical uses?
Answer
Progesterone receptor ANTAGONIST (competitive) + weak glucocorticoid receptor antagonist. Blocks endometrial support of pregnancy → shedding.

Uses:
• Medical abortion (with misoprostol) up to 10 weeks
• Cushing syndrome when surgery fails (anti-GR effect) — tests LO 6 nuclear receptor pharmacology
• Emergency contraception (off-label)
LO 4/6 — POMC Processing
What is POMC, what are its cleavage products, and how does this explain hyperpigmentation in Addison's?
Answer
POMC (pro-opiomelanocortin) is a 241-AA precursor in corticotropes, cleaved tissue-specifically → ACTH + β-endorphin + α-MSH (also β-lipotropin, γ-MSH).

Addison's disease: destroyed adrenal → no cortisol feedback → corticotropes synthesize POMC at high rate → ↑↑ACTH PLUS ↑↑α-MSH → melanocyte stimulation → skin darkening (palmar creases, buccal mucosa, old scars, sun-exposed areas). ACTH itself has weak MSH-like activity at very high concentrations.

Same mechanism in ectopic ACTH (SCLC) and in Nelson syndrome (post-adrenalectomy).
LO 4/6 — Paraneoplastic Endocrine Syndromes
Define paraneoplastic syndrome; give 3 classic ectopic-hormone examples.
Answer
Paraneoplastic syndrome = a malignancy secretes a hormone NORMALLY produced elsewhere (ectopic production), producing an endocrine syndrome not caused by the gland itself.

Classics:
• SCLC → ectopic ACTH (Cushing's, hyperpigmentation, hypokalemic alkalosis) + ectopic ADH (SIADH)
• Squamous cell lung cancer → PTHrP → hypercalcemia
• Carcinoid tumor (small bowel → liver mets) → serotonin + substance P → flushing + diarrhea + wheezing
• Pancreatic NET → insulinoma, glucagonoma, VIPoma, gastrinoma (Zollinger-Ellison)
• Renal cell carcinoma → erythropoietin → secondary polycythemia

Question 1 of 16 — Prolactinoma & Feedback
A 30-year-old woman presents with 12 months of amenorrhea, infertility, and galactorrhea. She is not pregnant. Serum prolactin 340 ng/mL. TSH 2.0, free T4 normal. MRI shows a 9-mm sellar mass. Which of the following is the best first-line therapy?
ATranssphenoidal adenoma resection
BCabergoline (dopamine D2 agonist)
COctreotide (somatostatin analog)
DLevothyroxine
EPituitary radiation
B — Cabergoline. Dopamine tonically inhibits prolactin release; D2 agonists (cabergoline, bromocriptine) shrink prolactinomas AND normalize PRL in >80% of cases. Surgery (A) is reserved for drug-intolerant, vision-threatening, or drug-resistant tumors. This uniquely "medical-first" approach reflects the special tonic-inhibition control of PRL (LO 4).
Question 2 of 16 — TSH/TRH Feedback
A 45-year-old woman has fatigue, cold intolerance, and weight gain. Labs: free T4 LOW, TSH 0.3 mIU/L (low), prolactin mildly elevated, morning cortisol LOW, ACTH low, FSH/LH low. MRI shows a 2.1-cm sellar mass. Which pattern best describes her thyroid axis dysfunction, and why is her prolactin elevated?
APrimary hypothyroidism; hyperprolactinemia from ↑TRH stimulating lactotropes
BSecondary hypothyroidism; hyperprolactinemia from stalk effect disrupting dopamine delivery
CTertiary hypothyroidism; prolactinoma co-secreting TSH and PRL
DEuthyroid sick syndrome; hyperprolactinemia from chronic illness
EPrimary adrenal insufficiency with pituitary reactive changes
B — Secondary hypothyroidism + stalk effect. LOW T4 with LOW (or inappropriately normal) TSH = pituitary failure. Add LOW ACTH and LOW FSH/LH → this is panhypopituitarism from a non-functioning macroadenoma compressing normal pituitary tissue. PRL RISES modestly (stalk effect): tumor compression of the stalk blocks dopamine delivery → lactotropes disinhibited. A would show HIGH TSH + modest PRL rise. True prolactinoma usually gives PRL >200 and less severe pan-pituitary suppression. Direct LO-5/LO-6 application.
Question 3 of 16 — Acromegaly Screening
A 48-year-old man reports enlargement of his hands, feet, and jaw over 3 years, new hypertension, and sleep apnea. You suspect acromegaly. Which single initial test is MOST reliable for screening, and why?
ARandom serum growth hormone level
BSerum IGF-1 level
COvernight dexamethasone suppression test
DRandom morning prolactin level
E24-hour urinary free cortisol
B — Serum IGF-1. GH secretion is highly PULSATILE (driven by GHRH pulses + ultra-short-loop autoregulation), so random GH may miss active disease. IGF-1 is produced by liver in response to GH and integrates GH exposure over 24 hours → stable index. Elevated IGF-1 → confirm with OGTT (normal GH suppresses with glucose; acromegaly does NOT). Reinforces why pulsatile hormones require integrated readouts (LO 5 pulsatility principle).
Question 4 of 16 — Cushing Localization
A 40-year-old woman with moon facies, truncal obesity, striae, and hypertension has 24h urinary free cortisol 4× normal. Low-dose dexamethasone fails to suppress cortisol. High-dose dexamethasone suppresses cortisol by 60%. ACTH is modestly elevated. What is the most likely diagnosis?
ACortisol-secreting adrenal adenoma
BPituitary ACTH-secreting adenoma (Cushing disease)
CEctopic ACTH from small-cell lung cancer
DExogenous glucocorticoid use
EPseudo-Cushing from alcoholism
B — Cushing disease. Classic test pattern: low-dose dex fails (any cortisol excess), high-dose dex SUPPRESSES → pituitary source still retains some feedback sensitivity. ACTH is mildly elevated. Ectopic ACTH (C) doesn't suppress at ANY dose + has higher ACTH + often hypokalemic alkalosis. Adrenal (A) and exogenous (D) have LOW ACTH (feedback-suppressed pituitary). Tests the LO-5 / LO-6 logic of feedback loops applied clinically.
Question 5 of 16 — Sheehan Syndrome
A 34-year-old woman is admitted 2 days after vaginal delivery complicated by 2.5 L postpartum hemorrhage requiring massive transfusion. She cannot breastfeed despite attempts. Over the next 2 months she develops fatigue, cold intolerance, constipation, and amenorrhea. Imaging reveals a shrunken pituitary. Which of the following best explains her failure to lactate as the earliest clinical clue?
AInfarction of the posterior pituitary, depriving the breast of oxytocin
BIschemic necrosis of the hypertrophied anterior pituitary → loss of prolactin
CAntibody-mediated destruction of lactotropes
DAcute hyperprolactinemia from stalk compression
EUterine atony with disseminated intravascular coagulation
B — Sheehan syndrome. Pregnancy doubles lactotrope mass, outstripping the portal blood supply. When obstetric hemorrhage causes hypotension, the hypertrophied anterior pituitary (dependent on the low-pressure portal system) infarcts. Prolactin loss → lactation failure is the first sign. Later, loss of GH → fatigue; FSH/LH → amenorrhea; TSH → hypothyroidism; ACTH → adrenal insufficiency. The POSTERIOR pituitary has a different (hypophyseal artery) blood supply and is typically SPARED.
Question 6 of 16 — Continuous Leuprolide
A 66-year-old man with metastatic prostate cancer is started on continuous leuprolide injections. Over the first 7 days he reports worsening bone pain, urinary obstruction, and a rise in testosterone. By week 4, symptoms improve and testosterone is at castrate levels. Which mechanism explains this biphasic response?
ALeuprolide directly stimulates Leydig cells in the testis
BTransient stimulation of LH/FSH followed by GnRH receptor down-regulation due to continuous (non-pulsatile) agonism
CImmunologic rejection of gonadotropes after repeated injection
DDevelopment of anti-leuprolide antibodies after week 3
EConversion of androgens to estrogens by aromatase
B. Pulsatile GnRH is the physiologic signal. Leuprolide given CONTINUOUSLY acts as an agonist at GnRH receptors initially (surge in LH/FSH/T → the "tumor flare") but after ~1–2 weeks, constant stimulation DOWN-REGULATES GnRH receptors on gonadotropes → LH/FSH fall → castrate testosterone. Add-on flutamide (androgen receptor antagonist) prevents the flare. Directly demonstrates the LO-5 principle that pulsatility matters.
Question 7 of 16 · LO 4/6 — Ectopic ACTH & Dex Suppression
A 45-year-old man presents with central obesity, purple striae, hypertension, and easy bruising. Morning cortisol 28 μg/dL (normal <18), ACTH 95 pg/mL (elevated). Abdominal CT shows no adrenal mass. Chest X-ray reveals a small solitary pulmonary nodule. Neither low-dose nor high-dose dexamethasone suppresses cortisol. Which best explains this clinical picture?
APituitary adenoma secreting excess ACTH
BEctopic ACTH secretion from a lung neoplasm (paraneoplastic syndrome)
CPrimary adrenal adenoma with autonomous cortisol production
DAdrenal insufficiency with secondary pituitary compensation
EHypothyroidism causing compensatory ACTH elevation
B — Ectopic ACTH from small cell lung cancer (SCLC). Tumor-derived ACTH is NOT subject to pituitary feedback, so neither low- nor high-dose dexamethasone suppresses it. Pituitary Cushing's (A) would partially suppress at high dose. Adrenal adenoma (C) and exogenous glucocorticoid (D/E) would have LOW ACTH. SCLC is notorious for paraneoplastic hormone secretion (ectopic ACTH + SIADH). Also classic: hyperpigmentation from POMC cleavage → ACTH + α-MSH simultaneously elevated.
Question 8 of 16 · LO 4 — McCune-Albright
An 8-year-old girl is brought to clinic with vaginal bleeding and breast development. Physical exam reveals café-au-lait spots on the trunk with irregular ("coast of Maine") borders and multiple bony deformities. Pelvic ultrasound shows ovarian cysts. Serum LH and FSH are LOW. Which genetic mechanism underlies this constellation?
AGermline inactivating mutation in p53
BSomatic activating mutation in GNAS (Gsα) — constitutively active Gs
CLoss-of-function mutation in FGFR3
DGermline activating mutation in RET
EInactivating PTEN mutation
B — McCune-Albright syndrome. Somatic (post-zygotic) activating mutation of GNAS → constitutively active Gsα → tonic cAMP in affected tissues. Precocious puberty comes from Gs activation in GONADS (independent of LH/FSH) — hence gonadotropins are LOW (peripheral precocious puberty). Café-au-lait from Gs in melanocytes. Fibrous dysplasia from Gs in bone. Germline mutations are embryonic lethal — disease survives only as mosaicism. RET (D) → MEN 2; p53 (A) → Li-Fraumeni.
Question 9 of 16 · LO 5 — Leuprolide Flare & Anti-Androgen
A 72-year-old man with prostate cancer is started on leuprolide (GnRH agonist). He is given bicalutamide (anti-androgen) concurrently. On day 2 his testosterone RISES dramatically before declining over 2 weeks. Which explains BOTH the initial testosterone surge AND the rationale for concurrent anti-androgen?
ALeuprolide is initially a GnRH antagonist, then switches to agonist
BInitial agonism → LH surge → testosterone flare; continuous agonism → receptor downregulation → suppression. Anti-androgen blocks tumor stimulation during flare.
CAnti-androgens prevent the surge by direct testicular inhibition
DLeuprolide activates a different GPCR pathway initially
ETestosterone elevation is unrelated to GnRH receptor activation
B. Biphasic GnRH-agonist effect: (1) initial continuous agonism → LH/FSH surge → testosterone spike (FLARE — bone pain, urinary retention, spinal cord compression in metastatic disease). (2) Sustained agonism → GnRH receptor desensitization + downregulation → LH/FSH fall → castrate testosterone. Bicalutamide (AR antagonist) blocks peripheral androgen action during the flare window. GnRH ANTAGONISTS (degarelix) skip the flare entirely by blocking the receptor directly.
Question 10 of 16 · LO 4 — Tamoxifen Endometrial Risk
A 55-year-old postmenopausal woman is treated with tamoxifen for ER+ breast cancer. After 3 years she develops endometrial adenocarcinoma. Bone density remains normal. Which property of tamoxifen explains this side-effect profile?
APure ER antagonist in all tissues
BSERM: ER antagonist in breast, ER agonist in uterus and bone
CIrreversible binding prevents all estrogen signaling
DActivates a different ER isoform in uterus
EEndometrial cancer is age-related, not drug-related
B. Tamoxifen is a Selective Estrogen Receptor Modulator — its agonist/antagonist activity depends on local coactivator/corepressor availability. BREAST (antagonist) → tumor growth blocked. UTERUS (agonist) → endometrial proliferation → ↑ cancer risk. BONE (agonist) → density preserved. Raloxifene differs by being an ANTAGONIST in uterus as well → preferred long-term for postmenopausal osteoporosis.
Question 11 of 16 · LO 4/5 — TRH and Prolactin
A 28-year-old woman presents with amenorrhea and galactorrhea. Prolactin 85 ng/mL (normal <25). TSH 8.2 mIU/L (normal 0.4–4.0). Free T4 0.6 ng/dL (low). Which is the PRIMARY mechanism driving her hyperprolactinemia?
AProlactin-secreting pituitary adenoma
BPrimary hypothyroidism → ↑TRH → stimulation of both TSH and prolactin
CDopamine deficiency from hypothalamic lesion
DExcess GnRH suppressing dopamine
EPrimary gonadal dysfunction
B. TRH has TWO pituitary targets: TSH (main) AND prolactin (lactotropes have TRH receptors). In primary hypothyroidism, low T4 removes feedback → ↑TRH → ↑ both TSH AND prolactin. Treating the hypothyroidism (levothyroxine) normalizes BOTH labs. Always check thyroid function in hyperprolactinemia. A prolactinoma would typically have prolactin >200 AND normal TSH. Tests the LO-4 coupling of hypothalamic releasers to multiple pituitary hormones.
Question 12 of 16 · LO 4 — hCG / TSH Cross-Reactivity
A patient with choriocarcinoma develops thyrotoxicosis without thyroid disease. TSH suppressed <0.01, free T4 elevated. hCG 500,000 mIU/L. Which receptor does hCG activate to explain thyrotoxicosis?
AGH receptor (JAK-STAT)
BTSH receptor — cross-reactivity due to shared α-subunit
CNuclear T3 receptor in thyroid follicles
DFGFR3 in thyroid tissue
EInsulin receptor with compensatory T3 rise
B. hCG, TSH, LH, FSH are all glycoprotein hormones that share an identical α-subunit. At extremely high hCG levels (molar pregnancy, choriocarcinoma, hyperemesis gravidarum), partial β-subunit similarity allows hCG to cross-activate the TSH receptor → thyroid stimulation → T4/T3 release → secondary thyrotoxicosis. TSH is appropriately suppressed by feedback — but the thyroid is being driven non-physiologically by hCG. Treat the underlying malignancy.
Question 13 of 16 · LO 4 — Cholera Toxin Mechanism
A 45-year-old man returning from Southeast Asia presents with severe watery diarrhea after eating contaminated seafood. Stool osmotic gap is normal (secretory). Labs: K⁺ 2.1 (severe hypokalemia), pH 7.18 (metabolic acidosis). Which toxin–G-protein interaction best explains his intestinal secretion?
APertussis toxin → Gi inactivation → unopposed adenylyl cyclase → ↑cAMP → Cl⁻ secretion
BCholera toxin → Gs ADP-ribosylation (locks ON) → adenylyl cyclase constitutive → ↑cAMP → Cl⁻ secretion
CBotulinum toxin → SNARE cleavage → loss of parasympathetic tone
DStaphylococcal enterotoxin → direct epithelial damage → bloody diarrhea
ERotavirus → villous destruction → fat malabsorption
B. Cholera toxin ADP-ribosylates the α-subunit of Gs → locks Gs in GTP-bound active state → adenylyl cyclase permanently ON → massive cAMP rise → PKA phosphorylates CFTR → net Cl⁻ + Na⁺ + H₂O secretion → rice-water diarrhea. Pertussis affects Gi in respiratory epithelium (different organ system, different G-protein). Both are bacterial exotoxins that modify G-proteins at a conserved arginine residue.
Question 14 of 16 · LO 4 — Achondroplasia Mechanism
An infant is born with short stature and rhizomelic limb shortening; intelligence is normal. Genetic testing reveals a heterozygous ACTIVATING mutation in FGFR3. Parents have normal height. Which inheritance and molecular mechanism explain this presentation?
AAutosomal recessive loss-of-function → lack of bone growth signaling
BAutosomal dominant gain-of-function → constitutive FGFR3 signaling → excessive inhibition of bone growth
CX-linked dominant → only males affected
DMitochondrial inheritance → maternal only
ESomatic mutation → confined to affected tissues
B. Achondroplasia = activating (gain-of-function) FGFR3 mutation — a receptor tyrosine kinase. Constitutive kinase activity inhibits growth-plate chondrocyte proliferation → shortened long bones. Autosomal dominant; heterozygous = classic phenotype; homozygous = embryonic lethal. ~80% de novo mutations (paternal germline) → two normal-height parents can have an affected child. Tests LO 6 receptor signaling applied to skeletal development.
Question 15 of 16 · LO 4 — Dopamine D2 Antagonist & Prolactin
A 52-year-old man with schizophrenia is started on risperidone. Within weeks he develops gynecomastia and sexual dysfunction. Labs: prolactin 88 ng/mL (elevated), testosterone 180 ng/dL (low). Which mechanism most likely explains this adverse effect?

Step 1 Traps: Each statement is TRUE or FALSE — many reverse or mix facts that routinely trip students up.
Trap 01 — Posterior Pituitary Blood Supply
"In Sheehan syndrome, the posterior pituitary infarcts before the anterior pituitary because it has a higher metabolic demand."
FALSE. The posterior pituitary is SPARED in Sheehan syndrome — it has its own direct arterial supply from hypophyseal arteries. The ANTERIOR pituitary relies on the LOW-PRESSURE hypophyseal portal system, which is especially vulnerable to hypotension, especially after pregnancy-induced hypertrophy. That's why loss of prolactin (failure to lactate) is the FIRST sign.
Trap 02 — Prolactin Control
"Dopamine D2 agonists like cabergoline INCREASE prolactin secretion by stimulating lactotropes, making them the treatment of choice for prolactinoma."
FALSE — reversed. Dopamine is the PROLACTIN-INHIBITING hormone. D2 AGONISTS (cabergoline, bromocriptine) DECREASE prolactin by mimicking the tonic inhibition. They shrink prolactinomas because lactotropes express D2 and respond to dopaminergic signaling with suppression + apoptosis.
Trap 03 — Cushing Dex Test
"Ectopic ACTH-secreting tumors suppress cortisol under HIGH-dose dexamethasone because the pituitary-adrenal axis remains feedback-sensitive."
FALSE. Ectopic tumors (e.g., small-cell lung cancer) produce ACTH INDEPENDENTLY of any feedback — they do NOT suppress at any dose of dex. It's pituitary Cushing disease that suppresses at HIGH dose (retained but set-point-shifted feedback). This is the specific logic the dex test exploits: "Does the source still listen to cortisol?" Pituitary says "yes" (with enough push); ectopic says "no."
Trap 04 — Pituitary Apoplexy
"In pituitary apoplexy, levothyroxine should be administered immediately before steroid replacement because the hypothyroid state can depress consciousness."
FALSE — potentially fatal reversal. ALWAYS give hydrocortisone FIRST, then thyroid. Thyroid hormone accelerates metabolism and increases cortisol clearance. Giving T4 to an ACTH-deficient patient can precipitate adrenal crisis. Rule: "Steroids before thyroid."
Trap 05 — Stalk Section
"After complete transection of the pituitary stalk, all six anterior pituitary hormones fall precipitously, including prolactin."
FALSE. Prolactin RISES because dopamine (its tonic inhibitor) no longer reaches lactotropes. All OTHER anterior hormones fall (loss of their releasing hormones). This unique exception is a testing favorite — reflects that PRL's default setting is "ON" and the hypothalamus keeps it OFF.
Trap 06 — Central vs Nephrogenic DI
"Administration of desmopressin (DDAVP) significantly raises urine osmolality in both central and nephrogenic DI, so it cannot be used to distinguish between them."
FALSE. Desmopressin is the KEY distinguisher. Central DI RESPONDS (urine osm ↑ >50%) because the missing piece is ADH, and we supply it exogenously. Nephrogenic DI does NOT respond (kidney can't recognize ADH). A partial intermediate response suggests partial central DI or partial nephrogenic.
Trap 07 — LH Surge Feedback
"The LH surge that triggers ovulation is caused by rising progesterone levels exerting positive feedback on GnRH and the pituitary."
FALSE. The LH surge is driven by rising ESTRADIOL at the end of the follicular phase → switches from negative to positive feedback. Progesterone is LOW in the follicular phase and rises AFTER ovulation under LH's influence (corpus luteum). After ovulation, progesterone then exerts NEGATIVE feedback to prevent additional surges during luteal phase.
Trap 08 — CVO Physiology
"Because the hypothalamus sits above the blood-brain barrier, hypothalamic osmoreceptors can directly sense plasma sodium levels through the main cortex."
FALSE. Most of the hypothalamus HAS a BBB. Osmoreceptor neurons are localized to specific circumventricular organs — especially the OVLT (organum vasculosum of the lamina terminalis) — which uniquely LACK a BBB. These project back to the supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei to drive ADH release and thirst. Damage to this narrow region causes adipsic DI (loss of thirst drive plus loss of ADH response), which is far more dangerous than classic central DI.
Trap 09 — Adrenal Axis Misconception
"A patient with pan-hypopituitarism from Sheehan syndrome will be unable to produce epinephrine in response to stress because their ACTH-driven adrenal output has failed."
FALSE. ACTH controls the adrenal CORTEX (cortisol, aldosterone, androgens) — not the MEDULLA. The adrenal medulla is a modified sympathetic ganglion, innervated directly by preganglionic sympathetic fibers. Sheehan syndrome abolishes cortisol production, but catecholamine release from chromaffin cells is preserved. This separation explains why Addison's/hypopituitarism patients develop hypoglycemia and hypotension (from cortisol loss) but maintain a tachycardic stress response.
Trap 10 · LO 4 — Cholera vs. Gi
"Cholera toxin activates Gi, which increases intracellular cAMP and drives intestinal secretion."
FALSE. Cholera toxin ADP-ribosylates Gs (NOT Gi), locking Gs permanently ON → adenylyl cyclase constitutively active → cAMP rises. It is pertussis toxin that affects Gi (inactivates it). Common mix-up: Cholera = Gs ON, Pertussis = Gi OFF.
Trap 11 · LO 4 — Pertussis Mechanism
"Pertussis toxin ACTIVATES Gi, which increases cAMP in the respiratory epithelium to cause whooping cough."
FALSE. Pertussis toxin INACTIVATES Gi (ADP-ribosylates Gi → locks it OFF). Because Gi normally INHIBITS adenylyl cyclase, losing Gi inhibition causes a relative ↑cAMP → immune/epithelial dysfunction, lymphocytosis, prolonged cough. The mechanism is "loss of brakes," not direct activation.
Trap 12 · LO 4 — Achondroplasia Mutation
"Achondroplasia is caused by a LOSS-OF-FUNCTION mutation in FGFR3 that removes bone-growth inhibition."
FALSE. Achondroplasia is a GAIN-OF-FUNCTION (activating) mutation in FGFR3 — the receptor is constitutively active, signaling even without ligand. Because FGFR3 normally inhibits growth-plate chondrocyte proliferation, more FGFR3 signaling = MORE inhibition = SHORT bones. Loss-of-function would give TALL stature (opposite phenotype).
Trap 13 · LO 5 — Leuprolide Onset
"Leuprolide (a GnRH agonist) immediately suppresses LH and FSH secretion from the anterior pituitary."
FALSE. Leuprolide initially STIMULATES GnRH receptors → LH/FSH surge (flare) in the first 1–2 weeks. Only with SUSTAINED continuous stimulation do the receptors down-regulate → LH/FSH fall → castrate-level sex steroids. In prostate cancer, concurrent anti-androgen (bicalutamide) blunts the flare. GnRH antagonists (degarelix, cetrorelix) work immediately without flare.
Trap 14 · LO 4 — SERM Misconception
"Tamoxifen is a pure estrogen receptor antagonist in all tissues and therefore cannot cause endometrial hyperplasia."
FALSE. Tamoxifen is a SERM (selective ER modulator): antagonist in BREAST, agonist in UTERUS and BONE. Its uterine agonist activity drives endometrial proliferation → increased endometrial cancer risk (~2–3×). Raloxifene differs — antagonist in both breast AND uterus → preferred for long-term postmenopausal osteoporosis. Tissue selectivity reflects local coactivator availability, not drug identity alone.
Trap 15 · LO 4 — McCune-Albright Inheritance
"McCune-Albright syndrome arises from a GERMLINE activating GNAS mutation inherited from one of the parents."
FALSE. The mutation is SOMATIC (post-zygotic) — occurring during early development → mosaic distribution of affected cells. Germline activating GNAS is embryonic lethal, so the syndrome exists ONLY in mosaic form. This is why clinical features are patchy/unilateral and highly variable between patients. Contrast pseudohypoparathyroidism 1a: germline INACTIVATING GNAS (tolerable if maternally inherited).
Trap 16 · LO 4 — Pseudohypoparathyroidism Calcium
"Pseudohypoparathyroidism 1a presents with HIGH PTH and HIGH calcium because the parathyroid glands are overactive."
FALSE. PHP 1a has HIGH PTH (compensatory) but LOW calcium and HIGH phosphate — because the inactivating Gsα mutation prevents PTH from signaling at kidney/bone. It is a peripheral hormone resistance, not over-secretion. Albright hereditary osteodystrophy features (short stature, short 4th/5th metacarpals, round face) co-occur. Maternal imprinting: phenotype only if the mutation is inherited from the MOTHER.
Trap 17 · LO 4 — ANP Signaling
"ANP (atrial natriuretic peptide) signals through a Gs-coupled GPCR to raise cAMP and promote natriuresis."
FALSE. ANP/BNP use a membrane guanylyl cyclase receptor → produce cGMP (NOT cAMP) → activate PKG → natriuresis + vasodilation + ↓aldosterone + ↓renin. Only soluble guanylyl cyclase uses NO as its ligand. cAMP comes from Gs-coupled GPCRs (e.g., PTH, glucagon, ACTH, TSH). Classic exam trap: cGMP hormones are ANP/BNP/NO; cAMP hormones are listed by "FLAT ChAMP."
Trap 18 · LO 4 — hCG/TSH Subunit
"hCG and TSH share the same β-subunit, which is why hCG can cause thyrotoxicosis in molar pregnancy."
FALSE — reversed. hCG and TSH share the identical α-subunit; β-subunits are UNIQUE. Pregnancy tests detect β-hCG precisely for this reason. Thyrotoxicosis in molar pregnancy reflects partial similarity of hCG-β to TSH-β that allows cross-reactivity at the TSH receptor when hCG is astronomical (>100,000 mIU/mL).
Trap 19 · LO 5 — Continuous GnRH
"Continuous GnRH agonist therapy INCREASES LH and FSH throughout treatment because it is the opposite of a GnRH antagonist."
FALSE. Continuous GnRH agonism causes an INITIAL LH/FSH surge (flare) but within ~2 weeks sustains receptor DOWN-REGULATION → LH/FSH fall to castrate levels. Antagonists block receptors directly → immediate suppression without flare. The critical concept: pulsatility matters for GnRH — continuous agonism is effectively suppressive after the flare window.
Trap 20 · LO 4 — RU-486 Activity
"RU-486 (mifepristone) is a progesterone receptor AGONIST and supports pregnancy."
FALSE. Mifepristone is a competitive PR ANTAGONIST → blocks progesterone's endometrial support → pregnancy termination (when combined with misoprostol). Also weak GR antagonist — used for refractory Cushing syndrome. Pure antagonist at both nuclear receptor targets.
Trap 21 · LO 4 — JAK Kinase Location
"JAK kinase is an intrinsic part of cytokine receptors like the GH receptor — the kinase is built into the receptor polypeptide."
FALSE. Cytokine receptors (GH, prolactin, EPO, cytokine receptors) have NO intrinsic kinase activity. When the ligand binds, they recruit cytoplasmic JAK kinases (most commonly JAK2) — which then transphosphorylate the receptor + STAT proteins. Contrast RTKs (insulin, FGFR) where the kinase IS intrinsic to the receptor. Laron syndrome = GH receptor mutation → failure to recruit JAK2 → no IGF-1.

LO 4 · Prolactin
Prolactinoma — The "Medical-First" Pituitary Tumor
The most common functional pituitary adenoma. Because dopamine TONICALLY suppresses PRL, a D2 agonist can SHRINK the tumor and normalize levels — the only pituitary adenoma for which medical therapy is first-line.
Female pattern: amenorrhea (PRL suppresses GnRH → ↓LH/FSH) + galactorrhea + infertility → detected early as a microadenoma.
Male pattern: decreased libido, erectile dysfunction, visual loss (macroadenoma at diagnosis — symptoms of gonadal suppression often ignored longer).
Treatment: cabergoline 0.25 mg twice weekly → >80% normalize in months; tumor shrinks. Surgery only if intolerant or drug-resistant.
Pregnancy: microadenomas rarely grow; macroadenomas may expand (stop cabergoline, monitor).
LO 4 · GH
Acromegaly & Gigantism — GH-Secreting Adenoma
GH excess causes growth of soft tissues, bone, and organs. Before epiphyseal closure → gigantism (tall stature). After closure → acromegaly (jaw, hands, feet, organomegaly; closed plates can't extend length).
Metabolic: GH antagonizes insulin → impaired glucose tolerance / diabetes. Cardiomegaly with diastolic dysfunction → leading cause of death. Sleep apnea. HTN. Colonic polyps/cancer risk ↑.
Dx: IGF-1 screens; OGTT confirms (GH fails to suppress <1 ng/mL with oral glucose). MRI sella.
Tx:
• Surgery (transsphenoidal) — first line
• Octreotide / lanreotide — somatostatin analogs (remember: somatostatin = GHIH)
• Pegvisomant — GH-receptor antagonist (blocks downstream effects)
• Cabergoline — some response, especially in GH/PRL co-secretors
LO 5/6 · Cortisol Axis
Cushing Disease — The High-Dose Dex Exception
Pituitary ACTH-secreting adenoma retains some feedback sensitivity — SUPPRESSES with high-dose dexamethasone. Contrast with ectopic ACTH (feedback-independent). This is a direct clinical application of feedback-loop logic.
Screening tests: 24-hour urine free cortisol, late-night salivary cortisol, 1-mg overnight dex. All are "is there Cushing?" tests.
Localization: once Cushing confirmed, measure ACTH. If HIGH → pituitary or ectopic. Differentiate with high-dose dex test + inferior petrosal sinus sampling (IPSS — most accurate).
Treatment: transsphenoidal resection of adenoma. Ketoconazole / metyrapone / mifepristone / pasireotide if unresectable.
Key clinical clue: hyperpigmentation suggests HIGH ACTH (POMC cleavage also yields MSH).
LO 2 · ADH
Central DI — When the Hypothalamus Stops Making ADH
Trauma, surgery, craniopharyngioma, stalk lesions, idiopathic autoimmune. Loss of SON/PVN ADH production → polyuria with dilute urine + hypernatremia.
Water deprivation test: both central and nephrogenic fail to concentrate urine. Desmopressin challenge resolves central (kidney works if given ADH) but not nephrogenic (kidney problem).
Triphasic post-op DI: (1) acute DI from neuronal shock (0–5 days); (2) transient SIADH as dying neurons dump stored ADH (5–10 days); (3) permanent DI (if extensive). Sodium MUST be monitored carefully to avoid overcorrection-induced cerebral edema or osmotic demyelination.
Tx: desmopressin (oral, nasal, SC) — selective V2 agonist, no V1 vasoconstriction.
LO 4 · FSH/LH
Kallmann Syndrome — Migration Failure
GnRH neurons ORIGINATE in the olfactory placode and migrate with olfactory fibers into the hypothalamus. KAL1 (ANOS1) mutation disrupts migration → absent GnRH + absent olfactory bulbs.
Presentation: primary amenorrhea / failure to undergo puberty + anosmia (often self-unrecognized). Boys: micropenis, cryptorchidism, absent pubertal growth spurt. Midline defects (cleft lip/palate) common.
Labs: LOW GnRH, LOW LH/FSH, LOW sex steroids = hypogonadotropic hypogonadism.
Tx:
• Testosterone/estrogen replacement to induce puberty and maintain bone density
• Pulsatile GnRH pump for fertility
• HCG + FSH for induction of spermatogenesis / ovulation

Highlights LO 2/4 (hypothalamic nuclei develop from olfactory lineage).
LO 6 · Iatrogenic Axis Suppression
Chronic Steroids → HPA Axis Suppression
Exogenous glucocorticoids bind the same receptor as cortisol → long-loop suppression of CRH and ACTH → adrenal atrophy. Abrupt cessation can precipitate adrenal crisis.
Risk threshold: prednisone >5 mg/day for >3 weeks, or equivalent = significant HPA suppression. Full recovery may take months.
Taper rule: taper by ~10% every 1–2 weeks. Switch to alternate-day dosing. Never stop suddenly.
Stress-dose coverage: patients on chronic steroids need stress-dose hydrocortisone for surgery, severe illness, major trauma (body can't mount a cortisol response).
Same logic applies to: anabolic steroids (HPG suppression → testicular atrophy, azoospermia) and exogenous thyroid hormone (TSH suppression).
LO 1/2 · CVOs
Circumventricular Organs as Endocrine & Drug Gateways
CVOs lack a BBB — the median eminence, posterior pituitary, OVLT, subfornical organ, pineal, and area postrema. They let the brain directly sample plasma hormones and let certain drugs reach CNS tissue without tight junction penetration.
Clinical and pharmacologic implications:
• Intranasal desmopressin works because the posterior pituitary has fenestrated capillaries; topical formulations also benefit from the nearby median eminence.
• Ondansetron, aprepitant, metoclopramide all target the AREA POSTREMA (CTZ) — outside the BBB. That's why systemic dosing is effective against chemotherapy nausea.
• Lesions of the OVLT — from craniopharyngioma, germinoma, or surgical damage — cause ADIPSIC DI: loss of both ADH response AND thirst drive → severe hypernatremia. Requires scheduled water intake, not just desmopressin.
• Pineal germinoma → Parinaud syndrome (upward gaze palsy, light-near dissociation) + precocious puberty (from ectopic hCG-like β-hCG).
• Systemic melatonin doesn't need to cross the BBB at the pineal itself, but its sleep-modulating action does involve suprachiasmatic nucleus targeting.
LO 4 · Direct SNS Axis
The Adrenal Medulla — Hypothalamus → SNS, Not Hypothalamus → Pituitary
The adrenal medulla is the one peripheral endocrine organ controlled directly by the SYMPATHETIC nervous system — a third hypothalamic output pathway (beyond portal and posterior-pituitary routes). This is why ACTH deficiency (pituitary lesions) spares catecholamine production, while sympathetic blockade (ganglionic blockers, surgical sympathectomy) ablates it.
Key anatomy:
• Hypothalamic autonomic neurons project to the intermediolateral cell column of the thoracolumbar spinal cord.
• Preganglionic sympathetic fibers (T5–T11) pass THROUGH the celiac ganglion without synapsing and terminate directly on adrenal medulla chromaffin cells — essentially a modified sympathetic ganglion (no postganglionic neuron).
• ACh released on chromaffin cells triggers exocytosis of preformed epinephrine (80%) + NE (20%) within SECONDS.

Clinical relevance:
• Pheochromocytoma — autonomous chromaffin tumor → catecholamine excess independent of sympathetic input.
• Adrenal insufficiency (Addison's) affects CORTEX (cortisol, aldosterone, androgens) but preserves medullary catecholamines → this is part of why patients still have tachycardia responses under stress.
• Pre-op pheo management (α-blockade before β-blockade) is critical — unopposed α from continuing catecholamine release can precipitate hypertensive crisis.

Rules of the Axis High-Yield
Steroids before thyroid: in panhypopituitarism, always give cortisol BEFORE T4 (T4 accelerates cortisol clearance).
PRL is the exception: only AP hormone under tonic INHIBITION → stalk section RAISES it, not lowers.
Pulsatile GnRH matters: continuous GnRH = down-regulation (medical castration). Pulsatile = stimulation (fertility).
Posterior pituitary spared in Sheehan: different blood supply (hypophyseal arteries, not portal).
Hook effect: extremely high PRL may read falsely low — dilute the sample.
High-dose dex SUPPRESSES Cushing disease, not ectopic.
Taper steroids: >3 weeks of supraphysiologic dosing → HPA suppression; abrupt stop = crisis.

Traps Corner Common Errors
Do NOT reverse: PRL goes UP (not down) with stalk section.
Do NOT reverse: acidophils = GH + PRL; basophils = other 4.
Do NOT reverse: Rathke's pouch = anterior (oral ectoderm); neuroectoderm = posterior.
Do NOT confuse: Kallmann (low LH/FSH, GnRH migration defect) with hypergonadotropic hypogonadism (Klinefelter, Turner — gonadal failure with HIGH LH/FSH).
Do NOT confuse: SIADH (hypo-osmolar hyponatremia, concentrated urine) with cerebral salt wasting (similar Na but VOLUME DEPLETED).
Do NOT confuse: low-dose dex fails = "there's Cushing"; high-dose dex fails = "ectopic/adrenal source."

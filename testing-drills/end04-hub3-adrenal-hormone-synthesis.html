Question 1 of 5 — LO 1 · Steroid Nomenclature
A researcher labels four adrenocortical metabolites with radio-tracers. Compound W has a fused cyclopenta-perhydrophenanthrene (gonane) 17-carbon core with an additional 4-carbon side chain at C17, giving 21 carbons total. Compound X has the same core but only a 19-carbon skeleton (no side chain), with a ketone at C17. Compound Y has 18 carbons, an aromatic A ring, and a hydroxyl at C3. Compound Z is the 27-carbon unmodified starting material. Which set of identifications is most consistent with these descriptions?
AW = cortisol · X = DHEA · Y = estradiol · Z = cholesterol
BW = testosterone · X = aldosterone · Y = estrone · Z = pregnenolone
CW = aldosterone · X = cortisol · Y = testosterone · Z = cholesterol
DW = estradiol · X = cortisol · Y = DHEA · Z = cholesterol
EW = DHEA · X = cortisol · Y = testosterone · Z = pregnenolone
A. LO 1 nomenclature: all steroids derive from the gonane 4-ring nucleus. The carbon count identifies the class: C21 steroids include progestins + mineralocorticoids + glucocorticoids (cortisol), C19 steroids are androgens (DHEA, testosterone; ketone at C17 is typical), C18 steroids are estrogens (aromatic A ring → loss of the C19 methyl). Cholesterol itself is C27 before desmolase cleaves its tail to give C21 pregnenolone.
Question 2 of 5 — LO 2 · Common First Step (StAR + Desmolase)
A rare clinical syndrome called lipoid congenital adrenal hyperplasia presents with salt-wasting, low aldosterone, low cortisol, and low sex steroids; both 46,XY and 46,XX infants are phenotypically female. Histology shows adrenals massively expanded with cholesterol-laden lipid droplets but minimal steroid output. Genetic testing finds a loss-of-function mutation in the STAR gene. Which step of steroid biosynthesis is most directly impaired?
AConversion of HMG-CoA to mevalonate
BShuttling of cholesterol from outer to inner mitochondrial membrane
CAromatization of testosterone to estradiol
D5α-reduction of testosterone to DHT
E11β-hydroxylation of 11-deoxycortisol to cortisol
B — StAR shuttles cholesterol across the mitochondrial gap. LO 2 teaches that the rate-limiting step of ALL steroid biosynthesis is cholesterol → pregnenolone by desmolase (CYP11A1 / P450scc) on the inner mitochondrial membrane. Because cholesterol is stored in cytoplasmic droplets, physical access to desmolase is gated by StAR (Steroidogenic Acute Regulatory protein) — it transfers cholesterol from the OUTER to INNER mitochondrial membrane. ACTH → cAMP → PKA → phosphorylates StAR → burst of steroidogenesis. Lose StAR and ALL downstream steroid classes collapse, while cholesterol accumulates in the cytoplasm (the characteristic "lipoid" adrenals). Choice A is upstream (cholesterol synthesis).
Question 3 of 5 — LO 3 · Zona Glomerulosa Enzyme Profile
A researcher immunostains three adrenocortical zones for steroidogenic enzymes. Zone A stains positively for aldosterone synthase (CYP11B2) but not for 17α-hydroxylase (CYP17). Zone B stains for CYP17 and for 11β-hydroxylase (CYP11B1). Zone C stains for CYP17 plus strong 17,20-lyase activity. Which hormone is produced by Zone A?
ACortisol
BDHEA
CAldosterone
DAndrostenedione
EEstradiol
C — Aldosterone (zona glomerulosa). LO 3 map: ZG = "salt" (aldosterone, needs CYP11B2, lacks CYP17 so cannot make glucocorticoids or androgens), ZF = "sugar" (cortisol, needs both CYP17 hydroxylase activity and CYP11B1), ZR = "sex" (DHEA/androstenedione, driven by CYP17's additional 17,20-lyase activity). Zone C in the stem = ZR; Zone B = ZF; Zone A = ZG. The key mnemonic is "GFR — salt, sugar, sex, the deeper you go the sweeter it gets."
Question 4 of 5 — LO 3/4 · Zona Reticularis (17,20-Lyase)
A 22-year-old woman presents with premature adrenarche at age 4, persistent hirsutism, and oligomenorrhea. Her DHEA-S is markedly elevated. Ultrasound shows normal ovaries; ovarian-vein sampling is non-localizing. A diagnosis of "functional adrenal hyperandrogenism" is made. Which adrenal cortical zone and enzymatic activity is most directly responsible for her elevated DHEA-S?
AZona glomerulosa · aldosterone synthase
BZona fasciculata · 11β-hydroxylase
CZona reticularis · 17,20-lyase activity of CYP17
DAdrenal medulla · PNMT
EZona fasciculata · 21-hydroxylase
C. The zona reticularis is the primary source of DHEA and DHEA-sulfate via strong 17,20-lyase activity of CYP17 (which the ZF expresses only weakly). DHEA-S is the classic marker for adrenal androgen production (ovarian androgens are predominantly testosterone/androstenedione), and markedly elevated DHEA-S specifically localizes the problem to the adrenal — often an ZR-dominant lesion or adrenal hyperplasia. Note: PNMT in choice D is in the medulla (NE → Epi), unrelated to sex steroids.
Question 5 of 5 — LO 3 · Embryology & Zonation (Cortex vs Medulla)
During the Frank Starling lecture on development, students learn that the adrenal GLAND develops from two distinct embryologic origins. One origin gives rise to a tissue that responds to ACTH and produces steroid hormones; the other origin gives rise to a tissue that responds to preganglionic sympathetic fibers and produces catecholamines. Which of the following best describes the embryologic origin of the adrenal cortex?
ANeural crest cells that migrate from the dorsal neural tube
BMesoderm from the intermediate / urogenital ridge region
COral ectoderm (Rathke pouch) similar to the anterior pituitary
DEndoderm of the foregut
ENeural tube floor plate
B — Mesoderm (urogenital ridge). LO 3 anatomy + development detail. The adrenal cortex develops from intermediate mesoderm adjacent to the developing gonad; this shared mesodermal origin explains why the cortex and the gonad share many steroidogenic enzymes. The adrenal medulla, by contrast, is neural-crest-derived (choice A describes the medulla) — it functions as a modified sympathetic ganglion that releases catecholamines into the bloodstream rather than peripheral synapses. Anterior pituitary (C) is oral ectoderm. This dual-origin gland is a classic Step 1 topic.

Question 1 of 5 — LO 4 · 21-Hydroxylase Deficiency (Classic CAH)
A 14-day-old 46,XX neonate is admitted with lethargy, poor feeding, and vomiting. Exam shows clitoromegaly and partial labial fusion. Labs: Na⁺ 124 mEq/L, K⁺ 6.8 mEq/L, glucose 48 mg/dL, 17-OH progesterone 5,200 ng/dL (massively elevated). Adrenal ultrasound shows bilateral hyperplasia. Which enzyme is most likely deficient?
A21-hydroxylase (CYP21A2)
B11β-hydroxylase (CYP11B1)
C17α-hydroxylase (CYP17)
DAromatase (CYP19)
EDesmolase (CYP11A1)
A — 21-hydroxylase deficiency (~90% of CAH). Lose 21-hydroxylase and the axis cannot make cortisol OR aldosterone — hence the salt-wasting crisis (hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, hypotension) in the first 2 weeks of life. Precursors upstream of the block accumulate — 17-OH progesterone is pathognomonically elevated — and get shunted into the still-intact androgen pathway, virilizing 46,XX infants. 11β (B) = hypertension + virilization. 17α (C) = hypertension + NO virilization. Desmolase (E) is incompatible with life beyond a few days.
Question 2 of 5 — LO 4 · 11β-Hydroxylase Deficiency
A 10-year-old girl presents with premature pubic hair, acne, and rapid linear growth. She is incidentally noted to have blood pressure 150/95 mmHg. Labs: K⁺ 3.1 mEq/L, slight metabolic alkalosis, plasma 11-deoxycorticosterone (DOC) markedly elevated, renin suppressed, cortisol low-normal. Which enzyme deficiency best fits this picture?
A21-hydroxylase
B11β-hydroxylase
C17α-hydroxylase
D3β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase
EAldosterone synthase
B — 11β-hydroxylase deficiency. Unique combo: hypertension + virilization. Why? 11β-hydroxylase converts 11-deoxycortisol → cortisol AND 11-deoxycorticosterone (DOC) → corticosterone. Lose it and DOC — a weak mineralocorticoid that accumulates to strong levels — drives hypertension and hypokalemia. Cortisol is low → loss of negative feedback → ACTH drives androgen pathway → virilization. Renin is SUPPRESSED (not activated like in a volume-depleted state) because DOC causes volume expansion. Compare/contrast with 21-OH (salt-wasting hypotension + virilization) and 17α (hypertension without virilization).
Question 3 of 5 — LO 4 · 17α-Hydroxylase Deficiency
A 17-year-old phenotypic female presents for primary amenorrhea and absent breast development. BP is 162/104 mmHg. Labs: K⁺ 3.0 mEq/L, renin low, aldosterone low, DOC and corticosterone elevated, cortisol low, androstenedione low. Karyotype is 46,XY with intra-abdominal testes. Which enzyme deficiency best explains her presentation?
A21-hydroxylase
B11β-hydroxylase
C17α-hydroxylase (CYP17)
D5α-reductase
E21-hydroxylase + 11β-hydroxylase combined
C — 17α-hydroxylase deficiency. The "odd one out" — hypertension WITHOUT virilization. CYP17 is needed for both cortisol (via 17α-hydroxylation) AND all sex steroids (via 17,20-lyase). Lose it, and: cortisol ↓ (→ ACTH ↑ → drives corticosterone/DOC pathway → HTN and hypokalemia), androgens ↓ (→ phenotypic females regardless of karyotype — 46,XY presents as undervirilized female with absent breast development because estrogen also can't be made), aldosterone synthase is intact but suppressed by volume expansion from DOC. Mnemonic: "17 has NO sex" — no androgens, no estrogens.
Question 4 of 5 — LO 2/4 · Cortisol Biosynthesis Enzyme Sequence
A biochemist is tracing the enzymatic sequence from cholesterol to cortisol. She lists four committed enzymatic steps in random order: (W) cholesterol → pregnenolone, (X) 17-OH progesterone → 11-deoxycortisol, (Y) pregnenolone → progesterone, (Z) 11-deoxycortisol → cortisol. What is the correct order and enzyme for each step?
AW → Y → X → Z · desmolase · 3β-HSD · 21-hydroxylase · 11β-hydroxylase
BW → X → Y → Z · aromatase · 17α-hydroxylase · 3β-HSD · 21-hydroxylase
CY → W → X → Z · 3β-HSD · desmolase · 17α-hydroxylase · 11β-hydroxylase
DW → Y → Z → X · desmolase · 21-hydroxylase · 11β-hydroxylase · 17α-hydroxylase
EW → Y → X → Z · desmolase · aromatase · 17α-hydroxylase · 11β-hydroxylase
A. Cortisol pathway step-by-step: cholesterol → pregnenolone (desmolase / CYP11A1, rate-limiting, mitochondrial) → pregnenolone → progesterone (3β-HSD) → 17-OH progesterone (17α-hydroxylase / CYP17) → 17-OH prog → 11-deoxycortisol (21-hydroxylase / CYP21) → 11-deoxycortisol → cortisol (11β-hydroxylase / CYP11B1, mitochondrial). The 21-hydroxylase deficiency from Question 1 blocks at the third-to-last step, explaining why 17-OHP accumulates. Memorize: "17, 21, 11 — cortisol is done."
Question 5 of 5 — LO 4 · ACTH → Acute Cortisol Burst
A researcher injects cosyntropin (synthetic ACTH 1-24) into a healthy volunteer and measures serum cortisol. Cortisol begins to rise within 3 minutes, peaking by 30 minutes. This acute response depends on ACTH binding its receptor on zona fasciculata cells, which triggers cAMP-mediated phosphorylation of a specific intracellular protein, leading to a ~30-fold increase in steroidogenic flux. Which receptor-protein cascade best describes the acute (<30-minute) cortisol response to ACTH?
AACTH → MC1R → Gq → PLC → IP3/DAG → StAR degradation
BACTH → MC2R → Gs → cAMP → PKA → phosphorylates StAR → ↑cholesterol import into mitochondrion
CACTH → nuclear MC2R → directly transcribes 21-hydroxylase
DACTH → MC2R → Gi → inhibits adenylyl cyclase → ↓cAMP → ↑cortisol
EACTH → MC3R → Gs → cAMP → PKA → phosphorylates HMG-CoA reductase
B. Canonical ZF signaling: ACTH binds MC2R (the ACTH receptor, a Gs-coupled GPCR specific to the adrenal cortex) → adenylyl cyclase → cAMP → PKA → phosphorylates StAR → increased cholesterol shuttling from outer to inner mitochondrial membrane → more desmolase substrate → acute cortisol burst. Chronically, ACTH ALSO upregulates transcription of every steroidogenic enzyme — but the acute (minutes) response is StAR-mediated. MC1R is on melanocytes; MC3R/MC4R in hypothalamus handle appetite.

Question 1 of 5 — LO 5 · CBG in Pregnancy/OCP Users
A 28-year-old woman in her second trimester of pregnancy is incidentally noted to have an elevated serum TOTAL cortisol on routine labs, twice her non-pregnant baseline. She has no clinical evidence of Cushing syndrome (normal BP, no striae, no central weight gain, no hyperglycemia). Her late-night salivary cortisol and 24-hour urinary FREE cortisol are both normal. Which of the following best explains the discrepancy?
AOccult pregnancy-associated ACTH-secreting tumor
BEstrogen-stimulated hepatic CBG synthesis elevates total but not free cortisol
CPlacental 11β-HSD2 failure
DPregnancy-induced loss of negative feedback on the HPA axis
EExogenous glucocorticoid contamination of prenatal vitamin
B. Free hormone hypothesis applied to cortisol (LO 5). Cortisol-binding globulin (CBG / transcortin) carries ~90% of circulating cortisol. Estrogen upregulates hepatic CBG synthesis, so pregnancy and combined OCP use raise TOTAL cortisol without raising FREE cortisol — the clinically active fraction. Patients are eucortisolemic despite the high total number. Same principle applies to TBG and total T4. Clinical pearl: when screening for Cushing in a woman on OCP/pregnant, use 24-hr urinary free cortisol or salivary cortisol rather than total serum cortisol.
Question 2 of 5 — LO 6 · 11β-HSD2 & Licorice (Apparent MC Excess)
A 62-year-old man presents with new hypertension 180/110, hypokalemia 2.9, metabolic alkalosis, and suppressed renin AND suppressed aldosterone. He has been consuming black licorice candy 100 g/day for 6 months. His urinary cortisol:cortisone ratio is markedly elevated. Which molecular mechanism best explains this syndrome?
ADirect cortisol activation of the mineralocorticoid receptor (MR) in the kidney because glycyrrhetinic acid in licorice inhibits 11β-HSD2
BCortisol reduction of SGK1 in principal cells
CActivation of the β-adrenergic receptor on the juxtaglomerular apparatus
DInhibition of renal 1α-hydroxylase of vitamin D
EInhibition of aldosterone synthase by glycyrrhetinic acid
A — 11β-HSD2 inhibition (apparent mineralocorticoid excess). Cortisol and aldosterone bind the mineralocorticoid receptor (MR) with EQUAL affinity. Kidney principal cells protect MR from cortisol (which circulates at ~1,000× higher concentration than aldosterone) by expressing 11β-HSD2, which rapidly inactivates cortisol → cortisone. Glycyrrhetinic acid (the active licorice component) inhibits 11β-HSD2 → cortisol accumulates locally → over-activates MR → aldosterone-like effects (HTN, hypokalemia, alkalosis) — but with SUPPRESSED aldosterone (because the real RAAS sees volume-expanded state). Urinary cortisol:cortisone ratio is the giveaway. Same biochemistry underlies the syndrome of apparent mineralocorticoid excess (AME).
Question 3 of 5 — LO 6 · Aldosterone Mechanism
A researcher treats freshly-isolated renal collecting duct principal cells with aldosterone. Within 30 minutes she observes increased transcription of ENaC subunits, increased expression of Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase, and increased activity of serum-and-glucocorticoid-regulated kinase (SGK1). Within hours, apical ENaC insertion increases, and Na⁺ reabsorption rises. Which molecular mechanism best describes aldosterone action?
ABinding a membrane GPCR on the luminal surface → Gs → cAMP → PKA phosphorylates ENaC
BBinding intracellular mineralocorticoid receptor (MR), hormone-receptor complex binds GRE/HRE on DNA, drives transcription of SGK1/ENaC
CBinding a receptor tyrosine kinase → PI3K/Akt → GLUT4 translocation
DBinding JAK-STAT on the basolateral membrane
EBinding nuclear vitamin D receptor
B. LO 6 textbook steroid-hormone mechanism. Aldosterone is lipophilic → crosses the plasma membrane → binds the cytoplasmic mineralocorticoid receptor (MR) held in an HSP90 complex. Hormone-MR complex translocates to nucleus → binds hormone response elements → drives transcription of SGK1, ENaC subunits, and Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase. SGK1 phosphorylates Nedd4-2 → prevents ENaC ubiquitination → more ENaC on apical membrane. Net effect: Na⁺ reabsorption (with K⁺ and H⁺ secretion). Steroid actions are characteristically delayed (~30 min to hours) compared to peptide hormones because they require gene transcription.
Question 4 of 5 — LO 6 · Cortisol Mechanism & Cellular Metabolism
A 58-year-old woman has been taking prednisone 20 mg daily for 4 months to treat giant cell arteritis. On follow-up she has developed centripetal obesity, a new striae, hyperglycemia, proximal muscle weakness, and osteopenia. Which of the following best describes the predominant molecular mechanism by which cortisol (and prednisone, its analog) produces these effects?
ACompetes with insulin for the insulin receptor tyrosine kinase
BBinds the glucocorticoid receptor / HSP90 complex; GR translocates to nucleus and trans-activates gluconeogenic genes and trans-represses pro-inflammatory genes
CActivates a plasma membrane GPCR → cAMP → gene transcription
DBinds the same nuclear receptor as thyroid hormone
ECatalyzes dephosphorylation of PEPCK
B. Cortisol binds the cytoplasmic glucocorticoid receptor (GR) / HSP90 chaperone complex. Hormone binding releases HSP90, dimerized GR translocates into the nucleus and performs TWO distinct genomic actions: (1) Trans-activation at glucocorticoid response elements — induces PEPCK, glucose-6-phosphatase, tyrosine aminotransferase (gluconeogenesis); lipocortin/annexin A1 (anti-inflammatory); and (2) Trans-repression of NF-κB and AP-1 — blocks cytokines, COX-2, adhesion molecules. Chronic over-dosing → all the features listed: diabetogenic, myopathic, osteoporotic, centripetal obesity — all explained by activated GR transcription programs.
Question 5 of 5 — LO 5/6 · Dexamethasone Pharmacology
A 45-year-old woman with suspected Cushing syndrome undergoes a low-dose overnight dexamethasone suppression test: 1 mg dexamethasone is given at 11 PM and serum cortisol is measured at 8 AM the following morning. Which property of dexamethasone makes it preferred over hydrocortisone for this diagnostic application?
ADexamethasone cross-reacts with the cortisol immunoassay, so the assay still detects it
BDexamethasone has a much longer half-life than cortisol AND it does NOT cross-react with the cortisol immunoassay, so it suppresses endogenous cortisol while not confounding the measurement
CDexamethasone binds only the mineralocorticoid receptor, not the glucocorticoid receptor
DDexamethasone is water-soluble and excreted unchanged
EDexamethasone cannot cross the blood-brain barrier, so it acts only on the adrenal
B. Combined LO 5 (half-life) + LO 6 (mechanism). Dexamethasone is a synthetic glucocorticoid with: (1) long half-life (~36-54 h; cortisol ~90 min) → single evening dose suppresses the morning ACTH surge; (2) very high GR affinity (~25× cortisol) → tiny dose suffices to exert negative feedback; (3) critically, it does NOT cross-react with standard cortisol immunoassays → the measured 8 AM cortisol is genuine endogenous cortisol. In normals, DEX suppresses ACTH → cortisol falls below 1.8 µg/dL. In Cushing (any cause), autonomous cortisol secretion is not suppressed. High-dose dex (8 mg) later distinguishes pituitary-dependent Cushing disease (suppresses) from ectopic ACTH (does not).

Ext Q1 — LO 3 · Zona Fasciculata & Anti-Inflammatory Hormone — BRS Physio Ch 29 Q11
Which portion of the adrenal gland secretes a hormone with antiinflammatory actions?
AThe medulla
BThe zona reticularis
CThe zona fasciculata
DThe zona glomerulosa
ENone of the above
C — Zona fasciculata. LO 3 map: ZF secretes cortisol, which has anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressive effects. ZR secretes androgens (DHEA, androstenedione). ZG secretes aldosterone. Adrenal medulla secretes catecholamines. Remember GFR = "Salt, Sugar, Sex" corresponding to glomerulosa → fasciculata → reticularis.
Ext Q2 — LO 3/4 · Adrenal Androgens in Females — BRS Physio Ch 29 Q12
Which of the following is a significant action of adrenal androgens in females?
AStimulation of libido
BHypertrophy of sebaceous glands
CDevelopment of pubic hair
DDevelopment of axillary hair
EAll of the above
E — All of the above. DHEA and androstenedione are the major adrenal androgens. In females, the adrenal gland is the ONLY significant source of circulating androgens, so adrenal androgens drive libido, sebaceous gland hypertrophy/acne, and pubic + axillary hair development (adrenarche). Contrast with males, who primarily rely on testicular testosterone. Adrenal androgens also provide substrate for peripheral aromatization to estrogens, especially in adipose tissue — important in postmenopausal women.
Group B · Biosynthesis & CAH Variants (LOs 2, 4)
Ext Q3 — LO 4 · Classic Salt-Wasting CAH — Waite & Sheakley Q40
A 2-week-old male infant is brought to the hospital after vomiting for most of the day. He appears underweight and dehydrated. Physical exam shows penile enlargement and subtle hyperpigmentation. He is hypotensive. Serum electrolytes: Na⁺ 120 mEq/L, K⁺ 5.6 mEq/L, glucose 55 mg/dL. Abdominal imaging shows bilateral adrenal hyperplasia. Which of the following sets of serum changes is most likely?
A↓ Aldosterone · ↓ Cortisol · ↓ Corticosterone · ↓ ACTH · ↓ Insulin · ↓ GH
B↓ Aldosterone · ↓ Cortisol · ↓ Corticosterone · ↑ ACTH · ↓ Insulin · ↑ GH
C↑ Aldosterone · ↓ Cortisol · ↓ Corticosterone · ↓ ACTH · ↑ Insulin · ↓ GH
D↑ Aldosterone · ↓ Cortisol · ↓ Corticosterone · ↑ ACTH · ↓ Insulin · ↑ GH
E↑ Aldosterone · ↑ Cortisol · ↓ Corticosterone · ↑ ACTH · ↑ Insulin · ↑ GH
B. Classic 21-hydroxylase deficiency: enzyme deficit blocks both the aldosterone path (ZG) and the cortisol path (ZF). Aldosterone ↓ → salt wasting (hyponatremia + hyperkalemia + hypotension). Cortisol ↓ → hypoglycemia, and loss of feedback → ACTH ↑. Excess ACTH produces MSH from POMC → hyperpigmentation. Precursors shunt into androgens → penile enlargement. Stress/hypoglycemia → GH ↑ (counter-regulatory). Insulin ↓ from hypoglycemia. Corticosterone requires 21-OH too so it drops.
Ext Q4 — LO 4 · Blocking Antibody & 17α-Hydroxylase — Waite & Sheakley Q38
A 15-year-old girl presents with a 3-week history of nausea and 2 months of unexpected weight loss. Menarche has not yet occurred. Her exam reveals immature secondary sexual development, temp 97.8°F, BP 138/89. Gums are hyperpigmented. Labs reveal hypernatremia, hypokalemia, and low fasting glucose. Blood tests show increased titer of autoantibodies that block the normal action of the target antigen. Which autoantibody is most likely responsible?
AAntithyroglobulin
BAntithyroperoxidase
CAnti-TSH-receptor
DAnti-17α-hydroxylase
EAnti-21β-hydroxylase
D — Anti-17α-hydroxylase (blocking). Block 17α-OH (CYP17) → low cortisol → ACTH ↑ → hyperpigmentation + drive into the intact mineralocorticoid pathway (→ DOC, corticosterone excess → hypernatremia, hypokalemia, HTN). CYP17 also provides 17,20-lyase for sex steroid production; losing it blocks all gonadal/adrenal androgen output → primary amenorrhea and absent sexual development. The key distinguishing pattern from 21-OH is "HTN + LOW androgens" (vs salt-wasting + HIGH androgens in 21-OH).
Ext Q5 — LO 4 · CAH Virilization of Scalp/Facial Pattern — BRS Biochem Q29
A female patient presents to her primary care doctor with thinning scalp hair, excessive facial hair, and high ACTH and low cortisol levels in her blood. The physician should seriously consider which of the following problems?
AA tumor of the anterior pituitary that produces abnormally large amounts of POMC
BA tumor of the adrenal medulla that secretes abnormally large amounts of its normal hormone product
CA genetic deficiency of an enzyme in the pathway for cortisol synthesis
DA mutation in the gene for CRH that decreases the production of this polypeptide
EAn insulinoma
C — Enzyme deficiency (non-classic CAH). The combination of high ACTH + low cortisol + virilization is a shortcut for adrenal cortical enzyme deficiency. Low cortisol disinhibits ACTH, and ACTH stimulates the intact androgen branch → virilization. A POMC-producing pituitary tumor (A) would produce high ACTH but ALSO high cortisol (Cushing). Adrenal medulla (B) makes catecholamines, not sex steroids. CRH mutation (D) would give LOW ACTH. Insulinoma (E) causes hypoglycemia, not this picture. This is how non-classic CAH (mild 21-OH deficiency) often presents in adult women.
Group C · Regulation, Feedback & Circadian (LOs 3, 4, 5)
Ext Q6 — LO 4 · ACTH → Cortisol Axis — BRS Physio Ch 29 Q10
Stimulation of the adrenal gland by adrenocorticotropic hormone has the most direct effect on release of:
AEpinephrine
BNorepinephrine
CCortisol
DAndrogens
EAldosterone
C — Cortisol. ACTH is the PRIMARY regulator of cortisol (and, to a lesser extent, adrenal androgen) release from zona fasciculata. Although ACTH is required for synthesis of ALL adrenal steroids (desmolase is ACTH-sensitive), aldosterone and androgens are primarily controlled by OTHER mechanisms: aldosterone by the renin-angiotensin system and serum K⁺, catecholamines (medulla) by the sympathetic nervous system. A tumor that overproduces ACTH (Cushing disease) predominantly raises cortisol.
Ext Q7 — LO 4 · Stimuli of Aldosterone Release — BRS Physio Ch 29 Q13
Aldosterone secretion by the adrenal gland is stimulated by:
AHypokalemia
BHigh plasma Na⁺ concentration
CAngiotensin II
DAtrial natriuretic peptide
EAll of the above
C — Angiotensin II. The primary stimuli for aldosterone release from ZG are hyperkalemia (NOT hypokalemia) and angiotensin II. ACTH plays a permissive (not primary) role. ANP INHIBITS aldosterone (opposes Na retention). High plasma Na⁺ would inhibit renin → less AT-II → LESS aldosterone, not more. Mechanism: hyperkalemia depolarizes ZG cells opening voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels; AT-II binds AT1 GPCR → PLC → IP3/DAG → Ca²⁺ → aldosterone synthase.
Ext Q8 — LO 3/4 · Primary Hyperaldosteronism (Conn's) — Waite & Sheakley Q41
A 50-year-old man presents with headache and weakness. He is hypertensive and hypokalemic. Serum aldosterone is elevated; imaging shows an adenoma in the adrenal zona glomerulosa. Which of the following changes to serum renin and serum angiotensin II would support the diagnosis of primary hyperaldosteronism?
A↑ Renin · ↑ AT-II
B↓ Renin · ↓ AT-II
C↑ Renin · ↓ AT-II
D↓ Renin · ↑ AT-II
EUnchanged · Unchanged
B — Both low. Autonomous aldosterone production from a ZG adenoma (Conn syndrome) causes Na retention + volume expansion → high BP sensed by juxtaglomerular apparatus → renin suppression. With renin suppressed, angiotensin II is also low (renin and AT-II almost always move together because renin is the rate-limiting enzyme). The diagnostic criterion is a high aldosterone:renin ratio. Secondary hyperaldosteronism (e.g., renal artery stenosis) would have both renin and AT-II HIGH.
Ext Q9 — LO 4 · Cushing Disease (Pituitary) vs Syndrome — Waite & Sheakley Q42
The husband of a 63-year-old woman reports that his wife's personality has significantly changed over the past 2 years. The woman admits to memory problems. Physical examination reveals centralized obesity, a round face, and thin limbs. Labs show fasting hyperglycemia. Her ACTH levels are elevated at baseline and rise further in response to CRH infusion. Which is the most likely diagnosis?
AAddison's disease
BConn's disease
CCushing's disease
DGlucocorticoid therapy
EHypopituitarism
C — Cushing's DISEASE (pituitary). Classic hypercortisolism picture + ACTH that is elevated AND responds to CRH = CRH-responsive pituitary ACTH adenoma = Cushing disease. Distinctions to nail: Cushing SYNDROME = any cause of hypercortisolism; Cushing DISEASE = ACTH-producing pituitary adenoma specifically. Exogenous glucocorticoid therapy (D) would have LOW ACTH (suppressed by feedback). Ectopic ACTH from a lung carcinoid would have HIGH ACTH but would NOT respond appropriately to CRH stimulation. High-dose dexamethasone suppression: pituitary Cushing suppresses, ectopic does not.
Ext Q10 — LO 3 · Circadian Rhythm Inversion — Waite & Sheakley Q44
A patient had a minor procedure under conscious sedation. One week later, ACTH and cortisol were measured: 7 AM ACTH 110 pg/mL, cortisol 15 µg/dL; 7:30 AM ACTH 90, cortisol 15; 7 PM ACTH 120, cortisol 20; 7:30 PM ACTH 210, cortisol 23. All values are within the normal reference range. Which conclusion best describes these data?
AExcessive pulsatility of ACTH
BInverted circadian rhythm
CNo abnormalities
DPrimary adrenal insufficiency
ESecondary adrenal insufficiency
B — Inverted circadian rhythm. LO 3/regulation detail: normal cortisol/ACTH peaks at 6-8 AM and nadirs near midnight. This patient shows evening peak > morning peak, the reverse of physiology — typical of night-shift workers or disrupted sleep-wake cycles. All values fall in the reference range, so there is no adrenal insufficiency (D,E). Pulsatility (A) is normal every 30-60 min — not what's abnormal here. Circadian regulation is hypothalamic (SCN) → CRH → ACTH → cortisol.
Ext Q11 — LO 4 · Potassium Directly Triggers Aldosterone — Waite & Sheakley Q45
An increase in serum levels of which of the following electrolytes will DIRECTLY increase aldosterone secretion?
ACalcium
BChloride
CMagnesium
DPotassium
ESodium
D — Potassium. Hyperkalemia depolarizes zona glomerulosa cells → opens L-type voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels → Ca²⁺ influx → triggers vesicle fusion and direct aldosterone release. This is a very tight feedback loop — small rises in serum K⁺ acutely drive aldosterone to enhance K⁺ excretion. Angiotensin II is the other primary stimulus (independent of K⁺). Na⁺, Ca²⁺, Cl⁻, and Mg²⁺ have no direct clinically-relevant effect on ZG cells.

Ext Q12 — LO 6 · Addison's Disease Clinical Picture — BRS Biochem Q24
A 43-year-old male high-level executive presents to his physician due to persistent dizziness when standing quickly. He also has chronic fatigue, some muscle weakness, and an unusual craving for salty foods. The physician notices that the patient has a constant "bronze tan". Labs show normal cholesterol, hypoglycemia, hyperkalemia, and hyponatremia. The underlying cause of these problems is which of the following?
AAdrenal insufficiency
BPituitary insufficiency
CLack of insulin
DLack of glucagon
ELack of GH
A — Adrenal insufficiency (Addison disease). LO 6 synthesis: this is the FULL flipside of every cortisol + aldosterone action. No cortisol → hypoglycemia + fatigue + hypotension; no aldosterone → salt wasting → hyponatremia + hyperkalemia + salt craving + orthostasis. High ACTH (from loss of feedback) drives POMC → α-MSH → bronze hyperpigmentation. This is PRIMARY adrenal insufficiency (vs SECONDARY = pituitary, which would NOT hyperpigment and would leave aldosterone intact because RAAS, not ACTH, drives aldosterone). Autoimmune adrenalitis is the #1 cause in the developed world.

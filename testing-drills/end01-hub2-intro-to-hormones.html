Pheochromocytoma
Cell of origin, biochemical findings, classic symptom triad, and best screening test?
Answer
Origin: Chromaffin cells of adrenal medulla → secrete NE/Epi/DA
Triad: episodic HTN, palpitations, headache + diaphoresis
Best screen: Plasma free metanephrines (or 24-hr urine metanephrines/VMA) — more reliable than catecholamines (which are pulsatile).
Rule of 10s: 10% bilateral, 10% extra-adrenal, 10% malignant, 10% familial.
Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia
Most common enzyme defect, what hormones are ↑ and ↓, and how does presentation differ from 11β or 17α?
Answer
21-hydroxylase deficiency (90%): ↓cortisol + ↓aldosterone + ↑↑androgens
→ salt-wasting, hypotension, hyperkalemia, virilization of female neonates

11β-hydroxylase: ↓cortisol + ↑11-deoxycorticosterone (weak MC) + ↑androgens → HTN + virilization
17α-hydroxylase: ↑MC's → HTN + hypokalemia; ↓androgens → ambiguous genitalia/sexual infantilism
Steroid Biosynthesis Drugs
What enzyme is blocked by metyrapone, aminoglutethimide, and ketoconazole?
Answer
Metyrapone: blocks 11β-hydroxylase → ↓cortisol; used to test pituitary ACTH reserve
Aminoglutethimide: blocks desmolase (CYP11A1) → shuts down ALL steroid synthesis
Ketoconazole: inhibits multiple CYP450 steroid enzymes → used for Cushing's when surgery isn't possible
Vitamin D Activation
Trace the pathway from 7-dehydrocholesterol to active 1,25-(OH)₂-D₃. What enzyme is rate-limiting and how is it regulated?
Answer
Skin: 7-dehydrocholesterol + UVB → cholecalciferol (D₃)
Liver: 25-hydroxylase → 25-OH-D (calcidiol; the STORAGE form)
Kidney PCT: 1α-hydroxylase → 1,25-(OH)₂-D₃ (calcitriol; active)

1α-hydroxylase is RATE-LIMITING. ↑ by PTH, ↓ Pi, ↓ Ca²⁺. ↓ by FGF23, high 1,25-D (feedback).
Pseudohypoparathyroidism 1a
Defect, classic presentation, response to PTH infusion, and imprinting pattern?
Answer
Defect: Inactivating GNAS mutation → loss of Gsα → PTH receptor cannot signal cAMP
Presentation: Albright hereditary osteodystrophy — short stature, short 4th/5th metacarpals, obesity, round face
Labs: ↑PTH, ↓Ca²⁺, ↑Pi (end-organ resistance)
PTH infusion: no ↑ urinary cAMP or ↓ Pi (dx)
Imprinted: phenotype only if inherited from MOTHER
McCune-Albright Syndrome
Genetic defect, classic triad, and why is it always mosaic?
Answer
Defect: Post-zygotic activating GNAS mutation → constitutive Gsα → tonic cAMP in affected tissues
Triad: (1) Café-au-lait with ragged "coast of Maine" borders (2) Polyostotic fibrous dysplasia (3) Precocious puberty / endocrinopathies
Mosaic: germline GNAS mutation would be lethal; survives only as mosaic pattern → patchy disease.
Central vs. Nephrogenic DI
Which one involves a receptor signaling defect, and what test distinguishes them?
Answer
Central DI: ADH not made (pituitary/hypothalamic damage)
Nephrogenic DI: V2 receptor mutation (Gs-linked, cAMP-dependent) OR aquaporin-2 defect OR lithium/hypercalcemia

Desmopressin test: central responds (urine osm ↑ >50%); nephrogenic doesn't.
Treatment: Central → desmopressin; Nephrogenic → HCTZ + low-salt diet (or amiloride if lithium-induced)
C-Peptide Clinical Use
What is C-peptide, and why is it a critical clinical biomarker?
Answer
C-peptide: fragment cleaved from proinsulin during Golgi processing → stored and released in EQUIMOLAR amounts with endogenous insulin
Clinical use: distinguishes endogenous (C-peptide ↑) from exogenous insulin (C-peptide LOW or absent)
Factitious hypoglycemia: insulin ↑ but C-peptide ↓ → exogenous insulin injection
Insulinoma: both ↑
T1DM: both LOW (β-cell destruction)
Radioimmunoassay (RIA)
Basic principle, who invented it, and why is it so sensitive?
Answer
Principle: Radio-labeled hormone competes with patient hormone for a fixed amount of antibody. More patient hormone → less radio-labeled hormone bound.
Invented by Rosalyn Yalow (Nobel 1977)
Sensitivity: detects pg–fg quantities (1000× more sensitive than bioassay)
Modern descendent: ELISA — uses enzyme instead of radioisotope (safer, similar sensitivity).
Scatchard Plot / Kd
What does a Scatchard plot measure? What does a LOW Kd mean?
Answer
Scatchard plot: x-axis = bound hormone, y-axis = bound/free ratio. Slope = −1/Kd; x-intercept = total receptor number (Bmax).

Kd = hormone concentration where 50% of receptors are occupied.
Low Kd = HIGH affinity (need less hormone to fill receptors). Example: insulin receptor Kd ≈ 10⁻⁹ M.
Insulin Resistance in T2DM
Explain why T2DM is the archetype of hormone down-regulation and what compensatory change happens first.
Answer
Mechanism: Chronic overnutrition → chronic hyperinsulinemia → hepatic/muscle insulin receptors internalized and down-regulated → insulin resistance

Compensation: β-cells initially hypertrophy and secrete MORE insulin (hyperinsulinemia of early T2DM)
Eventual failure: β-cell exhaustion → insulin deficiency → overt T2DM; requires exogenous insulin
Drugs that help: metformin (↑sensitivity), TZDs (PPAR-γ), GLP-1 agonists
Carrier Protein Pitfalls
Two common clinical scenarios where TOTAL hormone is abnormal but FREE is normal — what's the takeaway?
Answer
1. Pregnancy: ↑estrogen → ↑TBG → ↑total T4 (free T4 unchanged = euthyroid)
2. OCPs / pregnancy: ↑CBG → ↑total cortisol (free cortisol unchanged = eucortisolism)

Takeaway: ALWAYS order FREE hormone to judge endocrine status. Total values include biologically inactive carrier-bound fraction.

Also: hypoalbuminemia → ↓total but normal free → don't be fooled.
L-DOPA / Carbidopa
Why is L-DOPA combined with carbidopa in Parkinson's treatment?
Answer
L-DOPA: crosses BBB (dopamine does NOT) → converted to DA by DOPA decarboxylase in CNS
Problem: Peripheral DOPA decarboxylase also converts L-DOPA → DA BEFORE it crosses BBB → peripheral side effects (nausea, hypotension) + less drug reaches CNS

Carbidopa: inhibits peripheral DOPA decarboxylase (does NOT cross BBB) → more L-DOPA reaches brain, fewer side effects. Classic pharmacology of a rate-limiting enzyme downstream.
Molar Pregnancy & hCG Cross-Reactivity
Why can a complete hydatidiform mole cause hyperthyroidism?
Answer
hCG-β is STRUCTURALLY SIMILAR to TSH-β (all four glycoprotein hormones — TSH, LH, FSH, hCG — share an α-subunit).

Extremely high hCG (>100,000 mIU/mL) → cross-reactivity at the TSH receptor → hCG-mediated hyperthyroidism.

Seen in: complete mole, choriocarcinoma, hyperemesis gravidarum. Resolves with tumor evacuation.
Eicosanoid Drug Targets
Match each eicosanoid pharmacologic class to its drug example + clinical use.
Answer
PGE₁ analog — misoprostol: gastric mucosal protection (NSAID ulcers), cervical ripening, medical abortion
PGE₁ — alprostadil: keeps PDA open in cyanotic neonatal congenital heart disease
PGE₂ — dinoprostone: labor induction, cervical ripening
PGF₂α analog — latanoprost, bimatoprost: glaucoma (↓ IOP)
PGI₂ — epoprostenol: pulmonary arterial hypertension
LTD₄-R antagonist — montelukast: asthma
5-LOX inhibitor — zileuton: asthma
COX inhibitors — NSAIDs, aspirin: pain, fever, inflammation; aspirin also cardioprotection (irreversibly acetylates platelet COX)
EC50 & Insulin Resistance
Explain the rightward shift of the insulin dose-response curve in T2DM, and why early T2DM features HYPERinsulinemia.
Answer
Rightward shift of the curve = increased EC50 (more insulin needed for the same response). Caused by receptor down-regulation + post-receptor signaling defects (SOCS proteins, IRS-1 serine phosphorylation) + lipotoxicity in muscle/liver.

Early T2DM: β-cells compensate by secreting MUCH more insulin → glucose stays near-normal at the cost of hyperinsulinemia (climb-the-shifted-curve strategy).

Late T2DM: β-cells exhaust → insulin falls → glucose rises sharply. C-peptide can confirm declining endogenous production.

Metformin and TZDs shift the curve LEFT (↑ sensitivity); GLP-1 agonists improve both β-cell function and peripheral sensitivity.

Question 1 of 6 — Catecholamine Synthesis / Pheo
A 34-year-old woman presents with episodic palpitations, severe headaches, and diaphoresis. BP in the ED is 210/120 mmHg; between episodes it is 128/82. Abdominal CT shows a 3 cm adrenal mass. 24-hour urine demonstrates markedly elevated metanephrines. Which enzyme's activity is most increased by neoplastic chromaffin cells in this tumor?
ATyrosine hydroxylase
BCatechol-O-methyltransferase (COMT)
CMonoamine oxidase (MAO)
DDOPA decarboxylase
EAromatase
A — Tyrosine hydroxylase. Pheochromocytoma is a chromaffin-cell tumor that massively overproduces catecholamines. The rate-limiting step is conversion of tyrosine → L-DOPA by tyrosine hydroxylase; overproduction of catecholamines is fundamentally due to increased TH activity (plus enhanced storage/release). COMT and MAO (B, C) DEGRADE catecholamines to metabolites — they are elevated in activity because there's more substrate, but they're not the source. DOPA decarboxylase (D) is downstream but not rate-limiting. Aromatase (E) is for estrogen synthesis.
Question 2 of 6 — Steroid Biosynthesis / CAH
A 3-week-old female neonate presents with vomiting, poor feeding, and ambiguous genitalia. Labs: Na⁺ 128, K⁺ 6.4, glucose 52 mg/dL. Serum 17-hydroxyprogesterone is markedly elevated. Which of the following enzymes is most likely deficient?
A11β-hydroxylase
B17α-hydroxylase
C21-hydroxylase
DDesmolase (CYP11A1)
EAromatase
C — 21-hydroxylase deficiency is the most common form of CAH (~90%). Loss of 21-OH → cannot make cortisol OR aldosterone → ↓cortisol + ↓aldosterone (salt-wasting, hyperkalemia, hypotension). Precursors shunt into the androgen pathway → virilization. 17-OH-progesterone accumulates because it's the substrate just upstream of 21-hydroxylase. 11β (A) → HTN + virilization. 17α (B) → ↓sex steroids + HTN. Desmolase (D) would halt all steroid synthesis.
Question 3 of 6 — Free Hormone Hypothesis
A 28-year-old pregnant woman at 18 weeks has total T4 of 18.4 μg/dL (upper limit normal 12) but feels well. Her pulse is 82, reflexes normal, and she has no tremor. TSH is 1.3 mIU/L. Which of the following is the most appropriate next step?
AStart methimazole for hyperthyroidism
BOrder radioactive iodine uptake
CReassure her — measure FREE T4 to confirm euthyroid state
DBegin propranolol for thyroid symptoms
EStart levothyroxine
C — Reassure + measure FREE T4. Pregnancy ↑↑estrogen → ↑hepatic TBG synthesis → more circulating TBG → total T4 RISES (more hormone bound to carrier). But TBG binding does not change FREE T4, which is the biologically active fraction. TSH confirms: normal TSH rules out primary hyperthyroidism. Treatment (A, D, E) is inappropriate in a euthyroid patient. Radioactive iodine (B) is contraindicated in pregnancy.
Question 4 of 6 — GPCR Signaling / Pseudohypopara
A 22-year-old woman with short stature, round face, and shortened 4th/5th metacarpals has Ca²⁺ 6.8 mg/dL, Pi 6.2 mg/dL, and PTH 220 pg/mL. Infusion of synthetic PTH fails to raise urinary cAMP. Which of the following is the most likely molecular defect?
AAutoimmune destruction of parathyroid glands
BLoss-of-function mutation of the PTH receptor
CLoss-of-function mutation of Gsα (GNAS)
DChronic hypomagnesemia
EActivating mutation of the calcium-sensing receptor
C — Gsα (GNAS) inactivating mutation → pseudohypoparathyroidism type 1a (Albright hereditary osteodystrophy). PTH receptor is a Gs-coupled GPCR. Without functional Gsα, PTH binding cannot stimulate adenylyl cyclase → no cAMP rise in kidney → end-organ resistance despite high PTH. The Ellsworth-Howard test (PTH infusion) differentiates: in true hypopara, urinary cAMP rises; in pseudohypopara, it does not. Classic features (short stature, short 4th/5th metacarpals) are driven by the same defect in bone tissue. A → low PTH; B would theoretically give similar result but PHP1a is the specific Gsα syndrome.
Question 5 of 6 — Receptor Sensitivity / Resistance
A 48-year-old obese man with BMI 36 has fasting glucose 148 mg/dL, HbA1c 8.1%, and fasting insulin level 42 μIU/mL (normal 2–25). His fasting C-peptide is 4.8 ng/mL (normal). Over the next 5 years his fasting insulin progressively declines and his glucose worsens. Which statement best explains this trajectory?
AAutoimmune destruction of pancreatic β-cells has occurred
BInitial insulin receptor down-regulation caused resistance; later β-cell exhaustion reduced insulin output
CAccumulating hepatic glucagon receptors increased gluconeogenesis
DHepatic insulin clearance decreased, reducing fasting insulin artifactually
EEstrogen-induced increase in SHBG altered hormone bioavailability
B. This is the classic T2DM trajectory: chronic hyperinsulinemia (from overnutrition/obesity) → insulin receptor down-regulation → insulin resistance → β-cells initially compensate with hyperinsulinemia (why early T2DM has HIGH insulin + high C-peptide). Over years, β-cell exhaustion leads to relative/absolute insulin deficiency → declining insulin, worsening glucose. A describes T1DM (low insulin throughout). Receptor down-regulation is the LO7-level concept being tested here.
Question 6 of 6 — C-peptide / Factitious Hypoglycemia
A 32-year-old nurse presents with episodic diaphoresis, confusion, and fingerstick glucose of 38 mg/dL. Labs obtained during the episode: insulin 85 μIU/mL (HIGH), C-peptide 0.3 ng/mL (LOW), pro-insulin undetectable. Which of the following best explains these findings?
AInsulinoma of the pancreas
BSurreptitious exogenous insulin administration
CSulfonylurea abuse
DGlucagonoma
EAdrenal insufficiency
B — Exogenous insulin. Endogenous insulin is cleaved from proinsulin in Golgi, releasing EQUIMOLAR amounts of insulin + C-peptide. Exogenous (injected) insulin has NO C-peptide and doesn't come from proinsulin. Thus: ↑insulin + ↓↓C-peptide = exogenous administration (factitious, commonly by medical personnel). Insulinoma (A) → ↑insulin + ↑C-peptide + ↑proinsulin. Sulfonylureas (C) → ↑insulin + ↑C-peptide (they stimulate endogenous release; sulfonylurea urine screen confirms). This question tests the critical clinical application of peptide-hormone processing (LO 2/7).

Vignette 3 of 4 — Nephrogenic DI from Lithium
A 55-year-old man with bipolar disorder on lithium presents with polyuria (urine output 8 L/day) and polydipsia. Serum Na⁺ 152, urine osmolality 120 mOsm/kg. After water deprivation, urine osm rises minimally to 180. After desmopressin (DDAVP), urine osm still does not rise above 200 mOsm/kg.

A 26-year-old woman taking combined oral contraceptives is referred for "high cortisol." Morning total serum cortisol is 32 μg/dL (upper normal 22). She has no features of Cushing's (no weight gain, striae, moon facies, HTN, or hyperglycemia). 24-hour urine free cortisol is NORMAL. Late-night salivary cortisol is NORMAL. BAdrenal adenoma producing cortisol

Step 1 Traps: Each statement is TRUE or FALSE — many reverse or mix facts that routinely trip students up.
Trap 01 — Receptor Logic
"Insulin and glucagon both use G-protein coupled receptors that signal through cAMP."
FALSE. Insulin uses a receptor TYROSINE KINASE (RTK) → IRS-1 → PI3K/MAPK. Only glucagon uses a GPCR (Gs → cAMP). This pairing is a classic exam trap — they are opposing counter-regulatory hormones with COMPLETELY different receptor architectures.
Trap 02 — Cortisol & Catecholamines
"In Addisonian crisis, patients are hypotensive because cortisol directly controls vascular tone."
FALSE — but close. Cortisol has no direct vasoconstrictive effect. Its role is PERMISSIVE: cortisol is required for catecholamines (NE/Epi) to exert their α₁-mediated vasoconstriction. Without cortisol, pressors lose effect. Classic LO-7 concept tested via hypotension refractory to IVF + norepinephrine in a crashing Addisonian patient — give stress-dose hydrocortisone.
Trap 03 — Glycoprotein Hormones
"TSH, LH, FSH, and hCG all share a common β-subunit, with the α-subunit being unique."
FALSE — reversed. The α-subunit is IDENTICAL across all four; the β-subunit is UNIQUE and gives each its specific receptor activity. This matters clinically: β-hCG is what you measure for pregnancy; unique β is why β-hCG of hCG can cross-react only partially with TSH receptor (same α, similar but not identical β).
Trap 04 — Steroid vs. Thyroid Hormone
"Thyroid hormone and cortisol are both steroid hormones derived from cholesterol."
FALSE. Thyroid hormone is an AMINE (derived from tyrosine + iodine on thyroglobulin), not a steroid. Cortisol IS a steroid (cholesterol-derived). However, thyroid hormone BEHAVES like a steroid: lipid-soluble, carrier-bound (TBG), nuclear receptor. This dual classification confuses students.
Trap 05 — C-peptide
"Elevated C-peptide indicates a patient is taking too much exogenous insulin."
FALSE — opposite. Elevated C-peptide = elevated ENDOGENOUS insulin production (insulinoma, sulfonylurea use). Exogenous insulin has NO C-peptide, so exogenous insulin administration produces HIGH insulin + LOW C-peptide. Classic factitious hypoglycemia picture (often medical personnel).
Trap 06 — PNMT Location
"PNMT is evenly distributed across all sympathetic postganglionic neurons, which is why NE and Epi are released together at every synapse."
FALSE. PNMT is localized to the adrenal medulla only — it requires high local cortisol from the adrenal cortex (via intra-adrenal portal system) for induction. Sympathetic postganglionic nerves lack this high cortisol exposure and therefore release primarily NE. The adrenal medulla releases ~80% epinephrine, 20% NE. This spatial segregation is the basis for the "glandular sympathetic" vs. "neural sympathetic" distinction.
Trap 07 — Down-regulation
"Up-regulation of hormone receptors is a defensive mechanism the cell uses to protect against toxic levels of hormone."
FALSE. Down-regulation (NOT up-regulation) is the defensive response to HIGH hormone exposure. Up-regulation happens with LOW hormone (e.g., denervation hypersensitivity, prolonged fasting → ↑insulin receptors). Easy memory trick: "Lots of hormone → Lose receptors (down); Little hormone → Lots of receptors (up)."
Trap 08 — Eicosanoid Pharmacology
"NSAIDs and glucocorticoids both work by blocking COX, so they are essentially interchangeable anti-inflammatory drugs."
FALSE. NSAIDs inhibit COX only → block prostaglandin/thromboxane synthesis but NOT leukotrienes. Glucocorticoids inhibit phospholipase A2 (upstream of both COX and LOX) → block ALL eicosanoids + have transcriptional effects on inflammatory cytokines. Corticosteroids are therefore much more broadly immunosuppressive and carry different clinical risks.
Trap 09 — EC50 Interpretation
"A patient with insulin resistance has a LEFT-shifted insulin dose-response curve because they need more insulin for the same effect."
FALSE — reversed. Insulin resistance shifts the curve to the RIGHT (higher EC50). More hormone is required for half-maximal response. A LEFT shift would mean more sensitivity (hypersensitive receptor, achieve max response at lower hormone levels) — the opposite of resistance.

LO 3 · Catecholamines
Pheochromocytoma — "The 10% Tumor"
A chromaffin-cell tumor of the adrenal medulla producing excess catecholamines. Classic "5 P's": pressure (HTN), pain (headache), palpitations, perspiration, pallor. Rule of 10s: 10% bilateral, 10% extra-adrenal (organ of Zuckerkandl), 10% malignant, 10% familial (MEN 2A/2B, NF1, VHL).
Biochemistry: Tyrosine hydroxylase activity is upregulated → massive NE/Epi synthesis. Continuous breakdown to metanephrines → measure 24-hr urine fractionated metanephrines or plasma free metanephrines (more sensitive than catecholamines which are pulsatile).
Pharmacology: Pre-op α-blockade first (phenoxybenzamine) then β-blocker. Never β-block first — unopposed α activity can cause hypertensive crisis. α-methyltyrosine (metyrosine) blocks tyrosine hydroxylase → reduces catecholamine synthesis before surgery.
LO 4 · Steroid Biosynthesis
Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia — Three Enzyme Defects
Autosomal recessive defects of adrenal steroidogenesis. Always three presentations to memorize: 21-OH (most common), 11β-OH, 17α-OH — each has a distinct cortisol/aldosterone/androgen profile.
21-hydroxylase (90% of cases): No cortisol, no aldosterone, ↑↑androgens. Salt-wasting, hyperkalemia, hypotension, virilization.
11β-hydroxylase: No cortisol, no aldosterone, but 11-deoxycorticosterone (weak MC) accumulates → HTN + hypokalemia + virilization. ↑ 11-deoxycortisol.
17α-hydroxylase: ↑ mineralocorticoids (DOC, corticosterone), no cortisol, no sex steroids → HTN + hypokalemia + sexual infantilism / ambiguous male genitalia.

Mnemonic: 21 → salt-wasting ("low BP"). 11β → HTN + virile. 17α → HTN, NO sex steroids.
LO 5 · Carrier Proteins
TBG in Pregnancy & OCPs — The Free Hormone Hypothesis in Action
Estrogen (pregnancy or OCPs) stimulates hepatic TBG synthesis → total T4 rises, but free T4 stays the same. Patient is clinically euthyroid. Same mechanism applies to CBG and cortisol.
The principle: Carrier proteins do NOT determine endocrine activity — they buffer delivery and extend half-life. Only FREE hormone can bind receptors. Ordering total T4 in a pregnant patient will mislead you; always use FREE T4 + TSH.

Same applies to testosterone in hyperthyroidism — ↑SHBG → ↑total T but ↓free T → hypogonadism symptoms DESPITE normal total T. Always ask: "Is my total measurement being shifted by a carrier change rather than a true hormone disturbance?"
LO 6 · GPCR Signaling
Pseudohypoparathyroidism & McCune-Albright — GNAS Mirror Images
Two opposing GPCR diseases from the same gene: loss-of-function GNAS → pseudohypoparathyroidism 1a. Activating GNAS (post-zygotic, mosaic) → McCune-Albright.
PHP1a (loss of Gsα): PTH cannot raise cAMP → end-organ resistance → ↑PTH but ↓Ca, ↑Pi. Also resistance to other Gs-coupled hormones (TSH, LH, FSH). Phenotype: Albright hereditary osteodystrophy (short stature, short 4th/5th metacarpals, round face, obesity).

McCune-Albright (gain-of-function Gsα): Constitutive cAMP in affected tissues → precocious puberty, polyostotic fibrous dysplasia, unilateral "coast of Maine" café-au-lait macules. Always MOSAIC; germline mutation is embryonically lethal. Tests LO 6 via same receptor family with opposite pathology.
LO 7 · Receptor Regulation
Insulin Resistance in T2DM — Down-regulation Personified
Chronic overnutrition → chronic ↑insulin → peripheral insulin receptor internalization + post-receptor defects → insulin resistance. β-cells initially hypersecrete (early T2DM = high insulin + high glucose), then exhaust (late T2DM = low insulin + high glucose, C-peptide declines).
Contrast with T1DM: autoimmune β-cell destruction → insulin always LOW + C-peptide always LOW. No insulin resistance at the start. Requires exogenous insulin from dx.

Pharmacologic target selection:
• Metformin — ↑sensitivity (works only if some insulin is available)
• Sulfonylureas — stimulate β-cells (fail when β-cells are exhausted)
• GLP-1 agonists / SGLT2 inhibitors — disease-modifying; useful throughout
• Exogenous insulin — always works (bypasses receptor problem via mass-action at what receptors remain)
LO 2 · Peptide Hormone Processing
Factitious Hypoglycemia — The C-peptide Test
Differential for a hypoglycemic patient with elevated insulin: insulinoma vs. sulfonylurea vs. exogenous insulin. C-peptide is the single best distinguisher — it is co-secreted with endogenous insulin but absent from exogenous preparations.
Insulin + C-peptide both HIGH: endogenous insulin excess → insulinoma OR sulfonylurea (check urine for sulfonylurea metabolites to distinguish).
Insulin HIGH, C-peptide LOW: exogenous insulin (factitious or medical misadventure).
Insulin LOW + C-peptide LOW: appropriate response — look elsewhere for hypoglycemia cause (adrenal insuff., alcohol, medication).

Directly demonstrates why peptide hormone SYNTHESIS mechanics matter clinically: the equimolar cleavage of proinsulin → insulin + C-peptide is what makes this workup possible.
LO 2 · Eicosanoids
Eicosanoid Pharmacology — Lipid Hormones as Drug Targets
Eicosanoids (prostaglandins, thromboxanes, leukotrienes, prostacyclin) are derived from arachidonic acid. They act LOCALLY via membrane GPCRs and represent one of the richest drug-target classes in medicine.
The arachidonic acid cascade: Phospholipase A2 liberates arachidonic acid from membrane phospholipids → two pathways:
• Cyclooxygenase (COX-1/2) → prostaglandins (PGE₂, PGF₂α, PGD₂), thromboxane A₂, prostacyclin (PGI₂)
• Lipoxygenase (5-LOX) → leukotrienes (LTB₄, LTC₄/D₄/E₄)

Drug targets:
• NSAIDs (aspirin, ibuprofen, naproxen) — COX inhibitors; reduce pain/fever/inflammation by blocking prostaglandin synthesis
• Corticosteroids — inhibit phospholipase A2 → block BOTH COX and LOX pathways (broader anti-inflammatory)
• Misoprostol — PGE₁ analog; protects gastric mucosa, used as abortifacient, induces labor
• Dinoprostone — PGE₂; cervical ripening in labor induction
• Latanoprost / bimatoprost — PGF₂α analog; reduces intraocular pressure in glaucoma
• Alprostadil — PGE₁; maintains ductus arteriosus patency in cyanotic congenital heart disease
• Montelukast / zafirlukast — LTD₄ receptor antagonists for asthma
• Zileuton — 5-LOX inhibitor for asthma

Classic fact: aspirin irreversibly acetylates COX → lasting platelet effect (platelets can't synthesize new COX) → 7–10 day bleeding effect, the basis of low-dose cardioprotection.
LO 7 · Dose-Response Curves
Insulin Resistance — Reading the Dose-Response Curve
Plot glucose disposal against insulin concentration. A healthy subject has a steep curve with EC50 ~10 µU/mL. In T2DM, the curve shifts to the RIGHT (higher EC50) and, in severe disease, the maximum also falls. This is the quantitative hallmark of "decreased cellular sensitivity."
What shifts the curve:
• Receptor number ↓ (down-regulation from chronic hyperinsulinemia) — RIGHTWARD SHIFT
• Post-receptor signaling defects (SOCS proteins, IRS-1 serine phosphorylation) — lower maximum + rightward shift
• Lipotoxicity / ectopic fat (intramyocellular + hepatic) — impairs GLUT4 translocation

Reading the curve clinically:
• Early T2DM: β-cells move along the shifted curve (compensatory hyperinsulinemia → glucose near-normal but insulin markedly elevated)
• Late T2DM: β-cells exhaust → insulin falls → glucose rises sharply
• Therapy targets: weight loss (shifts curve left), metformin (↑ sensitivity), GLP-1 agonists (improve β-cell function and sensitivity)

EC50 framework (LO 7) applies broadly — pheochromocytoma downregulates β-receptors over time (explains tachyphylaxis), long-term opioid use shifts μ-receptor dose-response rightward (tolerance), etc.
LO 1 · Historical
The First Hormone Therapy — Thyroid Extract for Myxedema
In the 1890s, Victor Horsley and George Murray demonstrated that implanting sheep thyroid or injecting thyroid extract reversed the effects of myxedema (severe hypothyroidism) in humans — one of the first examples of endocrine replacement therapy.
Why this matters: Horsley/Murray's work proved the principle that a molecule secreted by a gland could travel through blood to restore function at a distant organ. The earlier Berthold rooster experiment (1849) had already shown that reimplanted testes restored secondary sex characteristics without nervous connections — establishing the blood-borne nature of endocrine signaling.

These historical foundations underpin every modern endocrine replacement therapy: insulin for T1DM, levothyroxine for hypothyroidism, hydrocortisone for Addison's, testosterone/estrogen for hypogonadism, recombinant GH for deficiency. Today, levothyroxine is one of the most commonly prescribed drugs in the United States — a direct descendant of Horsley's thyroid extract.

Rate-Limiting Enzymes (Must Know) High-Yield
Catecholamine synthesis: Tyrosine hydroxylase (Tyr → L-DOPA; cofactor: BH4).
Steroid biosynthesis: Desmolase (CYP11A1); regulated by StAR protein + ACTH.
Vitamin D activation: 1α-hydroxylase in renal PCT; ↑ by PTH, ↓ by FGF23.
Heme synthesis: ALA synthase (mitochondrial; vitamin B6-dependent).
Cholesterol synthesis: HMG-CoA reductase (target of statins).
Receptor Signaling Mnemonics Memory Hook
Gs (↑cAMP): "FLAT ChAMP" — FSH, LH, ACTH, TSH, CRH, hCG, ADH (V2), MSH, PTH (+ GHRH, glucagon, β-adrenergic).
Gq (IP3/DAG/Ca): "HAVe 1 M&M" — H1-histamine, α₁-adrenergic, V1-ADH, 1st order: M1/M3 muscarinic, TRH/GnRH, oxytocin, angiotensin II, gastrin.
Gi (↓cAMP): M2 muscarinic, α₂-adrenergic, D2 dopamine, somatostatin, opioids.
RTK: Insulin, IGF-1, FGF23 + growth factor family.
JAK-STAT: "Acidophils" (GH, PRL) + cytokines.
Trap Corner Common Errors
Do NOT confuse: α/β subunits of glycoprotein hormones (α shared, β unique).
Do NOT confuse: Up-regulation vs. down-regulation — "lots of hormone = lose receptors."
Do NOT confuse: Total vs. free hormone — only free is active.
Do NOT confuse: Thyroid hormone is NOT a steroid (it's an amine); it just ACTS like one (nuclear receptor).
Do NOT confuse: Carbidopa blocks PERIPHERAL DOPA decarboxylase (doesn't cross BBB). L-DOPA crosses BBB; dopamine does NOT.
Do NOT confuse: PNMT is only in adrenal medulla — sympathetic nerves release NE.
Do NOT confuse: StAR (shuttle) vs. desmolase (enzyme) — both on step 1 of steroid synthesis, but different molecules.

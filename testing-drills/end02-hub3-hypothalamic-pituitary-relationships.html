Question 1 of 5 — LO 1 · Portal System
A researcher injects a rapidly-cleared dye into the median eminence of a rat brain and observes the pattern of dye distribution over 10 seconds. The dye first appears in small capillaries at the base of the hypothalamus, then enters a set of veins running down the infundibulum, and finally diffuses into a second capillary bed. In which structure does the dye ultimately appear in this second capillary bed?
ANeurohypophysis (posterior pituitary)
BAdenohypophysis (anterior pituitary)
CPineal gland
DSupraoptic nucleus
ECavernous sinus
B — Adenohypophysis. The classic hypophyseal portal system: first capillary plexus in the median eminence (a circumventricular organ — no BBB) → portal veins in the infundibulum → second capillary plexus in the anterior pituitary. This two-plexus route delivers hypothalamic releasing/inhibiting hormones in HIGH local concentration to anterior pituitary cells without diluting them systemically. Posterior pituitary (A) uses a DIFFERENT system — direct axonal projections + regular arterial blood supply.
Question 2 of 5 — LO 2 · Hypothalamic Nuclei
A 32-year-old woman with schizophrenia has been on risperidone (D2 antagonist) for 3 years. She develops amenorrhea, galactorrhea, and fatigue. Her serum prolactin is 180 ng/mL. Dysregulation of which hypothalamic nucleus best explains the rise in prolactin caused by this medication?
ASupraoptic nucleus
BParaventricular nucleus
CArcuate nucleus
DVentromedial nucleus
EMedial preoptic nucleus
C — Arcuate nucleus. The arcuate nucleus is the source of dopamine (PIH / prolactin-inhibiting hormone), which TONICALLY INHIBITS prolactin release from anterior pituitary lactotropes. Risperidone and other D2 antagonists block the dopamine receptor on lactotropes → loss of tonic inhibition → ↑PRL. SON/PVN (A, B) make ADH and oxytocin. VMN (D) makes GHRH. Medial preoptic (E) makes GnRH. Tests LO 2 (nuclei ↔ hormones) applied to drug-induced hyperprolactinemia.
Question 3 of 5 — LO 3 · Histology
A 58-year-old woman undergoes transsphenoidal resection of a pituitary adenoma. Histology shows a monomorphic population of tumor cells with eosinophilic cytoplasm on H&E. Immunohistochemistry is positive for a single peptide hormone. Preoperative serum studies revealed markedly elevated IGF-1 and failure of GH to suppress with oral glucose. From which cell type did this tumor most likely arise?
ASomatotrope (acidophil)
BCorticotrope (basophil)
CThyrotrope (basophil)
DGonadotrope (basophil)
EChromophobe
A — Somatotrope. Acidophilic (eosinophilic) anterior pituitary cells are either somatotropes (GH) or lactotropes (PRL). Elevated IGF-1 + failure of GH to suppress with OGTT = acromegaly → somatotrope adenoma. Basophils (B–D) stain with hematoxylin and produce the other four hormones ("B-FLAT": basophils = FSH, LH, ACTH, TSH). Chromophobes (E) are non-secretory supporting cells. This question explicitly tests LO 3 histology linked to the clinical endpoint.
Question 4 of 5 — LO 3 · Embryology
A 7-year-old boy with progressive headache, vomiting, and visual field defects is found on MRI to have a suprasellar mass with prominent calcifications. Surgical biopsy reveals a cystic tumor with squamous epithelium and "machine-oil"–like fluid rich in cholesterol crystals. The epithelial component of this tumor arose from a remnant of which embryologic structure?
ARathke's pouch (oral ectoderm)
BDiencephalon (neuroectoderm)
CNeural crest cells
DBranchial (pharyngeal) pouch
ENotochord
A — Rathke's pouch. Craniopharyngioma — the classic tumor of childhood arising from Rathke's pouch remnants (oral ectoderm that forms the adenohypophysis). The calcifications + "machine-oil" cyst contents + suprasellar location + bitemporal hemianopia pattern is pathognomonic. The neurohypophysis, in contrast, comes from diencephalic neuroectoderm (B). Reinforces LO 3 embryologic origin of the two pituitary lobes.
Question 5 of 5 — LO 2 · Nonapeptides
A researcher performs immunohistochemistry on the rat brain using antibodies specific for oxytocin and ADH. Staining lights up cell bodies in two specific hypothalamic nuclei; axonal processes carrying these hormones descend through the median eminence and infundibulum; and the signal concentrates in eosinophilic, rounded swellings at axon terminals in the posterior pituitary. These rounded terminal swellings are known as:
ANissl bodies
BPsammoma bodies
CHerring bodies
DBirbeck granules
ERussell bodies
C — Herring bodies. These are the swollen axon terminals of SON/PVN neurons where ADH and oxytocin (the nonapeptides) are stored bound to neurophysins before release into fenestrated capillaries of the posterior pituitary. Nissl bodies (A) are rough ER in neuronal cell bodies. Psammoma bodies (B) are calcifications in certain tumors. Directly tests LO 2/3 integration.

Question 1 of 5 — LO 4 · Prolactin Regulation
A 45-year-old man is admitted after a motor vehicle collision with basilar skull fracture and pituitary stalk disruption confirmed on MRI. On hospital day 3, his serum labs show: cortisol 2.1 µg/dL (low), free T4 0.4 ng/dL (low), testosterone 62 ng/dL (low), and — unexpectedly — prolactin 78 ng/mL (elevated). Which of the following best explains the selective rise in prolactin despite global anterior pituitary insufficiency?
AEctopic prolactin secretion from injured brain tissue
BPreexisting undiagnosed prolactinoma
CLoss of dopamine delivery from hypothalamus to lactotropes due to stalk injury
DStress-induced elevation from surgical trauma
ECross-reactivity of PRL assay with GH
C. Prolactin is the ONLY anterior pituitary hormone under TONIC INHIBITION (by arcuate-nucleus dopamine reaching lactotropes via the portal system). All other AP hormones require POSITIVE releasing factors. Stalk injury abolishes BOTH: releasing factors can't reach their targets (→ ↓TSH, ↓ACTH, ↓LH/FSH, ↓GH), and dopamine can't reach lactotropes (→ ↑PRL). This classic "stalk effect" pattern is pathognomonic and directly tests LO 4.
Question 2 of 5 — LO 4 · GH Axis
A 36-year-old man has enlarged hands, coarse facial features, new hypertension, and sleep apnea. Random serum GH is 4 ng/mL (normal <1 in healthy adults). An oral glucose tolerance test is performed: at 2 hours, GH is 5.2 ng/mL (normal response: suppression below 1 ng/mL). Serum IGF-1 is 3× the upper limit of normal. Why is the OGTT a better CONFIRMATORY test than random GH in this patient?
AGH is bound to IGFBPs and cannot be measured directly
BGH is secreted pulsatilely, so a random value is unreliable, but failure to suppress with glucose demonstrates autonomous (non-physiologic) secretion
CGlucose directly assays the somatotrope tumor
DIGF-1 does not rise until GH has been elevated for 6 months
EGlucose stimulates GHRH in normal subjects
B. Normal GH secretion is highly PULSATILE (driven by GHRH pulses + ultra-short autoregulation, nocturnal peaks, stress/exercise bursts). A single random measurement can be high in a normal person or low in an acromegalic at a trough. Glucose normally suppresses GH via long-loop metabolic feedback (insulin/IGF-1-mediated). In acromegaly, the tumor ignores this feedback → GH remains high after oral glucose. Failure to suppress is the diagnostic gold standard. Tests LO 5 feedback/pulsatility principle.
Question 3 of 5 — LO 5 · Long vs. Short Loop
A researcher studies negative feedback in the cortisol axis. In experiment A, infusion of dexamethasone (a synthetic glucocorticoid) into a rat's jugular vein causes a marked decrease in both CRH and ACTH. In experiment B, microinjection of ACTH directly into the rat's hypothalamus causes a decrease in CRH but has no effect on anterior pituitary ACTH. In experiment C, microinjection of CRH into the median eminence causes a transient decrease in CRH release from adjacent hypothalamic nuclei. Which feedback loops are being demonstrated in experiments A, B, and C, respectively?
AA = long loop; B = short loop; C = ultra-short loop
BA = short loop; B = long loop; C = positive feedback
CA = ultra-short; B = long; C = short
DA = long; B = ultra-short; C = short
EAll three are examples of positive feedback
A. Long loop = target-organ (or target-mimicking) hormone → suppresses hypothalamus AND pituitary (dex = cortisol-mimicking → ↓CRH + ↓ACTH). Short loop = pituitary hormone → suppresses hypothalamus (ACTH → ↓CRH). Ultra-short loop = hypothalamic hormone → suppresses its OWN release via axon collaterals (CRH → ↓CRH), generating pulsatility. This is a direct conceptual test of LO 5 feedback hierarchy.
Question 4 of 5 — LO 5 · Positive Feedback / LH Surge
A 28-year-old woman has her pituitary and ovarian hormones measured daily through one menstrual cycle. On day 11, estradiol is 320 pg/mL (high, end of follicular phase); on day 12, LH surges from 20 → 78 mIU/mL; ovulation follows on day 13. In the luteal phase, progesterone rises markedly and estradiol remains high, but LH remains suppressed. Which feedback mechanism best accounts for the LH surge on day 12?
ARising progesterone exerts positive feedback on GnRH and LH
BHigh sustained estradiol switches from negative to positive feedback on GnRH → LH
CUltra-short-loop feedback of GnRH causes auto-excitation
DInhibin B switches from inhibition to stimulation of FSH
EFSH directly stimulates LH receptors on gonadotropes
B. One of two classic positive-feedback scenarios in endocrine physiology. High, sustained estradiol (above ~200 pg/mL for >48 h) flips the GnRH/LH response from suppression to activation → massive LH surge → ovulation. Once ovulation occurs, the corpus luteum produces progesterone, which RESTORES negative feedback — so even though estradiol stays high, LH is suppressed in the luteal phase. Progesterone NEVER exerts positive feedback in this system. The other positive-feedback example is oxytocin/labor.
Question 5 of 5 — LO 4/5 · Pulsatility Principle
A 28-year-old man with idiopathic hypogonadotropic hypogonadism (low LH, FSH, testosterone; small testes; infertility) is being treated for fertility. Therapy option 1: daily injection of long-acting GnRH agonist. Therapy option 2: subcutaneous infusion pump delivering GnRH in 75-ng pulses every 90 minutes. Over 2 weeks, option 1 causes LH and FSH to fall to undetectable levels, while option 2 restores them toward normal. Which principle best explains this contrast?
ALong-acting GnRH agonists have weaker affinity for the GnRH receptor than endogenous GnRH
BContinuous GnRH stimulation causes down-regulation of pituitary GnRH receptors; pulsatile delivery preserves receptor sensitivity
CPulsatile delivery results in higher cumulative GnRH exposure
DContinuous GnRH is metabolized differently in the liver
EOnly pulsatile GnRH reaches the pituitary through the portal system
B. The LO 5 pulsatility principle in pure form. Continuous GnRH → receptor down-regulation on pituitary gonadotropes (same receptor mass, but internalized / desensitized) → LH/FSH fall. This is exactly why leuprolide given CONTINUOUSLY is used as "chemical castration" for prostate cancer and endometriosis. Pulsatile GnRH mimics the physiologic every-60-to-90-minute hypothalamic rhythm → LH/FSH respond normally → fertility can be restored (e.g., in Kallmann syndrome, hypothalamic amenorrhea). The same principle applies broadly — many hormones require pulsatility to avoid receptor desensitization.

Question 1 of 5 — Sheehan Syndrome
A 31-year-old woman delivered a healthy term infant 6 weeks ago after an obstetric course complicated by severe postpartum hemorrhage (blood loss ~2,800 mL) requiring 5 units of PRBC. She reports persistent fatigue, inability to breastfeed, no return of menses, cold intolerance, and orthostatic lightheadedness. Labs: TSH 0.5 (low-normal), free T4 0.5 ng/dL (low), prolactin <1 ng/mL (very low), 8 AM cortisol 2.4 µg/dL (low), ACTH 8 pg/mL (low), LH <0.5 IU/L, FSH 1.1 IU/L. Urine osmolality and serum Na⁺ are normal. Which of the following best explains ALL of her findings?
AIschemic necrosis of the pregnancy-hypertrophied anterior pituitary; posterior pituitary spared
BLymphocytic hypophysitis — autoimmune destruction of all pituitary cells
CPrimary hypothyroidism from postpartum thyroiditis with secondary pituitary suppression
DEmpty sella syndrome incidentally discovered postpartum
ECentral diabetes insipidus from pituitary stalk injury
A — Sheehan syndrome. Pregnancy doubles lactotrope mass, making the anterior pituitary dependent on increased portal flow. Major postpartum hemorrhage → hypotension → ischemic necrosis of the anterior lobe. LOW prolactin → failure to lactate (classically the first sign). LOW LH/FSH → amenorrhea. LOW TSH → secondary hypothyroidism. LOW ACTH → adrenal insufficiency. Posterior pituitary SPARED — different arterial supply explains preserved ADH (normal Na⁺ + osm). Primary thyroid disease (C) would give HIGH TSH. LO 6 "all iterations" applied to a classic case.
Question 2 of 5 — Exogenous Steroid Withdrawal
A 55-year-old man with polymyalgia rheumatica has been on prednisone 20 mg daily for 14 months. He feels well-controlled and stops taking the medication abruptly. Two days later, he presents to the ED with nausea, severe fatigue, diffuse abdominal pain, and hypotension (BP 82/50). Labs: Na⁺ 129, K⁺ 3.9 (normal), glucose 58, cortisol <1 µg/dL, ACTH 8 pg/mL (low). Aldosterone and renin are normal. Which of the following best describes the pathophysiology of his condition?
AAutoimmune destruction of the adrenal cortex (Addison's)
BChronic long-loop suppression of CRH and ACTH → adrenal atrophy; abrupt withdrawal of exogenous glucocorticoid left him with neither endogenous cortisol nor adrenal capacity to respond
CPituitary apoplexy
DBilateral adrenal hemorrhage (Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome)
EIsolated mineralocorticoid deficiency
B. Chronic exogenous glucocorticoid → long-loop feedback suppresses CRH AND ACTH → adrenal zona fasciculata atrophies over weeks-months. When the drug is stopped abruptly, endogenous cortisol production cannot restart quickly. Notice aldosterone and renin are normal — the zona glomerulosa is NOT ACTH-dependent and remains functional (which is why hyperkalemia from mineralocorticoid deficiency is absent here, unlike primary Addison's). Classic LO 6 "exogenous hormone" case. Always taper chronic steroids; stress-dose during illness.
Question 3 of 5 — Secondary vs. Tertiary Hypothyroidism
A 48-year-old man is diagnosed with hypothyroidism based on labs: free T4 0.4 ng/dL (LOW), TSH 1.0 mIU/L (inappropriately low-normal). Endocrinology orders a TRH stimulation test: after IV TRH administration, TSH rises briskly from 1.0 to 18 mIU/L at 30 minutes and returns to baseline by 2 hours. This response pattern is most consistent with which of the following?
APrimary hypothyroidism (thyroid gland failure)
BSecondary hypothyroidism (pituitary TSH failure)
CTertiary hypothyroidism (hypothalamic TRH failure)
DEuthyroid sick syndrome from chronic illness
EThyroid hormone resistance (TRβ mutation)
C — Tertiary (hypothalamic). If the pituitary is intact but wasn't receiving TRH, it will RESPOND briskly when exogenous TRH is provided → TSH rises normally or even exaggeratedly. If the problem were pituitary (B), exogenous TRH would not elicit much TSH rise because the gland itself is damaged. Primary (A) has HIGH baseline TSH. Thyroid resistance (E) has HIGH T4 + HIGH TSH. This test is a pure application of LO 6 — distinguishing hypothalamic from pituitary failure by adding the missing releasing hormone.
Question 4 of 5 — Ectopic ACTH vs. Pituitary Cushing
A 70-year-old man with a 60-pack-year smoking history presents with 2 months of proximal muscle weakness, new hypertension (BP 190/110), edema, and severe hypokalemia (K⁺ 2.5). He also has notable hyperpigmentation of skin creases and gums. Labs: 24-hr urine free cortisol 8× normal; ACTH 380 pg/mL (very high). Low-dose dexamethasone suppression test fails to suppress cortisol. High-dose dexamethasone suppression also fails (no change in cortisol). CT chest reveals a 3-cm perihilar mass. What is the most likely diagnosis?
ACushing disease (pituitary ACTH-secreting adenoma)
BEctopic ACTH secretion from small-cell lung carcinoma
CAdrenal cortisol-secreting adenoma
DExogenous corticosteroid use
EPseudo-Cushing from alcohol use
B — Ectopic ACTH. Key: BOTH low- AND high-dose dex fail to suppress → tumor is feedback-INDEPENDENT. Very high ACTH + hyperpigmentation (POMC cleavage produces MSH along with ACTH) + severe hypokalemic alkalosis (excess cortisol overwhelms 11β-HSD2 and acts at mineralocorticoid receptors) + lung mass in a smoker = small-cell lung carcinoma. Pituitary Cushing disease (A) SUPPRESSES at high dose. Adrenal (C) + exogenous (D) have LOW ACTH. Directly tests LO 5/6 feedback logic in a classic clinical integration.
Question 5 of 5 — Kallmann Syndrome
A 17-year-old boy is evaluated for delayed puberty. He has no axillary or pubic hair, small testes (3 mL volume), and has never detected olfactory cues in a standardized smell test. Labs: LH 0.8 IU/L (low), FSH 1.5 IU/L (low), testosterone 28 ng/dL (very low), prolactin normal. MRI shows absent olfactory bulbs but otherwise normal pituitary and hypothalamus. Genetic testing reveals a mutation in ANOS1 (KAL1). Which of the following best describes the underlying problem?
APrimary testicular failure (hypergonadotropic hypogonadism)
BKlinefelter syndrome (47,XXY)
CFailure of GnRH neurons to migrate from the olfactory placode to the hypothalamus
DAndrogen receptor mutation (complete androgen insensitivity)
EPituitary macroadenoma with gonadotrope compression
C — Kallmann syndrome. GnRH neurons arise from the olfactory placode and migrate into the hypothalamus alongside olfactory neurons. KAL1 (ANOS1) mutations disrupt this migration → absent olfactory bulbs (anosmia) + absent hypothalamic GnRH → hypogonadotropic hypogonadism (LOW LH/FSH → LOW T). Klinefelter (B) = HIGH LH/FSH (hypergonadotropic — primary testicular failure). AIS (D) has normal-to-high T. Classic tertiary/central deficiency via developmental anatomy — a beautiful LO 2/4/6 integration question.

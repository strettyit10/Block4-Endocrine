KEY: The anterior pituitary has NO direct neural input. The hypothalamus reaches it only via the hypothalamic-hypophyseal portal system — a vascular "express line" from median eminence to anterior pit. Severing the stalk blocks anterior pituitary signaling (except prolactin, which is under tonic inhibition, so it PARADOXICALLY rises).

Q1. A 32-year-old woman has severe postpartum hemorrhage with prolonged hypotension. Three weeks later, she cannot lactate, has amenorrhea, fatigue, and cold intolerance. Morning cortisol is low, TSH is low-normal, and free T4 is low. Which is the BEST explanation?
A. Autoimmune lymphocytic hypophysitis
B. Ischemic necrosis of the anterior pituitary (Sheehan syndrome)
C. Isolated TSH deficiency from a TRH-receptor mutation
D. Primary hypothyroidism
E. Rathke's cleft cyst compressing the stalk
B — Sheehan syndrome.
During pregnancy, lactotroph hyperplasia makes the anterior pituitary more susceptible to infarction when postpartum hemorrhage causes hypotension. Classic sequence: inability to lactate (prolactin loss first) → amenorrhea (LH/FSH) → hypothyroidism (low TSH with low T4 = secondary) → adrenal insufficiency (low cortisol, inappropriately low-normal ACTH). Posterior pituitary is usually spared.
Q2. A 42-year-old man presents with new bitemporal hemianopsia, headache, galactorrhea, and low libido. MRI shows a 1.6-cm sellar mass. Serum prolactin is 280 ng/mL (normal <20). The BEST initial therapy is:
A. Transsphenoidal surgical resection
B. Stereotactic radiation
C. Cabergoline (dopamine agonist)
D. Somatostatin analog (octreotide)
E. Levothyroxine
C — Cabergoline (dopamine agonist).
Prolactinomas are almost always first-line managed medically. Prolactin is under tonic dopaminergic inhibition from arcuate nucleus neurons; a D2 agonist restores inhibition, shrinking the tumor and reversing symptoms (including visual field defects in most cases). Cabergoline is preferred over bromocriptine for efficacy and tolerability. Surgery is reserved for medication failure, CSF leak, or acute visual compromise.
Q3. A 7-year-old boy has short stature, headaches, vomiting, and visual field defects. MRI shows a cystic, partly calcified suprasellar mass. Aspiration reveals “motor oil” fluid. The mass MOST likely arises from:
A. Remnants of the infundibular stalk
B. Meningothelial cells of the dura
C. Rathke's pouch remnants (oral ectoderm)
D. Pineal gland
E. Germ cells in the suprasellar region
C — Rathke's pouch remnants (craniopharyngioma).
Craniopharyngioma is a benign suprasellar tumor derived from oral ectoderm (Rathke's pouch) that normally forms the anterior pituitary. Bimodal age peaks: children (5–14) and adults (50–70). Hallmarks: cystic + solid with calcifications on imaging; “motor-oil” cholesterol-rich fluid on aspiration. Mass effects mimic pituitary adenoma (bitemporal hemianopsia) and can cause hypopituitarism or diabetes insipidus (stalk involvement).
Q4. A researcher studies pituitary histology. Which statement is TRUE about staining and cell type?
A. Somatotrophs are basophils; they secrete GH
B. Gonadotrophs are acidophils that stain with eosin
C. Corticotrophs are basophils and secrete POMC-derived ACTH, β-endorphin, and MSH
D. All anterior pituitary cell types are acidophils
E. Prolactinomas arise from thyrotrophs
C — Corticotrophs are basophils producing POMC-derived hormones.
Staining: Acidophils = GH (somatotrophs) + Prolactin (lactotrophs) — “GPA” (GH, PRL = Acidophil). Basophils = FSH, LH, ACTH, TSH (“B-FLAT”). Corticotrophs cleave POMC into ACTH, β-endorphin, and MSH; this is why primary adrenal insufficiency (high ACTH/POMC) causes hyperpigmentation.
Q5. A patient sustains a traumatic brain injury with pituitary stalk transection. Two weeks later, which of the following hormone patterns is MOST likely?
A. Prolactin ↑; ACTH, TSH, LH/FSH, GH all ↓
B. Prolactin ↓; ACTH, TSH, LH/FSH, GH all ↓
C. All anterior pituitary hormones ↑ due to loss of tonic inhibition
D. All anterior and posterior hormones ↑
E. ADH ↑, other hormones unchanged
A — Prolactin ↑; the other four ↓.
The anterior pituitary depends on the hypothalamic-hypophyseal portal system. Sectioning the stalk cuts off all releasing hormones → ACTH, TSH, LH, FSH, GH fall. Prolactin is uniquely under tonic dopaminergic inhibition — so losing dopamine delivery releases the brake and prolactin rises. Expect central diabetes insipidus (ADH ↓) from loss of posterior pituitary delivery.
Q6. A 45-year-old woman has weight gain, cold intolerance, and galactorrhea. TSH is 42 (high). Free T4 is low. Serum prolactin is 58 ng/mL (mildly elevated). Which BEST explains her hyperprolactinemia?
A. Prolactinoma co-secreting TSH
B. TRH excess in primary hypothyroidism also stimulates lactotrophs
C. Primary adrenal insufficiency
D. Dopamine excess
E. Ectopic prolactin production by a breast tumor
B — TRH is a potent stimulator of BOTH TSH and prolactin.
In primary hypothyroidism, low T4 removes negative feedback → TRH ↑ → TSH ↑ and prolactin ↑ (because TRH also acts on lactotrophs). Treating the underlying hypothyroidism with levothyroxine normalizes both TSH and prolactin. Always check TSH before pursuing pituitary MRI in a patient with hyperprolactinemia.
Q7. A biochemist compares the receptor classes used by pituitary regulators. Which pairing is CORRECT?
A. TRH on thyrotroph → Gs → cAMP
B. CRH on corticotroph → Gs → cAMP
C. Somatostatin on somatotroph → Gs → ↑cAMP
D. ACTH on adrenal zona fasciculata → Gq → IP3
E. Dopamine on lactotroph → Gs → stimulates PRL
B — CRH is Gs-cAMP on corticotroph.
Quick map: Gs (cAMP): CRH, GHRH, ACTH, TSH, LH, FSH, MSH, ADH-V2, glucagon, β-adrenergic. Gq (IP3/DAG): TRH, GnRH, ADH-V1, oxytocin, α1. Gi (↓cAMP): somatostatin, dopamine (D2), α2, M2. JAK-STAT: GH, prolactin. A wrong (TRH is Gq); C wrong (somatostatin is Gi); D wrong (ACTH is Gs); E wrong (dopamine inhibits via Gi).
Q8. During the normal menstrual cycle, the mid-cycle LH surge is triggered by:
A. Chronic low estradiol exerting continued negative feedback on the pituitary
B. Sustained high-concentration estradiol for ~48 hours switching feedback from negative to POSITIVE on the hypothalamus and pituitary
C. Progesterone withdrawal signaling GnRH pulse amplitude to fall
D. Inhibin B from granulosa cells directly stimulating LH
E. FSH binding LH receptors on theca cells
B — Sustained high estradiol switches feedback to POSITIVE.
Estradiol normally exerts negative feedback, but when serum estradiol exceeds ~200 pg/mL for ~48 hours (pre-ovulation), the feedback flips to positive on both hypothalamus (↑ GnRH pulse frequency/amplitude) and pituitary (↑ gonadotroph sensitivity) → LH surge → ovulation. This is one of the few physiologic positive-feedback loops; others include oxytocin in parturition and clotting cascade amplification.

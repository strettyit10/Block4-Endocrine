Clinical tie-ins (LO 1)
Postpartum pituitary ischemic necrosis. Lactotroph hyperplasia during pregnancy outgrows blood supply → infarcts with postpartum hypotension. First sign: inability to lactate (PRL loss). Then amenorrhea, hypothyroidism, adrenal insufficiency
Rathke's pouch epithelial tumor; suprasellar, cystic + calcified, "motor-oil" fluid. Bimodal: kids 5–14 and adults 50–70. Bitemporal hemianopsia + hypopituitarism + DI
Acute hemorrhage/infarct of a pituitary adenoma. Sudden severe headache + visual loss + ophthalmoplegia + acute adrenal crisis. Give stress-dose steroids — medical emergency

MNEMONIC: Releasing = "CRH TRH GnRH GHRH" (all -RH). Inhibiting = Somatostatin + Dopamine. Dopamine tonically inhibits prolactin — anything that cuts the stalk releases the brake → prolactin ↑.
Stalk effect (LO 2)
PROLACTIN rises; ACTH, TSH, LH/FSH, GH all fall. ADH also falls (central DI)
Prolactin is uniquely under TONIC dopaminergic inhibition — stalk cut blocks dopamine delivery → lactotrophs escape inhibition → PRL ↑. All other hormones need positive releasing signals via the portal system

POMC — corticotroph precursor (LO 3)
Pro-opiomelanocortin — single precursor protein cleaved in corticotrophs
ACTH + β-endorphin + MSH (α-MSH, β-MSH); also β-lipotropin

Prolactin specifics (LO 4)
TONIC INHIBITION by dopamine (arcuate → tuberoinfundibular tract). Unique among pituitary hormones
TRH also STIMULATES lactotrophs. Clinical: primary hypothyroidism → low T4 → TRH ↑ → TSH ↑ AND prolactin ↑ (galactorrhea + amenorrhea)
Estrogen stimulates lactotroph HYPERPLASIA and prolactin synthesis — why postpartum pituitary is vulnerable to infarction (Sheehan)
Estrogen + progesterone BLOCK prolactin's action on breast. After placenta delivery, E/P plummet → prolactin-driven lactogenesis begins
Prolactin INHIBITS pulsatile GnRH → ↓ LH/FSH → ↓ estradiol/testosterone → amenorrhea, infertility, ↓ libido

Prediction drills (LO 6)
Cortisol ↑ → CRH & ACTH ↓ → CONTRALATERAL adrenal ATROPHIES (loses trophic signal). Adenoma side stays enlarged
Suppresses CRH + ACTH chronically → endogenous cortisol ↓ → BILATERAL adrenal atrophy. Abrupt withdrawal → adrenal crisis → TAPER
ACTH ↓, TSH ↓, LH/FSH ↓, GH ↓, PROLACTIN ↑ (dopamine brake lost). Also ADH ↓ → central DI
Prolactin ↑ → suppresses GnRH → LH/FSH ↓ → testosterone/estradiol ↓ → amenorrhea, infertility, ↓ libido. Plus galactorrhea
hCG shares α-subunit with TSH → cross-reacts with TSH receptor → TSH ↓, free T4 normal-to-slightly-↑. Transient; resolves after peak hCG (~10 wk)
Low T4 → TRH ↑ → stimulates BOTH TSH and prolactin → galactorrhea/amenorrhea. Always check TSH before pituitary MRI in hyperprolactinemia
Dynamic tests (LO 5, 6)
Normal HPA suppresses → cortisol <1.8 μg/dL next AM. FAILURE to suppress = Cushing syndrome (any cause). Screens IN Cushing
Suppresses pituitary ACTH (Cushing DISEASE) but NOT ectopic ACTH (paraneoplastic) or adrenal adenoma (ACTH already low). Differentiates causes
Cosyntropin (synthetic ACTH). Normal: cortisol rises >18. Primary adrenal insufficiency: no response. Secondary: blunted acutely, improves with repeat dosing

Q1. A 32-year-old woman has severe postpartum hemorrhage with prolonged hypotension. Three weeks later she cannot lactate, has amenorrhea, fatigue, and cold intolerance. Cortisol low, TSH low-normal, free T4 low. Best explanation?
A. Autoimmune lymphocytic hypophysitis
B. Ischemic necrosis of the anterior pituitary (Sheehan syndrome)
C. Isolated TSH deficiency
D. Primary hypothyroidism
E. Rathke's cleft cyst compressing stalk
B — Sheehan syndrome.
Pregnancy causes lactotroph hyperplasia; anterior pituitary outgrows its blood supply → infarcts with postpartum hypotension. Classic sequence: inability to lactate (prolactin) → amenorrhea (LH/FSH) → hypothyroidism (low TSH + low T4 = secondary) → adrenal insufficiency. Posterior pituitary usually spared.
Q2. A 42-year-old man has new bitemporal hemianopsia, headache, galactorrhea, low libido. MRI shows 1.6-cm sellar mass. Prolactin 280 ng/mL. Best initial therapy?
A. Transsphenoidal surgical resection
B. Stereotactic radiation
C. Cabergoline (dopamine agonist)
D. Octreotide
E. Levothyroxine
C — Cabergoline.
Prolactinomas are managed medically first. Prolactin is under tonic dopaminergic inhibition; a D2 agonist restores inhibition, shrinking tumor and reversing symptoms (including visual field defects). Cabergoline preferred over bromocriptine (efficacy + tolerability). Surgery reserved for medication failure, CSF leak, or acute visual compromise.
Q3. A 7-year-old boy has short stature, headaches, vomiting, visual field defects. MRI: cystic, calcified suprasellar mass. Aspiration: “motor oil” fluid. Origin?
A. Infundibular stalk remnants
B. Meningothelial cells
C. Rathke's pouch remnants (oral ectoderm)
D. Pineal gland
E. Germ cells
C — Rathke's pouch remnants (craniopharyngioma).
Benign suprasellar tumor from oral ectoderm; bimodal peaks (kids 5–14, adults 50–70). Classic: cystic + solid + calcifications + “motor-oil” cholesterol fluid. Mass effects mimic pituitary adenoma (bitemporal hemianopsia) and can cause hypopituitarism + DI.
Q4. Which statement about pituitary staining is TRUE?
A. Somatotrophs are basophils
B. Gonadotrophs are acidophils
C. Corticotrophs are basophils that cleave POMC into ACTH, β-endorphin, and MSH
D. All anterior cells are acidophils
E. Prolactinomas arise from thyrotrophs
C — Corticotrophs are basophils with POMC cleavage.
Acidophils = GH (somatotrophs) + Prolactin (“GPA”). Basophils = FSH, LH, ACTH, TSH (“B-FLAT”). Corticotrophs cleave POMC into ACTH + β-endorphin + MSH — explains hyperpigmentation of primary adrenal insufficiency.
Q5. After pituitary stalk transection, which hormone pattern is most likely?
A. Prolactin ↑; ACTH, TSH, LH/FSH, GH all ↓
B. Prolactin ↓; all others ↓
C. All anterior hormones ↑
D. All anterior + posterior ↑
E. ADH ↑, others unchanged
A — Prolactin ↑, others ↓.
Anterior pituitary depends on the portal system. Stalk cut blocks all releasing hormones → ACTH, TSH, LH, FSH, GH fall. Prolactin is uniquely under tonic dopaminergic inhibition — losing dopamine releases the brake → PRL rises. Also expect central DI (ADH ↓).
Q6. 45-year-old woman: weight gain, cold intolerance, galactorrhea. TSH 42, free T4 low, prolactin mildly elevated. Cause of hyperprolactinemia?
A. Prolactinoma co-secreting TSH
B. TRH excess in primary hypothyroidism also stimulates lactotrophs
C. Primary adrenal insufficiency
D. Dopamine excess
E. Ectopic prolactin
B — TRH stimulates BOTH TSH and prolactin.
In primary hypothyroidism: low T4 → TRH ↑ → TSH ↑ AND prolactin ↑ (TRH acts on lactotrophs too). Treating hypothyroidism with levothyroxine normalizes both. Always check TSH before pituitary MRI in hyperprolactinemia workup.
Q7. Which receptor pairing is CORRECT?
A. TRH on thyrotroph → Gs → cAMP
B. CRH on corticotroph → Gs → cAMP
C. Somatostatin → Gs → ↑cAMP
D. ACTH on adrenal → Gq → IP3
E. Dopamine on lactotroph → Gs → stimulates PRL
B — CRH is Gs-cAMP on corticotroph.
Quick map: Gs: CRH, GHRH, ACTH, TSH, LH, FSH, MSH, ADH-V2, β-adrenergic. Gq: TRH, GnRH, ADH-V1, oxytocin, α1. Gi: somatostatin, dopamine (D2), α2, M2. JAK-STAT: GH, prolactin. A wrong (TRH is Gq); C wrong (somatostatin is Gi); D wrong (ACTH is Gs); E wrong (dopamine inhibits).
Q8. The mid-cycle LH surge is triggered by:
A. Chronic low estradiol exerting negative feedback
B. Sustained high estradiol for ~48 h switching feedback to POSITIVE on hypothalamus + pituitary
C. Progesterone withdrawal
D. Inhibin B stimulating LH
E. FSH binding LH receptors on theca
B — Sustained high estradiol switches feedback to POSITIVE.
Normally estradiol is inhibitory, but when serum E2 exceeds ~200 pg/mL for ~48 h (pre-ovulation), feedback flips to positive on both hypothalamus (↑ GnRH pulse frequency/amplitude) and pituitary (↑ gonadotroph sensitivity) → LH surge → ovulation. One of the few physiologic positive-feedback loops (others: oxytocin in parturition, clotting cascade).

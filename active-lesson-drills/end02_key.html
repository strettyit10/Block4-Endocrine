LO 1
ON EXAM — Rathke's pouch (oral ectoderm, ascends) → anterior pit; infundibulum (neural ectoderm, descends) → posterior pit; meet ~6 wk. Adenohypophysis = pars distalis + intermedia + tuberalis; neurohypophysis = pars nervosa + infundibular stem + median eminence. Anterior pit reached ONLY via portal system (2 plexuses); posterior pit by AXONS. Median eminence = circumventricular organ. Stalk cut → all anterior hormones fall EXCEPT prolactin (rises).

WHY a portal system? To deliver tiny amounts of releasing hormones DIRECTLY + CONCENTRATED to anterior pit cells without systemic dilution. The median eminence is a circumventricular organ (outside BBB, fenestrated capillaries) — large peptides (CRH, TRH, GnRH) can exit without crossing BBB.
Hypothalamohypophyseal tract (LO 1)
Supraoptic (SON) + Paraventricular (PVN)
MAGNOCELLULAR neurons (large cell bodies) — distinct from parvocellular neurons to median eminence
ADH (mostly SON) + Oxytocin (mostly PVN) — both 9-aa cyclic peptides
Synthesized as preprohormones in cell bodies, packaged with neurophysin in secretory vesicles, transported anterograde by kinesin/microtubules down axons
Neurophysin II (with ADH) + neurophysin I (with oxytocin) — co-secreted; no functional role in periphery
Herring bodies — eosinophilic swellings along axon terminals representing accumulated neurosecretory vesicles
Into a CAPILLARY (NOT a synapse) — why posterior pit is a "neurohemal organ"
Tuberoinfundibular tract (LO 1)
Arcuate nucleus → axon terminals at median eminence (NOT in posterior pit)
DOPAMINE — released into primary capillary plexus → long portal veins → reaches anterior pit lactotrophs
D2 receptor on lactotroph; coupled to Gi → ↓cAMP → INHIBITS prolactin synthesis + release
Stalk transection blocks dopamine delivery → loss of tonic inhibition → prolactin RISES. All other anterior pit hormones FALL (lose positive releasing-hormone delivery)

Clinical syndromes (LO 1)

Acute hemorrhage / infarction INTO existing adenoma. Triad: sudden severe headache + visual loss + ophthalmoplegia. Acute adrenal crisis → STRESS-DOSE STEROIDS. Medical emergency

POMC — corticotroph precursor (LO 3)
Pro-opiomelanocortin — single precursor protein cleaved in corticotrophs

Master prediction rules (LO 6)
Autonomous tumor at level X SUPPRESSES upstream regulators via negative feedback. E.g., adrenal adenoma making cortisol → ACTH ↓ → CRH ↓
Damage at level X reduces all DOWNSTREAM hormones (no upstream stimulation). Upstream regulators may rise due to lost feedback
In UNDER-activity: trophic hormone HIGH = primary, LOW = secondary. In OVER-activity: trophic hormone SUPPRESSED = primary (autonomous target), HIGH = secondary (autonomous pituitary adenoma)
Exogenous hormone (prednisone, T4, testosterone) functionally mimics "target hormone ↑" — suppresses upstream trophic hormones, endogenous gland ATROPHIES. Sudden withdrawal → crisis
Stalk transection: PRL RISES (loses dopamine inhibition); all other anterior pit hormones fall (lose releasing-hormone delivery); posterior pit also fails → central DI. Paradox unique to prolactin
LO 1–6 · USMLE Step 1 Style
10 questions — answers pre-revealed.
Q1. Embryologic origin of anterior pituitary?
A. Neural crest
B. Oral (surface) ectoderm — Rathke's pouch
C. Neural ectoderm — infundibulum
D. Endoderm
E. Splanchnic mesoderm
B — Rathke's pouch (oral ectoderm).
Ascending from the roof of the primitive oral cavity at 3–4 weeks. The infundibulum (option C) descends from the diencephalic floor and forms the POSTERIOR pituitary. They meet ~week 6. Persistent Rathke's remnants → craniopharyngioma or Rathke's cleft cyst.
Q2. Where is the PRIMARY capillary plexus of the portal system, and what is its significance?
A. Anterior pit; receives systemic arterial blood
B. Posterior pit; collects axon-terminal hormones
C. Median eminence; supplied by superior hypophyseal artery; collects hypothalamic releasing hormones
D. Cavernous sinus; drainage
E. Optic chiasm
C — Primary plexus in median eminence.
Branches of the superior hypophyseal artery form the primary capillary plexus at the median eminence (a circumventricular organ, outside BBB with fenestrated capillaries). Hypothalamic neurons release releasing/inhibiting hormones here → long portal veins → secondary plexus in anterior pit → concentrated delivery without systemic dilution.
Q3. Postpartum woman with severe hemorrhage, 3 weeks later cannot lactate, amenorrhea, fatigue. Labs: TSH low, T4 low, cortisol low. Posterior pit function preserved. Best explanation?
A. Thyroid autoimmune destruction
B. Pregnancy lactotroph hyperplasia outgrew portal blood supply → ischemic infarct of adenohypophysis; posterior pit spared (supplied by inferior hypophyseal artery)
C. Hypothalamic stroke
D. Waterhouse-Friderichsen
E. Empty sella
B — Sheehan syndrome.
Pregnancy → lactotroph hyperplasia → anterior pit outgrows its portal blood supply → postpartum hypotension → ischemic infarction of adenohypophysis. Posterior pit is supplied directly by the inferior hypophyseal artery, so it's typically spared (no DI). Hormone loss sequence often: PRL → FSH/LH → TSH → ACTH.
Q4. Complete pituitary stalk transection. Expected anterior pit hormones 2 weeks later?
A. All anterior hormones fall
B. Prolactin RISES; ACTH, TSH, LH/FSH, GH fall
C. All anterior hormones rise
D. Only ADH falls; anterior unaffected
E. Only prolactin falls
B — PRL ↑, others ↓.
Anterior pit depends entirely on portal delivery of releasing hormones. Stalk cut → no CRH/TRH/GnRH/GHRH delivered → ACTH/TSH/LH/FSH/GH fall. Prolactin is uniquely under tonic dopaminergic inhibition via the tuberoinfundibular tract — losing dopamine releases the brake → prolactin RISES. Also expect central DI from lost axon-terminal ADH delivery.
Q5. Bitemporal hemianopsia from pituitary adenoma — which fibers are compressed?
A. Uncrossed temporal retinal fibers in optic tract
B. Crossing NASAL retinal fibers at optic chiasm directly above pituitary
C. Crossed temporal fibers in LGN
D. Optic radiations in parietal lobe
E. Pretectal nuclei
B — Crossing nasal fibers at the optic chiasm.
Nasal retinal fibers carry information from the temporal visual fields and CROSS at the optic chiasm, which sits directly above the pituitary fossa. A pituitary mass growing superiorly compresses these crossing fibers → loss of both temporal visual fields. Uncrossed temporal retinal fibers (carrying nasal field) are typically spared.
Q6. 58-year-old on chronic high-dose prednisone for 8 months. Predict HPA axis.
A. CRH ↑, ACTH ↑, endogenous cortisol ↑, adrenals enlarged
B. CRH ↓, ACTH ↓, endogenous cortisol ↓, bilateral adrenal ATROPHY → abrupt withdrawal → crisis
C. CRH normal, ACTH ↑, cortisol ↑
D. CRH ↑, ACTH ↓
E. All normal
B — Suppressed axis; bilateral adrenal atrophy.
Prednisone mimics cortisol → suppresses CRH and ACTH chronically → endogenous cortisol falls → both adrenals atrophy. Abrupt withdrawal → atrophied adrenals can't respond → adrenal crisis. Always TAPER steroids after >3 weeks of supraphysiologic dosing.
Q7. Hashimoto patient started on excessive levothyroxine. Labs 6 weeks later?
A. TRH ↑, TSH ↑, T4 ↑
B. TRH ↓, TSH SUPPRESSED, T4 ↑
C. TRH ↑, TSH ↓, T4 ↓
D. TRH normal, TSH normal, T4 ↑
E. TRH ↓, TSH ↑, T4 normal
B — TRH & TSH suppressed; T4 ↑.
Exogenous levothyroxine raises free T4 → negative feedback at hypothalamus (suppresses TRH) and pituitary (suppresses TSH). TSH is the most sensitive indicator of over-replacement. Functionally identical to “target hormone ↑.”
Q8. 26-year-old male bodybuilder on exogenous testosterone for 18 months. Predict HPG axis.
A. GnRH ↑, LH ↑, endogenous T ↑
B. GnRH suppressed, LH/FSH suppressed, endogenous T ↓, testes ATROPHIED, oligo/azoospermia
C. GnRH ↑, LH ↓
D. GnRH ↓, LH ↑
E. Normal
B — Suppressed axis with testicular atrophy.
Exogenous testosterone provides target-hormone feedback → GnRH, LH, FSH all suppressed. Without LH/FSH + intratesticular testosterone, spermatogenesis fails → testicular atrophy and azoospermia. Recovery takes 6–12+ months after stopping. Functionally identical to “sex steroid ↑.”
Q9. Prostate cancer patient on long-acting GnRH agonist (leuprolide) for 3 months. Predict HPG axis.
A. LH/FSH ↑, testosterone ↑
B. LH/FSH ↓, testosterone ↓ (desensitization → medical castration)
C. LH/FSH normal, T ↑
D. LH/FSH ↑, T ↓ (direct testicular toxicity)
E. No effect
B — Medical castration.
Pituitary gonadotropes need pulsatile GnRH (every 60–90 min). Continuous agonist initially causes a flare (cover with antiandrogen first 2–4 weeks), then GnRH receptors are downregulated/desensitized → LH/FSH suppressed → testosterone falls to castrate range.
Q10. 45-year-old woman: weight gain, cold intolerance, galactorrhea. TSH 42, T4 low, prolactin mildly elevated. Cause of hyperprolactinemia?
A. Prolactinoma
B. Pregnancy
C. Primary hypothyroidism → low T4 → TRH ↑ → TRH stimulates BOTH thyrotrophs (TSH ↑) and lactotrophs (PRL ↑)
D. Adrenal insufficiency
E. Renal failure
C — TRH-driven hyperprolactinemia.
In primary hypothyroidism, low T4 removes negative feedback → TRH ↑ → TRH stimulates BOTH TSH and prolactin (lactotrophs also have TRH receptors). Treating with levothyroxine normalizes both. Always check TSH BEFORE pursuing pituitary MRI in hyperprolactinemia workup.

Q1. A researcher isolates a novel hormone and finds it is water-soluble, stored in large secretory vesicles in the cell of origin, and elicits a biological response within seconds of injection. Its receptor is MOST likely located where, and the downstream mechanism MOST likely involves which of the following?
A. Nuclear receptor → direct transcription of hormone-response genes
B. Cell-surface receptor → second-messenger cascade (e.g., cAMP, IP3)
C. Cytoplasmic receptor bound to HSP90 → homodimerization → nuclear translocation
D. Receptor complex bound to RXR in the nucleus → ligand-induced corepressor release
E. Plasma carrier protein → hormone delivered to the liver for clearance
B — Cell-surface receptor with second-messenger cascade.
Water-soluble + stored in vesicles + seconds-to-minutes action = classic peptide hormone profile. Peptides cannot cross the plasma membrane, so they must signal through membrane receptors (GPCR, RTK, JAK-STAT, ligand-gated ion channel, or guanylyl cyclase). A/C/D describe steroid or thyroid (lipid-soluble) signaling; E describes transport, not signaling.
Q2. A 35-year-old woman is euthyroid clinically (normal TSH, no symptoms). Her total T4 is elevated above the reference range. Review of systems reveals she recently became pregnant. Which of the following BEST explains her elevated total T4?
A. Autonomous thyroid hormone production from a toxic adenoma
B. Estrogen-driven increase in thyroid-binding globulin (TBG) raises total T4 while free T4 remains normal
C. Primary hypothyroidism with secondary TBG elevation
D. Increased PNMT activity in the thyroid
E. Elevated thyroid-stimulating antibodies crossing the placenta
B — Estrogen raises TBG → total T4 ↑ but free T4 unchanged.
Estrogen (pregnancy, OCPs) increases hepatic TBG synthesis. Because only free hormone is biologically active (free-hormone hypothesis), free T4 and TSH stay in normal range and the patient is clinically euthyroid. Always order free T4 when binding-protein status is changing. Same principle: liver failure or nephrotic syndrome lowers TBG → total T4 ↓ with normal free T4.
Q3. A 28-year-old man presents with episodic headaches, palpitations, and severe hypertension. Plasma metanephrines are markedly elevated. An adrenal mass is identified. The tumor cells express high levels of phenylethanolamine N-methyltransferase (PNMT). Which of the following BEST explains why PNMT is uniquely active in the adrenal medulla (compared with sympathetic post-ganglionic neurons)?
A. The adrenal medulla lacks tyrosine hydroxylase, so it bypasses NE synthesis
B. Only the medulla has dopamine β-hydroxylase
C. PNMT is induced by cortisol that drains from the adrenal cortex via a local portal system
D. The medulla uses serotonin instead of dopamine as a precursor
E. PNMT requires iodine, which is only concentrated in the medulla
C — Cortisol from the adrenal cortex induces PNMT.
The adrenal cortex and medulla share a portal venous system; high local cortisol induces PNMT (NE → Epi). Sympathetic ganglia lack this cortisol exposure, so they secrete NE, not Epi. Pathway: Tyrosine → (TH, rate-limiting) → DOPA → DA → (DBH) → NE → (PNMT) → Epi. Clinical: pheochromocytoma → elevated metanephrines (NE/Epi metabolites).
Q4. A 68-year-old woman with chronic kidney disease has low serum calcium, high PTH, and low 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D. Her 25-hydroxyvitamin D level is normal. Which enzymatic step is MOST likely impaired?
A. Skin conversion of 7-dehydrocholesterol to cholecalciferol
B. Hepatic 25-hydroxylase activity
C. Renal 1α-hydroxylase activity
D. Intestinal absorption of vitamin D
E. 24-hydroxylase activity in the kidney
C — Renal 1α-hydroxylase is impaired in CKD.
Activation cascade: skin (7-DHC + UVB → cholecalciferol) → liver (25-hydroxylase → 25-OH D, the storage form) → kidney (1α-hydroxylase → 1,25-(OH)2D, the ACTIVE form). Normal 25-OH D + low 1,25-(OH)2D localizes the defect to the kidney. 1α-hydroxylase is up-regulated by PTH & low phosphate; this activation is lost with renal failure.
Q5. A 42-year-old woman with acute promyelocytic leukemia (APL, t(15;17) PML-RARα) is started on all-trans retinoic acid (ATRA). Which BEST describes ATRA's mechanism of action at the molecular level?
A. ATRA binds a cell-surface GPCR and generates cAMP
B. ATRA binds retinoic acid receptor (RAR), which heterodimerizes with RXR on retinoic acid response elements (RAREs), releasing corepressors and allowing differentiation
C. ATRA competitively inhibits androgen receptor binding
D. ATRA is a covalent inhibitor of DNA methyltransferase
E. ATRA induces proteasomal degradation of tyrosine kinase receptors
B — RAR/RXR heterodimer on RAREs drives differentiation.
Retinoic acids signal via nuclear receptors: RAR and RXR. In APL, the PML-RARα fusion traps the complex in a repressive state. Pharmacologic ATRA overwhelms the block, corepressors leave, coactivators are recruited, and promyelocytes differentiate into neutrophils. ATRA is the prototype "differentiation therapy." RXR heterodimerizes with RAR, VDR, TR, PPAR, LXR, FXR.
Q6. A biochemist measures cortisol binding to the glucocorticoid receptor (GR) across a range of hormone concentrations. A Scatchard plot of the data yields a straight line with negative slope. If the slope is −0.1 nM−1 and the x-intercept is 500 pmol/mg protein, which BEST describes Kd and Bmax?
A. Kd = 0.1 nM; Bmax = 500 pmol/mg
B. Kd = 10 nM; Bmax = 500 pmol/mg
C. Kd = 0.1 nM; Bmax = 250 pmol/mg
D. Kd = 500 nM; Bmax = 0.1 pmol/mg
E. Cannot be calculated from a Scatchard plot
B — Kd = 10 nM; Bmax = 500 pmol/mg.
On a Scatchard plot (y = bound/free, x = bound): slope = −1/Kd and x-intercept = Bmax. Slope −0.1 nM−1 → Kd = 1 / 0.1 = 10 nM. X-intercept = 500 pmol/mg = Bmax. Kd is the ligand concentration at which 50% of receptors are occupied — a lower Kd means higher affinity.
Q7. A 23-year-old woman has diabetes mellitus. Her physician wants to distinguish Type 1 from Type 2 and evaluate residual pancreatic β-cell function. Which single laboratory test is MOST informative?
A. Serum insulin level
B. Hemoglobin A1c
C. C-peptide level
D. Fasting glucagon
E. Islet cell autoantibodies alone
C — C-peptide is the best marker of endogenous insulin secretion.
Proinsulin is cleaved in the β-cell into insulin + C-peptide in a 1:1 ratio. Exogenous insulin contains NO C-peptide. Thus: low/absent C-peptide = no endogenous production (Type 1, or factitious insulin use); elevated C-peptide = Type 2 or insulinoma. Insulin levels alone (A) cannot distinguish endogenous from injected.
Q8. A researcher applies GnRH to cultured pituitary gonadotropes in two conditions: (1) brief pulsatile exposure every 60 minutes, and (2) continuous infusion for 24 hours. Which of the following best describes LH/FSH secretion in each condition?
A. Pulsatile → sustained LH/FSH release; continuous → sustained LH/FSH release
B. Pulsatile → LH/FSH release maintained; continuous → receptor downregulation and suppressed LH/FSH (clinical basis of GnRH agonist therapy)
C. Pulsatile → suppression; continuous → enhancement
D. Both conditions identical — receptor density is the only determinant
E. Neither condition produces measurable LH/FSH release
B — Pulsatile maintains LH/FSH; continuous causes desensitization.
Pituitary gonadotropes require pulsatile GnRH (every ~60–90 min) to maintain normal LH/FSH secretion. Continuous GnRH (or long-acting GnRH agonists like leuprolide) desensitizes and downregulates the receptor → LH/FSH drops → "medical castration." This paradox is exploited clinically to treat prostate cancer, precocious puberty, endometriosis, and for IVF protocols.

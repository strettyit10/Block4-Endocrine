LO 1
ON EXAM — 5 signaling modes: endocrine (blood, systemic), paracrine (local diffusion), autocrine (self), JUXTACRINE (cell-cell contact via membrane proteins or gap junctions), nervous (synapse); plus neuroendocrine hybrid. Key discriminators: distance, speed, specificity, half-life. Exocrine (external surfaces) is NOT an intercellular hormone mode. Know classic examples and traps (paracrine vs autocrine; IGF-1 autocrine in cancer; GAP junctions in myocardium; Notch-Delta in development)

LO 4
ON EXAM — INITIAL STEP of steroidogenesis is the rate-limiting one. TWO sub-steps: (1) STAR SHUTTLES cholesterol from outer to INNER mitochondrial membrane (this is the acute regulated step — induced by ACTH via cAMP/PKA), (2) CYP11A1 (P450scc / "cholesterol side-chain cleavage enzyme" / DESMOLASE) on the inner mitochondrial membrane cleaves cholesterol → PREGNENOLONE. Cofactors: O2, NADPH via adrenodoxin/adrenodoxin reductase. StAR mutations → congenital lipoid adrenal hyperplasia. CYP11A1 deficiency → failure of ALL adrenal + gonadal steroidogenesis

ON EXAM — Carrier proteins (SHBG, CBG, TBG, albumin) buffer free hormone, extend half-life, but only FREE hormone is active; pregnancy/OCPs raise TBG → total T4 ↑, free T4 normal. 9 questions — weighted toward expanded LOs (signaling, catecholamines, steroid initial step).
Q1. A pancreatic islet δ-cell releases somatostatin, which binds receptors on nearby α- and β-cells within the same islet, inhibiting their secretion. Somatostatin released in this fashion is NOT found in appreciable concentrations in the systemic circulation. Which form of intercellular communication is this?
A. Endocrine
B. Paracrine
C. Autocrine
D. Juxtacrine
E. Neuroendocrine
B — Paracrine.
Local diffusion to adjacent cells of the same tissue, without entering systemic blood, defines paracrine. Contrast with: endocrine (blood, systemic); autocrine (same cell); juxtacrine (membrane-membrane contact); neuroendocrine (neuron releases hormone into blood). The islet has all of these: somatostatin→α/β (paracrine), insulin→distant muscle (endocrine), IGF-1→self on tumor cells (autocrine).
Q2. A newly identified signaling molecule binds a transmembrane ligand on an adjacent cell without any secretion into extracellular fluid. Binding triggers intracellular signaling in BOTH cells simultaneously. This is an example of which mode of intercellular communication?
A. Endocrine
B. Paracrine
C. Autocrine
D. Juxtacrine (contact-dependent)
E. Exocrine
D — Juxtacrine.
Juxtacrine = contact-dependent signaling via membrane-anchored ligands (e.g., Notch — Delta/Jagged), or via gap junctions (connexons). Examples: Notch–Delta in developmental fate decisions; immunological synapse (MHC–TCR); cardiac myocyte gap junctions (electrical coupling). No secreted intermediate — distinguishes it from paracrine.
Q3. A 19-year-old with Parkinson-like early-childhood dystonia has markedly low CSF dopamine, undetectable homovanillic acid, and normal phenylalanine. Brain MRI is normal. A trial of L-DOPA + carbidopa dramatically improves symptoms. Which enzyme is MOST likely deficient?
A. Phenylalanine hydroxylase (PAH)
B. Tyrosine hydroxylase (TH)
C. Aromatic L-amino acid decarboxylase (AADC)
D. Dopamine β-hydroxylase (DBH)
E. Phenylethanolamine N-methyltransferase (PNMT)
B — Tyrosine hydroxylase (TH) deficiency.
TH (Tyr → L-DOPA) is the rate-limiting catecholamine enzyme. Deficiency → low dopamine, low NE, low Epi, and low CSF HVA (dopamine metabolite). Phenylalanine is normal (PAH intact). L-DOPA BYPASSES the TH step, and carbidopa prevents peripheral AADC conversion so more L-DOPA reaches the brain. AADC deficiency would fail to improve with L-DOPA (A is not the answer because Phe is normal; PAH deficiency = PKU with high Phe).
Q4. A 28-year-old man presents with chronic orthostatic hypotension, ptosis, and anhydrosis of the upper body. Plasma norepinephrine is very low; dopamine is HIGH; DOPA is normal. The patient dramatically improves on droxidopa (a synthetic NE precursor). Which enzyme is deficient?
A. Tyrosine hydroxylase
B. AADC
C. Dopamine β-hydroxylase (DBH)
D. PNMT
E. COMT
C — Dopamine β-hydroxylase (DBH) deficiency.
DBH converts DA → NE inside the vesicle; loss → HIGH dopamine (spills over into synapses) with LOW NE and Epi. Classic: severe orthostatic hypotension, ptosis, anhidrosis, sexual dysfunction. Treatment = droxidopa (L-threo-DOPS), which bypasses DBH because AADC can decarboxylate it directly to NE in the peripheral nervous system.
Q5. After a patient is placed on long-term high-dose prednisone, PNMT expression in the adrenal medulla DECREASES, and epinephrine/NE ratio falls. Which BEST explains the normal relationship between cortisol and PNMT?
A. PNMT is a Gs-coupled receptor that is internalized by cortisol
B. PNMT is normally INDUCED by very high local cortisol reaching the medulla via the adrenal cortico-medullary portal system; exogenous steroids suppress pituitary ACTH, lowering endogenous cortisol in this portal → PNMT decreases
C. PNMT is a cortisol-binding protein that titrates free cortisol
D. PNMT uses cortisol as a cofactor
E. PNMT and cortisol are unrelated
B — Cortical cortisol INDUCES medullary PNMT via a local portal system.
The adrenal has a cortico-medullary portal venous system that bathes chromaffin cells in very high local cortisol → cortisol induces PNMT transcription → NE is methylated to Epi (the medulla's unique ability). Exogenous glucocorticoids suppress pituitary ACTH → endogenous cortical cortisol falls → the medulla's local cortisol exposure falls → PNMT decreases. This is also why sympathetic ganglia (no cortisol exposure) produce NE, not Epi.
Q6. A neonate presents with ambiguous genitalia, salt-wasting crisis, and bilaterally enlarged adrenal glands on imaging. ALL steroid hormones (cortisol, aldosterone, androgens, 17-OH progesterone) are UNDETECTABLE. Cholesteryl esters accumulate massively in the adrenal cortex. Which enzyme or protein is MOST likely defective?
A. 21-hydroxylase (CYP21A2)
B. 11β-hydroxylase (CYP11B1)
C. 17α-hydroxylase (CYP17A1)
D. Steroidogenic Acute Regulatory protein (StAR)
E. Aldosterone synthase (CYP11B2)
D — StAR mutation (congenital lipoid adrenal hyperplasia).
If cholesterol can't be shuttled from the outer to inner mitochondrial membrane, CYP11A1 has no substrate → pregnenolone cannot form → NO steroids of any class are made. Cholesterol accumulates in lipid droplets (massive adrenal enlargement). Phenotype: salt-wasting adrenal crisis and 46,XY neonates under-virilize (looking female) because testosterone requires pregnenolone too. This confirms StAR is the regulated, rate-limiting step of steroidogenesis.
Q7. An investigator studies the initial step of steroid biosynthesis. In vitro, adrenal mitochondria are provided with excess cholesterol, adrenodoxin, adrenodoxin reductase, NADPH, and O2. Which enzyme DIRECTLY converts cholesterol to pregnenolone, and where in the mitochondrion does it act?
A. CYP21A2 (21-hydroxylase) — smooth ER
B. CYP17A1 (17α-hydroxylase) — smooth ER
C. CYP11A1 (P450scc / desmolase) — inner mitochondrial membrane
D. 3β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase (3β-HSD) — smooth ER
E. HMG-CoA reductase — ER
C — CYP11A1 on the inner mitochondrial membrane.
CYP11A1 (= P450scc = “cholesterol side-chain cleavage enzyme” = “desmolase”) sits on the INNER mitochondrial membrane and cleaves the cholesterol side chain to yield pregnenolone. Cofactors required: NADPH, O2, and the adrenodoxin / adrenodoxin reductase electron shuttle. Access to substrate depends on StAR, which delivers cholesterol across the outer membrane into the inner membrane.
Q8. A 35-year-old woman on oral contraceptives has an elevated total T4 but normal TSH and free T4, and is clinically euthyroid. The MOST LIKELY mechanism is:
A. Autonomous thyroid adenoma
B. Estrogen-driven increase in TBG synthesis raises total T4 while free T4 remains normal
C. Elevated thyroid-stimulating antibody
D. Subclinical hyperthyroidism
E. Hypoalbuminemia
B — Estrogen raises TBG → total T4 ↑ but FREE T4 unchanged.
Carrier proteins buffer total hormone levels but only FREE hormone is active (free-hormone hypothesis). Estrogen (pregnancy, OCPs) increases hepatic TBG → more bound T4 → total T4 rises, but the pituitary senses normal free T4 → TSH normal → patient is euthyroid. Always order free T4 when carrier-protein status is changing.
Q9. A researcher compares two tissues: in tissue A, the biologic response reaches maximum when only 10% of receptors are occupied; in tissue B, the response plateaus only when >95% of receptors are occupied. Which BEST describes tissue A?
A. Tissue A has a HIGHER Kd than tissue B
B. Tissue A has spare receptors → EC50 < Kd
C. Tissue A has no receptors
D. Tissue A is undergoing tachyphylaxis
E. Tissue A is signaling through a competitive antagonist
B — Tissue A has spare receptors (EC50 < Kd).
Spare receptors means full biological response requires only partial receptor occupancy — EC50 (concentration giving half-maximal EFFECT) < Kd (concentration giving half-maximal BINDING). Amplification in downstream signaling (e.g., second-messenger cascades, enzyme amplification) drives full response at low occupancy. Classic examples: insulin on adipocytes; β-adrenergic receptors in heart. Tissues with no spare receptors (like skeletal muscle nicotinic) show EC50 = Kd.

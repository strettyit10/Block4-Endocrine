GH receptor mechanics (LO 1)
CYTOKINE-FAMILY receptor — single-pass transmembrane, no intrinsic kinase (NOT GPCR or RTK)
DIMERIZATION (two receptors per ligand) → associated JAK2 molecules trans-phosphorylate each other
JAK2 (Janus kinase 2)
STAT5 — phosphorylated → dimer → nucleus → drives IGF-1 transcription
Prolactin (also JAK2-STAT5). Others in the family: erythropoietin, leptin, IL-2, IFNs
Suppressors of cytokine signaling (SOCS-1, -3) are induced by STAT5 and bind phosphorylated JAK2/GH-R → feedback inhibition. Chronic GH → SOCS up → desensitization
IGF-1 biology (LO 1)
LIVER (hepatocytes) — >80% of circulating IGF-1; minor local production in chondrocytes, muscle, kidney (paracrine)
IGF-1R — Receptor Tyrosine Kinase (RTK), similar to insulin receptor (cross-talk at high [IGF-1])
IGFBP-3 (carries >90%) in a ternary complex with ALS (acid-labile subunit)
IGFBP-3 synthesis is GH-driven. Both IGF-1 and IGFBP-3 rise/fall with GH — measuring both improves axis assessment
Important in FETAL growth + placenta. Imprinting defects → Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome (overgrowth, macroglossia, Wilms risk). Minor postnatal role
GH: ~20 min, pulsatile (unreliable single measurement). IGF-1: ~15 h on IGFBP-3, stable (preferred screen)
KEY: GH has SHORT, pulsatile half-life (~20 min) and varies through the day. IGF-1 is stable (long half-life, carried on IGFBP-3) and reflects integrated GH exposure — preferred screening test for acromegaly/GHD.
LO 2

Metabolic signature of GH (LO 2)
Raises glucose (DIABETOGENIC): ↓ peripheral uptake + ↑ hepatic gluconeogenesis + insulin resistance
Raises FFA (lipolytic) — activates hormone-sensitive lipase in adipose
Directly opposes insulin: ↓ GLUT4 translocation, activates gluconeogenic enzymes, induces insulin resistance via SOCS + lipotoxicity. Chronic excess → glucose intolerance/diabetes in acromegaly
STRONGLY STIMULATES GH (counter-regulation). Basis of ITT — gold standard for GH DEFICIENCY
GH + CORTISOL + GLUCAGON + EPINEPHRINE — all oppose insulin; raise glucose during fasting/stress
Chronic GH → ↑ FFAs (lipotoxicity) inhibit muscle insulin signaling; ↑ SOCS → blocks insulin receptor signaling; direct ↓ GLUT4
Growth plate and linear growth (LO 2)
Chondrocytes — proliferate and hypertrophy → cartilage matrix → endochondral ossification → linear bone growth
Females ~14–16; males ~16–18. Signal to close: ESTROGEN in BOTH sexes (aromatized from testosterone in males). Estrogen-deficient states → tall stature with unfused plates
Pre (open plates) + GH excess → GIGANTISM (linear growth). Post (fused plates) → ACROMEGALY (appositional/width growth in hands, feet, jaw, soft tissues)
IGF-1 stimulates AA uptake via SLC-family transporters + ribosomal biogenesis → protein synthesis in muscle + chondrocytes
LO 3
ON EXAM — GH pulsatile, NIGHT-dominant (largest pulse in N3 SWS, ~1 h after sleep onset); lifetime: neonatal ↑ → childhood moderate → PUBERTY PEAK → adult decline → somatopause (~50%/decade); IGF-1 ↑ → somatostatin ↑; GH short-loop → GHRH ↓
24-hour GH rhythm (LO 3)
Pulsatile (mostly night-dominant) — ~4–8 pulses/24 h; largest during sleep
First few hours of night sleep, ~1 h after sleep onset
Slow-wave sleep (SWS) = N3 (non-REM deep). Sleep deprivation markedly blunts GH
Useless — GH is pulsatile with ~20 min half-life; near zero between pulses even in health
Serum IGF-1 — stable long half-life, integrates 24-hour GH exposure
OGTT with GH measurement: healthy GH suppresses to <1 ng/mL; acromegaly fails to suppress (may paradoxically rise)

Diagnosis of GH excess (LO 5)
Serum IGF-1 (elevated; reflects chronic GH excess)

MRI pituitary after biochemical confirmation — localize somatotroph adenoma

Treatment ladder for acromegaly (LO 5)
Transsphenoidal pituitary surgery — cures ~80% of microadenomas, ~50% of macroadenomas
Octreotide / lanreotide — SSTR2/5 agonists → ↓ GH release + tumor shrinkage
Pegvisomant — PEGylated mutant GH blocks receptor dimerization → no JAK-STAT → hepatic IGF-1 falls. Monitor IGF-1 (not GH)
Cabergoline — paradoxical GH suppression in a minority (esp. mixed GH-prolactin adenomas or high D2 expression)
Stereotactic (gamma knife) or conventional — reserved for refractory disease. 5–10 years for full effect; risks hypopituitarism

LO 1–5 · USMLE Step 1 Style
8 questions — answers pre-revealed. Covers all LOs.
Q1. A 48-year-old man with enlarging hands, prognathism, macroglossia, HTN, and glucose intolerance has markedly elevated fasting IGF-1. Best CONFIRMATORY test?
A. Random GH
B. GH after OGTT
C. Insulin tolerance test
D. Arginine-GHRH stim
E. Random cortisol
B — Failure to suppress GH on OGTT.
Acromegaly. Screen with IGF-1. Confirm with OGTT: normally glucose load suppresses GH to <1 ng/mL; in acromegaly the autonomous adenoma fails to suppress (may paradoxically rise). Random GH is uninformative (pulsatile). ITT and arginine-GHRH are for GH DEFICIENCY.
Q2. Primary intracellular signaling of the GH receptor?
A. Gs → cAMP → PKA
B. Gq → IP3/DAG
C. RTK → Ras/MAPK
D. Cytokine-family receptor → JAK2 → STAT5 → transcription
E. Steroid receptor → HSP90 dissociation
D — JAK2/STAT5.
GH-R is single-pass cytokine-family receptor; ligand-induced dimerization → trans-phosphorylation by associated JAK2 → phosphorylates STAT5 → STAT5 dimer → nucleus → IGF-1 transcription. Prolactin uses the same path. SOCS proteins provide negative feedback.
Q3. A 12-year-old boy with a somatotroph adenoma has accelerated linear growth (95th percentile). Why gigantism rather than acromegaly?
A. Tumor secretes IGF-2 instead of IGF-1
B. Epiphyseal plates not yet fused — IGF-1 drives longitudinal growth
C. GH receptors hyperactive
D. Concurrent precocious puberty
E. Prolactin co-secretion accelerates growth
B — Open epiphyseal plates.
Pre-puberty, plates remain open; IGF-1 stimulates chondrocyte proliferation → linear growth (gigantism). After plates fuse (estrogen-driven), bone grows only APPOSITIONALLY → enlarged hands, feet, jaw, soft tissues (acromegaly).
Q4. GH secretion during sleep?
A. Continuous through all sleep stages
B. Lowest during sleep
C. Largest pulse during REM
D. Largest pulse during slow-wave (N3) sleep, ~1 h after onset
E. Suppressed during sleep, peaks afternoon
D — Largest pulse in slow-wave (N3) sleep.
GH is pulsatile, night-dominant; largest pulse follows sleep onset during deep N3 (slow-wave) sleep. Sleep deprivation blunts GH. This is why a single random GH is uninformative and IGF-1 is preferred for screening.
Q5. 6-year-old: severe short stature, delayed bone age, chubby cherubic appearance. GH HIGH, IGF-1 LOW, no response to exogenous GH. Diagnosis?
A. GHRH receptor mutation
B. Craniopharyngioma
C. GH receptor mutation (Laron)
D. Congenital hypothyroidism
E. Constitutional growth delay
C — Laron syndrome (GH-R mutation).
GH normal/HIGH (feedback lost), IGF-1 LOW (no GH-R signaling). Short stature with normal proportions, chubby, delayed bone age. Exogenous GH useless. Tx: recombinant IGF-1 (mecasermin). Laron patients paradoxically have low rates of cancer and diabetes (blunted GH/IGF-1 signaling).
Q6. 54-year-old with persistent acromegaly 3 months post-op, not controlled on octreotide. Pegvisomant mechanism?
A. Somatostatin agonist
B. D2 agonist
C. GH receptor ANTAGONIST — blocks dimerization and JAK-STAT
D. GHRH antagonist
E. Recombinant IGF-1
C — GH receptor antagonist.
Pegvisomant is a PEGylated mutated GH analog that binds the receptor but blocks functional dimerization → no JAK2-STAT5 → hepatic IGF-1 falls. Follow IGF-1 (GH paradoxically rises as feedback is lost). Used when surgery + octreotide fail.
Q7. 28-year-old woman post-op/radiation for pituitary macroadenoma, now fatigue + central adiposity. Suspected GH deficiency. Best test?
A. Random GH
B. 24-h urinary GH
C. Insulin tolerance test
D. OGTT GH suppression
E. CT pituitary
C — Insulin tolerance test (ITT).
Gold standard for GHD: induced hypoglycemia (<40 mg/dL) normally triggers GH surge (>5 ng/mL). Failure = GHD. Alternative (safer for cardiac/seizure patients): GHRH + arginine. OGTT is for GH EXCESS. IGF-1 alone can be normal in up to 40% of adult GHD.
Q8. Direct metabolic effects of GH on peripheral tissues?
A. Promotes glucose uptake in muscle + hepatic glycogenesis
B. Inhibits lipolysis + promotes TG storage
C. ↑ lipolysis, ↓ muscle glucose uptake, ↑ hepatic gluconeogenesis → ↑ glucose and FFA
D. Stimulates β-cell insulin secretion
E. Cross-reacts with insulin receptor
C — Lipolytic, anti-insulin, gluconeogenic.
GH's direct effects oppose insulin: ↑ lipolysis in adipose (↑ FFA), ↓ glucose uptake in muscle/fat, ↑ hepatic gluconeogenesis → net hyperglycemia + hyperlipidemia. Chronic excess → glucose intolerance/diabetes in acromegaly. The indirect, growth-promoting effects (via IGF-1) are anabolic, insulin-like. "GH keeps glucose HIGH; IGF-1 grows the TISSUES."

LO 1
ON EXAM — 5 modes: endocrine (blood), paracrine (local), autocrine (self), JUXTACRINE (contact), nervous (synapse); plus neuroendocrine hybrid. Discriminators: distance, speed, specificity, duration. Exocrine (ducts to surfaces) is NOT a hormonal intercellular mode. IGF-1 autocrine in cancer; gap junctions in myocardium

PNMT uniqueness to adrenal medulla (LO 3)
Cortico-medullary portal venous system — cortical cortisol drains through the medulla, bathing chromaffin cells in cortisol ~100× systemic levels
Cortisol is a potent inducer of PNMT gene transcription → uniquely present in adrenal medulla
Post-ganglionic sympathetic neurons have no cortisol exposure → no PNMT → NE is the end product, not Epi
Adrenalectomy, hypopituitarism, or chronic exogenous steroids (suppress pituitary ACTH) → cortex loses cortisol output → medullary PNMT falls → Epi/NE ratio decreases

LO 4
ON EXAM — INITIAL STEP is the rate-limiting one. TWO sub-steps: (1) StAR shuttles cholesterol OMM → IMM (acute regulated step, ACTH-induced via cAMP/PKA); (2) CYP11A1 (P450scc/desmolase) on IMM cleaves cholesterol → PREGNENOLONE. Cofactors: O2 + NADPH via adrenodoxin/adrenodoxin reductase. StAR mutation → congenital lipoid adrenal hyperplasia. CYP11A1 deficiency → failure of ALL steroidogenesis

Why pregnenolone matters (LO 4)
Cortisol (glucocorticoid), aldosterone (mineralocorticoid), testosterone/DHEA (androgens), estradiol (via aromatase), progesterone (progestagens). Vitamin D shares cholesterol but splits off earlier
StAR induction + cholesterol ester hydrolysis → rapid cholesterol flux → CYP11A1 → pregnenolone. Cortisol rises within 15–30 min
Transcriptional upregulation of ALL steroidogenic enzymes (StAR, CYP11A1, CYP17, CYP21, CYP11B1) → steroidogenic capacity increases. Days–weeks → bilateral adrenal hyperplasia
LH binds LHCGR (Gs GPCR) on theca/Leydig/corpus luteum → cAMP/PKA → same StAR + CYP11A1 mechanism as ACTH in adrenal
Clinical correlates (LO 4)
StAR loss → cholesterol ester accumulation in cortex (massive bilateral adrenal enlargement); complete adrenal failure with salt-wasting crisis in infancy; 46,XY have female external phenotype (no fetal testicular testosterone)
Blocks CYP11A1 (+ CYP17, CYP21, aromatase) → steroidogenesis inhibitor. Off-label in Cushing syndrome to lower cortisol. Side effects: hypogonadism, gynecomastia, adrenal insufficiency
Because it blocks many CYPs: hypogonadism (low testosterone from testicular CYPs), gynecomastia (shift to estrogen), adrenal insufficiency (low cortisol)
StAR is the rate-limiting SHUTTLE — cholesterol supply is NOT the bottleneck. Rate is controlled by ACTH/PKA/StAR, not by cholesterol concentration. Hence hyperlipidemia does not cause hypercortisolism

Cellular sensitivity (LO 7)
Upregulation: chronic low ligand (β-receptor ↑ with prolonged β-blocker → rebound risk). Downregulation: chronic high ligand (insulin receptor internalized with hyperinsulinemia → insulin resistance)
LOW Kd = HIGH affinity (less ligand needed for half-occupancy). HIGH Kd = LOW affinity
Receptor reserve — full response at <100% occupancy — EC50 < Kd. Classic: insulin on adipocytes; β-adrenergic in heart
(1) Receptor phosphorylation (GRKs) → β-arrestin → uncoupling. (2) Receptor internalization (endocytosis). (3) Transcriptional downregulation. (4) Feedback proteins (SOCS, etc.)
Rapid loss of response with repeated dosing over min–hr (independent of downregulation). Example: oxymetazoline nasal decongestant — rapid β2 desensitization
Altered 2nd messenger generation, kinase/phosphatase balance, transcription factor availability, effector abundance — same occupancy, different response in different cell states
LO 1–7 · USMLE Step 1 Style
9 questions — answers pre-revealed. Weighted toward expanded LOs (signaling, catecholamines, steroid initial step).
Q1. A pancreatic islet δ-cell releases somatostatin, which binds receptors on nearby α- and β-cells within the same islet, inhibiting their secretion. Somatostatin is NOT appreciable in systemic circulation. Which mode?
A. Endocrine
B. Paracrine
C. Autocrine
D. Juxtacrine
E. Neuroendocrine
B — Paracrine.
Local diffusion to adjacent cells of the same tissue without entering systemic blood. Contrast: endocrine (blood/systemic), autocrine (same cell), juxtacrine (membrane-membrane contact), neuroendocrine (neuron releases into blood).
Q2. A newly identified signaling molecule binds a transmembrane ligand on an adjacent cell without any secretion. Binding triggers signaling in BOTH cells simultaneously. Which mode?
A. Endocrine
B. Paracrine
C. Autocrine
D. Juxtacrine (contact-dependent)
E. Exocrine
D — Juxtacrine.
Contact-dependent signaling via membrane-anchored ligands (Notch-Delta) or gap junctions (connexons). Examples: Notch-Delta in development; immunological synapse; cardiac myocyte gap junctions (electrical coupling). No secreted intermediate.
Q3. A 19-year-old with Parkinson-like early-childhood dystonia has low CSF dopamine, undetectable HVA, and NORMAL phenylalanine. L-DOPA + carbidopa dramatically improves symptoms. Which enzyme is deficient?
A. Phenylalanine hydroxylase
B. Tyrosine hydroxylase
C. AADC
D. DBH
E. PNMT
B — Tyrosine hydroxylase (TH) deficiency.
TH is the rate-limiting enzyme (Tyr → L-DOPA). Deficiency → low DA, low NE, low Epi, low HVA. Phe normal (PAH intact). L-DOPA BYPASSES TH; carbidopa prevents peripheral AADC conversion so more L-DOPA reaches brain. AADC deficiency would NOT respond to L-DOPA.
Q4. A 28-year-old man: chronic orthostatic hypotension, ptosis, anhidrosis. Plasma NE very low; dopamine HIGH; DOPA normal. Improves on droxidopa. Which enzyme is deficient?
A. TH
B. AADC
C. DBH
D. PNMT
E. COMT
C — Dopamine β-hydroxylase (DBH) deficiency.
DBH converts DA → NE INSIDE the vesicle; loss → HIGH dopamine (spills over), LOW NE/Epi. Classic: severe orthostatic hypotension, ptosis, anhidrosis, sexual dysfunction. Tx: droxidopa (L-threo-DOPS) — AADC converts it to NE, bypassing DBH.
Q5. Long-term high-dose prednisone decreases PNMT expression in the adrenal medulla. Which BEST explains the normal relationship between cortisol and PNMT?
A. PNMT is a Gs-coupled receptor internalized by cortisol
B. PNMT is INDUCED by very high local cortisol reaching the medulla via the adrenal cortico-medullary portal system; exogenous steroids suppress pituitary ACTH → cortical cortisol falls → PNMT ↓
C. PNMT is a cortisol-binding protein
D. PNMT uses cortisol as a cofactor
E. PNMT and cortisol are unrelated
B — Cortical cortisol INDUCES medullary PNMT via local portal.
Adrenal has cortico-medullary portal venous system → chromaffin cells see cortisol ~100× systemic levels → cortisol induces PNMT transcription → NE → Epi. Exogenous steroids suppress ACTH → cortical cortisol falls → PNMT ↓. Explains why sympathetic ganglia (no cortisol exposure) make NE but not Epi.
Q6. A neonate: ambiguous genitalia, salt-wasting, bilaterally enlarged adrenals on imaging. ALL steroid hormones are UNDETECTABLE. Cholesteryl esters accumulate in adrenal cortex. Which enzyme/protein is defective?
A. 21-hydroxylase
B. 11β-hydroxylase
C. 17α-hydroxylase
D. Steroidogenic Acute Regulatory protein (StAR)
E. Aldosterone synthase
D — StAR mutation (congenital lipoid adrenal hyperplasia).
If cholesterol can't be shuttled OMM → IMM, CYP11A1 has no substrate → NO pregnenolone → NO steroids of any class. Cholesterol accumulates in lipid droplets (massive adrenal enlargement). Salt-wasting crisis; 46,XY under-virilize (no testicular testosterone). Confirms StAR as regulated rate-limiting step.
Q7. In vitro, adrenal mitochondria given excess cholesterol + adrenodoxin + adrenodoxin reductase + NADPH + O2. Which enzyme DIRECTLY converts cholesterol to pregnenolone, and where?
A. CYP21A2 — smooth ER
B. CYP17A1 — smooth ER
C. CYP11A1 (P450scc / desmolase) — inner mitochondrial membrane
D. 3β-HSD — smooth ER
E. HMG-CoA reductase — ER
C — CYP11A1 on the INNER mitochondrial membrane.
CYP11A1 (= P450scc = “cholesterol side-chain cleavage enzyme” = “desmolase”) sits on the IMM and cleaves the cholesterol side chain to yield pregnenolone. Cofactors: NADPH, O2, and adrenodoxin/adrenodoxin reductase electron shuttle. Substrate access depends on StAR delivery.
Q8. A 35-year-old woman on OCPs has elevated total T4 but normal TSH and free T4, and is clinically euthyroid. Best explanation?
A. Thyroid adenoma
B. Estrogen-driven increase in TBG raises total T4; free T4 normal
C. Thyroid-stimulating antibody
D. Subclinical hyperthyroidism
E. Hypoalbuminemia
B — Estrogen raises TBG → total T4 ↑ but free unchanged.
Free-hormone hypothesis: only free hormone is active. Estrogen (pregnancy, OCPs) increases hepatic TBG → more bound T4 → total ↑, but pituitary sees normal free T4 → TSH normal → euthyroid. Order free T4 whenever binding-protein status is changing.
Q9. Tissue A reaches maximum response at 10% receptor occupancy; tissue B only at >95%. Which BEST describes tissue A?
A. Higher Kd than B
B. Spare receptors → EC50 < Kd
C. No receptors
D. Tachyphylaxis
E. Competitive antagonist
B — Spare receptors (EC50 < Kd).
Spare receptors = full biological response at partial receptor occupancy → EC50 (half-max EFFECT) < Kd (half-max BINDING). Downstream signaling amplification drives full response at low occupancy. Classic: insulin on adipocytes; β-adrenergic in heart. Tissues without spare receptors show EC50 = Kd.

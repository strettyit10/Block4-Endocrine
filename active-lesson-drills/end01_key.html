Define a receptor (LO 11)
(1) Specific, high-affinity, saturable ligand binding. (2) Coupling of binding to a biological response (signal transduction)
Binding proteins (TBG, CBG, SHBG, albumin) carry hormone in blood but produce NO cellular response. Only receptors trigger signal transduction

Scatchard plot & Kd (LO 15)
x-axis: [Bound hormone]; y-axis: [Bound] / [Free]
Slope = −1 / Kd (steeper negative = lower Kd = higher affinity)
Bmax (maximum binding; total receptor density)
Dissociation constant = ligand concentration at which 50% of receptors are occupied. Inverse of affinity
LOW Kd = HIGH affinity (needs less ligand for half-occupancy). HIGH Kd = LOW affinity
Sigmoidal (S-shaped) occupancy curve. At [L] = Kd, exactly 50% of receptors are occupied
Spare receptors & dose-response (LO 15)
Tissues have more receptors than needed for max response; full biologic effect can occur at <100% occupancy. Safety margin & amplification
With spare receptors: EC50 < Kd (half-max effect achieved before half receptors occupied). No spare: EC50 = Kd
Shifts dose-response curve RIGHT (↑ EC50); max response UNCHANGED. Surmountable by more agonist
DECREASES max response (↓ Vmax/Emax); EC50 often unchanged. NOT surmountable (binds allosteric site or irreversibly)
LO 1–15 · USMLE Step 1 Style
8 questions — answers pre-revealed. Covers all LO groups.
Q1. A researcher isolates a novel hormone and finds it is water-soluble, stored in large secretory vesicles, and elicits a response within seconds. Its receptor is MOST likely located where, and the mechanism MOST likely involves?
A. Nuclear receptor → direct transcription
B. Cell-surface receptor → second-messenger cascade (cAMP, IP3)
C. Cytoplasmic receptor bound to HSP90 → homodimer → nucleus
D. Receptor bound to RXR in the nucleus → ligand-induced corepressor release
E. Plasma carrier protein → hepatic clearance
B — Cell-surface receptor with second-messenger cascade.
Water-soluble + stored in vesicles + seconds-to-minutes action = classic peptide hormone profile. Peptides cannot cross the plasma membrane, so they signal through membrane receptors (GPCR, RTK, JAK-STAT, ligand-gated ion channel, or guanylyl cyclase). A/C/D describe steroid or thyroid (lipid-soluble) signaling; E describes transport, not signaling.
Q2. A 35-year-old pregnant woman is clinically euthyroid (normal TSH, no symptoms) but has elevated total T4. Which BEST explains this?
A. Autonomous thyroid hormone production from a toxic adenoma
B. Estrogen-driven increase in TBG raises total T4; free T4 remains normal
C. Primary hypothyroidism with secondary TBG elevation
D. Increased PNMT activity in the thyroid
E. Elevated thyroid-stimulating antibodies crossing the placenta
B — Estrogen raises TBG → total T4 ↑ but free T4 unchanged.
Estrogen (pregnancy, OCPs) increases hepatic TBG synthesis. Because only free hormone is biologically active (free-hormone hypothesis), free T4 and TSH stay in normal range and the patient is clinically euthyroid. Always order free T4 when binding-protein status is changing. Same principle: liver failure or nephrotic syndrome lowers TBG → total T4 ↓ with normal free T4.
Q3. A 28-year-old man has episodic headaches, palpitations, hypertension, and elevated plasma metanephrines. The adrenal mass shows high PNMT activity. Why is PNMT uniquely active in the adrenal medulla?
A. The medulla lacks tyrosine hydroxylase
B. Only the medulla has dopamine β-hydroxylase
C. PNMT is induced by cortisol draining from the adrenal cortex via a local portal system
D. The medulla uses serotonin as a precursor
E. PNMT requires iodine concentrated in the medulla
C — Cortisol from the adrenal cortex induces PNMT.
The adrenal cortex and medulla share a portal venous system; high local cortisol induces PNMT (NE → Epi). Sympathetic ganglia lack this cortisol exposure, so they secrete NE not Epi. Pathway: Tyrosine → (TH, rate-limiting) → DOPA → DA → (DBH) → NE → (PNMT) → Epi.
Q4. A 68-year-old woman with CKD has low Ca2+, high PTH, and low 1,25-(OH)2D. Her 25-OH D is normal. Which step is MOST likely impaired?
A. Skin conversion of 7-dehydrocholesterol to cholecalciferol
B. Hepatic 25-hydroxylase
C. Renal 1α-hydroxylase
D. Intestinal absorption of vitamin D
E. 24-hydroxylase activity
C — Renal 1α-hydroxylase is impaired in CKD.
Cascade: skin (7-DHC + UVB → cholecalciferol) → liver (25-hydroxylase → 25-OH D, storage form) → kidney (1α-hydroxylase → 1,25-(OH)2D, active). Normal 25-OH D + low 1,25-(OH)2D localizes the defect to the kidney. 1α-hydroxylase is up-regulated by PTH and low phosphate; activation is lost with renal failure.
Q5. A 42-year-old woman with APL (t(15;17) PML-RARα) is started on ATRA. Mechanism?
A. ATRA binds a cell-surface GPCR and generates cAMP
B. ATRA binds RAR, which heterodimerizes with RXR on RAREs, releasing corepressors and allowing differentiation
C. ATRA competitively inhibits androgen receptor binding
D. ATRA is a covalent inhibitor of DNA methyltransferase
E. ATRA induces proteasomal degradation of tyrosine kinase receptors
B — RAR/RXR heterodimer on RAREs drives differentiation.
Retinoic acids signal via nuclear receptors RAR and RXR. In APL, the PML-RARα fusion traps the complex in a repressive state. Pharmacologic ATRA overwhelms the block, corepressors leave, coactivators are recruited, and promyelocytes differentiate into neutrophils ("differentiation therapy"). RXR heterodimerizes with RAR, VDR, TR, PPAR, LXR, FXR.
Q6. A Scatchard plot for cortisol binding to GR has slope −0.1 nM−1 and x-intercept 500 pmol/mg. What are Kd and Bmax?
A. Kd = 0.1 nM; Bmax = 500 pmol/mg
B. Kd = 10 nM; Bmax = 500 pmol/mg
C. Kd = 0.1 nM; Bmax = 250 pmol/mg
D. Kd = 500 nM; Bmax = 0.1 pmol/mg
E. Cannot be calculated
B — Kd = 10 nM; Bmax = 500 pmol/mg.
On a Scatchard plot: slope = −1/Kd and x-intercept = Bmax. Slope −0.1 nM−1 → Kd = 1 / 0.1 = 10 nM. X-intercept = 500 pmol/mg = Bmax. Lower Kd = higher affinity.
Q7. A 23-year-old with diabetes — which test best distinguishes T1DM from T2DM and evaluates residual β-cell function?
A. Serum insulin level
B. Hemoglobin A1c
C. C-peptide
D. Fasting glucagon
E. Islet cell autoantibodies alone
C — C-peptide is the best marker of endogenous insulin secretion.
Proinsulin is cleaved in β-cells into insulin + C-peptide in a 1:1 ratio. Exogenous insulin contains NO C-peptide. Low/absent = no endogenous production (T1DM, factitious insulin). Elevated = T2DM or insulinoma. Insulin levels alone (A) can't distinguish endogenous from injected.
Q8. GnRH applied to pituitary gonadotropes in two conditions: (1) pulsatile every 60 min; (2) continuous for 24 h. LH/FSH response?
A. Pulsatile sustained; continuous sustained
B. Pulsatile maintained; continuous → receptor downregulation → suppressed LH/FSH (basis of GnRH agonist therapy)
C. Pulsatile suppressed; continuous enhanced
D. Identical responses; receptor density is the only variable
E. Neither produces measurable LH/FSH
B — Pulsatile maintains LH/FSH; continuous causes desensitization.
Pituitary gonadotropes need pulsatile GnRH (every ~60–90 min) for normal LH/FSH secretion. Continuous GnRH (or long-acting agonists like leuprolide) desensitizes/downregulates the receptor → LH/FSH drops → "medical castration." Clinical uses: prostate CA, precocious puberty, endometriosis, IVF protocols.

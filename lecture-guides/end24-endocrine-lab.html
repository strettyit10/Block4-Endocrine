Endocrine Lab Interpretation

LEC 23: Endocrine Laboratory

Interpreting Endocrine Tests — USMLE Step 1 Depth

LO1: Principles of Endocrine Lab Testing
▼

Master the distinction between static tests (single measurements) and dynamic tests (provocation/suppression), understand negative feedback principles, and recognize key pre-analytic considerations for accurate endocrine diagnosis.

Static vs Dynamic Testing▼

STATIC TESTS: Single measurement at one time point; assess baseline hormone levels.

  • Morning cortisol (8 AM peak)
  • Fasting glucose
  • TSH
  • Serum calcium
  • Simple, fast, useful for screening

DYNAMIC TESTS: Provoke or suppress the axis to assess reserve or autonomy.

  • STIMULATION tests: Used when deficiency suspected. Questions: "Will the axis respond to stimulation?"
  • Cosyntropin test (adrenal reserve)
  • GnRH stimulation (pituitary LH/FSH)
  • TRH stimulation (TSH)
  • Glucagon test (GH)
  • Insulin tolerance test (GH, ACTH)
  • GHRH + arginine (GH)
  • SUPPRESSION tests: Used when excess suspected. Questions: "Can the axis be suppressed normally?"
  • Dexamethasone suppression test (cortisol)
  • Oral glucose tolerance test for GH (acromegaly)
  • Water load test (vasopressin)
  • Saline infusion for aldosterone
Negative Feedback Principle▼

In primary gland failure, trophic hormone concentration rises due to loss of negative feedback inhibition.

Classic Pattern:

  • Primary hypothyroidism: ↑TSH, ↓T4 (thyroid gland fails → TSH compensates)
  • Primary adrenal insufficiency: ↑ACTH, ↓cortisol (adrenal fails → ACTH compensates)
  • Primary hypogonadism: ↑LH/FSH, ↓testosterone (gonads fail → LH/FSH compensate)

Central (Secondary) Deficiency: Both trophic hormone AND target gland hormone are ↓.

  • Pituitary failure → ↓TSH and ↓T4 (unlike primary)
  • Pituitary ACTH deficiency → ↓ACTH and ↓cortisol
Basal Values & Reference Ranges▼

Morning Cortisol (8 AM, the peak):

  • <3 mcg/dL = likely adrenal insufficiency (inadequate morning rise)
  • >18 mcg/dL = unlikely adrenal insufficiency (sufficient reserve)
  • 3–18 mcg/dL = equivocal → cosyntropin stimulation test required

Fasting Insulin & Glucose:

  • Calculate HOMA-IR = [fasting insulin (µU/mL) × fasting glucose (mg/dL)] / 405
  • HOMA-IR >2 suggests insulin resistance

ACTH Collection (Critical!):

  • Must be collected in chilled EDTA tube (lavender-top) on ice
  • Immediately centrifuged and frozen (pulsatile; degrades rapidly)
  • Transport on ice to lab
  • Improper handling → falsely low ACTH → misdiagnosis
When to order STIMULATION vs SUPPRESSION tests
STIMULATION: When you suspect DEFICIENCY (low hormone). Test: "Does the gland respond?" Example: Low morning cortisol → cosyntropin test. SUPPRESSION: When you suspect EXCESS (high hormone). Test: "Can you turn it off?" Example: High cortisol → dexamethasone suppression test.
Red Flag: ACTH Handling Improper ACTH collection/storage = falsely low result = misdiagnosis of adrenal insufficiency as secondary (when it's actually primary).
8 AM Cortisol <3 = likely AI; >18 = unlikely AI; 3–18 = need stimulation test.
Negative Feedback Primary disease: ↑trophic hormone. Central disease: ↓trophic hormone.
Dynamic Testing Use stimulation to unmask deficiency; use suppression to confirm excess.
LO2: Thyroid Function Tests — Complete Interpretation
▼

TSH is the most sensitive test for primary thyroid dysfunction. Understand free T4, antibodies, and the pitfalls of thyroid testing including biotin interference, heterophile antibodies, and TSH suppression lags.

TSH (Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone)▼

Normal Range: 0.4–4.0 mIU/L (varies by assay; trimester-dependent in pregnancy)

  • Most sensitive test for primary thyroid dysfunction
  • Log-linear relationship with free T4: small change in T4 → large change in TSH
  • 3rd-generation assays detect down to 0.01 mIU/L → can detect suppressed TSH

Interpretation Patterns:

  • TSH ↑ alone: Subclinical hypothyroidism (normal T4, high TSH)
  • TSH ↓ alone: Subclinical hyperthyroidism (normal T4, low TSH)
  • TSH ↑ + ↓T4: Primary hypothyroidism (Hashimoto's)
  • TSH ↓ + ↑T4: Primary hyperthyroidism (Graves', toxic nodule)
  • TSH ↓ + ↓T4: Central hypothyroidism (pituitary or hypothalamus)

Key Pitfalls:

  • Biotin interference: Biotin supplements ↓TSH (false low). Stop biotin 2–3 days before testing.
  • Heterophile antibodies: May cause falsely high or low TSH on immunoassay.
  • Pregnancy: Lower TSH limits in 1st trimester (0.1–2.5 mIU/L). Use trimester-specific ranges.
  • NTIS (non-thyroidal illness): ↓TSH in sick patients due to illness, not thyroid disease.
  • TSH lag after treatment: TSH lags weeks to months behind T4 changes. Don't adjust dose on TSH alone early in treatment.
Free T4 (FT4) & Total T4/T3▼

Free T4 (FT4): Unbound, biologically active T4.

  • Measured by equilibrium dialysis (gold standard) or immunoassay
  • NOT affected by TBG changes (major advantage over total T4)
  • Use when: TSH discordant with clinical picture; central hypothyroidism (monitor FT4, not TSH); pregnancy

Total T4 & T3: Bound + unbound hormone.

  • Affected by TBG (thyroid-binding globulin) concentration:
  • ↑TBG (pregnancy, OCP, estrogen) → ↑total T4/T3 (FT4 normal)
  • ↓TBG (androgens, nephrotic syndrome, cirrhosis) → ↓total T4/T3 (FT4 normal)
  • Rarely used alone; mostly historical interest

Free T3: Useful in T3 toxicosis (elevated T3 with normal T4 in Graves' disease).

  • T3:T4 ratio elevated in Graves' (autonomous thyroid activity favors T3 production)
Thyroid Antibodies▼

Anti-TPO (Thyroid Peroxidase Antibody):

  • Most sensitive for Hashimoto's thyroiditis
  • Also elevated in Graves' disease (overlap)
  • Present in ~10% of euthyroid women → indicates risk for postpartum thyroiditis

Anti-Tg (Thyroglobulin Antibody):

  • Less specific; present in ~60% of Hashimoto's
  • Also seen in Graves' (less common than anti-TPO)

TRAb / TSI (TSH Receptor Antibody / TSH Receptor Immunoglobulin):

  • Specific for Graves' disease
  • IgG crosses placenta → risk of neonatal Graves' (transient)
  • Used to predict Graves' remission (declining TRAb = better prognosis)

Anti-TSH Receptor Blocking Antibody:

  • Causes hypothyroidism (different from stimulating TRAb)
  • Rare; seen in some patients with Graves' who develop atrophic thyroiditis
Thyroglobulin & Calcitonin▼

Thyroglobulin (Tg): Tumor marker for differentiated thyroid cancer (papillary, follicular).

  • Used post-thyroidectomy + radioactive iodine (RAI) ablation
  • Should be undetectable after total thyroidectomy + RAI
  • Rising Tg = recurrence/metastasis
  • Pitfall: Anti-Tg antibodies can falsely lower Tg measurements (interference)

Calcitonin: Tumor marker for medullary thyroid carcinoma (MTC).

  • Elevated pre-operatively → obtain RET mutation testing for MEN2 syndrome
Thyroid Antibodies & Disease Associations
Anti-TPO: Most sensitive for Hashimoto's. Anti-Tg: Less specific, ~60% Hashimoto's. TRAb (TSI): Specific for Graves'; IgG crosses placenta. Anti-TSH-R blocking: Causes hypothyroidism (rare).
TFT Interpretation: Primary vs Central Hypothyroidism
Primary Hypothyroidism: ↑TSH, ↓FT4 (Hashimoto's). Central Hypothyroidism: ↓TSH, ↓FT4 (pituitary/hypothalamus). Subclinical: ↑TSH, normal FT4 (primary); ↓TSH, normal FT4 (secondary).
TSH Most Sensitive For primary thyroid dysfunction. Small FT4 changes → large TSH changes (log-linear).
Biotin Interference Biotin supplements falsely ↓TSH. Stop 2–3 days before testing.
TRAb Specific for Graves' Diagnostic for Graves' disease; predicts remission if declining.
Free T4 in Pregnancy Use FT4 in pregnancy (not total T4). TSH normal range shifts in 1st trimester.
LO3: Adrenal Function Tests
▼

Master cortisol assessment (basal, 24h UFC, late-night salivary), suppression tests (DST), ACTH interpretation for diagnosis of Cushing's syndrome, adrenal insufficiency, and primary aldosteronism screening.

Cortisol Tests▼

8 AM Serum Cortisol:

  • <3 mcg/dL = likely adrenal insufficiency (AI)
  • >18 mcg/dL = unlikely adrenal insufficiency
  • 3–18 mcg/dL = equivocal → cosyntropin stimulation test needed

24-Hour Urinary Free Cortisol (UFC):

  • Elevated >3× upper limit normal → Cushing's syndrome
  • Normal reference: <50 mcg/24h (varies by lab)
  • Advantage: integrates cortisol over 24 hours; less affected by stress/diurnal variation
  • Disadvantage: collection errors (incomplete sample)

Late-Night Salivary Cortisol:

  • Nadir should be very low at midnight/bedtime
  • Elevated at midnight → Cushing's syndrome (loss of normal suppression)
  • Advantages: convenient, non-invasive, at-home collection possible, sensitive for Cushing's
ACTH Interpretation▼

ACTH distinguishes ACTH-dependent vs ACTH-independent pathology:

  • High ACTH + High Cortisol: ACTH-dependent Cushing's (pituitary adenoma, ectopic ACTH)
  • Low/Normal ACTH + High Cortisol: ACTH-independent Cushing's (adrenal tumor, CAH)
  • High ACTH + Low Cortisol: Primary adrenal insufficiency (ACTH compensates)
  • Low ACTH + Low Cortisol: Secondary/central adrenal insufficiency (pituitary ACTH deficiency)

Critical Pre-analytic Requirements:

  • Chilled EDTA tube (lavender-top) on ice
  • Immediate centrifugation (pulsatile, degrades rapidly)
  • Transport on ice; freeze immediately
  • Improper handling → falsely LOW ACTH → misdiagnosis
Cosyntropin Stimulation Test▼

Standard Dose (250 mcg) Cosyntropin:

  • Give 250 mcg IV or IM; measure cortisol at 0, 30, 60 min
  • Peak cortisol ≥18 mcg/dL = normal (rules out primary adrenal insufficiency)
  • Peak <18 mcg/dL = adrenal insufficiency (inadequate response)

Low-Dose Cosyntropin (1 mcg):

  • More sensitive for mild secondary (central) adrenal insufficiency
  • Standard test picks up primary AI well, but may miss mild secondary AI
  • 1 mcg ACTH → peak cortisol ≥18 mcg/dL = normal (same cutoff)

Difference: 250 mcg "supraphysiologic" dose masks mild secondary AI; 1 mcg is more physiologic.

Dexamethasone Suppression Test (DST)▼

1 mg Overnight DST (screening):

  • Give 1 mg dexamethasone at 11 PM → measure 8 AM cortisol next day
  • Normal: cortisol <1.8 mcg/dL (suppressed by dexamethasone feedback)
  • Abnormal: cortisol ≥1.8 mcg/dL → suggests Cushing's syndrome (fails suppression)
  • Screening test; high false positives (depression, obesity, alcohol)

High-Dose DST (8 mg or 2 mg q6h × 2 days):

  • Distinguishes pituitary Cushing's (ACTH-secreting adenoma) from ectopic ACTH or adrenal tumors
  • Suppression >50% from baseline cortisol: Pituitary Cushing's (adenoma still partially responsive to GC feedback)
  • No suppression (<50%): Ectopic ACTH (ectopic tumors ignore dexamethasone) or adrenal tumor
Aldosterone & Primary Aldosteronism▼

Aldosterone-to-Renin Ratio (ARR) — Screening:

  • Patient sitting upright for ≥5 min; fasting if possible; off interfering drugs (ACEi, ARB, β-blockers, K-sparing diuretics, NSAIDs)
  • ARR = aldosterone (ng/dL) / renin (ng/mL/h) or plasma renin activity
  • ARR >20–30 (with aldosterone >15 ng/dL) = positive screen → proceed to confirmatory test

Confirmatory Tests (if positive ARR):

  • Saline Loading Test: IV infusion of 2L 0.9% saline over 4 hours. Normal: aldosterone suppresses to <5 ng/dL. Primary aldosteronism: fails to suppress (aldosterone >5 ng/dL).
  • Oral Sodium Loading: High sodium diet for 3–5 days + measure 24h urine aldosterone. Normal: <12 mcg/24h. Primary aldosteronism: >12 mcg/24h.
  • Fludrocortisone Suppression: Fludrocortisone for 4 days + measure upright aldosterone. Suppression <6 ng/dL = normal.

Adrenal Venous Sampling (AVS):

  • Gold standard to lateralize (unilateral adenoma vs bilateral hyperplasia)
  • Guides surgery (unilateral case = adrenalectomy; bilateral = medical management)
Other Adrenal Tests▼

DHEA-S (Dehydroepiandrosterone Sulfate):

  • Marker of adrenal androgen secretion
  • ↓ in secondary adrenal insufficiency (ACTH-dependent; ACTH drives DHEA-S)
  • ↑ in CAH (congenital adrenal hyperplasia) — adrenal hyperplasia with shunting to adrenal androgens

24-Hour Urine Metanephrines:

  • Screening for pheochromocytoma
  • Preferred over plasma catecholamines (more stable analyte)

Plasma Free Metanephrines:

  • Alternative to urine metanephrines; more sensitive but less specific
Cushing's Diagnostic Algorithm
1) Confirm hypercortisolism: 24h UFC, late-night salivary cortisol, 1mg DST. 2) Measure ACTH: high = ACTH-dependent, low = ACTH-independent. 3) If ACTH-high, do high-dose DST: suppression = pituitary Cushing's, no suppression = ectopic ACTH or adrenal tumor.
Low-Dose vs High-Dose Cosyntropin
Standard 250 mcg: Supraphysiologic dose; good for primary AI; may miss mild secondary AI. Low-Dose 1 mcg: More physiologic; more sensitive for central/secondary AI. Same cortisol cutoff (≥18 mcg/dL).
Morning Cortisol Ranges <3 = likely AI; >18 = unlikely AI; 3–18 = need stimulation test.
ACTH Handling Critical Chilled tube, ice transport, immediate processing. Poor handling → falsely low ACTH.
High-Dose DST for Cushing's Suppression >50% = pituitary. No suppression = ectopic or adrenal tumor.
ARR Screening ARR >20–30 (aldosterone >15) = positive screen for primary aldosteronism.
LO4: Pituitary Function Tests
▼

Pituitary hormones are pulsatile. Master static markers (IGF-1, prolactin), dynamic stimulation tests (ITT, GHRH+arginine, GnRH), suppression tests for GH excess, water deprivation test for DI diagnosis, and recognize hook effect pitfall in prolactin.

GH (Growth Hormone) Axis▼

IGF-1 (Insulin-like Growth Factor-1):

  • Best integrated marker of GH secretion (more stable than pulsatile GH)
  • IGF-1 reflects GH secretion over hours/days, not minute-to-minute
  • Normal ↑ with age in children; peaks in young adults; ↓ with age
  • Low IGF-1 = GH deficiency (consider stimulation test)
  • High IGF-1 = GH excess (acromegaly/gigantism)

GH Stimulation Tests (GH Deficiency):

  • Insulin Tolerance Test (ITT): Gold standard. IV insulin (0.1 U/kg) → hypoglycemia. GH response normal if >5 µg/L. Also assesses ACTH/cortisol response.
  • GHRH + Arginine: Combination GH secretagogues. GH >4–5 µg/L = normal response.
  • Glucagon Test: Alternative if ITT contraindicated (seizure risk). GH >3 µg/L normal.

GH Suppression Test (GH Excess/Acromegaly):

  • OGTT: 75g glucose load. GH should suppress to <1 ng/mL (or <0.4 by newer assays). If GH nadir >1 ng/mL = acromegaly (GH fails to suppress normally).
Prolactin▼

Normal Range: <20 ng/mL in women; <15 ng/mL in men (varies by assay)

  • Slight elevation with stress/venipuncture → re-check if mildly elevated

Interpretation:

  • Prolactin >200 ng/mL: Almost always prolactinoma (macroprolactinoma common). Macroadenomas usually >200.
  • Prolactin 20–200 ng/mL: Differential includes drugs, stalk effect (non-functioning adenoma compresses stalk → ↓dopamine), other pituitary disease
  • Elevated + amenorrhea + galactorrhea: Prolactinoma until proven otherwise

Hook Effect (High-Dose Hook):

  • Paradoxically high prolactin can give falsely LOW result on sandwich immunoassay
  • Occurs when prolactin so high it saturates capture antibodies → underestimation
  • Solution: Dilute the serum sample (1:10 or 1:100) to unmask true high value
  • RED FLAG: If clinical picture suggests prolactinoma but prolactin "only mildly elevated" → consider hook effect

Macroprolactin:

  • Large prolactin complexes (IgG-bound) that are biologically inactive
  • Falsely elevates measured prolactin on standard assay
  • Check macroprolactin if elevated prolactin without clinical symptoms
LH/FSH & GnRH Stimulation▼

Basal LH/FSH:

  • Normal ranges vary widely (pulsatile secretion)
  • Single values less reliable than dynamic testing
  • High LH/FSH (with low testosterone) = primary hypogonadism (testicular failure)
  • Low LH/FSH (with low testosterone) = secondary hypogonadism (hypothalamic/pituitary)

GnRH Stimulation Test:

  • Give GnRH bolus; measure LH/FSH response at 30 min
  • Intact HPG axis (normal hypothalamus/pituitary): LH/FSH ↑ responsively
  • Hypogonadotropic hypogonadism: Blunted LH/FSH response (hypothalamic or pituitary disorder)
  • Primary hypogonadism: LH/FSH basally ↑ (no further rise with GnRH); testis is problem, not pituitary

Clomiphene Citrate Challenge Test:

  • Clomiphene blocks estrogen feedback → should stimulate GnRH/LH/FSH if axis intact
  • Assesses HPG axis reserve
Water Deprivation Test (Diabetes Insipidus)▼

Indication: Suspected diabetes insipidus (polyuria + polydipsia with low urine osmolality)

Protocol:

  • Restrict water (NPO) → monitor urine osmolality and serum osmolality
  • Continue until urine osmolality plateaus (doesn't increase despite dehydration) or serum osmolality >295 mOsm/kg
  • Then administer desmopressin (DDAVP, synthetic ADH)
  • Re-measure urine osmolality 30–60 min post-DDAVP

Interpretation:

  • Central DI (Pituitary/Hypothalamic): Dilute urine despite dehydration (osmolality <300) → CONCENTRATES after DDAVP (ADH receptor is intact, just missing ADH)
  • Nephrogenic DI (Kidney Resistance to ADH): Dilute urine → FAILS to concentrate after DDAVP (ADH receptor defective; DDAVP can't work)
  • Primary Polydipsia (Psychogenic): Concentrated urine on water deprivation (normal ADH response; problem is excess drinking)
Central vs Nephrogenic DI
Central DI: Dilute urine on dehydration → concentrates after DDAVP (problem is missing ADH; kidney works fine). Nephrogenic DI: Dilute urine on dehydration → fails to concentrate after DDAVP (kidney doesn't respond to ADH).
Hook Effect in Prolactin
Very high prolactin paradoxically gives falsely LOW result on sandwich immunoassay (saturation of capture antibodies). Solution: Dilute serum sample. RED FLAG: Prolactinoma suspected but prolactin only mildly elevated → consider hook effect.
IGF-1 Best Marker IGF-1 integrates GH secretion. Low = GH deficiency; high = GH excess.
Hook Effect Risk Very high prolactin → falsely low on immunoassay. Dilute sample to unmask.
DDAVP Response Central DI responds to DDAVP; nephrogenic DI does not.
Prolactin >200 = Prolactinoma Almost always macroprolactinoma. Levels 20–200 require differential.
LO5: Diabetes & Glucose Tests
▼

Master diagnosis of diabetes (FPG, OGTT, HbA1c), gestational diabetes screening, C-peptide for classification, and 72-hour fast protocol for insulinoma workup. Recognize HbA1c pitfalls and reactive hypoglycemia testing.

Fasting Plasma Glucose (FPG)▼
  • Normal: <100 mg/dL
  • Prediabetes (Impaired Fasting Glucose): 100–125 mg/dL
  • Diabetes: ≥126 mg/dL (on two separate occasions to diagnose)

Requires ≥8 hour fast. Variability ±5–10% day-to-day.

Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT)▼

Standard 75g OGTT (Nonpregnant Adults):

  • Give 75g glucose; measure at 0, 1h (sometimes), 2h
  • 2-hour glucose: <140 = normal; 140–199 = IGT (impaired glucose tolerance); ≥200 = diabetes

Gestational Diabetes (GDM) — One-Step Approach (IADPSG):

  • 75g OGTT (fasting and 2-hour values)
  • ≥1 abnormal value = GDM diagnosis:
  • Fasting ≥92 mg/dL, 1h ≥180 mg/dL, or 2h ≥153 mg/dL

Gestational Diabetes — Two-Step Approach (Carpenter-Coustan):

  • Step 1 (Screen): 50g glucose challenge test (non-fasting). 1h glucose ≥130–140 mg/dL (lab-dependent) → proceed to step 2
  • Step 2 (Diagnostic): 100g OGTT (fasting). Abnormal values: fasting ≥95, 1h ≥180, 2h ≥155, 3h ≥140 mg/dL. ≥2 abnormal = GDM.

OGTT for Acromegaly Screening:

  • GH should suppress to <1 ng/mL with glucose load
  • If GH nadir >1 ng/mL → acromegaly (GH fails to suppress)

OGTT for Reactive Hypoglycemia:

  • 5-hour OGTT (rarely indicated)
  • Hypoglycemia <55 mg/dL at 2–4 hours with symptoms = reactive hypoglycemia
HbA1c (Glycated Hemoglobin)▼

Mechanism: Non-enzymatic glycosylation of hemoglobin A N-terminal valine (β-chain). Reflects 2–3 month average glucose (RBC lifespan ~120 days).

  • Normal: <5.7%
  • Prediabetes: 5.7–6.4%
  • Diabetes: ≥6.5%

Estimated Average Glucose (eAG): eAG = (28.7 × HbA1c) – 46.7 mg/dL

False LOW HbA1c:

  • Hemolytic anemia (↑RBC turnover → ↓RBC age → less glycosylation time)
  • Sickle cell disease (hemolysis)
  • G6PD deficiency (hemolysis)
  • Pregnancy (↑RBC turnover)
  • Recent blood transfusion (fresh RBCs)

False HIGH HbA1c:

  • Iron deficiency anemia (↓RBC count → older RBCs → more glycosylation time)
  • Splenectomy (↑RBC lifespan)
  • Uremia (carbamylated hemoglobin interferes with assay)
  • Chronic kidney disease (similar carbamylation effect)
C-Peptide Interpretation▼

What is C-Peptide? Cleavage product of proinsulin. Equimolar with endogenous insulin but not cleared as rapidly → better marker of endogenous insulin production.

Clinical Uses:

  • LOW C-peptide: T1DM (↓beta cell mass), exogenous insulin use (suppresses C-peptide)
  • NORMAL/HIGH C-peptide: T2DM (adequate beta cells), insulinoma (very high C-peptide + low glucose), SU/meglitinide ingestion (stimulates endogenous insulin)

Distinguish Insulinoma vs Factitious Hypoglycemia (Exogenous Insulin):

  • Insulinoma: Elevated insulin + elevated C-peptide + hypoglycemia
  • Exogenous insulin: Elevated insulin + LOW C-peptide + hypoglycemia (exogenous insulin suppresses endogenous)
  • Insulin antibodies: Check if available; SU use → high insulin, high C-peptide

Assess Residual Beta Cell Function:

  • C-peptide is better marker than insulin (not degraded as rapidly)
  • Higher C-peptide = more endogenous insulin production capacity
Insulin & Proinsulin▼

Insulin (Fasting): Normal fasting <12 µU/mL (varies by assay). Elevated fasting insulin = insulin resistance or insulinoma.

Insulin:Glucose Ratio:

  • >0.3 during hypoglycemia (glucose <55 mg/dL) = inappropriate insulin secretion (insulinoma)

Proinsulin:

  • Precursor of insulin (cleaved to insulin + C-peptide)
  • Elevated in insulinoma (tumors secrete proinsulin in addition to insulin)
  • Normal fasting proinsulin <12 pmol/L
  • Proinsulin >30% of total insulin = insulinoma
72-Hour Fast (Insulinoma Diagnosis)▼

Indication: Suspected insulinoma (hypoglycemia with symptoms + inappropriate insulin)

Protocol:

  • Supervised fast (NPO) for up to 72 hours
  • Measure at baseline and then q4-6h (or at symptom onset): glucose, insulin, C-peptide, proinsulin, β-hydroxybutyrate
  • Terminate fast if glucose <55 mg/dL with symptoms (Whipple's triad) or after 72 hours

Whipple's Triad:

  • Documented hypoglycemia (glucose <55 mg/dL)
  • Symptoms of hypoglycemia (tremor, diaphoresis, altered mental status, palpitations)
  • Relief with glucose administration

Diagnosis of Insulinoma:

  • Hypoglycemia + inappropriately elevated insulin (insulin/glucose ratio >0.3)
  • Elevated C-peptide (endogenous source)
  • Elevated proinsulin (>30% total insulin)
  • ↓β-hydroxybutyrate (exogenous insulin suppresses ketogenesis; endogenous insulinoma also suppresses)
Distinguish Insulinoma vs Factitious Hypoglycemia
Insulinoma: High glucose + high insulin + high C-peptide + high proinsulin. Exogenous insulin: Low glucose + high insulin + LOW C-peptide + normal/low proinsulin (exogenous insulin suppresses endogenous). SU ingestion: Low glucose + high insulin + high C-peptide.
HbA1c False Values
False LOW: Hemolytic anemia, sickle cell, G6PD, pregnancy, recent transfusion (↑RBC turnover). False HIGH: Iron deficiency anemia, splenectomy, uremia, chronic kidney disease (older RBCs or carbamylation).
HbA1c ≥6.5% = Diabetes Reflects 2–3 month average glucose. Use eAG for patient communication.
C-Peptide Distinguishes Source Low C-peptide + high insulin = exogenous. High C-peptide + high insulin = endogenous (insulinoma, T2DM).
72-Hour Fast Gold Standard For insulinoma. Hypoglycemia + high insulin + high C-peptide = insulinoma.
Proinsulin >30% Total Insulin Suggests insulinoma (tumors secrete proinsulin).
LO6: Calcium, PTH & Vitamin D Lab Interpretation
▼

Master calcium correction for albumin, PTH interpretation patterns to diagnose primary/secondary hyperparathyroidism, hypoparathyroidism, and non-PTH mediated hypercalcemia. Understand vitamin D metabolism, PTHrP in malignancy, and FHH distinction from primary HPT.

Calcium Measurement & Correction▼

Total Calcium: 8.5–10.2 mg/dL (includes bound to albumin + ionized + complexed)

Albumin Correction (CRITICAL):

  • Corrected Ca = Measured Ca + 0.8 × (4 – [albumin in g/dL])
  • For every 1 g/dL ↓albumin below 4, add 0.8 mg/dL to total Ca
  • Example: Measured Ca 7.8, albumin 2.0: Corrected Ca = 7.8 + 0.8 × (4 – 2.0) = 7.8 + 1.6 = 9.4 mg/dL (not low!)

Ionized Calcium: 1.1–1.35 mmol/L (4.5–5.3 mg/dL)

  • Directly biologically active (not affected by albumin)
  • Use in ICU, acidosis, or when albumin abnormal
  • Acidosis ↓protein binding → ↑ionized Ca (despite same total); alkalosis ↑protein binding → ↓ionized Ca (tetany)
PTH (Parathyroid Hormone)▼

Intact PTH (iPTH): 10–65 pg/mL (varies by assay). Measures biologically active 1-84 PTH fragment.

Interpretation Patterns (Using PTH + Calcium):

  • ↑PTH + ↑Ca: Primary hyperparathyroidism (PTH drives Ca up)
  • ↑PTH + ↓Ca or normal Ca: Secondary hyperparathyroidism (reactive; PTH compensates for low Ca from other cause — vitamin D deficiency, CKD, malabsorption)
  • ↓PTH + ↓Ca: Hypoparathyroidism (PTH deficiency or resistance)
  • ↓PTH + ↑Ca: Non-PTH mediated hypercalcemia (malignancy, granulomatous disease, vitamin D toxicity, thiazides, FHH)

Intraoperative PTH (IOPTH) Monitoring:

  • Measured during parathyroid surgery to confirm gland removal
  • PTH drops >50% at 10 min post-gland removal → confirms cure
  • Validates that the diseased gland was removed and residual/ectopic parathyroids not responsible
PTHrP (PTH-Related Protein)▼

PTHrP Mechanism: Secreted by malignancies. Acts like PTH on PTH1 receptor → ↑Ca reabsorption, ↑PO₄ wasting.

Humoral Hypercalcemia of Malignancy (HHM):

  • Most common cause of hypercalcemia in hospitalized patients
  • Classic cancers: Squamous cell (lung, head/neck, cervix), renal cell, breast
  • Lab pattern: ↑Ca, ↓PO₄, but PTH is LOW (suppressed by high Ca) — key distinction from primary HPT
  • PTHrP level can be measured to confirm; usually >2 pmol/L in HHM

Other Mechanisms of Malignancy Hypercalcemia:

  • ↑1,25-OH₂D (lymphomas; autonomous 1α-hydroxylase in macrophages)
  • Osteolytic metastases (↑local RANKL → osteoclast activation)
Vitamin D▼

25-OH-D (Calcidiol): Measure for vitamin D status.

  • Normal: >30 ng/mL
  • Insufficiency: 20–29 ng/mL
  • Deficiency: <20 ng/mL

1,25-OH₂-D (Calcitriol): Active form; rarely measured clinically.

  • Regulated by PTH (↑PTH → ↑1α-hydroxylase) and FGF23 (↓1α-hydroxylase)
  • Measured when: Suspected enzyme defect (1α-hydroxylase deficiency), granulomatous disease, or hypercalcemia workup
  • Elevated 1,25-OH₂D: Granulomatous disease (sarcoidosis, TB, histoplasmosis) — macrophages autonomously express 1α-hydroxylase → hypercalcemia
Hypercalcemia Differential Diagnosis▼

PTH HIGH (→ ↑Ca):

  • Primary hyperparathyroidism: Most common cause outpatient (adenoma ~80%)

PTH LOW (→ ↓Ca feedback suppressed):

  • Malignancy (HHM): PTHrP mimics PTH → ↑Ca, ↓PO₄, ↓PTH
  • Granulomatous disease (Sarcoidosis, TB): ↑1,25-OH₂D from macrophage 1α-hydroxylase → ↑Ca absorption, ↑1,25-OH₂D, normal-low PTH, responds to steroids
  • Vitamin D toxicity: Excess calcitriol (supplements, malignancy producing calcitriol) → ↑Ca, ↓PTH
  • Thiazide diuretics: Reduce urinary Ca loss → mild hypercalcemia
  • Milk-alkali syndrome: Excessive calcium + absorbable alkali (e.g., calcium carbonate + sodium bicarbonate)
  • FHH (Familial Hypocalciuric Hypercalcemia): CaSR (calcium-sensing receptor) mutation → inappropriately high set-point for suppression
FHH vs Primary Hyperparathyroidism▼

FHH (Familial Hypocalciuric Hypercalcemia):

  • CaSR mutation → "resetting" of calcium set-point
  • Benign; asymptomatic hypercalcemia in multiple family members
  • Lab hallmark: Urine calcium VERY LOW (despite high serum Ca)
  • Calcium:creatinine clearance ratio <0.01 in FHH (normal >0.02)
  • PTH normal or mildly elevated (appropriate for Ca level, but inappropriately high for patient's "set-point")

Primary Hyperparathyroidism:

  • Adenoma, hyperplasia, or carcinoma → excessive PTH
  • Lab hallmark: High urinary calcium (↑filtered load from high Ca)
  • Calcium:creatinine clearance ratio >0.02
  • PTH high (clearly suppresses Ca inappropriately)

Key Distinction: 24-hour urine calcium (or Ca:Cr clearance) easily separates them. FHH does NOT need surgery (surgery ineffective).

24-Hour Urine Calcium▼
  • Elevated (normal >24h reference): Primary hyperparathyroidism (↑filtered load from high serum Ca), vitamin D toxicity
  • Low: FHH (hallmark; urine Ca reabsorption↑), thiazide diuretics (↓urinary Ca loss), hypoparathyroidism (↓serum Ca → ↓filtered load)
Calcium Correction Formula
Corrected Ca = Measured Ca + 0.8 × (4 – albumin in g/dL). Example: Measured 7.8, albumin 2.0 → 7.8 + 0.8 × 2 = 9.4 mg/dL.
Hypercalcemia Differential: PTH-Mediated vs Non-PTH
PTH HIGH: Primary HPT. PTH LOW: Malignancy (HHM via PTHrP), granulomatous disease (↑1,25-OH₂D), vitamin D toxicity, thiazides, FHH. Key test: Urine calcium (low in FHH; high in primary HPT).
Always Correct Calcium for Albumin Falsely low/high total Ca if albumin abnormal. Use formula.
PTH + Ca Pattern Diagnostic ↑PTH + ↑Ca = primary HPT. ↓PTH + ↑Ca = non-PTH mediated.
FHH Urine Calcium Very Low Ca:Cr clearance <0.01. Primary HPT has high urine Ca.
PTHrP in Malignancy ↑Ca, ↓PO₄, ↓PTH (classic HHM pattern). Distinguish from primary HPT by low PTH.

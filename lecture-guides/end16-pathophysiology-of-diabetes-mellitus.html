Lecture 16 | Endocrine Block | HoyaDocs

LO1: Normal Glucose Homeostasis & Insulin Physiology

▼
Fasting vs Fed State
  • Fasting: glucose 70-100 mg/dL maintained by liver glycogenolysis + gluconeogenesis; adipose lipolysis releases FFAs
  • Fed: glucose ↑ → pancreatic β cells secrete insulin → ANABOLIC state (storage)
Pancreatic Islets of Langerhans
  • β cells (~70%): secrete insulin + C-peptide + amylin
  • α cells (~20%): glucagon
  • δ cells (~5%): somatostatin (inhibits insulin & glucagon)
  • PP cells & F cells: pancreatic polypeptide
Click to reveal: GLUT2 & Glucokinase
GLUT2: glucose transporter in β cells; LOW affinity, HIGH capacity → glucose sensor
Glucokinase: rate-limiting enzyme; phosphorylates glucose → glucose-6-phosphate (key step in insulin secretion)
Insulin Secretion Mechanism (5 Steps)
  • 1. Glucose enters β cell via GLUT2
  • 2. Glucokinase phosphorylates → G6P → glycolysis → ↑ ATP:ADP ratio
  • 3. ↑ ATP → closes K⁺-ATP channels (SUR1/Kir6.2) → depolarization
  • 4. Depolarization → opens voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels → ↑ intracellular Ca²⁺
  • 5. ↑ Ca²⁺ → insulin granule exocytosis
💡Incretin Effect: GLP-1 & GIP (from gut L & K cells) → ↑ cAMP in β cells → ↑ glucose-dependent insulin secretion. Explains oral > IV glucose for insulin response.
Insulin Actions
TissueEffect
Liver↑ glycogen synthesis, ↓ gluconeogenesis, ↑ glycolysis, ↑ fatty acid synthesis
Muscle↑ glucose uptake (GLUT4), ↑ glycogen synthesis, ↑ protein synthesis
Adipose↑ glucose uptake (GLUT4), ↑ lipogenesis, ↓ lipolysis
Key Concepts
  • C-peptide: equimolar with insulin; marker of endogenous insulin production (NOT in exogenous insulin)
  • First-phase response: rapid insulin surge (1-3 min); LOST EARLY in T2DM
  • Glucagon: α cell hormone; released when glucose ↓; ↑ glycogenolysis + gluconeogenesis
  • Counter-regulatory hormones: glucagon, epinephrine, cortisol, GH → raise glucose in hypoglycemia
Mnemonic: "Insulin = ANABOLIC (store everything); Glucagon = CATABOLIC (release everything)"
🎯Tested Concepts: GLUT2 function, glucokinase as glucose sensor, incretin effect, C-peptide interpretation, first-phase response loss in T2DM
⚠️Common Traps: Confusing GLUT2 (high capacity) with GLUT4 (insulin-responsive); forgetting C-peptide is absent in exogenous insulin therapy

LO2: Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus

▼
Definition & Epidemiology
  • Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic β cells → ABSOLUTE insulin deficiency
  • Peaks in childhood/adolescence; strong HLA association (HLA-DR3, DR4, DQ2, DQ8)
  • Can occur at any age; LADA (Latent Autoimmune Diabetes of Adults) = T1DM in adult onset
Pathogenesis
  • Genetic + Environmental: HLA susceptibility + viral trigger (enterovirus, Coxsackie B4, molecular mimicry)
  • Immune response: CD4+ T cells activate macrophages + CD8+ cytotoxic T cells → insulitis → β cell killing
  • β cell destruction: progressive over months-years; symptoms when ~80-90% destroyed
Click: Autoantibodies in T1DM
Anti-GAD65: most common & sensitive
Anti-IA-2: islet antigen-2 (tyrosine phosphatase-like)
Anti-insulin (IAA): especially in young children
Anti-ZnT8: zinc transporter 8
ICA: islet cell antibodies
→ Used for risk stratification & diagnosis
Clinical Features
  • Acute onset; young patient (often child/teen)
  • DKA (Diabetic Ketoacidosis) often presenting event
  • Histology: lymphocytic infiltrate (insulitis); eventual islet atrophy
  • Unlike T2DM: NO insulin resistance, NO metabolic syndrome, NO islet amyloid; patient is LEAN
LADA (Latent Autoimmune)
  • Initial presentation mimics T2DM (slow onset in adults ~40-60y)
  • BUT has autoantibodies (anti-GAD65 most common) → eventually insulin-dependent
  • Clinical pearl: treat early with insulin (don't delay with oral agents)
Mnemonic: "T1DM = Thin, Teen, Type I (autoimmune), anti-GAD, Terrible ketoacidosis"
🎯Tested: Autoantibodies (anti-GAD most sensitive), HLA associations, insulitis histology, acute DKA presentation, C-peptide LOW in T1DM
⚠️Traps: LADA initially looks like T2DM but is autoimmune; don't forget to check antibodies in slow-onset adult diabetes; confusing "relative" vs "absolute" insulin deficiency

LO3: Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus

▼
Definition & Epidemiology
  • Progressive insulin resistance + RELATIVE (not absolute) insulin deficiency
  • 90-95% of DM cases; associated with obesity, sedentary lifestyle, age, family history
  • Strong genetic component (polygenic)
Ominous Octet (DeFronzo Pathogenesis)
  • 1. Insulin resistance: muscle, liver, adipose → ↓ GLUT4 translocation, defective signaling
  • 2. β cell failure: hypersecretes initially → exhaustion → relative deficiency (first-phase response loss is EARLIEST sign)
  • 3. ↑ Hepatic glucose: gluconeogenesis; liver doesn't respond to insulin suppression
  • 4. ↑ Glucagon: α cell dysfunction; not suppressed by insulin or glucose
  • 5. ↓ Incretin effect: GLP-1, GIP secretion/response impaired
  • 6. ↑ Renal glucose threshold: SGLT2 reabsorption ↑; threshold ~180 mg/dL → glycosuria above this
  • 7. ↑ Lipolysis: excess FFAs → ectopic fat → worsens insulin resistance + β cell lipotoxicity
  • 8. Brain dysfunction: impaired satiety, altered food reward
Insulin Resistance Mechanisms
  • FFA pathway: ↑ FFAs → diacylglycerol → PKC → serine phosphorylation of IRS-1 (instead of tyrosine) → impaired signaling
  • Inflammation: TNF-α, IL-6 from adipose → same IRS-1 impairment
  • Mitochondrial dysfunction: ↓ ATP production → ↑ insulin resistance
Click: Metabolic Syndrome Criteria
NCEP-ATP III: Need ≥3 of 5
• Central obesity (waist >102cm M, >88cm F)
• ↑ TG (>150)
• ↓ HDL (<40M, <50F)
• ↑ BP (>130/85)
• ↑ Fasting glucose (>100)
Islet Changes
  • Islet amyloid: IAPP (amylin) deposits in islets → contributes to β cell failure/toxicity
  • NO insulitis or autoantibodies (unlike T1DM)
Mnemonic: "T2DM = TWO problems: Resistance + β cell failure; TUBBY (obesity), Trickles to hyperglycemia (slow onset)"
🎯Tested: Ominous octet (especially first-phase response loss as earliest sign), insulin resistance mechanisms, metabolic syndrome criteria, islet amyloid, C-peptide HIGH in T2DM
⚠️Traps: "Relative" not absolute insulin deficiency; first-phase loss is earliest (before overt hyperglycemia); SGLT2 threshold ~180 mg/dL; confusing serine vs tyrosine phosphorylation of IRS-1

LO4: DKA vs Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS)

▼
Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)
  • Cause: Absolute insulin deficiency (T1DM usually; T2DM with stress/illness)
  • Triad: hyperglycemia + anion gap metabolic acidosis + ketonemia/ketonuria
  • Pathophysiology: ↓ insulin → ↑ glucagon → ↑ lipolysis → ↑ FFAs → liver oxidizes to acetyl-CoA → ketone bodies
Click: Ketone Bodies & Electrolytes
Ketones: β-hydroxybutyrate (most abundant, measured by blood ketones), acetoacetate, acetone (fruity breath, not acidic)
Glucose: typically 250-500 (not always extremely high)
K⁺: TOTAL BODY depleted BUT serum often HIGH initially (acidosis → K⁺ shifts out of cells; no insulin → K⁺ doesn't enter cells). MUST give K⁺ if <3.5 BEFORE/DURING insulin. NEVER start insulin if K⁺ <3.3!
  • Factitious hyponatremia: high glucose draws water out → lowers Na; correct: add 1.6-2.4 mEq/L Na per 100 mg/dL glucose >100
  • Phosphate depleted (osmotic diuresis)
DKA Treatment
  • IV fluids (NS or LR)
  • CRITICAL: Check K⁺ FIRST — if <3.3, repleted before insulin; if 3.3-3.5, repleted concurrently
  • Insulin drip (after K⁺ assured)
  • Address precipitant
  • Dextrose when glucose <200 (to continue insulin, close gap without hypoglycemia)
  • Resolution: anion gap closure is endpoint (NOT normalization of glucose); HCO3 >15, anion gap normal
Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS)
  • Cause: Relative insulin deficiency (T2DM); sufficient insulin to prevent ketosis, NOT to control glucose
  • Glucose: EXTREMELY HIGH (>600, often >1000 mg/dL)
  • NO or mild ketosis (some insulin still present in T2DM)
  • Severe dehydration: very high osmolality (>320 mOsm/kg) → altered mental status, coma
  • Osmolality calc: 2(Na) + Glucose/18 + BUN/2.8
HHS Treatment
  • Aggressive fluid replacement (hypotonic fluids after initial NS)
  • Insulin (cautious — less urgent than DKA)
  • Careful electrolyte monitoring
  • Mortality: ~5-20% (elderly, comorbidities)

Mnemonic: "DKA = Diabetic Kids Are ketotic (young, T1DM, ketones, acidosis); HHS = Hyperglycemic Horrible State (elderly, T2DM, no ketones, osmolar coma)"
⚠️Critical Trap: K⁺ in DKA — total body DEPLETED but serum HIGH initially (acidosis). When you give insulin → K⁺ shifts back IN → serum drops → HYPOKALEMIA & cardiac dysrhythmias. MUST replace K⁺ before/during insulin!

LO5: Chronic Complications of Diabetes

▼
Mechanisms of Hyperglycemic Damage
  • 1. Non-enzymatic glycation → AGEs: bind RAGE receptors → ↑ oxidative stress, inflammation, vascular damage; HbA1c is an AGE
  • 2. Polyol pathway: aldose reductase converts glucose → sorbitol (osmotically active, accumulates in lens/nerves/retina); ↓ NADPH → ↓ antioxidants
  • 3. PKC activation: ↑ DAG → PKC → ↑ VEGF → neovascularization (retinopathy), ↑ permeability (nephropathy)
  • 4. Hexosamine pathway: glucose → glucosamine → O-GlcNAc modification → altered transcription → vascular dysfunction
Microvascular Complications
Click: Diabetic Retinopathy
Leading cause of blindness in working-age adults
Background (NPDR): microaneurysms (FIRST SIGN), dot-blot hemorrhages, hard exudates (lipid), cotton-wool spots (nerve infarcts)
Proliferative (PDR): neovascularization (VEGF-driven), vitreous hemorrhage, tractional retinal detachment
Diabetic macular edema: any stage; central vision loss
Tx: tight glucose control, annual exams, laser photocoagulation, anti-VEGF (bevacizumab/ranibizumab)
Click: Diabetic Nephropathy
Leading cause of ESRD in US
Stages: microalbuminuria (30-300 mg/day) → macroalbuminuria (>300) → ↓ GFR → ESRD
Histology: Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules (PATHOGNOMONIC — nodular glomerulosclerosis, mesangial PAS+ nodules); GBM thickening + mesangial expansion
Tx: RAAS blockade (ACEi/ARB reduce intraglomerular pressure), SGLT2 inhibitors (reduce hyperfiltration, cardioprotective)
Screening: urine UACR annually
Click: Diabetic Neuropathy
MOST COMMON chronic complication
Distal symmetric sensorimotor: "stocking-glove" distribution; vibration loss FIRST (large fiber) → pain/temp (small fiber) → motor; ↑ risk foot ulcers, Charcot joint
Autonomic: gastroparesis (delayed gastric emptying → erratic glucose; treat with metoclopramide/erythromycin), orthostatic hypotension, erectile dysfunction (MOST COMMON autonomic manifestation), neurogenic bladder, sudomotor dysfunction
Mononeuropathy/CN palsies: CN III most common (pupil SPARED — unlike aneurysm; diabetes damages central fibers due to vasa nervorum ischemia; pupillomotor fibers on outside)
Macrovascular Complications
  • CAD (MI): most common cause of death in T2DM; often "silent MI" (autonomic neuropathy → no chest pain)
  • Stroke (CVA): ↑ ischemic stroke risk
  • PAD (peripheral arterial disease): claudication, foot ulcers, gangrene
Mnemonic: "Microvascular = Retina, Renal, neuropatHy (3 Rs + H); Macrovascular = CAD, CVA, PVD"
🎯Tested: Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules (specific to diabetic nephropathy), microaneurysms as first sign of retinopathy, autonomic neuropathy manifestations (erectile dysfunction most common), CN III palsy with spared pupil
⚠️Traps: CN III palsy in diabetes has spared pupil (central ischemia) vs aneurysm where pupil is involved (outer compression); forgetting erectile dysfunction is MOST COMMON autonomic manifestation, not gastroparesis

LO6: Metabolic Syndrome, Insulin Resistance & Diabetic Foot

▼
Metabolic Syndrome Pathophysiology
  • Cluster of insulin resistance features → ↑ cardiovascular risk
  • Visceral fat pathology: ↑ FFAs + adipokines (TNF-α ↑, IL-6 ↑, leptin ↑, adiponectin ↓) → hepatic glucose production + systemic insulin resistance
  • Adiponectin: anti-inflammatory, insulin-sensitizing; DECREASED in obesity/T2DM → therapeutic target
  • Leptin resistance: satiety hormone impaired; obesity paradoxically has high leptin but resistance
Diabetic Foot Syndrome
  • Integration of complications: neuropathy + PAD + immune dysfunction
  • Neuropathy: ↓ protective sensation → unnoticed trauma → ulcers
  • PAD: poor wound healing → chronic ulcers → gangrene
  • Immune dysfunction: ↑ susceptibility to infection → polymicrobial osteomyelitis
  • Charcot foot: neuropathic arthropathy; joint collapse → rocker-bottom deformity
  • Prevention: daily foot inspection, proper footwear, regular podiatry, glucose control
  • Most common cause of non-traumatic lower limb amputation
Lipotoxicity & Glucotoxicity
  • Lipotoxicity: excess FFAs in β cells → ceramide → apoptosis
  • Glucotoxicity: chronic hyperglycemia → oxidative stress → β cell dysfunction
  • Vicious cycle: both worsen diabetes progression
HbA1c (Glycosylated Hemoglobin)
Click: HbA1c Basics
Reflects average glucose over 2-3 months (RBC lifespan ~120 days)
Correlates with complications risk
Target: <7% for most adults
TRAPS (when unreliable):
• Hemolytic anemia → shorter RBC life → falsely LOW HbA1c
• Sickle cell → falsely LOW
• Iron deficiency → older RBCs → falsely HIGH
• Pregnancy → more RBC turnover → falsely LOW
Click: Diabetes Screening Criteria
Diagnosis (need confirmation by repeat test):
• HbA1c ≥6.5%
• Fasting glucose ≥126 mg/dL
• 2-hr glucose ≥200 on OGTT
• Random glucose ≥200 WITH symptoms
Mnemonic: "HbA1c = Hemoglobin Archives glucose for 3 months (Average blood Accuracy)"
🎯Tested: Metabolic syndrome criteria (≥3 of 5 features), adiponectin as protective hormone, HbA1c as 2-3 month average, screening thresholds, TRAPS (hemolysis falsely lowers, iron deficiency falsely raises)
⚠️Traps: HbA1c falsely LOW in hemolytic anemia/sickle cell (shorter RBC life); falsely HIGH in iron deficiency (older RBCs); forgetting to repeat test for diagnosis confirmation

MCQ Practice (18 Questions)

▼
1. A 24-year-old presents with polyuria, polydipsia, and weight loss over 2 weeks. Labs show glucose 450 mg/dL, pH 7.28, HCO3 15, and blood ketones positive. Which of the following is MOST specific for confirming the diagnosis of Type 1 DM versus factitious hypoglycemia?
  • A) Serum glucose >250 mg/dL
  • B) C-peptide <0.6 ng/mL
  • C) Positive ketones
  • D) Metabolic acidosis with anion gap
  • E) HbA1c >9%
B) C-peptide <0.6 ng/mL — C-peptide is EQUIMOLAR with endogenous insulin (cleaved from proinsulin). In T1DM, C-peptide is suppressed (absolute deficiency). In factitious hypoglycemia from exogenous insulin injection, C-peptide is LOW (no endogenous secretion). In insulinoma, C-peptide is HIGH. This distinguishes true autoimmune insulin deficiency.
2. Which of the following autoantibodies is the MOST SENSITIVE marker of ongoing autoimmunity in Type 1 DM?
  • A) Anti-IA-2
  • B) Anti-GAD65
  • C) Anti-insulin (IAA)
  • D) Anti-ZnT8
  • E) Islet cell antibodies (ICA)
B) Anti-GAD65 — Anti-GAD65 (glutamic acid decarboxylase) is the MOST COMMON and MOST SENSITIVE autoantibody in T1DM. Present in ~90% of newly diagnosed T1DM. Most useful for screening and early detection.
3. A 52-year-old obese man with T2DM presents with fatigue. His fasting glucose is 145 mg/dL despite metformin monotherapy. Which of the following is the EARLIEST defect in T2DM pathophysiology?
  • A) Hepatic glucose overproduction
  • B) Loss of first-phase insulin response
  • C) Impaired GLP-1 secretion
  • D) α cell dysfunction
  • E) Elevated renal glucose threshold
B) Loss of first-phase insulin response — Loss of the first-phase (rapid 1-3 min) insulin response is the EARLIEST sign of β cell failure in T2DM. This occurs before overt hyperglycemia develops, even before increased fasting glucose. This precedes hepatic glucose overproduction and α cell dysfunction.
4. A 28-year-old with Type 1 DM arrives at the ED with DKA. Labs: glucose 380, pH 7.15, HCO3 10, K+ 6.2. Which is the CORRECT next step in management?
  • A) Start high-dose insulin drip immediately
  • B) Administer dextrose IV to lower glucose osmotically
  • C) Repleted K+ to >3.5, then start insulin
  • D) Give bicarbonate to correct acidosis
  • E) Check for hypomagnesemia first
C) Replete K+ to >3.5, then start insulin — CRITICAL: In DKA, total body K+ is DEPLETED (osmotic diuresis + vomiting). Serum K+ is HIGH initially (acidosis shifts K+ OUT of cells; lack of insulin prevents K+ uptake). When insulin is given, K+ shifts BACK INTO cells → HYPOKALEMIA → cardiac dysrhythmias, respiratory failure. MUST give K+ BEFORE or CONCURRENTLY with insulin (if K+ 3.3-3.5). NEVER start insulin if K+ <3.3!
5. A 68-year-old with T2DM is admitted with glucose 1100 mg/dL, osmolality 340 mOsm/kg, pH 7.35, HCO3 18. Ketones are negative. Which diagnosis best explains this presentation?
  • A) Diabetic ketoacidosis
  • B) Hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state (HHS)
  • C) Mixed DKA-HHS
  • D) Hypoglycemia with counterregulation
  • E) Non-ketotic hyperosmolar coma
B) Hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state (HHS) — HHS: EXTREMELY HIGH glucose (>600, often >1000), NO ketones (sufficient insulin in T2DM prevents ketosis), VERY HIGH osmolality (>320), minimal/no acidosis, usually T2DM patient, older age. DKA has ketones and acidosis; HHS does NOT.
6. A 35-year-old woman presenting with polydipsia and weight loss. Labs: glucose 320, anti-GAD65 positive, C-peptide 2.1 ng/mL (normal 0.8-3.1). She does NOT require insulin at diagnosis. What is this patient's diagnosis?
  • A) Type 1 DM
  • B) Type 2 DM
  • C) LADA (Latent Autoimmune Diabetes of Adults)
  • D) MODY
  • E) Secondary diabetes
C) LADA (Latent Autoimmune Diabetes of Adults) — LADA: adult-onset (slow onset), autoimmune (anti-GAD65 positive), initially resembles T2DM (doesn't need insulin at first), eventually becomes insulin-dependent. This is autoimmune T1DM in adults. Distinguishing feature is presence of autoantibodies + initial lack of insulin dependence.
7. In the polyol pathway of diabetic complications, elevated aldose reductase activity converts glucose to sorbitol. Which tissue accumulation of sorbitol is MOST associated with cataract formation?
  • A) Retina
  • B) Lens
  • C) Peripheral nerve
  • D) Kidney glomerulus
  • E) Vascular endothelium
B) Lens — Sorbitol is osmotically active and cannot easily cross cell membranes. In lens, sorbitol accumulates → osmotic influx of water → swelling → cataracts (osmotic cataracts in diabetes). This is the classic manifestation of polyol pathway in diabetic eye disease.
8. A 45-year-old diabetic patient with hypertension complains of erectile dysfunction. Which pathophysiologic mechanism BEST explains this autonomic complication?
  • A) Loss of parasympathetic innervation to penile smooth muscle
  • B) Sympathetic overstimulation
  • C) Hyperglycemia-induced oxidative stress affecting NO synthase and endothelial function
  • D) Aldosterone-mediated sodium retention
  • E) Direct β cell toxicity in penile tissue
C) Hyperglycemia-induced oxidative stress affecting NO synthase and endothelial function — Erectile dysfunction is the MOST COMMON manifestation of diabetic autonomic neuropathy. Mechanism: chronic hyperglycemia → oxidative stress → ↓ NO availability (NO synthase impaired) → impaired vasodilation → erectile dysfunction. Also involves endothelial dysfunction and loss of parasympathetic tone over time.
9. A 58-year-old diabetic presents with acute diplopia and right eye ptosis. Pupil is normal (spared). Which cranial nerve is affected, and what is the pathophysiologic mechanism?
  • A) CN II; demyelination
  • B) CN III; vasa nervorum ischemia (central fiber infarction)
  • C) CN IV; microvascular occlusion
  • D) CN VI; nerve root compression
  • E) CN III; compressive lesion from aneurysm
B) CN III; vasa nervorum ischemia (central fiber infarction) — CRUCIAL DISTINCTION: Diabetic CN III palsy presents with SPARED PUPIL (pupil-sparing), whereas CN III palsy from posterior communicating artery aneurysm involves the PUPIL. Why? Diabetes causes ischemia of the vasa nervorum → central fibers of CN III are damaged (motor fibers). Pupillomotor fibers run on the OUTSIDE of the nerve → spared. Aneurysm compresses the OUTSIDE of nerve → pupil involved.
10. A 50-year-old with T2DM has a urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR) of 45 mg/g (abnormal) and estimated GFR of 88 mL/min. A kidney biopsy shows nodular glomerulosclerosis with PAS-positive material. What is the pathognomonic lesion?
  • A) Segmental sclerosis (focal segmental glomerulosclerosis)
  • B) Wire-loop deposits (lupus nephritis)
  • C) Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules
  • D) Spike-and-dome deposits (membranous nephropathy)
  • E) Crescent formation (crescentic GN)
C) Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules — PATHOGNOMONIC for diabetic nephropathy. These are mesangial nodules of PAS-positive material (hyaline). Occur in ~25% of T1DM and ~10-20% of T2DM patients. Combined with GBM thickening and mesangial expansion, they define diabetic glomerulosclerosis.
11. A 46-year-old diabetic undergoes annual eye exam. Fundoscopy shows multiple small red dots and blot hemorrhages, but NO new vessels. Which stage of diabetic retinopathy is present?
  • A) Proliferative diabetic retinopathy (PDR)
  • B) Non-proliferative diabetic retinopathy (NPDR) / Background retinopathy
  • C) Diabetic macular edema
  • D) Pre-retinopathy (no visible lesions yet)
  • E) Hypertensive retinopathy
B) Non-proliferative diabetic retinopathy (NPDR) / Background retinopathy — NPDR features: microaneurysms (FIRST SIGN of retinopathy), dot-blot hemorrhages, hard exudates (lipid deposits), cotton-wool spots (nerve fiber infarcts). NO new vessel formation. PDR adds neovascularization, vitreous hemorrhage, tractional detachment.
12. Increased free fatty acid (FFA) flux in T2DM promotes insulin resistance via which mechanism?
  • A) Direct inhibition of GLUT4 transporter
  • B) FFAs → DAG → PKC → serine phosphorylation of IRS-1 (instead of tyrosine)
  • C) FFAs compete with glucose for oxidation
  • D) FFAs increase GLP-1 secretion
  • E) FFAs bind to PPAR-γ receptors
B) FFAs → DAG → PKC → serine phosphorylation of IRS-1 (instead of tyrosine) — FFAs are metabolized to diacylglycerol (DAG). DAG activates PKC, which phosphorylates IRS-1 on SERINE (instead of the normal tyrosine). Serine phosphorylation prevents downstream signaling (PI3K/Akt), blocking GLUT4 translocation → insulin resistance. This is a KEY mechanism of FFA-induced insulin resistance.
13. A 55-year-old man with metabolic syndrome is found to have decreased serum adiponectin and elevated TNF-α. What is the expected metabolic consequence?
  • A) Improved insulin sensitivity
  • B) Worsened insulin resistance and systemic inflammation
  • C) Increased β cell proliferation
  • D) Enhanced GLP-1 secretion
  • E) Decreased visceral adiposity
B) Worsened insulin resistance and systemic inflammation — Adiponectin is anti-inflammatory and insulin-sensitizing; DECREASED in obesity/metabolic syndrome. TNF-α is pro-inflammatory and pro-insulin resistance. The combination (↓ adiponectin + ↑ TNF-α) worsens insulin resistance, promotes hepatic glucose production, and increases cardiovascular risk.
14. A 62-year-old woman with diabetes has an HbA1c of 5.8%. However, she has active hemolytic anemia (reticulocyte count 8%). Which of the following is TRUE?
  • A) Her true average glucose is HIGHER than the HbA1c suggests
  • B) Her true average glucose is LOWER than the HbA1c suggests
  • C) HbA1c accurately reflects her glucose control
  • D) Hemolytic anemia does not affect HbA1c
  • E) Her glucose control is excellent based on HbA1c
A) Her true average glucose is HIGHER than the HbA1c suggests — HbA1c reflects average glucose over RBC lifespan (~120 days). In hemolytic anemia, RBC lifespan is SHORTENED → less time for hemoglobin to accumulate glucose → HbA1c is falsely LOW. Her actual glucose control is likely WORSE than HbA1c suggests. This is a TRAP: falsely reassuring HbA1c in hemolysis.
15. A 40-year-old with diabetic neuropathy complains of delayed gastric emptying and erratic blood glucose levels. Which vagal nerve branch is affected?
  • A) Vagal parasympathetic fibers to the stomach (gastroparesis)
  • B) Sympathetic fibers to pancreatic α cells
  • C) Splanchnic nerve autonomic fibers
  • D) Pelvic autonomic fibers
  • E) Somatic sensory nerves to stomach
A) Vagal parasympathetic fibers to the stomach (gastroparesis) — Diabetic gastroparesis is vagal neuropathy → loss of parasympathetic innervation to stomach → delayed gastric emptying → erratic nutrient absorption → unpredictable glucose spikes/dips. Treated with metoclopramide (dopamine antagonist, enhances acetylcholine) or erythromycin (motilin agonist).
16. A 35-year-old with newly diagnosed T2DM presents without overt hyperglycemia. However, an oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) shows an exaggerated 2-hour glucose response despite normal fasting glucose. What pathophysiologic mechanism is MOST likely responsible?
  • A) Impaired incretin effect
  • B) Elevated renal glucose threshold
  • C) Increased hepatic glycogenolysis
  • D) Pancreatic insufficiency
  • E) Autoimmune β cell destruction
A) Impaired incretin effect — The incretin effect accounts for ~50-70% of total insulin secretion in response to oral glucose. GLP-1 and GIP (secreted by gut L and K cells) amplify insulin secretion (glucose-dependent). In early T2DM, incretin response is impaired → blunted insulin secretion in response to oral glucose → 2-hour OGTT glucose ↑. This is part of the "ominous octet."
17. In SGLT2 (sodium-glucose cotransporter 2) dysfunction in T2DM, which of the following occurs?
  • A) SGLT2 expression is DECREASED → lower renal glucose reabsorption → glucose loses in urine
  • B) SGLT2 expression is INCREASED → higher renal glucose reabsorption → kidneys retain more glucose
  • C) SGLT2 dysfunction causes hyperkalemia
  • D) SGLT2 is located in the distal convoluted tubule
  • E) SGLT2 has HIGH affinity for glucose
B) SGLT2 expression is INCREASED → higher renal glucose reabsorption → kidneys retain more glucose — In T2DM, SGLT2 expression is upregulated in the proximal tubule. SGLT2 reabsorbs ~90% of filtered glucose. Increased expression → kidneys reabsorb even more glucose → even when plasma glucose exceeds threshold (~180 mg/dL), kidneys still retain glucose. This contributes to hyperglycemia. SGLT2 inhibitors reduce this reabsorption → increase glucosuria → lower glucose.
18. A 58-year-old with T2DM and iron deficiency anemia has an HbA1c of 7.5%. Which of the following is TRUE?
  • A) Her true average glucose is HIGHER than HbA1c suggests (HbA1c falsely LOW)
  • B) Her true average glucose is LOWER than HbA1c suggests (HbA1c falsely HIGH)
  • C) HbA1c is accurate; iron deficiency does not affect it
  • D) Iron deficiency only affects serum iron, not glucose control
  • E) She should use fructosamine instead of HbA1c
B) Her true average glucose is LOWER than HbA1c suggests (HbA1c falsely HIGH) — In iron deficiency anemia, RBCs are older/more mature (lower reticulocyte count) → hemoglobin has MORE TIME to accumulate glucose → HbA1c is falsely HIGH. Her actual glucose control is BETTER than HbA1c suggests. This is a TRAP: falsely elevated HbA1c can mask good glucose control.

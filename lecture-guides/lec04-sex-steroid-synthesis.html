▼ Cholesterol → Pregnenolone: The Committed Step

⭐ All steroid hormones derive from cholesterol. The first and rate-limiting step is cholesterol transport into the mitochondria.

🔑 Key Players

  • StAR protein (Steroidogenic Acute Regulatory protein): transports cholesterol from outer to inner mitochondrial membrane — rate-limiting step; mutated in congenital lipoid adrenal hyperplasia
  • CYP11A1 (P450scc): cleaves the cholesterol side chain → pregnenolone; located on inner mitochondrial membrane; requires NADPH and O₂
  • Pregnenolone: universal steroid precursor for all steroid hormones (cortisol, aldosterone, sex steroids)

⚠️ TRAP: StAR vs. CYP11A1 — Don't Confuse Them ▼
  • StAR = transport protein (moves cholesterol across membrane); CYP11A1 = the enzyme that cleaves cholesterol to pregnenolone
  • Both are rate-limiting for steroidogenesis, but StAR is regulated acutely (by ACTH/LH) while CYP11A1 is constitutively expressed
  • Congenital lipoid adrenal hyperplasia = StAR deficiency → can't make ANY steroid → life-threatening salt-wasting + sex reversal (46,XY appears female)

▼ Key Enzymes in Sex Steroid Synthesis

⭐ Five enzymes account for virtually all steroid synthesis questions on Step 1: 3β-HSD, CYP17A1, 17β-HSD, aromatase, and 5α-reductase.

🔬 Enzyme Functions

  • 3β-HSD (3β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase): converts Δ5 steroids (pregnenolone, DHEA) → Δ4 steroids (progesterone, androstenedione); expressed in all steroidogenic tissues
  • CYP17A1 (17α-hydroxylase / 17,20-lyase): dual activity — 17α-hydroxylase adds OH at C17 (needed for cortisol); 17,20-lyase cleaves C17-C20 bond (needed for androgens/estrogens); absent in zona glomerulosa (cannot make sex steroids)
  • 17β-HSD: reduces androstenedione → testosterone; reduces estrone → estradiol; bidirectional enzyme
  • Aromatase (CYP19A1): converts androgens (testosterone, androstenedione) → estrogens (E2, E1); expressed in granulosa cells, adipose tissue, liver, brain, bone; inhibited by aromatase inhibitors (anastrozole, letrozole, exemestane)
  • 5α-reductase type 2: converts testosterone → DHT (more potent); expressed in prostate, external genitalia, hair follicles, sebaceous glands; inhibited by finasteride/dutasteride

⭐ HY: Two-Cell, Two-Gonadotropin Model
  • LH → theca cells → androstenedione/testosterone → diffuses to granulosa cells
  • FSH → granulosa cells → upregulates aromatase → converts androgens → estradiol
  • This is the basis of polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS): ↑LH/FSH ratio → excess androgen production, insufficient aromatase → hyperandrogenism + anovulation
⚠️ TRAP: CYP17A1 Deficiency Pattern ▼
  • 17α-hydroxylase deficiency: can't make sex steroids OR cortisol → shunts all precursors to mineralocorticoids → hypertension + hypokalemia
  • 46,XY: female external genitalia (no T/DHT), no pubic/axillary hair, primary amenorrhea
  • 46,XX: female phenotype but primary amenorrhea, no secondary sex characteristics, hypertension
  • BOTH sexes: ↑ACTH (no cortisol negative feedback) → ↑DOC/corticosterone → hypertension + hypokalemia

🧠 Testing Your Understanding!

Testing Your Understanding — LO 1, Question 1

Trace the enzymatic steps from cholesterol to the three major classes of sex steroids (androgens, estrogens, progestogens), naming the key enzymes at each branch point. Which single enzyme is required for both the Δ5 and Δ4 pathways, and what would happen to sex steroid production if it were deficient?

Consider: StAR, CYP11A1, CYP17A1 (17α-hydroxylase / 17,20-lyase), 3β-HSD, aromatase; Δ5 vs. Δ4 pathway; pregnenolone, DHEA, androstenedione, progesterone

✓ Answer: 3β-HSD is required in both pathways

StAR shuttles cholesterol into the mitochondria; CYP11A1 cleaves the side chain to form pregnenolone. 3β-HSD converts pregnenolone→progesterone (Δ4 pathway) AND DHEA→androstenedione (Δ5→Δ4 shift) — making it the key enzyme at both branches. CYP17A1 adds 17α-hydroxylase activity (and 17,20-lyase for androgen production). Aromatase converts androgens to estrogens in granulosa cells and peripheral tissues. A 3β-HSD deficiency would impair all sex steroid synthesis distal to pregnenolone/DHEA, causing ambiguous genitalia in 46,XY individuals and salt-wasting (since mineralocorticoids are also blocked).

Testing Your Understanding — LO 1, Question 2

Why is the StAR protein considered the "rate-limiting step" in steroidogenesis, even though it is not technically an enzyme? What clinical syndrome results from loss-of-function StAR mutations, and why is it more severe than most other enzyme deficiencies?

Consider: inner vs. outer mitochondrial membrane, lipoid CAH, lipid accumulation, CYP11A1 access, adrenal insufficiency

✓ Answer: StAR is the cholesterol transporter; loss causes Lipoid CAH

CYP11A1 resides on the inner mitochondrial membrane but cholesterol arrives from the outer membrane. StAR facilitates rapid transport across this gap — without it, the substrate cannot reach the enzyme even if the enzyme is intact. Loss-of-function StAR mutations cause Lipoid Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia: all steroidogenesis (glucocorticoids, mineralocorticoids, sex steroids) is virtually absent. Cholesterol accumulates and destroys the adrenal cortex over time, making it more catastrophic than partial pathway blocks.

▼ Testosterone Synthesis & Transport

⭐ Testosterone is primarily made in Leydig cells (testes) under LH stimulation; 95% of circulating testosterone in males comes from the testes.

🔬 Synthesis in Leydig Cells

  • LH → Gs → ↑cAMP → PKA → StAR upregulation → cholesterol transport into mitochondria → pregnenolone → … → testosterone
  • Testosterone diffuses into Sertoli cells → binds Androgen Binding Protein (ABP) → high local [T] in seminiferous tubules needed for spermatogenesis
  • Testosterone also aromatized to estradiol in multiple tissues (Sertoli cells, adipose, brain)

🔗 SHBG (Sex Hormone-Binding Globulin)

  • Major binding protein for sex steroids in blood; high affinity for DHT > T > E2
  • Only free (unbound) testosterone is biologically active
  • SHBG ↑: estrogen (OCP, pregnancy, liver disease), hyperthyroidism, aging → less free T available
  • SHBG ↓: androgens, obesity, insulin resistance, hypothyroidism, glucocorticoids → more free T available

  • A woman on OCP may have high SHBG → low free T → symptoms of androgen deficiency despite "normal" total T
  • In PCOS: low SHBG (from insulin resistance) → ↑free T → hyperandrogenism even if total T is borderline

🧠 DHT = "Down There" Tissues

DHT acts on tissues that were important in fetal external genitalia development and postnatal sexual characteristics:

  • Development of external genitalia (fetal)
  • Hair follicles (androgenic alopecia)
  • Tissue of prostate (BPH)

Testosterone handles everything else: muscle, bone, Wolffian ducts, spermatogenesis, libido.

⚠️ TRAP: Finasteride Effects ▼
  • Finasteride blocks 5α-reductase → ↓DHT → shrinks prostate (BPH) + ↓hair loss; testosterone levels INCREASE (less converted to DHT)
  • Side effects: sexual dysfunction (↓libido, ED, ejaculatory disorder), gynecomastia (↑T → ↑aromatization to E2)
  • Post-finasteride syndrome: persistent sexual dysfunction after stopping
  • Dutasteride: blocks both type 1 and type 2 5α-reductase (more complete DHT suppression)

▼ Adrenal Androgens

⭐ Adrenal zona reticularis produces weak androgens (DHEA, DHEAS, androstenedione) under ACTH control; important in females and in adrenarche.

🔬 Adrenal Androgen Overview

  • DHEA (dehydroepiandrosterone): weak androgen from zona reticularis; sulfated to DHEAS for circulation (longer half-life); converted peripherally to testosterone/E2
  • DHEAS: most abundant circulating androgen; made exclusively by adrenal (not gonads); useful marker of adrenal androgen excess vs. gonadal source
  • Androstenedione: made by adrenal AND gonads; converted to testosterone (17β-HSD) and estrone (aromatase) in peripheral tissues
  • Adrenarche: onset of adrenal androgen production (~age 6–8), precedes gonadarche; responsible for pubic/axillary hair, body odor, mild acne
⭐ HY: ↑DHEAS = Adrenal Source of Androgen Excess
  • If a virilized woman has markedly elevated DHEAS → source is adrenal (adrenal tumor, CAH, ectopic ACTH)
  • If DHEAS is normal/mildly elevated → ovarian source more likely (PCOS, ovarian tumor)
  • Postmenopausal women: adrenal androgen → peripheral aromatization → estrone (E1) is main estrogen source

🧠 Testing Your Understanding!

Testing Your Understanding — LO 2, Question 1

A 50-year-old man with benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) is started on finasteride. Predict the changes in serum testosterone and DHT after treatment begins. Which tissues would be most affected by the drop in DHT, and which would be relatively spared? Explain your reasoning.

Consider: 5α-reductase type 2 location, testosterone vs. DHT potency, prostate, seminal vesicles, skin/hair follicles, androgen receptor affinity

✓ Answer: Testosterone rises, DHT falls; prostate/scalp most affected

Finasteride inhibits 5α-reductase type 2. With less testosterone converted to DHT peripherally, serum testosterone rises (reduced clearance/conversion) while DHT falls significantly. Tissues most dependent on DHT (5α-reductase type 2-rich): prostate, seminal vesicles, skin, scalp follicles — these shrink or lose androgenic drive. Tissues that respond to testosterone directly (e.g., muscle, bone, libido via hypothalamic-pituitary axis) are relatively spared, because testosterone itself activates AR (just with ~5× lower affinity than DHT).

Testing Your Understanding — LO 2, Question 2

Explain why individuals with 5α-reductase type 2 deficiency are born with ambiguous or female-appearing genitalia despite having a 46,XY karyotype, yet undergo significant virilization at puberty. What is preserved vs. absent in this syndrome?

Consider: DHT for external genitalia development, testosterone for Wolffian structures, AMH for Müllerian regression, puberty LH surge, peripheral 5α-reductase type 1

✓ Answer: DHT required in utero for external genitalia; testosterone handles Wolffian + puberty virilization

In utero: DHT (via 5α-reductase type 2) drives differentiation of the genital tubercle, urogenital sinus → male external genitalia. Without it: undervirilized/female-appearing genitalia. However, testosterone alone (not DHT) maintains Wolffian structures (epididymis, vas deferens, seminal vesicles) — so these are present. AMH from Sertoli cells regresses Müllerian structures normally → no uterus/tubes. At puberty: the large testosterone surge activates AR directly and some peripheral conversion via type 1 5α-reductase (still intact) drives significant virilization — voice deepening, muscle mass, phallus enlargement.

⚠️ TRAP: Genomic vs. Non-Genomic Estrogen Actions ▼
  • Genomic (classical): E2 enters cell → binds cytoplasmic ER → ER dimerizes → enters nucleus → binds ERE (estrogen response element) → gene transcription (minutes to hours)
  • Non-genomic (rapid): membrane-associated ER → activates kinase cascades (PI3K, MAPK, eNOS) → rapid vasodilation, neuroprotection (seconds to minutes)
  • Most clinical effects are genomic; cardiovascular benefits of estrogen are partly non-genomic

🧠 Testing Your Understanding!

Testing Your Understanding — LO 3, Question 1

A postmenopausal woman with osteoporosis has a first-degree relative with ER-positive breast cancer. She asks about pharmacologic options. Compare the tissue-specific ERα/ERβ effects of tamoxifen vs. raloxifene in breast, bone, and uterus. Which agent is more appropriate for this patient?

Consider: SERM = tissue-selective agonist/antagonist; tamoxifen uterine agonism; raloxifene no uterine stimulation; both protect bone; breast antagonism shared

✓ Answer: Raloxifene is preferred — bone protection + breast antagonism without uterine agonism

Tamoxifen: breast antagonist (✓ cancer prevention), bone agonist (✓ osteoporosis), but uterine agonist (↑ endometrial cancer risk) — not ideal for long-term osteoporosis prevention. Raloxifene: breast antagonist (✓), bone agonist (✓ FDA-approved osteoporosis), uterine neutral (no endometrial stimulation) — preferred for this patient. Both carry DVT/PE risk. Mechanism: SERMs bind ER but recruit different co-activator/co-repressor complexes depending on tissue ER subtype (ERα vs ERβ) and tissue-specific cofactor expression.

Testing Your Understanding — LO 3, Question 2

Explain why estradiol (E2) levels fall dramatically at menopause while estrone (E1) remains detectable. What is the primary source of each estrogen in postmenopausal women, and how does this shift have clinical implications?

Consider: follicular estradiol production (gone at menopause), peripheral aromatization of adrenal androgens, adipose tissue aromatase, obesity, endometrial cancer risk

✓ Answer: E2 from ovarian follicles ceases; E1 from peripheral aromatization persists

Premenopause: granulosa cells are the dominant source of E2 (most potent estrogen). At menopause, follicular depletion eliminates ovarian E2 production. Postmenopause: adrenal glands still make androstenedione; peripheral aromatase (especially in adipose tissue) converts it to estrone (E1) — a weaker estrogen but still bioactive. Clinical implication: obese postmenopausal women have higher E1 levels (more adipose aromatase), increasing risk for estrogen-sensitive cancers (endometrial, breast). This is why aromatase inhibitors (anastrozole, letrozole) are effective in postmenopausal ER+ breast cancer.

▼ Corpus Luteum & Progesterone Synthesis

⭐ The corpus luteum is the ruptured follicle transformed by the LH surge into a progesterone-secreting structure; it survives for ~14 days unless rescued by hCG.

🔬 Formation & Life Cycle

  • LH surge → ovulation → follicle ruptures → granulosa and theca cells luteinize → corpus luteum (CL)
  • CL secretes: progesterone (dominant), estradiol, and inhibin A
  • Progesterone peaks ~day 21 (mid-luteal phase) of 28-day cycle
  • Without pregnancy: CL involutes after ~14 days (luteolysis) → ↓P4, ↓E2 → menstruation
  • With pregnancy: embryo secretes hCG starting ~week 1–2 → hCG binds LH receptors → CL rescued → continued P4 production until placenta takes over (~weeks 8–10)

🧠 Testing Your Understanding!

Testing Your Understanding — LO 4, Question 1

During early pregnancy, what hormone "rescues" the corpus luteum and maintains progesterone production? At what gestational age does this become less critical, and what takes over? If the corpus luteum were surgically removed at 7 weeks gestation, what would you expect to happen — and why?

Consider: hCG vs. LH receptor, luteal-placental shift (~weeks 8–10), progesterone withdrawal, first-trimester miscarriage risk, progesterone supplementation

✓ Answer: hCG rescues corpus luteum; placenta takes over by week 8–10; removal at 7 weeks → likely miscarriage without supplementation

hCG (from syncytiotrophoblast) mimics LH, binding LH/hCG receptors on the corpus luteum to sustain progesterone secretion through week ~8–10. This is the luteal-placental shift — after which the placenta produces sufficient progesterone independently (placenta has its own CYP11A1 and 3β-HSD; it uses fetal/maternal cholesterol). At 7 weeks, the shift is not complete — removing the corpus luteum causes a progesterone drop → uterine contractility, implantation loss → spontaneous abortion. Treatment: exogenous progesterone supplementation until the placenta is self-sufficient (~10–12 weeks).

Testing Your Understanding — LO 4, Question 2

Describe the role of progesterone during the secretory phase of the menstrual cycle. Why does progesterone withdrawal at the end of the luteal phase trigger menstruation? How does this mechanism differ from the anovulatory cycle?

Consider: endometrial glandular development, spiral artery vasospasm, prostaglandin release, corpus luteum lifespan (14 days), anovulation = no corpus luteum = no progesterone withdrawal

✓ Answer: Progesterone primes secretory endometrium; withdrawal → prostaglandin-mediated shedding

During the secretory phase: progesterone (from corpus luteum) transforms the estrogen-primed endometrium — stimulates glandular secretion, inhibits myometrial contractions, reduces LH/FSH via negative feedback. At day 28: corpus luteum involutes → progesterone/estrogen fall → endometrial stromal cells release prostaglandins (PGF2α) → spiral artery vasospasm → ischemia → menstrual shedding. In anovulatory cycles: no ovulation → no corpus luteum → no progesterone. Estrogen continues unopposed → endometrium proliferates continuously (→ risk of hyperplasia/carcinoma) and menstruation becomes irregular/heavy due to lack of organized withdrawal.

▼ 5α-Reductase Type 2 Deficiency

⭐ 46,XY with female external genitalia at birth who virilize at puberty — the "guevedoces" (Dominican Republic) — classic USMLE scenario.

🔬 Mechanism & Phenotype

  • Karyotype: 46,XY
  • Mechanism: Can't convert testosterone → DHT → DHT-dependent structures underdeveloped at birth
  • Internal genitalia: NORMAL male (testes, epididymis, vas deferens, seminal vesicles) — testosterone-dependent Wolffian structures intact; AMH present → no uterus/tubes
  • External genitalia at birth: Female (or ambiguous) — small/micropenis resembling clitoris, labioscrotal folds resemble labia
  • At puberty: LH surge → massive ↑testosterone → partial virilization (penis growth, voice deepening, muscle mass, testes descend)
  • Labs: ↑Testosterone:DHT ratio (>30:1 is diagnostic); ↓DHT; normal testosterone; normal LH/FSH (feedback intact)
  • Management: Gender assignment; testosterone (if male-identified); cosmetic/surgical correction; counseling
⚠️ TRAP: 5α-Reductase Deficiency vs. CAIS ▼
  • Both are 46,XY with female external genitalia at birth — differentiate by what happens at puberty
  • 5α-reductase deficiency: VIRILIZES at puberty (↑testosterone works), breast development minimal
  • CAIS: does NOT virilize at puberty (AR dysfunctional), gets breast development (aromatization to E2 still works), sparse/absent pubic + axillary hair
  • Lab key: 5α-reductase deficiency has HIGH T:DHT ratio; CAIS has high T AND high LH (resistance)

▼ Complete Androgen Insensitivity Syndrome (CAIS)

⭐ 46,XY with completely female phenotype, absent pubic/axillary hair, amenorrhea, and intra-abdominal testes — caused by androgen receptor (AR) mutation.

🔬 Mechanism & Features

  • Karyotype: 46,XY; X-linked recessive (AR gene mutation)
  • Mechanism: Androgen receptor dysfunctional → body cannot respond to testosterone OR DHT
  • Internal genitalia: Absent uterus/fallopian tubes (AMH from Sertoli cells = intact, works fine); absent vas deferens/epididymis (testosterone-dependent Wolffian structures require AR → can't develop)
  • External genitalia: Female (clitoris, labia); blind vaginal pouch (no uterus)
  • Puberty: Breast development (testosterone aromatized to E2 → ER intact); absent or sparse pubic + axillary hair (androgen-dependent); primary amenorrhea
  • Labs: ↑Testosterone (high-normal male range), ↑LH (AR resistance → no feedback inhibition), normal FSH, no E2 rise from ovaries (none), high T → aromatized to E2
  • Gonads: Undescended testes (in labia or inguinal canal); risk of malignancy (gonadoblastoma, dysgerminoma) — recommend removal after puberty (allow feminization first)
  • Diagnosis: Karyotype (46,XY); absent uterus on pelvic ultrasound; ↑testosterone with female phenotype; molecular AR gene sequencing
  • Management: Gonadectomy after puberty; estrogen replacement; vaginal dilation/surgery; psychological support; gender identity counseling
⭐ HY: Why Does CAIS Get Breast Development?
  • Testes produce testosterone normally → testosterone is aromatized → estradiol (E2)
  • Estrogen receptor (ER) is intact → E2 acts on breast tissue → gynecomastia/feminization
  • No androgen competition to oppose estrogen (AR dysfunctional) → complete feminization
  • This is why "testicular feminization syndrome" was the old name

Testing Your Understanding — LO 5, Question 1

A 16-year-old presents with primary amenorrhea, absent pubic and axillary hair, well-developed breasts, a blind vaginal pouch, no uterus on ultrasound, karyotype 46,XY, and serum testosterone in the male-normal range with elevated LH. Identify the diagnosis and explain the underlying pathophysiology — specifically why this individual has testes, no uterus, female breast development, and absent body hair.

Consider: CAIS vs. 5α-reductase deficiency, androgen receptor mutation, AMH action, testosterone-to-estrogen aromatization in peripheral tissue, LH feedback

✓ Answer: Complete Androgen Insensitivity Syndrome (CAIS)

CAIS = loss-of-function mutation in the androgen receptor (AR). Testes are present (SRY present, gonadal differentiation normal) and produce testosterone + AMH normally. AMH regresses Müllerian structures (no uterus, no upper vagina). However, AR is nonfunctional — so testosterone cannot masculinize Wolffian structures or external genitalia → female-appearing phenotype. Peripheral aromatization of testosterone → estradiol drives breast development (estrogen receptor is intact). Absent body hair because hair follicles require functional AR for androgen-driven growth. LH is elevated because testosterone cannot exert negative feedback at the hypothalamus/pituitary. Distinguished from 5α-reductase deficiency by: no virilization at puberty (AR is broken), breast development (aromatization), completely absent body hair.

Testing Your Understanding — LO 5, Question 2

Compare 21-hydroxylase deficiency (the most common form of CAH) to 11β-hydroxylase deficiency. Both affect cortisol synthesis — why does 21-hydroxylase deficiency cause salt-wasting while 11β-hydroxylase deficiency causes hypertension? Trace the steroidogenic pathways to explain.

Consider: CYP21A2 block → no aldosterone or cortisol; ACTH → androgen shunting; 11β-hydroxylase block → 11-deoxycorticosterone (DOC) accumulates; mineralocorticoid effect of DOC

✓ Answer: 21-OHase → no aldosterone (salt-wasting); 11β-OHase → DOC accumulates (hypertension)

21-Hydroxylase (CYP21A2) deficiency: blocks conversion of progesterone→11-deoxycorticosterone AND 17-OH-progesterone→11-deoxycortisol. Result: no cortisol, no aldosterone → salt-wasting (↑renin, hyperkalemia), hypotension, adrenal crisis. ACTH rises (no cortisol feedback) → steroid shunting into androgen pathway → virilization (clitoromegaly in 46,XX). 11β-Hydroxylase (CYP11B1) deficiency: blocks conversion of 11-deoxycorticosterone (DOC)→corticosterone AND 11-deoxycortisol→cortisol. DOC accumulates and has mineralocorticoid activity → Na retention, hypokalemia, hypertension. Aldosterone is also low (ACTH drives DOC accumulation but not aldosterone synthesis directly), yet the excess DOC compensates mineralocorticoid function — so patients are hypertensive rather than salt-wasting.

In IVF protocols, GnRH antagonist (or agonist) suppresses pituitary LH to prevent premature ovulation. The corpus luteum requires LH (or hCG) stimulation to sustain progesterone output. With pituitary suppressed, LH is absent → corpus luteum undergoes early luteolysis → progesterone falls → endometrial support fails → implantation failure. Exogenous progesterone bridges this gap until the developing embryo produces hCG and eventually the placenta becomes steroidogenically autonomous (luteal-placental shift ~weeks 8–10). Luteal phase support is standard of care for ALL IVF cycles using GnRH analogues.

A 2-week-old neonate with 46,XX karyotype presents with ambiguous genitalia (clitoromegaly and labial fusion) and a salt-wasting crisis (hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, hypotension). Labs: ↑17-OH-progesterone, ↓cortisol, ↓aldosterone. Which enzyme is deficient?

A) 11β-hydroxylase

B) 17α-hydroxylase

C) 21-hydroxylase

D) 3β-HSD

E) StAR protein

✓ Correct Answer: C — 21-Hydroxylase

Classic presentation of salt-wasting congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH) due to 21-hydroxylase deficiency. The enzyme block: ↑17-OH-progesterone (substrate accumulation → diagnostic marker), ↓cortisol → ↑ACTH → shunts precursors to androgens (virilization of 46,XX) + ↓aldosterone → salt-wasting crisis. The elevated androgens in utero cause virilization of XX external genitalia. 11β-hydroxylase deficiency also causes CAH with virilization but causes hypertension (not salt-wasting) from ↑DOC. 17α-hydroxylase causes hypertension, no virilization.

Question 13

A 17-year-old assigned female at birth presents with primary amenorrhea, normal breast development, and completely absent pubic and axillary hair. Karyotype: 46,XY. Pelvic ultrasound: no uterus, gonads in inguinal canal. Testosterone: 680 ng/dL (male range). LH: markedly elevated. What is the most important next management step after confirming the diagnosis?

A) Start testosterone replacement immediately

B) Begin GnRH pulsatile therapy for fertility

C) Plan gonadectomy to prevent malignancy, then estrogen replacement

D) Perform vaginal dilation immediately and obtain uterine transplant

E) Refer for 5α-reductase inhibitor therapy

✓ Correct Answer: C — Plan gonadectomy to prevent malignancy, then estrogen replacement

This is CAIS. The undescended testes in CAIS carry a significant risk of malignancy (gonadoblastoma, dysgerminoma) — estimated 3–22% lifetime risk. Standard management: allow puberty to complete naturally (the testes are the source of aromatizable testosterone → estrogen → breast development and bone density), THEN perform gonadectomy. After removal: lifelong estrogen replacement to maintain bone density and quality of life. Vaginal dilation may be needed (blind vaginal pouch) for sexual function. Genetic counseling for family members (X-linked recessive). Fertility is not possible (no uterus/ovaries).

Question 14

A 14-year-old 46,XY patient with known 5α-reductase type 2 deficiency was raised as female. At puberty, he begins to virilize with penile growth, voice deepening, and testicular descent. Which finding would be expected on labs?

A) Low testosterone, high DHT, high LH

B) High testosterone, low DHT, high testosterone:DHT ratio

C) Low testosterone, low DHT, low LH

D) High testosterone, high DHT, normal LH

E) Low testosterone, high LH, high FSH (hypergonadotropic)

✓ Correct Answer: B — High testosterone, low DHT, high testosterone:DHT ratio

5α-reductase type 2 deficiency: testosterone is produced normally by Leydig cells but cannot be converted to DHT in target tissues. At puberty: LH surge → high testosterone → virilization via testosterone's direct androgen receptor activity (AR works; it's the 5α-reductase that's broken). Labs: ↑testosterone, ↓DHT, ↑T:DHT ratio (>30:1 diagnostic). LH may be slightly elevated (partial AR resistance feedback). In CAIS, the AR itself is broken → no virilization at puberty + very high LH (complete AR resistance). The distinguishing feature: 5α-reductase deficiency VIRILIZES at puberty; CAIS does NOT.

Question 4

A 15-year-old 46,XY patient raised female presents with primary amenorrhea and bilateral inguinal masses confirmed to be testes. The endocrinologist recommends eventual gonadectomy. What is the PRIMARY indication for removing the testes?

A) Testes produce virilizing testosterone levels that will cause progressive masculinization

B) Undescended testes in CAIS carry significantly elevated risk of gonadoblastoma/dysgerminoma

C) Testes produce excess estrogen, increasing endometrial hyperplasia risk

D) Testosterone suppresses pituitary LH, causing permanent hypogonadotropic hypogonadism

E) Testes interfere with exogenous estrogen receptor signaling needed for further feminization

✓ Correct Answer: B — Undescended testes have elevated gonadoblastoma/dysgerminoma risk

In CAIS, undescended (intra-abdominal or inguinal) testes carry ~2–3% lifetime risk of germ cell malignancy (gonadoblastoma in childhood, seminoma/dysgerminoma in adulthood). Question 5

A 46,XX newborn presents with clitoromegaly, posterior labial fusion, and salt-wasting crisis on day 7 of life. Serum 17-hydroxyprogesterone is markedly elevated. Which enzyme is deficient, and why does 17-OHP accumulate?

A) 11β-hydroxylase (CYP11B1); cannot convert 11-deoxycortisol → cortisol, causing 17-OHP to back up

B) 21-hydroxylase (CYP21A2); cannot convert 17-OHP → 11-deoxycortisol, accumulating 17-OHP and shunting to androgens

C) 3β-HSD; cannot convert 17-OH-pregnenolone → 17-OHP, causing paradoxical 17-OHP accumulation

D) 17α-hydroxylase (CYP17A1); cannot add the 17-OH group, so 17-OHP cannot form

E) StAR protein; cholesterol accumulation cross-reacts with 17-OHP immunoassay

✓ Correct Answer: B — 21-Hydroxylase (CYP21A2) deficiency

21-Hydroxylase (CYP21A2) is required for: (1) 17-OHP → 11-deoxycortisol → cortisol; (2) Progesterone → deoxycorticosterone → aldosterone. Without CYP21A2: 17-OHP accumulates (pathognomonic marker; detected on newborn screening); ACTH rises (absent cortisol feedback) → substrate shunted into androgen pathway (→ androstenedione → testosterone → DHT) → virilization of 46,XX fetus. Absent aldosterone → salt-wasting (hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, hypotension, dehydration). Compare: 11β-hydroxylase deficiency also causes virilization + elevated 17-OHP (mildly) but causes hypertension (DOC accumulates → mineralocorticoid activity) rather than salt-wasting.

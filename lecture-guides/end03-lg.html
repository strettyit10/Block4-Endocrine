1GH signaling pathways — hypothalamus to pituitary to liver to tissues

The Gist Growth hormone (GH) is a 191-AA protein released from acidophilic somatotropes of the anterior pituitary. It acts on nearly all cells in the body — directly (via the GH receptor on muscle, adipose, and liver) AND indirectly (via IGF-1 / somatomedin C, which liver hepatocytes release in response to GH). Hypothalamic control is dual: GHRH stimulates, somatostatin (GHIH) inhibits. GH uses a JAK-STAT receptor; IGF-1 uses a distinct RTK receptor. GH has high homology to prolactin and can bind the PRL receptor.

📚 Going Deeper — every step of the pathway

1 · Why GH signaling has a hypothalamus-pituitary-liver-tissue cascade

Three levels of amplification and control
  • Level 1 — Hypothalamic control:
    • GHRH (stimulates) and somatostatin (inhibits) are co-released from separate hypothalamic neurons.end03 lecture
    • They converge on the same signaling pathway (cAMP) in somatotropes, but in opposite directions.FA 3.02 Growth hormone
  • Level 2 — Pituitary integration:
    • Somatotropes release GH in pulses when GHRH dominates.end03 lecture
    • Somatostatin sharpens the pulses by cutting off secretion between peaks.
  • Level 3 — Hepatic IGF-1 amplification:
    • Most of GH's growth effects are mediated by IGF-1, not GH directly.FA 3.02 GH; B&B 5.01
    • IGF-1 half-life is ~36× longer than GH, creating sustained anabolic signaling.
  • Level 4 — Target tissue response:
    • IGF-1 acts on bone, cartilage, muscle, and most cell types via the IGF1-R (RTK).
    • GH also has direct effects on muscle and adipose via the GH-R (JAK-STAT).

2 · GHRH and somatostatin — opposing pathways that converge

GHRH (Growth Hormone-Releasing Hormone)
  • Structure:
    • 44-AA peptide from the ventromedial and arcuate nuclei.FA 3.01 Hypothalamic-pituitary hormones
  • Signal transduction:
    • Binds a Gs-coupled GPCR on somatotropes.
    • ↑ cAMP → PKA → Ca²⁺ influx → vesicle fusion → GH release.
Somatostatin (SS / GHIH)
  • Structure:
    • 14- or 28-AA cyclic peptide from the periventricular nucleus.FA 3.01
  • Signal transduction:
    • Binds a Gi-coupled GPCR on somatotropes.
    • ↓ cAMP → suppresses GH release.
  • Clinical use:
    • Octreotide and lanreotide are somatostatin analogs used to suppress GH in acromegaly.AI Generated · see end13

3 · GH receptor & JAK-STAT — how GH induces IGF-1

Receptor mechanism
  • Structure:
    • GH-R is a single-pass transmembrane receptor with no intrinsic kinase activity.FA 3.14 Signaling pathways; B&B 5.06
    • GH binds → receptor dimerizes → recruits JAK2 kinases.
  • Downstream signaling:
    • JAK2 autophosphorylates and phosphorylates receptor cytoplasmic tails.
    • STAT5 binds phosphorylated receptor, gets phosphorylated, dimerizes, translocates to nucleus.
    • STAT5 binds DNA → induces IGF-1 transcription.FA 3.02 Growth hormone
Other hormones that use JAK-STAT
  • Acidophilic pituitary hormones:
    • GH and prolactin — both from acidophils.
  • Hematopoietic hormones:
    • EPO and TPO.
  • Cytokines:
    • Most interleukins, interferons, leptin.FA 3.14 Signaling pathways

4 · IGF-1 and the somatomedin family

  • IGF-1 (somatomedin C):
    • 70-AA peptide, mostly from the liver.end03 lecture
    • Stimulates mitogenic + anabolic processes + cell survival in nearly all cell types.
  • IGF-1 receptor:
    • Distinct from GH receptor.
    • Receptor tyrosine kinase (RTK) — structurally related to insulin receptor.
    • Activates MAPK (growth) and PI3K-AKT (survival, metabolism) pathways.FA 3.02 Growth hormone; FA 3.14 Signaling
  • Other somatomedins:
    • IGF-2 (somatomedin A) and others play roles in fetal growth.
    • IGF-1 is by far the most studied and clinically important.

5 · Carrier proteins — GHBP and IGFBP

  • GHBP (GH-binding protein):
    • Binds ~50% of circulating GH.
    • Structurally, GHBP is the extracellular domain of the GH receptor.end03 lecture
    • Small GH molecule otherwise freely filtered by the kidney — binding slows this.
    • Even with GHBP, GH half-life remains only ~20 minutes — hence its pulsatile nature.
  • IGFBP (IGF-binding proteins):
    • 6 subtypes (IGFBP-1 through IGFBP-6); IGFBP-3 is most common.
    • Nearly all IGF-1 circulates bound.
    • Binding greatly extends half-life to ~12 hours.end03 lecture; FA 3.02 GH
  • IGFBP proteases:
    • Tissue-level proteases cleave IGFBP → free IGF-1 → activates local IGF1-R.
    • Allows targeted release despite the long-circulating bound pool.

6 · GH-PRL cross-reactivity

  • Homology:
    • GH and prolactin share a common ancestor and have high sequence homology.end03 lecture
  • Receptor cross-binding:
    • GH can bind the prolactin receptor with decent affinity.
    • Explains why GH-secreting adenomas can cause galactorrhea even without coexisting hyperprolactinemia.AI Generated · see end08 pituitary adenomas
  • Human placental lactogen (hPL):
    • Placental hormone structurally similar to GH and PRL — GH-like effects during pregnancy.FA 3.01 Hypothalamic-pituitary hormones

2Physiological effects of GH and IGF-1

The Gist GH has a dual personality. Directly, it's anti-insulin: it spares glucose (raises blood sugar), drives lipolysis, and causes insulin resistance in skeletal muscle and adipose. Indirectly (via IGF-1 from the liver), it drives tissue growth — linear bone growth, protein anabolism, organ growth, cell survival. The balance depends on nutritional state: fed + balanced meal → growth; fasting or carb-heavy → glucose-sparing + lipolysis. Insulin is permissive for GH → IGF-1 because it drives hepatic GH-R expression. GH's chronic effect is diabetogenic.

📚 Going Deeper — every tissue, every fuel state

1 · Direct vs. indirect effects in detail

Why this dual system exists
  • Physiologic logic:
    • Direct effects handle acute metabolic needs (glucose/fat).
    • Indirect effects drive chronic growth and repair.
    • The split lets GH do "short-term fuel management" AND "long-term growth" from one hormone.end03 lecture

2 · The diabetogenic effect

Mechanism of GH-induced insulin resistance
  • Where it happens:
    • Skeletal muscle and adipose tissue.end03 lecture
  • What GH does:
    • Reduces insulin sensitivity in these tissues.
    • Decreases glucose uptake and increases lipolysis.
    • Net effect: glucose is preserved for the brain; fat is burned for energy.
  • Chronic overexposure:
    • Prolonged GH excess → sustained insulin resistance → clinically evident diabetes mellitus.
    • Classic feature of acromegaly.FA 3.02 GH; Bootcamp 3.04 Acromegaly

3 · Tissue-specific effects

Liver
  • Via GH (direct):
    • ↑ gluconeogenesis → raises serum glucose.
    • ↑ IGF-1 production (JAK-STAT → STAT5 → IGF-1 transcription).FA 3.02 GH
  • Insulin's permissive role:
    • Insulin drives GH-R expression on hepatocytes.
    • Without insulin (uncontrolled T1DM), GH → IGF-1 is blunted → growth failure in children.end03 lecture
Adipose tissue
  • Direct GH effects:
    • ↑ lipolysis → ↑ free fatty acids in circulation.
    • ↓ glucose uptake (insulin resistance).
  • Clinical translation:
    • GH excess → decreased body fat, increased muscle mass.
    • GH replacement therapy in GHD → reduced adiposity.Bootcamp 3.05 GHD
Skeletal muscle
  • Direct effects:
    • ↓ glucose uptake (insulin resistance).
    • ↑ amino acid uptake (substrate for protein synthesis).
  • Indirect effects via IGF-1:
    • ↑ protein synthesis.
    • ↑ muscle mass and strength.FA 3.02 GH
Bone and cartilage
  • IGF-1 is the growth engine:
    • Chondrocytes of the epiphyseal plate proliferate under IGF-1.
    • Linear bone growth occurs until epiphyseal closure.FA 3.02 GH; Bootcamp 3.04
  • After epiphyseal closure:
    • Linear growth stops.
    • Continued GH excess thickens bones (jaw, hands, feet) — acromegaly.

4 · Fed vs. fasted physiology

Fed state — growth dominates
  • Hormonal environment:
    • ↑ amino acids → stimulate GH.
    • ↑ glucose → insulin release.
    • Insulin permits hepatic GH → IGF-1.end03 lecture
  • Net effect:
    • Anabolism and tissue growth.
    • This is why kids need balanced meals to grow.
Fasted state — fuel sparing dominates
  • Hormonal environment:
    • ↓ glucose → stimulates GH release (removes inhibition).
    • ↓ insulin → blunts hepatic GH-R → less IGF-1.end03 lecture
  • Net effect:
    • GH's direct anti-insulin effects dominate.
    • Glucose spared for brain; adipose broken down for fuel.

5 · Insulin's permissive role

  • Key concept:
    • Insulin up-regulates GH-R expression on hepatocytes.
    • Without adequate insulin, hepatic IGF-1 production falters despite high GH.end03 lecture
  • Clinical manifestation:
    • Children with poorly controlled T1DM often have growth failure despite high GH levels.
    • IGF-1 levels are the actual limiting factor.AI Generated · see end17

6 · Pregnancy and human placental lactogen (hPL)

  • hPL:
    • Also called human chorionic somatomammotropin (hCS).
    • Placental hormone structurally similar to GH and PRL.end03 lecture; FA 3.01
  • Physiology:
    • hPL mimics GH effects in pregnancy — diabetogenic, lipolytic.
    • Helps mobilize maternal fuel for the fetus.
    • Contributes to gestational insulin resistance and gestational diabetes.AI Generated · obstetrics
  • Feedback on GH:
    • hPL suppresses maternal GH (via feedback on hypothalamus).

3Regulation of GH secretion — circadian and lifetime patterns

The Gist GH secretion is pulsatile (roughly every 4 hours) with sharp peaks and troughs — the dual regulatory system (GHRH stimulates, somatostatin inhibits) is what sharpens the pulses. Three feedback loops (long loop = IGF-1; short loop = GH itself; ultrashort loop = hypothalamic autoinhibition) all contribute. Circadian: biggest GH pulse during deep sleep (~midnight), with additional stimulation from exercise, stress, and melatonin. Lifetime: low at birth → high in childhood → peak at puberty (sex steroids stimulate) → declines to an adult plateau → declines again in old age.

📚 Going Deeper — pulsatility, feedback, circadian and lifetime patterns

1 · Pulsatile secretion — what creates the pulses?

The dual-regulator mechanism
  • How the pulses form:
    • Somatotropes are continuously exposed to waves of GHRH (stimulatory) and somatostatin (inhibitory).
    • When GHRH dominates, a burst of GH is released.
    • When somatostatin dominates, secretion is shut off, sharpening the trough.end03 lecture
  • Frequency:
    • ~Every 4 hours across the day.
    • The largest pulse is during deep (slow-wave) sleep.
  • Why pulsatility matters:
    • Prevents target-cell desensitization to GH.
    • Continuous GH exposure would blunt downstream signaling (similar principle to GnRH pulsatility).FA 3.02 GH

2 · Three feedback loops

Ultrashort loop
  • Mechanism:
    • GHRH (and somatostatin) neurons inhibit their own subsequent firing through local collaterals.
    • Creates pulsatile release.end03 lecture
Short loop
  • Mechanism:
    • GH itself feeds back on the hypothalamus.
    • Increases somatostatin release and decreases GHRH.
Long loop
  • Mechanism:
    • IGF-1 from the liver feeds back on BOTH hypothalamus and pituitary.
    • ↑ somatostatin; ↓ GH release directly from somatotropes.FA 3.02 GH; B&B 5.01
  • Clinical relevance:
    • In GH-secreting adenomas, high IGF-1 usually fails to suppress tumor GH output (autonomous secretion).
    • Basis of the glucose suppression test (below) — normal feedback is absent.Bootcamp 3.04 Acromegaly

3 · Circadian regulation

Deep sleep is the main physiological stimulus
  • Timing:
    • GH peaks during stage 3/4 (slow-wave) sleep, typically around midnight.end03 lecture
  • Melatonin stimulates GH:
    • Pineal melatonin release overlaps the first stage of sleep.
    • Melatonin has direct stimulatory effects on GH release.
  • Clinical implications:
    • Children with sleep disorders or chronic sleep deprivation may have blunted growth.
    • GH stim testing can be done overnight when ambiguous.AI Generated · see end13
Exercise is a potent stimulator
  • Why:
    • Transient hypoglycemia and increased catecholamines during exercise stimulate GH release.
    • Used in GH stimulation tests.AI Generated · see end13

4 · Lifetime pattern

Infancy
  • Low at birth:
    • Serum GH rises rapidly after the first few months.
    • Fetal growth is primarily driven by IGF-2, not GH.end03 lecture
Childhood
  • High baseline:
    • Steady GH drives linear growth and organ development.
    • Poor sleep or poor nutrition at this stage stunts growth.
Puberty — the peak
  • Why highest:
    • Rising sex steroids (estradiol and testosterone) sensitize somatotropes and stimulate GH release.end03 lecture; FA 3.02 GH
    • Creates the "growth spurt."
  • Epiphyseal closure:
    • Estrogen eventually drives epiphyseal closure → terminates linear growth.
    • After this, GH excess thickens bones and soft tissues (acromegaly) rather than lengthening.
Adulthood
  • Plateau:
    • GH declines from pubertal peak to a lower adult baseline.
    • Role shifts from growth to tissue maintenance and metabolic regulation.
Old age ("somatopause")
  • Further decline:
    • Declining sex steroids with aging → reduced GH secretion.
    • Contributes to age-related sarcopenia and increased adiposity.end03 lecture

5 · Sex steroids as GH amplifiers at puberty

  • Mechanism:
    • Estradiol and testosterone both enhance GHRH release and somatotrope sensitivity.FA 3.02 GH
  • Testosterone's indirect effect:
    • Testosterone is aromatized peripherally to estradiol, which is actually the primary driver of the pubertal GH surge.
  • Clinical:
    • Children with hypogonadism (Klinefelter, Turner, Kallmann) often have blunted pubertal GH surge → short adult stature unless gonadal hormones are replaced.AI Generated · repro/end05

4Physiologic stimulators and inhibitors of GH

The Gist Anything that signals "fuel scarcity" or "growth need" stimulates GH. Anything that signals "satiated" or "chronic stress" inhibits GH. Key stimulators: low glucose, low free fatty acids, high amino acids, fasting, deep sleep, exercise, sex steroids, ghrelin, and (acutely) physiologic cortisol. Key inhibitors: high glucose, high FFA, hypercortisolemia (chronic), and pregnancy (via hPL).

📚 Going Deeper — every stimulator and inhibitor in detail

1 · Metabolic stimuli and inhibitors

Glucose
  • ↓ glucose → ↑ GH:
    • Hypoglycemia is a powerful stimulator.end03 lecture
    • Clinical use: insulin tolerance test — induce hypoglycemia, check that GH rises appropriately.
  • ↑ glucose → ↓ GH:
    • Hyperglycemia suppresses GH.
    • Clinical use: oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) — give 75g glucose, check GH. Normal: GH falls. Acromegaly: GH fails to suppress.FA 3.02 GH; Bootcamp 3.04 Acromegaly
Free fatty acids (FFA)
  • ↓ FFA → ↑ GH:
    • Low fat availability signals fuel need.
  • ↑ FFA → ↓ GH:
    • High circulating fat suppresses GH.end03 lecture
Amino acids
  • ↑ amino acids → ↑ GH:
    • Especially arginine and leucine.end03 lecture; FA 3.02 GH
    • Arginine stimulation test is used clinically for GH reserve assessment.

2 · Nutritional state

  • Fasting / caloric deprivation:
    • Low glucose + low insulin drives GH release.end03 lecture
    • GH's direct anti-insulin effects dominate → glucose-sparing, lipolysis.
  • Chronic malnutrition:
    • High GH but LOW IGF-1 (because insulin is low, so hepatic GH-R is down).
    • Growth failure despite high GH.AI Generated · pediatrics / nutrition

3 · Sleep, stress, and exercise

  • Deep sleep (stage 4, slow-wave):
    • Largest physiologic GH pulse.end03 lecture
    • Melatonin contributes.
  • Exercise:
    • Increases GH through transient hypoglycemia and catecholamine release.end03 lecture
  • Stress — the paradox:
    • Acute / physiologic cortisol → permissive, enhances GH.
    • Chronic / hypercortisolemia → suppresses GH.end03 lecture; FA 3.02 GH

4 · Hormonal amplifiers

Sex steroids
  • Estradiol (dominant) and testosterone:
    • Enhance GHRH release and somatotrope responsiveness.end03 lecture
    • Drive the pubertal GH surge.
  • Peripheral aromatization:
    • Testosterone → estradiol (via aromatase).
    • Estradiol is the main driver of the pubertal growth spurt even in males.FA 3.02 GH; repro xref
Ghrelin
  • Origin:
    • Secreted by gastric oxyntic cells and hypothalamic arcuate nucleus.
  • Actions:
    • Stimulates GH release.
    • Also promotes appetite ("hunger hormone").FA 3.02 GH; B&B 5.01

5 · Inhibitors in detail

Hypercortisolemia
  • Mechanism:
    • Chronically high cortisol suppresses GHRH and somatotrope sensitivity.end03 lecture
  • Clinical:
    • Cushing's syndrome → growth failure in children.
    • Chronic glucocorticoid therapy can cause GHD → short stature.AI Generated · see end21
Pregnancy and hPL
  • hPL:
    • Placental lactogen (also called human chorionic somatomammotropin).
    • Structurally similar to GH and PRL; GH-like effects.end03 lecture; FA 3.01
  • Feedback:
    • hPL feedback-suppresses maternal GH (makes sense — redundancy).
    • Maternal IGF-1 rises due to hPL's GH-like activity on liver.

6 · GH stimulation and suppression testing

Testing for GH excess (acromegaly / gigantism)
  • Screening:
    • Serum IGF-1 (stable, 12-h half-life).FA 3.02 GH; Bootcamp 3.04
  • Confirmatory — oral glucose suppression test (OGTT):
    • Give 75 g oral glucose.
    • Normal: GH suppresses to <1 ng/mL.
    • Acromegaly: GH fails to suppress (often paradoxically rises).
  • Imaging:
    • MRI pituitary to localize adenoma.
Testing for GH deficiency
  • Insulin tolerance test:
    • Give IV insulin → induce hypoglycemia.
    • Normal: GH should rise (>5–10 ng/mL).
    • GHD: GH fails to rise.AI Generated · see end13
  • Arginine stimulation test:
    • IV arginine should stimulate GH.
    • Safer alternative when hypoglycemia is contraindicated.
  • GHRH + arginine test:
    • Combined stimulus — very sensitive.

5Disorders of GH signaling — deficiency, insensitivity, excess

The Gist Four main scenarios: GH deficiency (pituitary dwarfism, ↓ GH + ↓ IGF-1), Laron syndrome (GH receptor defect, ↑ GH + ↓ IGF-1), gigantism (GH excess before epiphyseal closure, tall stature), and acromegaly (GH excess after epiphyseal closure, enlarged soft tissues + bone thickening). Most GH excess comes from a GH-secreting pituitary adenoma — the 2nd most common functional adenoma after prolactinoma. Diagnose with IGF-1 screening + OGTT failure to suppress. Treat with transsphenoidal surgery first; medical adjuncts include somatostatin analogs (octreotide, lanreotide), the GH receptor antagonist pegvisomant, and dopamine agonists (less effective).

📚 Going Deeper — each disorder in detail

1 · GH deficiency (pituitary dwarfism)

Mechanism
  • Cause:
    • Decreased production of GH by the anterior pituitary.end03 lecture
    • Can be congenital (pituitary developmental disorders) or acquired (tumors, trauma, radiation, Sheehan).
  • Presentation:
    • Proportionate short stature in children.
    • Reduced muscle mass, central adiposity, exercise intolerance in adults.Bootcamp 3.05 GH Deficiency
Labs
  • Low IGF-1 and low GH (pulsatile).
  • Failed GH rise on stimulation testing (insulin tolerance or arginine).AI Generated · see end13
Treatment
  • Recombinant GH (somatropin):
    • Daily subcutaneous injection.
    • Start young for best height outcomes.
    • Monitor IGF-1 levels to titrate dose.AI Generated · see end13

2 · Laron syndrome (GH insensitivity)

Mechanism
  • Molecular defect:
    • Loss-of-function mutation in the GH receptor (or its JAK-STAT signaling machinery).end03 lecture; FA 3.02 GH
  • Consequences:
    • High GH (no negative feedback from IGF-1).
    • Low IGF-1 (receptor defect blocks hepatic IGF-1 synthesis).
    • Short stature despite high GH.
Clinical pearl
  • Paradoxical health benefits:
    • Laron patients have very low cancer incidence and decreased diabetes risk.
    • Low IGF-1 signaling appears protective for some diseases of aging.AI Generated · research context
Treatment
  • Mecasermin (recombinant IGF-1):
    • Bypasses the broken GH receptor.
    • GH itself is useless.AI Generated · see end13

3 · Gigantism

Mechanism
  • GH excess before epiphyseal closure:
    • High GH + high IGF-1 → excessive linear bone growth.end03 lecture
  • Usual cause:
    • GH-secreting pituitary adenoma in childhood.
Clinical features
  • Very tall stature (can reach 7+ feet).
  • Often disproportionate in the limbs.
  • Severe cardiovascular and metabolic complications — gigantism is more severe than acromegaly despite similar biochemistry.end03 lecture

4 · Acromegaly

Mechanism
  • GH excess after epiphyseal closure:
    • Long bones cannot lengthen, but can thicken.
    • Soft tissues and organs grow.end03 lecture
  • Usual cause:
    • GH-secreting pituitary adenoma (2nd most common functional adenoma after prolactinoma).Bootcamp 3.04 Acromegaly
Classic features (memorize)
  • "Big and thick":
    • Enlarged jaw (prognathism), hands, feet.
    • Coarse facial features, macroglossia, widely spaced teeth.
    • Thick skin, skin tags, oily skin.
  • Associated complications:
    • Diabetes mellitus (insulin resistance).
    • Hypertension, cardiomyopathy, heart failure (leading cause of death).
    • Carpal tunnel syndrome (median nerve entrapment).
    • Obstructive sleep apnea (soft tissue enlargement).
    • Colon polyps and increased colorectal cancer risk.FA 3.02 GH; Bootcamp 3.04
  • Mass effect from macroadenoma:
    • Bitemporal hemianopsia.
    • Headache.
    • Hypopituitarism from compression.AI Generated · see end08

5 · Pituitary adenomas — functional and non-functional

Size classification
  • Microadenoma:
    • <10 mm.
    • Often detected because of functional hormone secretion (hormone excess) rather than mass effect.end03 lecture; Pathoma 15.01
  • Macroadenoma:
    • ≥10 mm.
    • Can cause mass effect: bitemporal hemianopsia, cranial nerve palsies, hypopituitarism.
Functional adenoma frequency (descending)
  • Prolactinoma — most common.
  • GH-secreting adenoma — 2nd most common (causes gigantism/acromegaly).
  • ACTH-secreting (Cushing disease).
  • TSH-secreting (TSHoma, rare).
  • Non-functional adenomas — present with mass effect only.Pathoma 15.01 Pituitary Gland; AI Generated · see end08

6 · Diagnostic workup for GH excess

  • Step 1 — Screening:
    • Serum IGF-1 (age-adjusted).
    • Stable, long half-life; best first test.FA 3.02 GH; Bootcamp 3.04
  • Step 2 — Confirmation:
    • Oral glucose suppression test (75g glucose).
    • Normal: GH <1 ng/mL. Acromegaly: fails to suppress; may paradoxically rise.
  • Step 3 — Localization:
    • MRI pituitary with contrast.
    • If pituitary negative → consider ectopic GHRH source (rare, e.g., pancreatic NET).AI Generated · see end08
  • Step 4 — Comorbidity screening:
    • HbA1c, BP, echocardiogram, sleep study, colonoscopy.

7 · Treatments in detail

Surgery — first-line
  • Transsphenoidal adenoma resection:
    • Goal: biochemical cure (normal IGF-1 + post-op GH <1 on OGTT).
    • Microadenoma cure rate >85%; macroadenoma lower.Bootcamp 3.04 Acromegaly; AI Generated · see end13
Medical therapies
  • Somatostatin analogs (octreotide, lanreotide):
    • Mimic physiological somatostatin (Gi inhibition of GH).
    • First-line medical therapy.AI Generated · see end13; Lippincott Pharm
  • GH receptor antagonist (pegvisomant):
    • Pegylated GH analog that binds GH-R but doesn't trigger signaling.
    • Normalizes IGF-1 (but GH levels rise because it doesn't feedback).
    • Used when somatostatin analogs fail.
  • Dopamine agonists (cabergoline, bromocriptine):
    • Less effective for acromegaly than for prolactinoma.
    • Useful if GH adenoma co-secretes prolactin.
  • Radiation:
    • Reserved for refractory cases.
    • Slow onset (years).

A 7-year-old has short stature, low GH on stim testing, and low IGF-1. Diagnosis and treatment?
GH deficiency (pituitary dwarfism). Treat with recombinant GH (somatropin).
A child has short stature with high GH and low IGF-1. Diagnosis and treatment?
Laron syndrome (GH receptor defect). Treat with recombinant IGF-1 (mecasermin) — giving GH won't work.
A 30-year-old reports enlarging ring, shoe, and glove size, plus frontal bossing and coarse features. First test?
Serum IGF-1. If elevated, confirm with oral glucose tolerance test (failure of GH to suppress).
Leading cause of death in untreated acromegaly?
Cardiovascular disease — cardiomegaly and cardiomyopathy leading to heart failure.
Treatment order for acromegaly?
Transsphenoidal surgery → somatostatin analog (octreotide / lanreotide) → pegvisomant (GH-R antagonist) or dopamine agonist → radiation in refractory cases.
Why does pegvisomant raise GH while normalizing IGF-1?
Pegvisomant blocks the GH receptor, so IGF-1 production drops and the long-loop feedback is lost. GH secretion rises (no IGF-1 feedback) but its action is blocked at the receptor.
Which pituitary adenoma is most common? Second most common?
Prolactinoma is most common; GH-secreting adenoma is second.

Objective-by-objective bottom line

#One-line answer
1GH (191 AA, acidophil) → JAK-STAT receptor on liver → IGF-1 (somatomedin C, RTK). GHRH stimulates (Gs), somatostatin inhibits (Gi). Carriers: GHBP (50% of GH), IGFBP (nearly all IGF-1, T½ 12 h).
2DIRECT (anti-insulin): ↑ glucose, lipolysis, IR in muscle + adipose → diabetogenic. INDIRECT (via IGF-1): tissue growth, protein synthesis, bone growth. Insulin is permissive for hepatic GH-R.
3Pulsatile every 4 h. Biggest pulse during deep sleep (~midnight). Lifetime: low → high in childhood → peak at puberty (sex steroids) → declines.
4Stimulators: ↓ glucose, ↓ FFA, ↑ AA, fasting, deep sleep, exercise, sex steroids, ghrelin, acute cortisol. Inhibitors: ↑ glucose, ↑ FFA, chronic cortisol, pregnancy (hPL).
5GHD (low GH + IGF-1, give somatropin) / Laron (high GH + low IGF-1, give mecasermin) / Gigantism (pre-closure) / Acromegaly (post-closure). Screen IGF-1, confirm OGTT. Tx: surgery → octreotide → pegvisomant.

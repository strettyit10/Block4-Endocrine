LO1: Thyroid Gland Histology

🎯 Tested Concepts
Follicular unit, scalloping, PAS staining, C cell origin, medullary carcinoma amyloid
⚠️ High-Yield Traps
C cells ≠ follicular cells; PTC has Orphan Annie eyes (not follicular carcinoma); medullary = amyloid
⭐ HY Bullets
FLAT=inactive, TALL=active TSH; Colloid=glycoprotein; C cells=neural crest origin
Thyroid Follicle: Functional Unit ▼

Structure & Composition

  • Follicle = sphere of follicular cells (thyrocytes) surrounding homogeneous eosinophilic colloid in the lumen
  • Colloid = thyroglobulin (glycoprotein); stains pink (eosinophilic) on H&E
  • PAS stain positive = carbohydrate in thyroglobulin
  • Simple cuboidal to columnar epithelium lining follicle

Follicular Cell States (FLAT vs TALL)

  • Inactive follicles: FLAT squamous cells (low cuboidal), abundant colloid, little scalloping
  • Active follicles (TSH-stimulated): TALL columnar cells, columnar nuclei, scalloping of colloid at periphery (peripheral vacuoles = active resorption)
  • Scalloping = hallmark of increased thyroid activity (reabsorption of colloid)
"FLAT follicular cells = Low/inactive; TALL columnar = TSH stimulated = Active"
KEY: Scalloping of colloid periphery indicates active resorption and hormone synthesis — sign of stimulated thyroid.
Parafollicular C Cells ▼

Location & Morphology

  • NOT in lumen of follicles — located between follicles in interfollicular stroma
  • Larger than follicular cells, pale eosinophilic cytoplasm
  • Neural crest origin (NOT endodermal like follicular cells) — key embryology point
  • Scattered throughout thyroid, increase with age

Function & Staining

  • Produce calcitonin (serum calcium regulation)
  • Chromogranin A positive (neuroendocrine marker)
  • Calcitonin positive by immunohistochemistry
  • Precursor to medullary thyroid carcinoma (C cell malignancy)
KEY: C cells = Neural crest origin, produce calcitonin, prone to medullary carcinoma with amyloid deposits. Not follicular-derived!
Gland Architecture & Vascular Supply ▼

Organization

  • Thin connective tissue capsule surrounds gland
  • Septa divide gland into lobules
  • Follicles of varying sizes clustered together

Vascular & Lymphatic Features

  • Rich vascular supply — reflects high metabolic activity
  • Fenestrated capillaries — allow efficient hormone uptake into circulation
  • Abundant lymphatic drainage (important for papillary thyroid carcinoma spread)

Quick Review Flip Cards

What stain marks thyroid colloid?
PAS positive (glycoprotein in thyroglobulin)
What does colloid scalloping indicate?
Active resorption → TSH stimulation & increased activity
C cell embryologic origin?
Neural crest (NOT endoderm). → Medullary carcinoma = C cell tumor

LO2: Parathyroid Gland Histology

🎯 Tested Concepts
Chief vs oxyphil cells, adenoma vs hyperplasia, PTH-secreting cells, embryologic origin
⚠️ High-Yield Traps
Oxyphil cells do NOT produce PTH; inferior parathyroids from 3rd pouch (not 4th); adenoma=single gland >80%
⭐ HY Bullets
Chief=PTH producer; Oxyphil=mitochondria-rich, no hormone; hyperplasia=4 glands (CKD)
Anatomy & Cell Types ▼

Gland Anatomy

  • 4 parathyroid glands: paired superior (4th pharyngeal pouch) & inferior (3rd pharyngeal pouch)
  • Embedded in posterior thyroid capsule
  • Size of grain of rice; yellow-brown color
  • Richly vascularized (rapid hormone secretion response)

Chief Cells (Primary Producer)

  • Most numerous cell type
  • Small, polygonal, pale eosinophilic cytoplasm (sparse glycogen)
  • Central round nucleus
  • Arranged in cords, sheets, or follicles
  • Produce PTH (stored in secretory granules)

Oxyphil Cells (Non-Secretory)

  • Larger than chief cells, intensely eosinophilic (bright pink) cytoplasm
  • Packed with mitochondria (oxidative metabolism)
  • Do NOT produce PTH — common trap!
  • Increase in number with age
  • Function unclear; may provide metabolic support

Other Cell Types

  • Water-clear cells: Rare; glycogen-rich; associated with primary hyperparathyroidism
  • Fat cells: Increase with age (30-70% fat in adult gland)
"Chief = PTH producer (chief job); Oxyphil = lots of Oxygen (mitochondria) but no PTH"
Adenoma vs Hyperplasia Patterns ▼

Parathyroid Adenoma

  • Single enlarged gland (most common cause primary hyperparathyroidism, >80%)
  • Sheets of chief cells with increased secretory granules
  • Rim of normal parathyroid tissue at periphery (key finding)
  • Other 3 glands suppressed (normal/atrophic)

Parathyroid Hyperplasia

  • ALL 4 glands enlarged
  • Chief cell hyperplasia pattern (chief cells proliferate)
  • Common cause: Secondary hyperparathyroidism (CKD) due to hypocalcemia & hyperphosphatemia
  • Also: MEN syndromes (1, 2A, 2B)
KEY: Adenoma = single gland (85-90% primary HPT); Hyperplasia = all 4 glands (secondary HPT from CKD)

Quick Review Flip Cards

Which cell produces PTH?
Chief cells (pale eosinophilic, small, polygonal)
Why are oxyphil cells pink on H&E?
Packed with mitochondria (high metabolic rate)
Adenoma vs hyperplasia: how many glands?
Adenoma = 1 gland (+rim of normal); Hyperplasia = all 4

LO3: Adrenal Gland Histology — Cortex Zones

🎯 Tested Concepts
GFR zones, products, 17α-hydroxylase absence in glomerulosa, spongiocytes, ACTH/AngII regulation
⚠️ High-Yield Traps
Glomerulosa lacks 17α-hydroxylase (no cortisol); fasciculata has clear cytoplasm (lipid washout); hyperplasia in CKD affects cortex
⭐ HY Bullets
GFR = Glomerulosa/Fasciculata/Reticularis; Salt/Sugar/Sex products; ACTH stimulates F+R only
GFR Zones Overview (Salt-Sugar-Sex) ▼

Zone Organization (Outer to Inner)

  • Zona Glomerulosa (outermost, ~15%)
  • Zona Fasciculata (middle, ~75%)
  • Zona Reticularis (innermost, ~10%)
"GFR = Salt (Glomerulosa), Sugar (Fasciculata), Sex (Reticularis)"
KEY: Three distinct zones with unique morphology, enzyme expression, products, and regulation patterns.
Zona Glomerulosa: Aldosterone Production ▼

Morphology

  • Outermost zone, represents ~15% of cortex
  • Cells arranged in rounded clusters (glomeruli) appearance
  • Columnar to cuboidal epithelium

Product & Function

  • Produces ALDOSTERONE (mineralocorticoid)
  • Regulates sodium reabsorption & potassium secretion
  • Stimulated by: Angiotensin II and elevated K+
  • NOT responsive to ACTH (has different regulation)

Unique Enzyme Expression

  • LACKS 17α-hydroxylase — CRITICAL POINT
  • Therefore CANNOT make cortisol (requires 17α-hydroxylase)
  • Cannot make androgens (requires 17α-hydroxylase)
  • Only makes aldosterone from pregnenolone → progesterone → DOC → corticosterone → aldosterone
KEY: Glomerulosa lacks 17α-hydroxylase → aldosterone-only production. No ACTH regulation here!
Zona Fasciculata: Cortisol Production ▼

Morphology: Spongiocytes

  • Middle zone, represents ~75% of cortex (largest)
  • Cells arranged in long parallel cords (fascicles)
  • "Spongiocytes" = foamy/vacuolated cytoplasm (lipid-filled)
  • On H&E: clear cytoplasm (lipid dissolves away in fixation)
  • Large central nucleus

Product & Regulation

  • Produces CORTISOL (glucocorticoid, ~95% of glucocorticoid activity)
  • Stimulated by ACTH (responds to pituitary-adrenal axis)
  • Lipid droplets = stored cholesterol (steroid precursor)

Functional Significance

  • Rich in mitochondria (energy for steroid synthesis)
  • High degree of endoplasmic reticulum (enzyme synthesis)
  • ACTH binding → increased lipid uptake & steroid synthesis
  • In Cushing's syndrome: fasciculata hyperplasia (ACTH-driven)
"SPONGiocyte = sCLEAR (no lipid on stain); produces Sugar (cortisol)"
Zona Reticularis: Androgen Production ▼

Morphology

  • Innermost zone, represents ~10% of cortex
  • Cells arranged in anastomosing (interconnecting) network
  • Compact, eosinophilic cytoplasm (bright pink — little lipid)
  • Dark nuclear chromatin

Products & Regulation

  • Produces ANDROGENS: DHEA (dehydroepiandrosterone) and androstenedione
  • Stimulated by ACTH (shares regulation with fasciculata)
  • Minor contribution to adrenal androgens in steady state
  • Becomes important during stress or in tumors (excessive androgens = virilization)

Functional Notes

  • Eosinophilic appearance = less lipid storage than fasciculata
  • Involution occurs with age (innermost layers first)
  • Hyperplasia with chronic ACTH stimulation (ACTH-secreting tumors, Cushing's disease)
KEY: Reticularis = androgens; responds to ACTH; eosinophilic (lipid-poor); forms interconnected network.

Quick Review Flip Cards

What enzyme is ABSENT from glomerulosa?
17α-hydroxylase → cannot make cortisol or androgens
Fasciculata cells look clear. Why?
Lipid dissolves during fixation → clear (spongiocytes)
GFR products: Salt, Sugar, ?
Sex (androgens). Glomerulosa/Fasciculata/Reticularis

LO4: Adrenal Gland Histology — Medulla

🎯 Tested Concepts
Chromaffin cells, catecholamine staining, neural crest origin, chromogranin A, pheochromocytoma
⚠️ High-Yield Traps
Medulla has NO capsule (continuous with cortex); Pheochromocytoma = modified sympathetic neurons; RULE OF 10s
⭐ HY Bullets
Chromaffin cells = postganglionic neurons; 80% epi/20% norepi; splanchnic innervation; S100+ sustentacular cells
Medullary Architecture & Cell Types ▼

Embryology & General Features

  • Adrenal medulla = modified sympathetic ganglion
  • Neural crest origin (not mesodermal like cortex)
  • NO capsule separating medulla from cortex — direct continuity
  • Cortex wraps entirely around medulla in cross-section

Chromaffin Cells (Neuroendocrine)

  • Large, polygonal cells
  • Basophilic granular cytoplasm (neurosecretory granules)
  • Arranged in nests and cords around sinusoids (fenestrated capillaries)
  • Named for brown color with chromaffin stain (chromium salts oxidize catecholamines)
  • Modified postganglionic sympathetic neurons (lost axons, became secretory)

Supporting Cell Types

  • Sustentacular (support) cells: surround chromaffin cells, S100 positive
  • Ganglion cells: scattered sympathetic neurons among chromaffin cells
  • Preganglionic sympathetic fibers (splanchnic nerves): directly innervate chromaffin cells for rapid catecholamine release
"CHROMaffin → CHROMium stain (brown with chromaffin) → CATecholamines (epi/norepi)"
Catecholamine Products & Neural Control ▼

Products

  • EPINEPHRINE (~80%) — adrenaline
  • NOREPINEPHRINE (~20%) — noradrenaline
  • Released directly into sinusoid circulation

Regulation: Direct Neural Control

  • Preganglionic sympathetic splanchnic nerves directly innervate chromaffin cells
  • NOT hormonal control (unlike cortex with ACTH)
  • Stress → splanchnic nerve impulses → immediate catecholamine release
  • "Fight-or-flight" response

Neuroendocrine Markers

  • Chromogranin A positive (neuroendocrine tumor marker)
  • Synaptophysin positive (synaptic vesicle marker)
  • These markers used to diagnose pheochromocytoma
KEY: Medulla is directly innervated (not ACTH-controlled); produces 80% epi + 20% norepi via splanchnic nerve stimulation.
Pheochromocytoma: Chromaffin Cell Tumor ▼

Definition & Origin

  • Tumor of chromaffin cells (adrenal medulla)
  • Can arise from extra-adrenal paraganglia (sympathetic chain neurons)
  • Catecholamine-secreting (epinephrine & norepinephrine)

Histologic Features

  • "Zellballen" (nest/ball) pattern of chromaffin cells
  • Chromaffin cells surrounded by sustentacular cells (S100+)
  • Chromogranin A positive, synaptophysin positive
  • Variable cellularity, pleomorphism can occur

Clinical & Pathologic Notes

  • RULE OF 10s (though numbers now thought to underestimate): 10% malignant, 10% bilateral, 10% extra-adrenal, 10% in children, 10% familial
  • Presents with hypertension, palpitations, sweating (catecholamine release)
  • Metastatic lesions may occur in bone, liver, lung
KEY: Pheochromocytoma = chromaffin cell tumor with "zellballen" pattern; chromogranin A+; presents with adrenergic symptoms.

LO5: Histopathology Correlations — Recognizing Key Lesions

🎯 Tested Concepts
PTC nuclei, follicular carcinoma invasion, medullary amyloid, Hashimoto's Hürthle cells, adenoma rim
⚠️ High-Yield Traps
PTC diagnosed by nuclear features (not capsule invasion); Follicular diagnosed by invasion only; Medullary = amyloid
⭐ HY Bullets
Orphan Annie eyes = PTC; Psammoma bodies in PTC; Hürthle = Hashimoto's; Medullary amyloid = calcitonin-derived
Thyroid Pathology: Inflammatory & Neoplastic ▼

Graves' Disease (Hyperthyroidism)

  • Hyperplastic follicles with tall columnar epithelium
  • Prominent scalloping of colloid (active resorption)
  • Lymphocytic infiltrate (autoimmune)
  • Little colloid in lumens (used up by TSI-driven synthesis)
  • Increased vascularity

Hashimoto's Thyroiditis (Autoimmune)

  • Dense lymphocytic infiltrate with germinal centers (B-cell hyperplasia)
  • Hürthle cell change = oxyphilic metaplasia of follicular cells (large, pink, granular cytoplasm due to mitochondrial accumulation)
  • Follicle destruction with loss of follicles
  • Fibrosis replacing gland (eventually hypothyroidism)

Multinodular Goiter

  • Variable follicle sizes — some markedly enlarged (macro-follicles), some small
  • Follicular hyperplasia in areas
  • Fibrosis, hemorrhage, calcification (degeneration)
  • Results from iodine deficiency or autonomous growth
"Graves = Hyperplasia + scalloping (active); Hashimoto = lymphocytes + Hürthle cells"
Thyroid Cancers: Diagnostic Features ▼

Papillary Thyroid Carcinoma (PTC) — Most Common

  • Papillary architecture (not follicular)
  • Orphan Annie eye nuclei = ground-glass, empty-appearing, overlapping nuclei (KEY DIAGNOSTIC FEATURE)
  • Nuclear grooves (longitudinal)
  • Psammoma bodies = concentric calcifications (pathognomonic)
  • Does NOT require capsular invasion for diagnosis — nuclear features sufficient for PTC diagnosis
  • Spreads to lymph nodes (good prognosis, ~95% 10-year survival)

Follicular Thyroid Carcinoma (FTC)

  • Follicular architecture (looks similar to benign thyroid)
  • DIAGNOSED BY CAPSULAR OR VASCULAR INVASION ONLY (critical distinction from adenoma)
  • Cannot distinguish from adenoma by cytology alone — must have excision & histology
  • No nuclear features to rely on (unlike PTC)
  • More aggressive than PTC; ~80% 10-year survival

Medullary Thyroid Carcinoma (MTC) — C Cell Origin

  • Spindle and polygonal cells (variable morphology)
  • Amyloid deposits (Congo red positive, apple-green birefringence under polarized light)
  • Amyloid = calcitonin-derived (stacked calcitonin molecules)
  • Chromogranin A positive, calcitonin positive
  • Associated with MEN 2A, 2B syndromes (RET mutations)
  • Spreads to lymph nodes and distant sites; intermediate prognosis

Anaplastic (Undifferentiated) Carcinoma

  • Bizarre giant cells, pleomorphism, mitotic figures, necrosis
  • Loss of follicular or papillary architecture
  • Most aggressive thyroid cancer; ~10% 10-year survival
  • Rapid progression; often presents at stage III-IV
KEY: PTC = papillary + Orphan Annie eyes + psammoma bodies; FTC = invasion-based diagnosis; MTC = amyloid (calcitonin-derived)
Parathyroid & Adrenal Pathology ▼

Parathyroid Adenoma (Primary HPT)

  • Single enlarged gland
  • Sheets of chief cells with increased secretory granules
  • Rim of normal parathyroid tissue at periphery (compressed)
  • Other 3 glands appear normal/atrophic (suppressed by hypercalcemia)

Adrenocortical Adenoma

  • Lipid-rich cells resembling zona fasciculata
  • Well-circumscribed, complete fibrous capsule
  • Can produce cortisol (Cushing's) or aldosterone (primary hyperaldosteronism)
  • Benign by definition (no invasion)

Adrenocortical Carcinoma

  • Large, invasive mass with necrosis
  • Vascular invasion present
  • Pleomorphism, high mitotic rate
  • Weiss criteria for malignancy (necrosis, mitotic rate, vascular invasion, capsular invasion, etc.)
  • Aggressive; can produce multiple hormones (cortisol, androgens, aldosterone)
"Adenoma = capsule + no invasion; Carcinoma = invasion + necrosis + pleomorphism"

Practice MCQs: USMLE-Style Questions

1. A 45-year-old woman with Graves' disease undergoes thyroid biopsy. Which of the following histologic findings would be MOST consistent with this diagnosis?
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: Graves' disease shows hyperplastic follicles with tall columnar epithelium (TSH receptor antibodies stimulate growth) and prominent scalloping of colloid due to active resorption. Lymphocytic infiltrate is present but NOT as organized as Hashimoto's. A) Hashimoto's (germinal centers + Hürthle cells); C) Papillary thyroid carcinoma; D) Multinodular goiter; E) Follicular carcinoma.
2. A histology slide shows a thyroid tumor with ground-glass nuclei, nuclear grooves, and psammoma bodies. The tumor has a papillary architecture. What is the most likely diagnosis?
Explanation: Papillary thyroid carcinoma (PTC) is characterized by papillary architecture, "Orphan Annie eye" nuclei (ground-glass, overlapping), nuclear grooves, and psammoma bodies. Notably, PTC does NOT require capsular invasion for diagnosis—nuclear features are sufficient. A) FTC also has follicular architecture but is diagnosed by invasion only; C) MTC has amyloid and calcitonin; D) Anaplastic is undifferentiated; E) Adenoma is benign.
3. A 32-year-old man presents with elevated serum calcium and PTH. A parathyroid gland is removed and histology shows sheets of chief cells with a rim of normal parathyroid tissue at the periphery. What is the most likely diagnosis?
Explanation: The rim of normal parathyroid tissue at the periphery is the classic finding in parathyroid adenoma. This represents compressed normal parathyroid tissue. In hyperplasia, all 4 glands are enlarged without a well-defined adenoma. Adenoma accounts for ~80-90% of primary hyperparathyroidism cases.
4. Which of the following cell types in the parathyroid gland produces parathyroid hormone?
Explanation: Chief cells are the primary PTH-secreting cells in the parathyroid. They are small, polygonal, pale eosinophilic cells arranged in cords/sheets. Common trap: oxyphil cells look "active" with intense eosinophilia (due to mitochondria) but do NOT produce PTH.
5. A 28-year-old patient with primary hyperaldosteronism undergoes CT and a 1.5 cm adrenal nodule is found. Histology shows a well-circumscribed mass of lipid-rich cells resembling zona fasciculata. What is the diagnosis?
Explanation: Adrenocortical adenoma presents as a lipid-rich, well-circumscribed mass with a fibrous capsule and no evidence of invasion. Histologically similar to zona fasciculata. Benign by definition. A size <4 cm, no necrosis, and no vascular invasion support benignity.
6. Which zone of the adrenal cortex LACKS the enzyme 17α-hydroxylase and therefore cannot produce cortisol or androgens?
Explanation: Zona glomerulosa lacks 17α-hydroxylase, so it can only produce aldosterone. This enzyme is essential for cortisol (from 17-OHP) and androgen synthesis, making glomerulosa unable to produce these hormones. This is a critical enzyme distinction tested frequently.
7. A 52-year-old man with hypertension and hypokalemia is found to have elevated aldosterone and suppressed plasma renin. An adrenal nodule is identified. Histologically, cells are arranged in rounded clusters (glomeruli). What is being produced?
Explanation: Rounded clusters (glomeruli arrangement) = zona glomerulosa, which produces aldosterone. Clinical findings (hypertension, hypokalemia, suppressed renin) confirm primary hyperaldosteronism. The histologic arrangement is pathognomonic for glomerulosa.
8. Histology of the adrenal cortex shows cells in long parallel cords with extensive lipid-filled cytoplasm. These cells appear pale/clear on routine H&E staining. Which zone is this?
Explanation: Zona fasciculata has spongiocytes (lipid-rich cells) arranged in fascicles (parallel cords). The lipid dissolves during fixation, leaving a pale/clear cytoplasm on H&E. This is the largest zone (~75%) and produces cortisol (regulated by ACTH).
9. A 35-year-old man presents with palpitations, sweating, and hypertension. A CT scan reveals a 3 cm adrenal mass. Histology shows a "zellballen" (nest/ball) pattern of large, polygonal cells with basophilic granular cytoplasm. What is the most likely diagnosis?
Explanation: Pheochromocytoma of the adrenal medulla presents with the classic "zellballen" pattern. Chromaffin cells (neuroendocrine origin) arranged in nests surrounded by sustentacular cells. The clinical presentation (catecholamine symptoms) and histology are pathognomonic.
10. A pathology specimen from a pheochromocytoma is stained with an antibody against chromogranin A. Which finding would you expect?
Explanation: Chromogranin A is a neuroendocrine tumor marker found in secretory granules of chromaffin cells. Positive staining in pheochromocytoma is diagnostic. Sustentacular cells (S100+) provide support but don't contain chromogranin A.
11. A thyroid tumor shows follicular architecture with nuclear and morphologic features identical to benign follicular adenoma. However, vascular invasion is noted on histology. What is the diagnosis?
Explanation: Follicular thyroid carcinoma is diagnosed entirely on the basis of capsular or vascular invasion—NOT by cytologic features. Without invasion, it's an adenoma despite identical nuclear morphology. This is why excision and histology are essential for follicular lesions (FNA cannot distinguish adenoma from carcinoma).
12. Histology of a medullary thyroid carcinoma shows spindle cells with deposits that stain positive with Congo red and show apple-green birefringence under polarized light. What protein is being deposited?
Explanation: Medullary thyroid carcinoma (MTC) is derived from C cells and produces calcitonin. Calcitonin molecules aggregate to form amyloid, which stains Congo red positive and shows apple-green birefringence under polarized light. This is pathognomonic for MTC.
13. A 58-year-old woman with a history of autoimmune thyroiditis undergoes thyroid biopsy. Histology shows dense lymphocytic infiltrate with germinal centers, large pale cells with granular eosinophilic cytoplasm replacing follicular epithelium, and fibrosis. What is this light pink granular change in follicular cells called?
Explanation: Hürthle cell change (oxyphilic metaplasia) is seen in Hashimoto's thyroiditis and other autoimmune conditions. Follicular cells accumulate mitochondria, giving them a large, pink, granular cytoplasm appearance. This is a pathognomonic finding in Hashimoto's along with lymphocytic infiltrate and germinal centers.
14. C cells of the thyroid are found scattered between follicles in the interfollicular stroma. What is their embryologic origin?
Explanation: C cells are neural crest in origin (NOT endodermal like follicular cells). They migrate into the thyroid and produce calcitonin. This distinction is important because C cell-derived tumors (medullary thyroid carcinoma) have different behavior and associations (MEN 2A, 2B, familial MTC) than follicular cell tumors.
15. A 38-year-old man with secondary hyperparathyroidism (from chronic kidney disease) undergoes parathyroidectomy. Which of the following histologic patterns is expected?
Explanation: Secondary hyperparathyroidism (from CKD-induced hypocalcemia and hyperphosphatemia) causes hyperplasia of ALL FOUR parathyroid glands with chief cell proliferation. In contrast, adenoma (primary HPT) shows a single enlarged gland with a rim of normal compressed tissue. This distinction guides surgical planning.

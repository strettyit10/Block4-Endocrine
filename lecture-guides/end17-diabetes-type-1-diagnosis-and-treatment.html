ADA Diagnostic Criteria

Any ONE of the following, confirmed on repeat testing (except if symptomatic with random glucose ≥200):

  • FPG ≥126 mg/dL (fasting = no caloric intake ≥8h)
  • 2-hour plasma glucose ≥200 mg/dL during 75g OGTT
  • HbA1c ≥6.5%
  • Random plasma glucose ≥200 mg/dL WITH classic symptoms (polyuria, polydipsia, unexplained weight loss)

Prediabetes

Increased risk of T2DM; intervention with lifestyle modification recommended:

  • IFG (Impaired Fasting Glucose): FPG 100–125 mg/dL
  • IGT (Impaired Glucose Tolerance): 2-hr OGTT 140–199 mg/dL
  • HbA1c: 5.7–6.4%

Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM)

Screening: At 24–28 weeks gestation

Approach: 1-step (75g OGTT) or 2-step (50g GCT → if elevated → 100g OGTT)

GDM Diagnosis (IADPSG/ADA 2013; 75g OGTT): Any ONE value positive

  • Fasting ≥92 mg/dL
  • 1-hour ≥180 mg/dL
  • 2-hour ≥153 mg/dL

Maternal Risks: Preeclampsia, C-section

Fetal Risks: Macrosomia → shoulder dystocia, neonatal hypoglycemia

Long-term: ~50% develop T2DM within 10 years postpartum

T1DM vs T2DM

Feature T1DM T2DM
Age of onset Younger (often childhood) Older (usually >40y, but ↑ in youth)
Body habitus Lean Obese (often)
Onset Acute Insidious
DKA at presentation Common Rare (HHS instead)
Autoantibodies (anti-GAD65) Positive Negative
C-peptide Low High/normal (initially)

MODY (Maturity-Onset Diabetes of the Young)

Rare monogenic diabetes; autosomal dominant inheritance:

  • MODY2 (Glucokinase mutation): Mild hyperglycemia, often asymptomatic
  • MODY3 (HNF1α mutation): Responsive to sulfonylureas

HbA1c Targets

  • <7% for most adults
  • <6.5% if tight control achievable without hypoglycemia
  • <8% for elderly/frail patients
  • <6% in pregnancy (T1DM)
  • Goals must be individualized
TRAP: Diagnostic confirmation requires repeat testing UNLESS patient is symptomatic with random glucose ≥200 (then single test sufficient). Don't overdiagnose based on single value in asymptomatic patient.
Mnemonic: "FHR26 = Fasting ≥126, HbA1c ≥6.5, Random (with symptoms) ≥200, 2-hr OGTT ≥200"

Insulin Pharmacokinetics Summary

Insulin Type Onset Peak Duration Key Notes
Lispro/Aspart/Glulisine (Rapid) 5–15 min 30–90 min 3–5 hr Inject just before/with meals; structurally modified
Regular (Short-acting) 30–60 min 2–4 hr 5–8 hr Inject 30 min before meals; used in IV drips
NPH (Intermediate) 1–2 hr 4–8 hr 12–18 hr Cloudy; twice-daily dosing; peak → nocturnal hypo risk
Glargine (Lantus)/Detemir (Long-acting) 1–2 hr Flat (no peak) 20–24 hr Once daily; CANNOT mix with other insulins
Degludec (Ultra-long) 1 hr Flat (no peak) >42 hr Once daily; most stable basal profile
Afrezza (Inhaled) 5 min 15–20 min 2–3 hr Rapid; CI in asthma/COPD

Structural Modifications

Lispro (Humalog): Pro→Lys and Lys→Pro swap at B28-B29 → ↓ self-aggregation → faster absorption

Aspart (NovoLog): Pro→Asp at B28 → rapid action

Glargine (Lantus): 2 Arg added at C-terminus + Asn→Gly → precipitates at SC pH → slow, flat absorption

Detemir (Levemir): Fatty acid chain → binds albumin → prolonged action

Click to reveal: Insulin mixing rules
Glargine & Degludec: DO NOT mix (precipitate). NPH can mix with regular. Clear before cloudy (regular before NPH).
TRAP: Glargine CANNOT be mixed with other insulins—it precipitates. Must give as separate injection.
Mnemonic: "RNIL = Regular, NPH, Intermediate, Long-acting (in order of duration)"

Physiologic Insulin Replacement

Goal: Mimic normal insulin secretion pattern with:

  • Basal insulin: Covers hepatic glucose output between meals & overnight (e.g., glargine/degludec once daily or detemir BID)
  • Bolus (prandial) insulin: Covers carbohydrate intake at meals (rapid-acting analog lispro/aspart/glulisine)
  • Correction doses: Extra rapid-acting for elevated pre-meal glucose

Basal-Bolus Regimen ("Intensive Therapy")

  • 4+ injections/day (most closely mimics physiology)
  • REQUIRED for T1DM management
  • Insulin-to-carb ratio (ICR): Units per grams of carbohydrate (e.g., 1 unit per 15g carbs)
  • Insulin sensitivity factor (ISF): mg/dL glucose drop per unit insulin

Continuous Subcutaneous Insulin Infusion (CSII = Insulin Pump)

Delivers programmable basal rate + patient-activated boluses; uses rapid-acting analog only.

Advantages:

  • Tighter glucose control
  • Flexibility in meal timing/size
  • ↓ Hypoglycemia (variable basal rates)
  • Improved quality of life

Disadvantages:

  • DKA risk if pump failure (no long-acting reserve)
  • Infection at infusion site
  • Cost

Closed-Loop Systems (Artificial Pancreas)

CGM + insulin pump + algorithm → automatically adjusts insulin delivery based on glucose trends

Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM)

  • Sensor under skin measures interstitial glucose every 5 min
  • Replaced every 7–14 days
  • Gives trends and alarms; reduces hypoglycemia unawareness
  • Time-in-range (TIR) 70–180 mg/dL: Target >70% TIR
  • HbA1c targets still guide overall control
Click to reveal: Honeymoon period definition
Shortly after T1DM diagnosis, remaining β cells partially recover from reduced glucotoxicity → temporary ↓ insulin requirements. Eventually lost as autoimmune destruction continues.

Dawn Phenomenon vs Somogyi Effect

Feature Dawn Phenomenon Somogyi Effect
Cause ↑ GH + cortisol (4–8 AM) Nocturnal hypoglycemia → counter-regulatory response
Overnight glucose Normal LOW (hypoglycemia)
Morning glucose Elevated (hyperglycemia) Elevated (rebound hyperglycemia)
Treatment ↑ Basal insulin dose ↓ Nighttime insulin (LESS SUPPORTED by modern evidence)
TRAP: Dawn phenomenon and Somogyi effect both present with morning hyperglycemia. Must check 3 AM glucose to distinguish: normal = dawn phenomenon; low = Somogyi. Somogyi is less common than originally thought with modern insulin analogs.
Mnemonic: "Basal-Bolus = 4+ injections mimicking physiology; Pump = flexibility but DKA risk; CGM = trends & alarms"
LO4: DKA Management (Detailed)
▼
⚠️ Critical Traps
Hold insulin ONLY if K⁺ <3.3 (not just any hypokalemia)
Add dextrose when glucose <200—don't stop insulin
Anion gap closure = endpoint (not glucose normalization)
Urine ketones detect acetoacetate NOT BHB (early DKA may be negative!)

DKA Diagnostic Criteria

  • Glucose >250 mg/dL (usually)
  • pH <7.3 OR serum bicarbonate <18 mEq/L
  • Anion gap >12 (Na - [Cl + HCO3])
  • Serum/urine ketones positive

Severity:

  • Mild: pH 7.25–7.30, bicarb 15–18
  • Moderate: pH 7.00–7.24, bicarb 10–15
  • Severe: pH <7.0, bicarb <10

Precipitants

  • Infection (most common, ~40%) — UTI, pneumonia, etc.
  • New-onset T1DM
  • Insulin omission
  • MI, pancreatitis
  • Medications: steroids, atypical antipsychotics, SGLT2 inhibitors (euglycemic DKA)

Pathophysiology of Ketone Production

  1. ↓ Insulin → ↑ Glucagon
  2. ↑ Lipolysis → Free fatty acids (FFAs) to liver
  3. FFAs → β-oxidation → acetyl-CoA → TCA cycle overwhelmed
  4. Excess acetyl-CoA → Ketone bodies (β-hydroxybutyrate [BHB] → acetoacetate → acetone)

Key point: Nitroprusside/Acetest detects acetoacetate, NOT BHB. Early in DKA, BHB predominates → urine ketones may be NEGATIVE. Use serum BHB measurement for accurate assessment.

As DKA resolves, BHB → acetoacetate → urine ketones can appear to WORSEN even as patient improves clinically.

DKA Treatment Protocol (FIKP)

1. IV FLUIDS

  • Initial: NS (0.9% NaCl) 1 L/hr for first 1–2h
  • Maintenance: 250–500 mL/hr after initial bolus
  • Switch to 0.45% NaCl when Na+ corrects
  • Add dextrose (D5W–D10W) when glucose reaches ~200 mg/dL (continue insulin to close anion gap!)

2. POTASSIUM REPLACEMENT

  • Replace if K⁺ <5.5 mEq/L
  • CRITICAL: HOLD insulin if K⁺ <3.3 (dangerous hypokalemia when insulin starts) → replete K to >3.3 THEN start insulin
  • Goal K⁺: 4–5 mEq/L

3. INSULIN

  • Regular insulin: 0.1 units/kg bolus → 0.1 units/kg/hr drip (or 0.14 units/kg/hr without bolus)
  • Adjust to decrease glucose 50–75 mg/dL/hr
  • CRITICAL: Continue EVEN WHEN GLUCOSE REACHES 200 (reduce rate, add dextrose)—goal is anion gap closure, NOT just glucose normalization

4. BICARBONATE

  • NOT routinely recommended
  • Only if pH <6.9 (severe acidosis)
  • Risks: ↓ K⁺, paradoxical CNS acidosis, delayed ketone clearance

5. PHOSPHATE

  • Replace if <1 mg/dL; may need for severe DKA

6. MONITORING

  • Glucose q1h
  • BMP q2–4h
  • Watch for cerebral edema (esp. children) — treat with mannitol; don't correct Na too fast

DKA Resolution Criteria

Glucose <200 AND TWO of the following:

  • Bicarbonate ≥15 mEq/L
  • pH ≥7.3
  • Anion gap ≤12

Then transition to SC insulin (overlap IV & SC by 1–2h to avoid rebound DKA).

Euglycemic DKA (Important!)

SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin, etc.) can cause DKA with glucose that appears NORMAL or only mildly elevated (hence "euglycemic").

Mechanism: SGLT2 inhibition → ↑ glucagon, ↑ gluconeogenesis → ketone accumulation despite normal glucose

Recognition: Often MISSED because glucose isn't high. Must maintain high clinical suspicion; use serum BHB + anion gap.

Note: SGLT2 inhibitors are NOT FDA approved for T1DM (risk of euglycemic DKA); used off-label only in select patients.

Click to reveal: FIKP mnemonic expansion
Fluids (NS 1L/hr, then 0.45%, add dextrose at glucose ~200), Insulin (0.1 units/kg/hr; continue despite glucose ≥200 until anion gap closes), Potassium (check first; HOLD insulin if K <3.3), close the gaP (anion gap is endpoint, not glucose)
TRAP 1: Holding insulin because glucose is <200 is WRONG. The goal is anion gap closure. Decrease insulin rate and add dextrose instead.
TRAP 2: Urine ketones are negative early in DKA (BHB predominates) but appear to worsen as DKA resolves (BHB converted to acetoacetate). Use serum BHB for accurate assessment.
TRAP 3: Euglycemic DKA with SGLT2 inhibitors presents with NORMAL glucose. Don't miss it—check anion gap and serum BHB.
Mnemonic: "DKA treatment = FIKP = Fluids, Insulin (after K checked), Potassium, close the gaP"
LO5: Hypoglycemia in T1DM — Recognition & Management
▼
🎯 Tested Concepts
Symptoms: adrenergic vs neuroglycopenic
Hypoglycemia unawareness and recovery
15–15 Rule for mild-moderate
Glucagon (IM, SC, nasal) for severe
Glucagon limitations (glycogen stores)

Hypoglycemia Severity

  • Alert: Glucose <70 mg/dL
  • Significant: <54 mg/dL
  • Severe: <40 mg/dL (unconscious)

Symptoms

Adrenergic (early warning, glucose ~60–70 mg/dL): Mediated by epinephrine

  • Sweating, tremor, palpitations, anxiety, hunger

Neuroglycopenic (later, more severe, glucose <50 mg/dL): Brain glucose deprivation

  • Confusion, slurred speech, visual changes, seizures, coma

Hypoglycemia Unawareness

Definition: Loss of adrenergic warning symptoms; common in long-standing T1DM

Mechanism: Autonomic neuropathy + repeated hypoglycemia → blunted counter-regulatory response

Treatment: Strict hypoglycemia avoidance for 2–3 weeks → partially restores awareness. CGM with alerts is essential in these patients.

Counter-Regulatory Response (Normal)

Hypoglycemia triggers:

  1. Glucagon (first-line defense in non-diabetics)
  2. Epinephrine (primary defense in T1DM after glucagon response is lost)
  3. Cortisol + GH (delayed backup)

Key point: In T1DM, glucagon response is lost (α cells cannot respond appropriately) → epinephrine becomes main defense. If autonomic neuropathy → blunted epinephrine too → SEVERE hypoglycemia risk.

Hypoglycemia Treatment

Mild-Moderate (Conscious Patient)

"15–15 Rule":

  1. 15g fast-acting carbohydrate (e.g., 4 oz juice, 3–4 glucose tablets, regular soda)
  2. Recheck glucose in 15 min
  3. Repeat if still low

After recovery: eat complex carbs to prevent rebound.

Severe (Unconscious, Cannot Take Oral)

  • Glucagon (IM or SC): 1 mg in adults
  • Nasal glucagon (Baqsimi): Alternative formulation
  • IV dextrose: D50W 50 mL = 25g glucose (if IV access available)
Click to reveal: Glucagon limitations
Glucagon mobilizes hepatic glycogen. Won't work if: • Starvation/fasting (depleted glycogen) • Chronic alcohol intoxication (depleted stores) • Addison's disease (↓ cortisol cofactors)

Drug-Induced Hypoglycemia

  • Insulin & sulfonylureas: Most common causes
  • Sulfonylureas: Prolonged action → prolonged hypoglycemia → may need dextrose drip
  • Beta-blockers: Mask adrenergic symptoms + ↓ glycogenolysis
  • Quinolones, salicylates: Can enhance insulin effect

Glucagon Emergency Kit

  • Injection (Glucagen): IM or SC
  • Nasal powder (Baqsimi): no needle required
  • Family/household should have kit available
TRAP: Glucagon works by mobilizing hepatic glycogen. Won't work in starvation, alcohol intoxication, or Addison's disease (depleted glycogen or inadequate cortisol cofactors).
Mnemonic: "15-15 Rule for mild hypoglycemia; GLUCAGON for severe (unconscious); family should have kit ready"

Monitoring Glucose Control

Self-Monitoring Blood Glucose (SMBG)

  • Fingerstick minimum 4x/day in T1DM (before meals, bedtime)

Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM)

  • Superior for detecting patterns, hypoglycemia, overnight trends
  • Time-in-range (TIR 70–180 mg/dL): Target >70%

HbA1c

  • Every 3 months until at goal, then every 6 months
  • Target <7% for most T1DM (≤6.5% if achievable without significant hypoglycemia)

LBGI/HBGI

  • Low/High Blood Glucose Index for risk stratification

Annual Complication Screening

Complication Screening Test Frequency
Retinopathy Dilated eye exam Annually (or q2y if stable, low risk)
Nephropathy UACR + creatinine (eGFR) Annually
Neuropathy Monofilament, vibration, temperature, reflexes Annually
Cardiovascular Lipid panel, BP at every visit Every visit
Thyroid Anti-TPO at diagnosis; TSH if symptomatic (T1DM ↑ risk Hashimoto's & Graves') At diagnosis, then PRN
Celiac disease Anti-tissue transglutaminase (anti-tTG) IgA at diagnosis At diagnosis

Adjunctive Therapies in T1DM (NOT Alternatives to Insulin!)

Pramlintide (Synthetic Amylin)

  • Injected before meals
  • Effects: ↓ postprandial glucose (↓ gastric emptying, ↓ glucagon, ↑ satiety)
  • CRITICAL: Must ↓ premeal insulin dose 50% to avoid hypoglycemia
  • FDA approved as adjunct to insulin in T1DM

SGLT2 Inhibitors in T1DM

  • NOT FDA approved for T1DM (risk of euglycemic DKA)
  • Used off-label in some patients

GLP-1 Agonists in T1DM

  • Investigational; not routinely used

Sick Day Rules (T1DM + Illness)

  • NEVER stop insulin in T1DM even if not eating (illness causes insulin resistance + ketone production)
  • Check glucose & ketones q4h
  • Extra doses for ketones or hyperglycemia
  • If ketones present + unable to keep fluids down → go to ER

Immunotherapy & Prevention

Teplizumab (Anti-CD3 mAb)

  • FDA approved for Stage 2 T1DM (2+ antibodies + dysglycemia but NOT yet Stage 3/overt diabetes)
  • Effect: Delays progression to Stage 3 by ~2–3 years

Ongoing Research

  • Antigen-specific immunotherapy
  • Stem cell transplant
  • Encapsulated islet cells

Exercise Considerations

  • Exercise ↓ glucose (↑ GLUT4 translocation, ↑ insulin sensitivity)
  • T1DM must adjust insulin and/or carb intake around exercise
  • Delayed hypoglycemia can occur hours after exercise (muscles replenish glycogen)
Click to reveal: Pramlintide dosing adjustments
When adding pramlintide, REDUCE premeal insulin dose by 50% to avoid hypoglycemia. Then titrate both pramlintide and insulin based on glucose response.
Click to reveal: Teplizumab vs preventive therapies
Teplizumab (anti-CD3): Stage 2 T1DM PREVENTION (2+ antibodies + dysglycemia). Delays overt T1DM by 2-3 years. NOT for overt diabetes treatment. Ongoing trials for earlier stages.
TRAP: Pramlintide REQUIRES 50% reduction in premeal insulin dose. Failure to reduce insulin → severe hypoglycemia.
TRAP: Teplizumab is for Stage 2 T1DM PREVENTION (autoantibodies + dysglycemia), not for treatment of overt diabetes.
Mnemonic: "T1DM monitoring = SCAN: Sugar (glucose), Complications (annual screens), A1c (q3-6 months), Nutrition/lifestyle"

MCQs: 18 USMLE-Style Questions
▼
1. A 24-year-old female presents with polyuria, polydipsia, and a 2-week history of weight loss. Labs show fasting plasma glucose 285 mg/dL, pH 7.20, bicarbonate 12 mEq/L, and positive serum ketones. Which is the MOST appropriate next step?
Correct: B
This is DKA (glucose >250, pH <7.3, positive ketones). Treatment: IV fluids (NS), check K+, and start insulin only AFTER K+ is >3.3 (if K+ <3.3, hold insulin to avoid cardiac arrhythmias from hypokalemia). A) Glargine alone insufficient for DKA. C) Metformin ineffective in DKA. D) Bicarbonate reserved for pH <6.9.
2. A 16-year-old with newly diagnosed T1DM is started on basal-bolus therapy with glargine (once daily) + lispro (with meals). Which of the following is an INCORRECT statement about this regimen?
Correct: A
Glargine CANNOT be mixed with other insulins (including lispro) because it precipitates at subcutaneous pH. They must be given as separate injections. All other statements are correct.
3. A patient with T1DM on insulin pump therapy experiences sudden hyperglycemia (glucose >300 mg/dL) and is found to have a disconnected infusion set. Which complication is he at HIGHEST risk of developing?
Correct: B
Pump therapy uses only rapid-acting insulin, which has a 3–5 hr duration. Loss of insulin delivery → DKA within hours (no long-acting reserve). This is a major disadvantage of pump therapy compared to basal-bolus with long-acting insulin (glargine/detemir).
4. A 28-year-old with T1DM presents with confusion and sweating. Fingerstick glucose is 38 mg/dL. Which is the MOST appropriate immediate management?
Correct: B
Patient has SEVERE hypoglycemia (glucose <40, confused) and cannot safely swallow oral carbs → IM glucagon is first-line. A) Unsafe (aspiration risk). C) Needs IM glucagon first while IV access being obtained.
5. A 34-year-old with longstanding T1DM reports that he no longer experiences tremor or sweating when his glucose drops to 60 mg/dL. He is referred for evaluation. What is the mechanism of this HYPOGLYCEMIA UNAWARENESS?
Correct: B
Hypoglycemia unawareness in long-standing T1DM results from autonomic neuropathy (affects epinephrine release) + repeated hypoglycemic episodes (blunt counter-regulatory response). Adrenergic symptoms (tremor, sweating) are lost. Treatment: strict glucose control for 2–3 weeks + CGM for safety.
6. A 31-year-old with T1DM is in DKA with glucose 320 mg/dL, pH 7.15, bicarbonate 10 mEq/L, K+ 2.8 mEq/L. After IV fluid bolus, what is the CORRECT next step regarding insulin and potassium?
Correct: C
K+ <3.3 is DANGEROUS. Insulin drives K+ intracellularly, worsening hypokalemia → cardiac arrhythmias. HOLD insulin until K+ >3.3, then replete K+ and start insulin. B) Wrong—cannot "wait" for K+ to rise. D) Bicarbonate only for pH <6.9.
7. A 55-year-old in DKA receives IV fluids and insulin. After 6 hours, glucose is now 210 mg/dL, pH 7.28, bicarbonate 16 mEq/L, anion gap 14. Which is the MOST appropriate management?
Correct: B
The GOAL of DKA treatment is anion gap closure, NOT just glucose normalization. At glucose 210, anion gap is still 14 (elevated) → continue insulin (at reduced rate) + add dextrose to prevent hypoglycemia. Stop insulin only when anion gap ≤12, pH ≥7.3, and bicarb ≥15.
8. A 26-year-old female with T1DM on SGLT2 inhibitor (empagliflozin) for off-label glucose control presents with nausea, fatigue, and shortness of breath. Labs: glucose 184 mg/dL, pH 7.22, bicarbonate 13 mEq/L, serum BHB 4.8 mmol/L (high). What is the diagnosis?
Correct: B
SGLT2 inhibitors can cause EUGLYCEMIC DKA (glucose appears normal/low but ketosis present). This is often missed because glucose is not high. Key: serum BHB is elevated with metabolic acidosis at "normal" glucose. SGLT2i contraindicated in T1DM due to this risk.
9. A patient in DKA is given insulin drip. Urine ketone test (via nitroprusside) is NEGATIVE. The patient clinically improves. What is the MOST likely explanation?
Correct: B
Urine ketone tests (nitroprusside/Acetest) detect ACETOACETATE, not β-hydroxybutyrate (BHB). Early in DKA, BHB predominates → urine ketones negative. As DKA resolves, BHB is converted to acetoacetate → urine ketones become POSITIVE (paradoxically worsens). Use serum BHB for accurate assessment.
10. A 19-year-old with T1DM is started on pramlintide (synthetic amylin) to improve postprandial glucose control. What is the CRITICAL adjustment that MUST be made?
Correct: B
Pramlintide slows gastric emptying + ↓ glucagon + ↑ satiety → ↓ postprandial glucose. If premeal insulin is not reduced 50%, severe hypoglycemia will occur. Pramlintide is an adjunct, not a replacement for insulin.
11. A 22-year-old newly diagnosed with T1DM presents 3 months after diagnosis requiring only 0.3 units/kg/day (reduced from initial 0.8 units/kg/day). What is this phenomenon called?
Correct: B
The honeymoon period occurs shortly after T1DM diagnosis when glycemic control improves (↓ glucotoxicity), allowing remaining β cells to partially recover. Insulin requirements drop temporarily. Eventually, autoimmune destruction continues → permanent loss of β-cell function and ↑ insulin requirements again.
12. A patient with T1DM has consistent 3 AM glucose values of 55 mg/dL with morning glucose of 250 mg/dL. Which is the CORRECT management?
Correct: B
Low 3 AM glucose (55) + high morning glucose = Somogyi effect (nocturnal hypoglycemia → counter-regulatory response → rebound hyperglycemia). REDUCE nighttime insulin. If 3 AM glucose were normal + morning high = dawn phenomenon → increase basal insulin.
13. A 32-year-old with T1DM has HbA1c of 8.2% on basal-bolus therapy. He is counseled on intensive glucose control to reduce complications. What is his target HbA1c?
Correct: C
ADA target for most T1DM adults is <7%. For younger patients with long life expectancy and no contraindications, <6.5% is acceptable if achievable without significant hypoglycemia. Targets must be individualized. Elderly/frail patients: <8%.
14. A 40-year-old with T1DM for 20 years presents for annual complications screening. Which screening test is NOT routinely recommended?
Correct: C
Routine T1DM complications screening includes dilated eye exam, UACR, neuropathy testing, and lipid panel. CT chest for lung cancer screening is NOT a standard diabetes complication screen—applied per general cancer screening guidelines.
15. A 28-year-old with newly diagnosed T1DM and positive anti-GAD antibodies is informed about a new medication (teplizumab). What is its indication?
Correct: B
Teplizumab (anti-CD3) is FDA approved for Stage 2 T1DM PREVENTION (2+ autoantibodies + dysglycemia but not yet overt diabetes). It delays progression to Stage 3 by ~2–3 years. NOT for treatment of overt T1DM; NOT a cure.
16. A 45-year-old patient taking propranolol for hypertension develops hypoglycemia (glucose 48 mg/dL) while on T1DM insulin therapy. Why is this patient at higher risk?
Correct: B
β-Blockers mask adrenergic warning symptoms (tremor, sweating) of hypoglycemia (patient may not realize glucose is dropping) AND impair hepatic glycogenolysis (↓ counter-regulatory response). Combined effect: unrecognized, prolonged hypoglycemia.
17. A 35-year-old with T1DM on CGM reports achieving time-in-range (TIR) of 65% (70–180 mg/dL target). According to current guidelines, what is the goal TIR?
Correct: C
Current guidelines recommend TIR >70% (70–180 mg/dL range) as a major metric for glucose control in T1DM, especially with CGM use. Patient's TIR of 65% is slightly below goal and suggests need for adjustments to insulin regimen or carb intake.
18. A 29-year-old with T1DM develops an acute febrile illness (pneumonia). Which is the CORRECT sick-day rule?
Correct: B
Critical sick-day rule: NEVER stop insulin in T1DM during illness, even if not eating. Infection causes insulin resistance + increased ketone production → DKA risk. Check glucose & ketones q4h; give extra insulin doses for hyperglycemia/ketones; if ketones present + unable to keep fluids down → go to ER.

Understanding hormone classification is fundamental because chemical structure determines how hormones are synthesized, stored, transported, and how they interact with receptors. This classification directly predicts the mechanism of action on exams.

▼ Signaling System Types: Endocrine, Paracrine, Autocrine & Nervous

Understanding the route a signaling molecule takes to reach its target is as important as knowing what it does. Four systems: endocrine, paracrine, autocrine, and the nervous system.

🔗 Neuroendocrine Overlap

  • Hypothalamic-pituitary axis: Hypothalamic neurons release hormones (e.g., CRH, TRH, GnRH) into the portal blood → anterior pituitary — this is neuroendocrine signaling (nervous system controls endocrine release)
  • Adrenal medulla: Preganglionic sympathetic neurons → release ACh → chromaffin cells secrete epinephrine into blood = neuroendocrine (nervous system triggers endocrine release)
  • Posterior pituitary: ADH (vasopressin) and oxytocin are synthesized in hypothalamic neurons and released from axon terminals in posterior pituitary into blood = direct neurosecretion
⚠️ TRAP: Paracrine vs Autocrine — Easy to Confuse ▼
  • Paracrine: Signal acts on neighboring cells (different cells nearby) — e.g., somatostatin inhibiting adjacent islet cells
  • Autocrine: Signal acts on the same cell that produced it — classic in cancer (tumor cells secrete growth factors that stimulate their own receptors)
  • High-yield trick: IGF-1 autocrine signaling is tested in the context of tumor growth and GH axis — know which tissues use autocrine vs endocrine IGF-1

🔑 QUICK REFERENCE CARDS

🔑 PEPTIDE HORMONES
  • Water-soluble → cell surface receptors
  • Stored in secretory vesicles → FAST release on demand
  • Second messengers: cAMP, IP3/Ca²⁺, MAPK, cGMP
  • Action time: seconds to minutes
  • Made once, used many times (storage advantage)
🔑 STEROID HORMONES
  • Lipid-soluble → cross all membranes → intracellular nuclear receptors
  • NO vesicle storage (made on-demand only)
  • Gene transcription (direct DNA binding, NO second messengers)
  • Action time: hours to days
  • All from cholesterol (key exam point!)
🔑 CATECHOLAMINES (AMINE)
  • Water-soluble amines → cell surface receptors
  • Act like PEPTIDES (NOT like thyroid hormones)
  • Stored in vesicles; fast action (seconds)
  • Examples: Epinephrine, norepinephrine, dopamine
🔑 THYROID HORMONES (AMINE EXCEPTION!)
  • Lipid-soluble amines (EXCEPTION to amine rule)
  • Act like STEROIDS: nuclear receptors, gene transcription
  • T4 = inactive (90% secretion); T3 = active (via 5'-deiodinase)
  • Slow action (hours to days)
  • THIS IS HIGH-YIELD — expect exam questions on this!

▼ Peptide/Protein Hormones

⭐ Water-soluble → cell surface receptors → fast action via second messengers → pre-made and stored in vesicles

🧬 Synthesis Pathway — Numbered Steps

  1. Preprohormone synthesis at ribosomes on rough ER (initial transcript with signal peptide)
  2. Signal peptide removal → prohormone in ER lumen (signal sequence cleaved during translation)
  3. Transport to Golgi → packaging into secretory vesicles
  4. Proteolytic cleavage in secretory granules → active hormone + inactive byproducts (stored together)
  5. On-demand exocytosis → hormones released when signaling occurs (Ca²⁺ influx triggers)

Key Example: Proinsulin (prohormone) → cleaves into insulin (active hormone) + C-peptide (byproduct). Both released together; C-peptide is useful marker for endogenous insulin secretion.

⚡ Fast Facts

  • Cannot cross cell membranes (water-soluble)
  • Cell surface receptors only (G-protein coupled, RTK, JAK-STAT, or cGMP)
  • Act via second messengers (cAMP, IP3/Ca²⁺, MAPK, cGMP)
  • Action speed: seconds to minutes
  • Ready-to-go: made once, stored, released many times

⚠️ TRAP: ANP is a PEPTIDE, not lipid-soluble▼
  • ANP is a PEPTIDE, not lipid-soluble — ANP (atrial natriuretic peptide) sounds lipid-soluble based on its cardiac origin. WRONG — it's a peptide hormone! ANP is water-soluble → binds cell surface guanylyl cyclase receptor → produces cGMP → vasodilation + sodium/water excretion. Mnemonic: if it says "peptide" in the name, it's water-soluble with cell surface receptor and fast action.

▼ Steroid Hormones

⭐ Lipid-soluble → cross all membranes → intracellular nuclear receptors → slow action via gene transcription → synthesized on-demand (NO storage)

🧬 Key Synthesis Characteristics

  • Starting material: Cholesterol (always cholesterol)
  • Location: Mitochondria + smooth ER (multi-enzyme complex)
  • Timing: ON-DEMAND synthesis (tropic hormone activates enzymes)
  • Speed: Hours (not minutes; complex multi-step pathway)
  • Storage: Essentially none (not accumulated in vesicles)
  • Activation: ACTH activates cortisol synthesis; LH/FSH activate gonadal steroids

⚡ Fast Facts

  • Freely cross cell membrane AND nuclear envelope (lipid-soluble)
  • Intracellular/nuclear receptors (bind DNA in nucleus)
  • Act via altered gene transcription (slower but more durable)
  • Action speed: hours to days
  • ALL derived from cholesterol (exam question = know this!)

📊 Major Steroid Hormones & Sources

  • Adrenal cortex — Cortisol (zona fasciculata), aldosterone (zona glomerulosa), DHEA (zona reticularis)
  • Gonads — Testosterone (Leydig cells of testis), estrogen & progesterone (granulosa/theca cells of ovary)
  • Liver & kidney — Vitamin D (skin synthesis + liver hydroxylation) → Calcitriol (kidney activation)
⚠️ TRAP: Steroids CANNOT be stored — they're made on-demand▼
  • Steroids CANNOT be stored — they're made on-demand — Vignette example: "Patient takes exogenous steroids daily for 1 week; cortisol production drops, adrenal gland atrophies over days." This tests whether you understand ON-DEMAND synthesis. High exogenous steroid → negative feedback suppresses ACTH → adrenal downregulates steroid enzymes → organ atrophy. Steroids aren't "sitting in a vault" like peptides in vesicles. This is why abrupt steroid withdrawal can cause life-threatening adrenal crisis.
⚠️ TRAP: Vitamin D is a STEROID hormone▼
  • Vitamin D is a STEROID hormone — Exam question: "Which of these hormones is derived entirely from cholesterol?" Answer = Vitamin D (and all steroids). Don't be fooled by "vitamin" in the name — it's a steroid hormone! Vitamin D (cholecalciferol) → liver hydroxylation → 25-OH-vitamin D (storage form) → kidney hydroxylation → 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (calcitriol, the active form). The active form is lipid-soluble, uses nuclear receptor, increases Ca²⁺/phosphate absorption in gut.

▼ Amine Hormones

⭐ Derived from tyrosine. TWO completely different subclasses: catecholamines (like peptides) vs thyroid hormones (like steroids!)

3a. Catecholamines (Adrenal Medulla) — Behave Like Peptides

  • Solubility: Water-soluble
  • Receptors: Cell surface only (α-adrenergic, β-adrenergic)
  • Second messengers: β-receptors → Gs → cAMP; α-receptors → Gq → IP3/Ca²⁺
  • Action speed: Fast (seconds to minutes)
  • Storage: Secretory vesicles (chromaffin granules in adrenal medulla)
  • Examples: Epinephrine (adrenaline), norepinephrine, dopamine
  • Key point: Despite being "amines," they behave exactly like peptide hormones

⚠️ TRAP: Cortisol Requirement for Epinephrine Synthesis ▼
  • PNMT is induced by high local cortisol concentrations from the adrenal cortex → the adrenal medulla is surrounded by cortex blood draining through it (centripetal portal flow)
  • In primary adrenal insufficiency (Addison's) or bilateral adrenalectomy → PNMT is not induced → epinephrine drops, norepinephrine is maintained (NE is made in sympathetic neurons too)
  • This explains why patients with adrenal insufficiency lose epinephrine-mediated responses (less bronchodilation, less glycogenolysis) but retain norepinephrine effects

  • Sarcoidosis trap: Activated macrophages in granulomas express CYP27B1 → autonomous calcitriol production → hypercalcemia even without sun exposure

3b. Thyroid Hormones (Thyroid Gland) — KEY EXCEPTION: Behave Like Steroids

⚠️ HIGH-YIELD EXCEPTION: Thyroid hormones are AMINES but use NUCLEAR receptors and act like STEROIDS
  • Solubility: Lipid-soluble (unusual for amines!)
  • Receptors: Nuclear receptors (NOT cell surface) — can cross membranes
  • Action mechanism: Gene transcription (no second messengers)
  • Action speed: Slow (hours to days)
  • Storage: Thyroglobulin protein in thyroid follicular colloid (unique)
  • T4 vs T3:
    • T4 (thyroxine): Main secretory product (90%) but INACTIVE — prohormone
    • T3 (triiodothyronine): Active form — via 5'-deiodinase conversion in tissues
    • Potency: T3 is ~4x more potent than T4

💊 Clinical Pearl: T4 → T3 Conversion

  • Thyroid gland secretes mostly T4 (the "storage/transport form")
  • Peripheral tissues (liver, kidney, brown adipose, heart) convert T4 → T3 (the "active form")
  • Clinical treatment: Give levothyroxine (synthetic T4) → body converts to T3 as needed
  • Illness/stress: Can shift metabolism to inactive rT3 (reverse T3) instead of active T3
⚠️ TRAP: Thyroid hormones are AMINES that act like STEROIDS▼
  • Thyroid hormones are AMINES that act like STEROIDS — Students see "amine hormone" and assume cell surface receptor + second messenger. WRONG for thyroid! It's lipid-soluble and uses NUCLEAR receptors. A question asking "which second messenger is involved in thyroid hormone action?" is testing whether you know this exception — the answer is NONE. Thyroid hormones bind nuclear receptors and alter gene transcription directly (like steroids, not like peptides).

▼ Cell Surface Receptors (Water-Soluble Hormones)

1. G-Protein Coupled Receptors (GPCRs) — Three Main Subtypes

▼Gs (Stimulatory): ↑cAMP → ↑PKA (ACTIVATION)

Mechanism: Gs heterotrimer releases Gαs → activates adenylyl cyclase → generates cAMP → cAMP activates PKA (protein kinase A) → phosphorylates downstream targets → cellular stimulation.

Net effect: STIMULATION of cellular function (e.g., increased metabolism, secretion, contraction)

🧠 Mnemonic: FLAT ChaMP (Gs-coupled hormones)
▼
Mnemonic: "FLAT ChaMP"
  • FSH — follicle-stimulating hormone (ovarian follicle growth)
  • LH — luteinizing hormone (ovulation, androgen secretion)
  • ACTH — adrenocorticotropic hormone (cortisol synthesis)
  • TSH — thyroid-stimulating hormone (thyroid hormone synthesis)
  • CRH — corticotropin-releasing hormone (ACTH release)
  • hCG — human chorionic gonadotropin (progesterone secretion)
  • MSH — melanocyte-stimulating hormone (melanin production)
  • PTH — parathyroid hormone (calcium mobilization)
  • Glucagon — glucose mobilization (liver)
  • ADH-V2 — vasopressin V2 receptor (water reabsorption, kidney collecting duct)
  • Calcitonin — calcium lowering (osteoclast inhibition)
  • GHRH — growth hormone-releasing hormone (GH release)
▼Gi (Inhibitory): ↓cAMP (SUPPRESSION)

Mechanism: Gi heterotrimer releases Gαi → inhibits adenylyl cyclase → decreases cAMP → reduces PKA activation → cellular suppression.

Net effect: SUPPRESSION/INHIBITION of cellular function (negative feedback)

🧠 Mnemonic: SODA (Gi-coupled hormones)
▼
Mnemonic: "SODA"
  • Somatostatin — inhibits GH, TSH, insulin secretion (inhibitory hormone of GI/pancreas)
  • Opioids — various inhibitory effects (endogenous or exogenous)
  • Dopamine (D2 receptor) — inhibits prolactin release (tonic inhibition)
  • Alpha-2 adrenergic — negative feedback; inhibits norepinephrine release
▼Gq (G-Protein): ↑IP3 + ↑DAG (CALCIUM RELEASE + PKC)

Mechanism: Gq heterotrimer releases Gαq → activates phospholipase C (PLC) → cleaves PIP2 into IP3 (inositol 1,4,5-trisphosphate) + DAG (diacylglycerol). IP3 diffuses to ER → triggers Ca²⁺ release. DAG stays at membrane → activates PKC.

Net effect: Rapid increase in intracellular Ca²⁺ + PKC activation → cellular excitation (muscle contraction, secretion)

🧠 Mnemonic: GOAT H (Gq-coupled hormones)
▼
Mnemonic: "GOAT H"
  • GnRH — gonadotropin-releasing hormone (stimulates LH/FSH)
  • Oxytocin — milk letdown, uterine contraction
  • Angiotensin II — vasoconstriction, aldosterone release
  • TRH — thyrotropin-releasing hormone (stimulates TSH)
  • Histamine (H1 receptor) — inflammatory vasodilation

2. Receptor Tyrosine Kinases (RTKs)

Mechanism: Hormone binding → receptor dimerization → autophosphorylation of intracellular tyrosine residues → phosphorylated tyrosines act as docking sites for adapter proteins (IRS-1, Grb2) → triggers RAS/MAPK cascade → gene expression changes.

Net effect: Cell growth, proliferation, differentiation, metabolic changes.

⚠️ Key Exception: Insulin is RTK, NOT GPCR

  • Insulin — has intrinsic tyrosine kinase → RAS/MAPK → glycogen synthesis, lipogenesis, protein synthesis (anabolic)
  • IGF-1 — insulin-like growth factor 1 (growth, differentiation)
  • Growth factors: EGF, PDGF, FGF

3. JAK-STAT Receptors (Tyrosine Kinase-Associated)

Mechanism: Hormone binding → receptor dimerization → Janus kinases (JAK) recruited to cytoplasmic tails → JAKs phosphorylate each other + receptor → phosphorylated receptor provides docking for STATs (signal transducers and activators of transcription) → STATs phosphorylated → dimerize → translocate to nucleus → activate gene transcription.

Net effect: Gene expression changes (growth, differentiation, immune function).

Key JAK-STAT hormones:

  • Growth hormone (GH) — via JAK2/STAT5 (growth, lipolysis, gluconeogenesis)
  • Prolactin — via JAK2/STAT5 (milk production)
  • Cytokines — interleukins, interferons, erythropoietin

4. Receptor Guanylyl Cyclase (cGMP)

Mechanism: Hormone binding directly activates the intracellular guanylyl cyclase domain → generates cGMP → cGMP activates protein kinase G (PKG) → phosphorylates smooth muscle myosin light chain kinase (MLCK) → relaxation.

Net effect: Smooth muscle relaxation, vasodilation, natriuresis.

  • ANP (atrial natriuretic peptide) — sodium/water excretion, vasodilation
  • BNP (B-type natriuretic peptide) — similar to ANP (cardiac/ventricular origin)
  • NO (nitric oxide) — endothelial vasodilation (not a classical hormone)
⚠️ TRAP: Insulin is RTK, NOT GPCR▼
  • Insulin is RTK, NOT GPCR (Different from glucagon!) — Students confuse insulin with glucagon because both regulate glucose. BUT they use OPPOSITE receptors and have OPPOSITE effects:
    • Insulin: RTK → RAS/MAPK → cell growth, glycogen synthesis, lipogenesis (ANABOLIC)
    • Glucagon: Gs-GPCR → ↑cAMP → PKA → glycogenolysis, lipolysis (CATABOLIC)
⚠️ TRAP: GH and prolactin use JAK-STAT, NOT RTK▼
  • GH and prolactin use JAK-STAT, NOT RTK — Unlike insulin, GH and prolactin receptors lack intrinsic tyrosine kinase activity. They RECRUIT JAK2 to the cytoplasmic tail. This is clinically important: JAK inhibitors can be used to treat excessive GH/prolactin effects.
⚠️ TRAP: ADH has TWO receptors with OPPOSITE effects▼
  • ADH has TWO receptors with OPPOSITE effects —
    • V2 receptor (kidney collecting duct): Gs-GPCR → ↑cAMP → aquaporin-2 upregulation → water reabsorption (antidiuresis)
    • V1 receptor (blood vessels, GI smooth muscle): Gq-GPCR → IP3/Ca²⁺ → vasoconstriction, GI contraction
    High-dose ADH → V1 activation → vasoconstriction (used in variceal bleeding). Mnemonic: "V2 for kidney (2 = two), V1 for vessels."
⚠️ TRAP: cAMP → PKA → phosphorylase activation → glycogenolysis▼
  • cAMP → PKA → phosphorylase activation → glycogenolysis — This is THE classic pathway for Gs-coupled hormones (epinephrine, glucagon, ACTH, etc.). Know it cold: Gs → cAMP → PKA → phosphorylates phosphorylase kinase → phosphorylase kinase activates glycogen phosphorylase → breaks down glycogen. Tested constantly in fight-or-flight and fasting scenarios.

▼ Nuclear Receptors (Lipid-Soluble Hormones)

⭐ Hormone enters cell → binds intracellular receptor → hormone-receptor complex enters nucleus → binds DNA → alters gene transcription

Mechanism of Action (Numbered Steps)
  1. Hormone crosses cell membrane (lipid-soluble)
  2. Binds cytoplasmic or nuclear receptor protein (hormone response element-binding protein)
  3. Hormone-receptor complex translocates to nucleus (or is already there)
  4. Complex binds DNA at hormone response elements (HREs) flanking target genes
  5. Recruits coactivators/corepressors → altered gene transcription
  6. mRNA synthesis changes → new protein levels change → cellular effects

Ligands: All steroid hormones (cortisol, aldosterone, estrogen, progesterone, testosterone, DHEA, vitamin D) + thyroid hormones (T3 is active form; T4 is prohormone)

Key points about T4 vs T3:

  • T4 (thyroxine): 90% of thyroid secretion but INACTIVE — acts as prohormone
  • T3 (triiodothyronine): Only 10% of thyroid secretion but HIGHLY ACTIVE
  • Conversion: T4 → T3 via 5'-deiodinase in liver, kidney, brown fat, heart
  • Potency: T3 is ~4x more potent than T4
  • In hyperthyroidism: Both T3 and T4 are elevated (T3 causes symptoms more)

Testing Your Understanding — Question 1

A researcher blocks Gs proteins in a mouse anterior pituitary using a cell-permeable inhibitor. Which of the following hormones would experience the greatest reduction in intracellular cAMP and thus the most impaired cellular response?

✓ Answer: TSH (and all Gs-coupled hormones: FSH, LH, ACTH, GHRH, CRH, PTH, glucagon, ADH-V2, hCG, calcitonin)

🔑 Negative feedback is the rule (maintains homeostasis); positive feedback is rare (estrogen surge); tonic inhibition is dopamine on prolactin (always "ON" suppression).

Feedback loops are homeostatic mechanisms that maintain hormone levels within a narrow range. Understanding feedback is critical for interpreting endocrine lab results and diagnosing primary vs secondary endocrine disorders.

▼ Negative Feedback

⭐ End product of hormone cascade INHIBITS upstream regulators → creates self-limiting system → maintains homeostasis

Feedback Loop Structure (Numbered Steps)

  1. Stimulus activates hypothalamus → releases releasing hormone (RH)
  2. RH travels to pituitary → releases tropic hormone
  3. Tropic hormone reaches target endocrine gland → secretes end-product hormone
  4. End-product hormone rises → inhibits both RH (at hypothalamus) and tropic hormone (at pituitary)
  5. Negative feedback suppresses further hormone release until levels drop
  6. System returns to baseline → cycle continues as needed

💊 Classic Examples

Cortisol (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal Axis)

Hypothalamus (CRH) → Anterior pituitary (ACTH) → Adrenal cortex (cortisol)

Feedback: ↑Cortisol inhibits BOTH CRH and ACTH → prevents cortisol overproduction → maintains stable cortisol levels

Thyroid Hormones (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Thyroid Axis)

Hypothalamus (TRH) → Anterior pituitary (TSH) → Thyroid (T3/T4)

Feedback: ↑T3/T4 inhibit both TRH and TSH → maintains stable thyroid hormone levels

📊 Lab Interpretation Table — Negative Feedback Defects

End Hormone Level Tropic Hormone Level Diagnosis Example
↑↑ High ↓ Low (suppressed) PRIMARY disease (gland failure → extra feedback needed) Adrenal tumor producing cortisol (high cortisol suppresses ACTH)
↑↑ High ↑↑ High (ALSO high!) SECONDARY disease (broken feedback loop → pituitary/hypothalamus problem) ACTH-secreting pituitary tumor (high ACTH drives high cortisol, but ACTH should be suppressed)
↓ Low ↑↑ High (compensatory) PRIMARY disease (gland not responding; pituitary tries to compensate) Hashimoto's thyroiditis (low T4 → high TSH)
↓ Low ↓ Low SECONDARY disease (pituitary/hypothalamus problem) Pituitary adenoma compressing TSH cells (low TSH → low T4)
⚠️ TRAP: Primary vs Secondary: know your tropic hormone direction▼
  • "Primary" = end-organ problem (not pituitary): "Primary hypothyroidism" = thyroid gland problem → low T3/T4 → pituitary raises TSH (negative feedback backfires). PRIMARY disorder has OPPOSITE direction of tropic hormone change.
  • High TSH ≠ hyperthyroidism: High TSH → think primary hypothyroidism (low T3/T4, pituitary trying to compensate). Low TSH → think primary hyperthyroidism (Graves) — high T3/T4 suppresses TSH. Or think secondary (pituitary) failure.

▼ Positive Feedback

⭐ End product AMPLIFIES upstream signal → creates surge → not self-limiting — only physiologic example: estrogen → LH surge

In positive feedback, the end product of a hormone cascade stimulates (rather than inhibits) upstream regulators, creating amplification. This is rare in endocrinology because positive feedback creates runaway responses.

💊 Classic Example: Estrogen & LH Surge (Menstrual Cycle)

  • Late follicular phase: Developing ovarian follicle produces rising estrogen
  • Estrogen surge effect: Stimulates GnRH release from hypothalamus (and makes pituitary more sensitive to GnRH)
  • Result: Amplification → LH surge → triggers ovulation (positive feedback)
  • Key caveat: This only happens at HIGH estrogen levels (threshold effect, late follicular phase)
  • Low estrogen: Produces NEGATIVE feedback (as in luteal phase and postmenopause)

Why physiologic? The LH surge is self-limiting — estrogen drop after ovulation removes the positive feedback stimulus → cycle resets.

⚠️ TRAP: Estrogen positive feedback is LIMITED to the LH surge▼
  • Positive feedback only in reproductive system: Estrogen positive feedback ONLY applies to the LH surge in late follicular phase. Estrogen inhibits FSH/LH most of the time. Don't over-generalize.

▼ Tonic Inhibition

⭐ Signal is "always ON" to suppress hormone release; removal of suppression → hormone rises

Tonic inhibition is a special case where a signal CONSTITUTIVELY suppresses hormone release. The classic example is dopamine inhibition of prolactin.

💊 Dopamine & Prolactin — The Classic Example

  • Tonic inhibition: Dopamine (aka prolactin-inhibiting hormone, PIH) released from hypothalamus → travels to pituitary lactotropes → SUPPRESSES prolactin release
  • Default state: Dopamine "ON" → prolactin suppressed
  • Block dopamine: (metoclopramide, antipsychotics, pituitary stalk transection) → dopamine stops → prolactin rises → galactorrhea, amenorrhea
  • Enhance dopamine: (dopamine agonists: bromocriptine, cabergoline) → more dopamine suppression → prolactin drops → treat prolactinoma
⚠️ TRAP: Dopamine is the ONLY major inhibitory hypothalamic signal▼
  • Dopamine is INHIBITORY (unlike all other releasing hormones): TRH, CRH, GHRH, GnRH all stimulate. Dopamine INHIBITS prolactin — it is the only major inhibitory signal from hypothalamus to anterior pituitary. Block dopamine (antipsychotics, metoclopramide) → prolactin rises → galactorrhea/amenorrhea.

▼ 🧠 High-Yield Points for Exams

⚠️ Primary vs Secondary Endocrine Disease

  • PRIMARY: Gland itself is diseased → low hormone + HIGH tropic hormone (pituitary tries to compensate)
  • SECONDARY: Pituitary/hypothalamus diseased → low tropic hormone + low end-product hormone
  • Both HIGH: Broken feedback (pituitary tumor secreting hormone, or hormone-secreting tumor resistant to feedback)

💊 Clinical Application: Dexamethasone Suppression Test

  • Give exogenous dexamethasone (synthetic steroid) → should suppress ACTH & cortisol (negative feedback)
  • If cortisol stays HIGH despite high dex = feedback broken = ACTH-secreting tumor (Cushing's disease)
  • If cortisol LOW after dex = normal feedback

▼ The Free Hormone Hypothesis

⭐ Only FREE hormone is biologically available; BOUND hormone is inactive; changes in binding proteins shift equilibrium without changing biologic activity

Core Principle (Numbered Steps)

  1. Hormone equilibrium: Total hormone = Bound hormone + Free hormone (exists in dynamic equilibrium)
  2. Only free fraction acts: Only free hormone can cross membranes and bind receptors → produces biologic effects
  3. Bound fraction is reservoir: Protein-bound hormone is in equilibrium, releasing free hormone as it's consumed
  4. Binding protein changes: If binding protein increases (e.g., ↑TBG) → total hormone rises to maintain free hormone constant → appears abnormal on lab but patient is asymptomatic

💊 Clinical Examples

Example 1: Oral Contraceptives & TBG

Patient starts oral contraceptives (estrogen) → ↑TBG → total T4 rises (looks abnormal) but free T4 stays normal → patient euthyroid (no symptoms) → do NOT treat. If you check TSH, it's normal → confirms patient is euthyroid.

Example 2: Nephrotic Syndrome & CBG

Nephrotic syndrome → urinary loss of small proteins including CBG → ↓CBG → total cortisol drops (looks low) but free cortisol normal → patient NOT in adrenal crisis (symptoms would indicate shock) → do NOT treat with steroids.

⚡ Fast Facts

  • Lipid-soluble hormones (steroids, thyroid) bind avidly to proteins in blood
  • Water-soluble hormones (peptides) bind minimally or not at all (already water-soluble)
  • Bound hormone acts as a buffer/reservoir (maintains free hormone steady)
  • Free hormone hypothesis explains why total hormone can be abnormal while free is normal
  • Always measure FREE hormone if symptoms don't match total hormone levels

🧠 High-Yield Clinical Application

  • ⚠️ When Total Hormone is Abnormal but Patient is Asymptomatic:
    • Check FREE hormone level (or TSH for thyroid hormones)
    • If free hormone is normal → likely binding protein change, NOT true hormone excess/deficiency
    • Treat based on FREE hormone and symptoms, not total hormone
    • Example: ↑total T4 but normal free T4 + normal TSH = OCP-induced ↑TBG, not hyperthyroidism
⚠️ TRAP: ↑ Binding protein ≠ abnormal hormone action — check FREE hormone▼
  • 1. ↑TBG → ↑Total T4, normal free T4 and TSH:The classic "pregnant woman with elevated T4" question. Students often misinterpret this as hyperthyroidism. Remember: free T4 and TSH are normal → euthyroid, no treatment needed.
  • 2. Oral contraceptives (estrogen) increase TBG, SHBG, CBG:Can cause apparent "elevation" in total hormone levels without clinical hyperthyroidism or hyperadrenalism. Free hormone levels are maintained by feedback.
  • 3. Low albumin (liver disease, nephrotic syndrome) → ↓ total hormone:Albumin is a low-affinity, high-capacity binder. In severe illness or nephrotic syndrome, albumin loss or synthesis failure → total hormone falls, but free hormone may be normal if the high-affinity binders (TBG, SHBG, CBG) are intact. This is less commonly tested but important in ICU contexts.
  • 4. Nephrotic syndrome → loss of binding proteins:Nephrotic syndrome leads to urinary loss of small proteins like TBG and SHBG (they're immunoglobulins with molecular weight ~50–60 kDa). This causes ↓total T4 and ↓total testosterone, but free levels are maintained by feedback. Can also lose thyroid hormone in urine → may cause clinical hypothyroidism requiring higher T4 replacement.

▼ Receptor Binding Kinetics: Scatchard Plots & Kd

⭐ Kd = hormone concentration at 50% receptor occupancy (low Kd = high affinity); Scatchard plot: slope = −1/Kd, X-intercept = Bmax; semi-log curve inflection = EC50 ≈ Kd

📐 Defining Kd — Dissociation Constant

  • Kd = the hormone concentration at which 50% of receptors are occupied (half-maximal binding)
  • Units: molar (M) — typically in the nanomolar (nM) to picomolar (pM) range for hormones
  • Low Kd = high affinity (receptor binds hormone tightly at very low concentrations)
  • High Kd = low affinity (receptor only binds at higher concentrations)
  • Relationship: Kd = koff / kon — the ratio of how fast hormone dissociates vs. associates with receptor
  • Clinical relevance: Kd determines the "working concentration" of a hormone — physiologic hormone levels are typically in the range of the receptor's Kd so that the receptor can respond to changes in hormone concentration

📉 The Scatchard Plot

A Scatchard plot is a linearized transformation of binding data, used to determine Kd and Bmax graphically.

  • Y-axis: B/F (Bound hormone ÷ Free hormone)
  • X-axis: B (Bound hormone, in moles or nM)
  • Result: A straight line with:
    • Slope = −1/Kd (negative, steeper = lower Kd = higher affinity)
    • X-intercept = Bmax (total receptor number/capacity)
    • Y-intercept = Bmax/Kd
ScenarioScatchard Plot ChangeInterpretationClinical Example
Competitive inhibitor / antagonistSame X-intercept (same Bmax), shallower slope (Kd increases)Receptor number unchanged; affinity decreasedCompetitive hormone antagonist (e.g., spironolactone at aldosterone receptor)
Receptor downregulationSlope unchanged (same Kd), X-intercept shifts left (↓Bmax)Affinity unchanged; fewer receptorsChronic insulin excess → insulin receptor downregulation (↓sensitivity)

📈 Semi-Log Binding Curve

  • X-axis: Log [Hormone] (log scale)
  • Y-axis: % Maximum Binding (or % receptor occupancy)
  • Shape: Sigmoidal (S-shaped) curve
  • Key point: The inflection point (middle of the S-curve) = EC50 ≈ Kd — the hormone concentration producing half-maximal binding
  • Advantage over Scatchard: Directly shows the full binding curve; useful for comparing multiple hormones or receptor isoforms on one graph
  • Right shift of curve = lower affinity (higher Kd); left shift = higher affinity (lower Kd)
⚠️ TRAP: Interpreting Scatchard Plot Changes ▼
  • Slope changes only → Kd changed (affinity changed), Bmax same → competitive inhibition or allosteric modulation
  • X-intercept changes only → Bmax changed (receptor number changed), Kd same → receptor up- or downregulation
  • Both change → non-competitive inhibition or irreversible binding changing both affinity and number
  • Curvilinear Scatchard = multiple receptor populations or cooperativity (not seen in simple 1:1 hormone binding)

🔑 Peptide hormones: made once (complex synthesis), stored in vesicles, released many times (via exocytosis); Steroids: made on-demand, no storage; Secretion patterns (pulsatile, circadian, tonic) = physiologic regulation.

Hormone synthesis, secretion patterns, and regulation of release determine steady-state hormone levels. Understanding these mechanisms explains how hormones achieve physiologic effects and why perturbations cause disease.

▼ Peptide Hormone Synthesis Pathway

⭐ Preprohormone → Prohormone (Golgi) → Active hormone (secretory granules) → Exocytosis on demand

Numbered Synthesis Steps

  1. Gene transcription & translation: Preprohormone mRNA translated at ribosomes on rough ER
  2. Signal peptide-mediated translocation: Signal peptide directs nascent protein into ER lumen; cleaved co-translationally by signal peptidase
  3. ER to Golgi transport: Prohormone travels via COPII vesicles to Golgi apparatus
  4. Golgi processing: Proteolytic cleavage of prohormone by endopeptidases (e.g., PC1/3, PC2) → active hormone + inactive byproduct(s)
  5. Secretory granule formation: Hormones + byproducts packaged into dense-core secretory granules (both stored together)
  6. Regulated exocytosis: Granules stored near plasma membrane; signaling (Ca²⁺, depolarization) triggers SNARE-mediated fusion & exocytosis
  7. Release into bloodstream: Active hormone enters circulation; byproducts also released (used as markers, e.g., C-peptide)

💊 Key Example: Insulin Synthesis

  • Preproinsulin: Initial transcript with signal peptide
  • Proinsulin: Single-chain molecule in Golgi (A chain + B chain + C-peptide still connected)
  • Insulin: Mature form (A + B chains connected by disulfide bonds; C-peptide removed)
  • Stored together: Insulin + C-peptide stored in same granule → released together
  • Clinical use of C-peptide: Marker of endogenous insulin secretion (exogenous insulin has no C-peptide)

▼ Steroid Hormone Synthesis Pathway

⭐ Cholesterol (mitochondria) → Pregnenolone → Multiple intermediates (via various enzymes) → End-product hormone (on-demand)

Cortisol Synthesis (Example)

  1. Cholesterol uptake: Enters adrenocortical cell via LDL receptor or de novo synthesis
  2. Transport to mitochondria: StAR protein (steroidogenic acute regulatory protein) transfers cholesterol across outer mitochondrial membrane (rate-limiting step, activated by ACTH)
  3. P450scc (Side Chain Cleavage): Mitochondrial enzyme converts cholesterol → pregnenolone (removes 6-carbon side chain)
  4. 3β-HSD (3-beta-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase): Smooth ER enzyme converts pregnenolone → progesterone
  5. 17α-hydroxylase: Smooth ER enzyme converts progesterone → 17-OH-progesterone (committed to cortisol synthesis)
  6. 17,20-lyase: Smooth ER enzyme converts 17-OH-progesterone → DHEA (diverts to androgen pathway if needed)
  7. 21α-hydroxylase: Smooth ER enzyme converts 17-OH-progesterone → 11-deoxycortisol
  8. 11β-hydroxylase: Mitochondrial enzyme converts 11-deoxycortisol → cortisol (final, active product)

⚡ Fast Facts

  • Rate-limiting step: StAR protein (activated by ACTH via Gs → cAMP → PKA)
  • Each step requires specific enzyme: Deficiencies in any enzyme → block that step + accumulate precursor upstream
  • Synthesis occurs "on-demand": ACTH stimulation → StAR activation → cholesterol transport → rapid cortisol synthesis (within minutes)
  • No storage: Cortisol is made, released, and reused for feedback regulation

▼ Hormone Secretion Patterns

Pattern Definition Examples Physiologic Significance
Pulsatile/Episodic Discrete pulses of hormone release separated by lower basal levels GnRH (1 pulse/90 min in males; varies in females); LH & FSH (follow GnRH pulses) Prevents receptor downregulation; maintains gonadal sensitivity; disrupted patterns cause hypogonadism
Circadian (Diurnal) Hormone level follows 24-hour cycle (typically peaks at specific time) Cortisol (peaks at 6-8 AM, nadirs at midnight); ACTH (parallel to cortisol, peaks early AM) Matches circadian activity; disrupted in shift work or circadian disorders; explains why cortisol measured at 8 AM vs 8 PM differs
Tonic/Continuous Relatively constant baseline secretion maintained throughout day Thyroid hormones (T3/T4); insulin; many peptides with less obvious rhythmicity Provides steady-state hormone availability; allows feedback regulation at constant level
Biphasic Two distinct peak periods during 24-hour cycle (e.g., morning & evening) Melatonin (peaks at night); some cytokines Matches sleep-wake cycle; enables temporal regulation
Surge Brief, massive increase followed by drop LH surge (ovulation); prolactin surge (postpartum lactation initiation) Triggers rapid physiologic response (ovulation, labor); cannot be easily sustained
⚠️ TRAP: Cortisol timing & C-peptide interpretation▼
  • 1. Cortisol dawn phenomenon:Cortisol peaks at 8 AM, nadir at midnight. A 4 PM cortisol in Cushing's may be within "normal range" but still pathologic. Use midnight cortisol or late-night salivary cortisol (should be suppressed in healthy people) for diagnosis. Loss of diurnal variation = hallmark of Cushing's.
  • 2. Continuous GnRH agonist causes suppression, not stimulation (after initial flare):Students confuse GnRH agonist with GnRH itself. Pulsatile GnRH (natural) → stimulates LH/FSH. Continuous GnRH agonist (e.g., leuprolide) → initial flare of LH/FSH, then receptor desensitization → suppression. Used for precocious puberty (suppress LH/FSH), prostate cancer (suppress testosterone), uterine fibroids (suppress LH/FSH).
  • 3. GH peaks during sleep, not waking:GH is pulsatile but the largest pulse is during slow-wave sleep (Stage 3/4 NREM), not during the day. This is why untreated GH deficiency can present with poor sleep. Growth hormone-releasing hormone (GHRH) increases GH; somatostatin (SST) inhibits GH.
  • 4. C-peptide distinguishes endogenous from exogenous insulin:Exogenous insulin (injection) → high insulin, LOW C-peptide → factitious hypoglycemia. Insulinoma → high insulin, HIGH C-peptide → endogenous source. This is a classic USMLE question.

▼ C-Peptide: A Clinical Marker

⭐ C-peptide released with insulin from proinsulin cleavage; reflects endogenous insulin production (exogenous insulin has none); used to detect factitious hypoglycemia

🔬 Why C-Peptide Matters

  • Origin: Cleaved from proinsulin during insulin maturation in beta cells; stored and released with insulin
  • Exogenous insulin: Pure insulin (made by recombinant technology) contains NO C-peptide
  • Clinical test: Measure C-peptide to determine if hypoglycemia is due to:
    • Endogenous insulin secretion (C-peptide HIGH) = insulinoma or beta-cell dysfunction
    • Exogenous insulin injection (C-peptide LOW, insulin HIGH) = factitious hypoglycemia (patient gave self insulin)
  • Other uses: Assess beta-cell function in diabetes (preserved C-peptide = residual beta-cell activity)

▼ 🧠 High-Yield Clinical Points

⚠️ Enzyme Deficiencies in Steroid Synthesis

  • 21α-hydroxylase deficiency: Most common CAH; blocks cortisol & aldosterone → accumulates 17-OH-progesterone (used for diagnosis) + shunts to androgen pathway (virilization)
  • 11β-hydroxylase deficiency: Blocks cortisol → accumulates 11-deoxycortisol + 11-deoxycorticosterone (mild mineralocorticoid effect → HTN, hypokalemia)
  • 17α-hydroxylase deficiency: Rare; blocks cortisol & androgens → accumulates pregnenolone & progesterone (mineralocorticoid excess → HTN, hypokalemia, no virilization)
  • StAR protein deficiency: Blocks cholesterol transport → no steroid hormone synthesis (lipoid CAH, fatal without replacement)

💊 GnRH Pulse Frequency Matters

  • Normal pulsatile GnRH (1 pulse/90 min): LH & FSH released in pulses → normal gonadal function
  • Absent/rare GnRH pulses: Hypogonadotropic hypogonadism (low LH/FSH, low testosterone/estrogen)
  • Continuous GnRH (not pulsatile): Paradoxical suppression → gonadotrope downregulation → same as absence (used clinically: GnRH agonists treat precocious puberty by desensitizing LH/FSH)

📊 Cortisol Circadian Rhythm is High-Yield

  • Cortisol peaks 6-8 AM: Normal value ~15-25 mcg/dL
  • Cortisol nadirs midnight: Normal value ~3-10 mcg/dL
  • Loss of circadian rhythm: Seen in Cushing's syndrome, depression, critical illness → cortisol high at all times
  • Clinical testing: Late-night salivary cortisol (should be low) used to screen for Cushing's syndrome

🧠 Testing Your Understanding!

Testing Your Understanding — Question 3

A 32-year-old woman is admitted with fasting hypoglycemia (glucose 38 mg/dL). Labs: insulin 48 μIU/mL (elevated; normal fasting <12), C-peptide 0.1 ng/mL (suppressed; normal 0.8–1.5), proinsulin within normal limits. What is the most likely diagnosis?

Consider: insulinoma, factitious hypoglycemia, reactive hypoglycemia, sulfonylurea use

✓ Answer: Factitious hypoglycemia from exogenous insulin injection

High insulin + suppressed C-peptide = exogenous insulin. Insulin is synthesized as proinsulin → cleaved 1:1 into insulin + C-peptide in β-cells. Commercial insulin has no C-peptide, so exogenous injection raises serum insulin without raising C-peptide. The high exogenous insulin also suppresses endogenous β-cell secretion (feedback), further lowering C-peptide. Contrast with an insulinoma: endogenous hypersecretion → high insulin + high C-peptide + high proinsulin. Sulfonylureas also stimulate endogenous β-cell secretion → high insulin + high C-peptide (use urine sulfonylurea screen to distinguish). C-peptide is the key discriminator.

LO 1 — Hormone Classes

Question 1

A researcher isolates a hormone that is synthesized as a large precursor cleaved in the Golgi, stored in secretory granules, and acts within seconds of release. Which of the following additional properties would be expected?

A) Receptor located in the cytoplasm

B) Primary effect via gene transcription

C) Second messenger signaling via cAMP or IP3/DAG

D) Synthesis requires 17α-hydroxylase

E) Cannot be measured in the serum during fasting

✓ Correct Answer: C — Second messenger signaling via cAMP or IP3/DAG

The clues — synthesized from a large precursor (preprohormone → prohormone), cleaved in Golgi, stored in granules, fast action — all point to a peptide hormone. Peptide hormones are water-soluble, so they cannot cross the cell membrane; they act on cell surface receptors and use second messengers (cAMP via Gs, or IP3/DAG via Gq). A is wrong (cytoplasmic receptors = steroids/thyroid). B is wrong (gene transcription = steroids). D is wrong (17α-hydroxylase is for steroid synthesis). E is wrong (peptide hormones are measurable during fasting; e.g., glucagon rises with fasting).

Question 2

Cortisol is a steroid hormone. Which of the following best describes the consequence of its lipid solubility compared to peptide hormones?

A) Cortisol is stored in vesicles and released rapidly upon stimulation

B) Cortisol must bind a cell-surface G protein-coupled receptor

C) Cortisol effects begin within seconds because it acts via cAMP

D) Cortisol circulates mostly bound to CBG; only the free fraction is active

E) Cortisol is synthesized from tyrosine in the adrenal medulla

✓ Correct Answer: D — Cortisol circulates mostly bound to CBG; only the free fraction is active

Steroid hormones are lipid-soluble and not stored (synthesized on demand) — ruling out A. They cross the cell membrane and bind intracellular receptors — ruling out B. Gene transcription takes hours, not seconds — ruling out C. Steroids are derived from cholesterol, not tyrosine (tyrosine → catecholamines/thyroid hormones) — ruling out E. The correct answer is D: because steroids are lipophilic, they bind carrier proteins (CBG for cortisol, TBG for T4, SHBG for sex steroids) in blood, and only the free fraction can enter cells and exert effects.

Question 3

Thyroid hormone (T3/T4) is synthesized from tyrosine and is classified as an amine. However, it behaves most like which class of hormone with respect to mechanism of action?

A) Peptide hormone — acts on cell-surface receptors via cAMP

B) Steroid hormone — binds intracellular/nuclear receptors, alters gene transcription

C) Catecholamine — acts on α- and β-adrenergic receptors

D) Amine — binds GPCR on cell surface like serotonin

E) Peptide hormone — acts via JAK-STAT signaling

✓ Correct Answer: B — Steroid hormone — binds intracellular/nuclear receptors, alters gene transcription

This is a classic USMLE trap. Thyroid hormones are amines by structure (derived from tyrosine), but they are highly lipid-soluble (thanks to iodination) and act like steroids: they cross the cell membrane, bind nuclear receptors (TRβ1, TRα), and directly regulate transcription. Effects take hours to days. Do not confuse "amine" structure with "amine mechanism." Catecholamines (dopamine, epinephrine) are also tyrosine-derived amines but act on GPCRs — the opposite of thyroid hormone.

Question 4

A patient with an insulinoma undergoes fasting labs. Which set of results is most consistent with endogenous insulin hypersecretion versus exogenous insulin injection?

A) High insulin, low C-peptide, low proinsulin

B) High insulin, high C-peptide, high proinsulin

C) Low insulin, high C-peptide, high glucagon

D) High insulin, high C-peptide, undetectable proinsulin

E) High insulin, low glucose, elevated GLP-1

✓ Correct Answer: B — High insulin, high C-peptide, high proinsulin

Insulin is synthesized as proinsulin, which is cleaved into insulin + C-peptide (equimolar) in secretory granules. An insulinoma secretes excess endogenous insulin → all three rise together. In exogenous insulin injection (factitious hypoglycemia), commercial insulin has no C-peptide, so serum C-peptide is suppressed (Option A). This is the highest-yield distinction: elevated insulin + elevated C-peptide = endogenous source (insulinoma); elevated insulin + suppressed C-peptide = exogenous injection.

LO 2 — Receptors & Signaling

Question 5

A 14-year-old boy is found to have constitutively activating mutations in the Gs alpha subunit of pituitary somatotrophs. Which of the following would be expected?

A) Decreased cAMP, decreased GH secretion

B) Increased cAMP, increased GH secretion leading to gigantism

C) Increased IP3/DAG signaling, increased GH secretion

D) Decreased adenylyl cyclase activity, no change in GH

E) Increased Gi signaling, decreased cAMP, acromegaly

✓ Correct Answer: B — Increased cAMP, increased GH secretion leading to gigantism

GHRH acts via a Gs-coupled receptor → activates adenylyl cyclase → ↑cAMP → PKA → GH release. A constitutively activating Gsα mutation (McCune-Albright syndrome, or sporadic GH-secreting adenomas) locks Gs in the "on" state → continuous ↑cAMP → unregulated GH secretion. In a child (open epiphyses) → gigantism. In an adult (closed epiphyses) → acromegaly. This exact mutation (GNAS1 gene) is the most common somatic mutation in GH-secreting adenomas (~40%).

Question 6

Epinephrine stimulates hepatic glycogenolysis via β2 receptors (Gs → cAMP → PKA). It also stimulates α1 receptors (Gq → IP3 → Ca²⁺). A drug that selectively blocks Gq signaling would most directly impair which downstream event?

A) cAMP-dependent protein kinase A activation

B) Intracellular calcium release from the ER

C) Adenylyl cyclase activation

D) Phosphodiesterase inhibition

E) Nuclear receptor translocation

✓ Correct Answer: B — Intracellular calcium release from the ER

Gq → activates phospholipase C (PLC) → cleaves PIP2 into IP3 (inositol trisphosphate) + DAG. IP3 binds IP3 receptors on the ER → releases Ca²⁺ into cytosol. DAG + Ca²⁺ activate protein kinase C (PKC). Blocking Gq prevents PLC activation → no IP3 → no Ca²⁺ release from ER. Note: A and C describe Gs→cAMP signaling (not Gq). D is about PDE (which breaks down cAMP). E is nuclear receptors (steroids/thyroid hormones). The Gq pathway is tested often via oxytocin, TRH, GnRH, angiotensin II, and histamine H1.

Question 7

Growth hormone (GH) and prolactin act via which receptor type?

A) G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) — Gs subtype

B) Receptor tyrosine kinase (RTK)

C) Cytokine receptor — JAK-STAT pathway

D) Nuclear receptor — direct gene transcription

E) Ligand-gated ion channel

✓ Correct Answer: C — Cytokine receptor — JAK-STAT pathway

GH and prolactin bind receptors that belong to the cytokine receptor superfamily (also called hematopoietic receptors). They lack intrinsic kinase activity but associate with JAK2, which is activated upon ligand binding → JAK2 phosphorylates STAT proteins → STATs dimerize and translocate to nucleus → gene transcription. Other JAK-STAT hormones: erythropoietin, thrombopoietin, interferons, many interleukins. Insulin uses RTK (not JAK-STAT). This is a frequent USMLE distinguisher: GH/PRL ≠ RTK; insulin = RTK.

Question 8

A patient is started on a medication that inhibits phosphodiesterase (PDE). Which of the following best describes the immediate downstream effect?

A) Decreased cAMP accumulation → reduced PKA activity

B) Increased cAMP accumulation → enhanced PKA activity

C) Increased IP3 production → calcium release

D) Activation of nuclear receptors → increased transcription

E) Inhibition of adenylyl cyclase → decreased cAMP

✓ Correct Answer: B — Increased cAMP accumulation → enhanced PKA activity

Phosphodiesterase (PDE) is the enzyme that degrades cAMP (→ AMP) and cGMP (→ GMP). By inhibiting PDE, cAMP accumulates → enhanced PKA signaling. Clinical examples: sildenafil (PDE5 inhibitor → ↑cGMP in smooth muscle → vasodilation, used for ED/pulmonary HTN); theophylline/caffeine (PDE inhibitors → ↑cAMP in bronchial smooth muscle → bronchodilation); milrinone (PDE3 inhibitor → ↑cAMP in cardiac muscle → inotropy). These are all clinically important and USMLE-tested.

LO 3 — Feedback Regulation

Question 9

A 45-year-old man on chronic prednisone therapy for rheumatoid arthritis requires emergency surgery. The anesthesiologist administers a "stress dose" of hydrocortisone. What is the primary concern that prompted this decision?

A) Prednisone causes primary adrenal hyperplasia

B) Chronic exogenous glucocorticoids suppress CRH/ACTH → adrenal cortex atrophy → inability to mount a cortisol stress response

C) Prednisone blocks CRH receptors in the hypothalamus

D) Exogenous glucocorticoids increase ACTH through positive feedback

E) Chronic prednisone increases mineralocorticoid production

✓ Correct Answer: B — Chronic exogenous glucocorticoids suppress CRH/ACTH → adrenal cortex atrophy → inability to mount a cortisol stress response

Exogenous glucocorticoids (e.g., prednisone) exert negative feedback on the hypothalamus (↓CRH) and anterior pituitary (↓ACTH). Chronically suppressed ACTH leads to adrenal cortex atrophy over weeks to months. When the patient faces surgical stress and needs a cortisol surge, the atrophied adrenal glands cannot respond → adrenal insufficiency / Addisonian crisis. This is why steroid-dependent patients need "stress dose" steroids perioperatively. Stopping steroids abruptly can also trigger crisis. This is one of the most clinically important applications of feedback physiology.

Question 10

A 26-year-old woman at 36 weeks gestation develops an LH surge. Which type of feedback best explains this phenomenon?

A) Negative feedback — estrogen inhibits GnRH

B) Positive feedback — rising estradiol stimulates the GnRH/LH surge

C) Tonic inhibition — progesterone drives LH secretion

D) Feedforward regulation — CRH stimulates LH

E) Negative feedback — high LH suppresses further LH secretion

✓ Correct Answer: B — Positive feedback — rising estradiol stimulates the GnRH/LH surge

Positive feedback is rare in endocrinology but critical for reproduction. In the follicular phase of the menstrual cycle, rising estradiol (from the dominant follicle) initially exerts weak negative feedback, then — once it reaches a threshold (~200 pg/mL for ≥48 hrs) — it switches to positive feedback on the anterior pituitary and hypothalamus → massive LH surge → ovulation ~36 hours later. This is the only major example of positive feedback in endocrinology. All other HPA/HPT/HPG feedback is negative. Note: This occurs mid-cycle (day ~14), not at 36 weeks gestation — the question scenario is a distractor; the mechanism remains the same.

Question 11

Labs: TSH = 0.1 mIU/L (low), free T4 = 3.8 ng/dL (high). Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis, and where is the primary problem?

A) Primary hypothyroidism — thyroid gland failure

B) Secondary hypothyroidism — pituitary TSH deficiency

C) Primary hyperthyroidism — thyroid gland overproduction of T4

D) Tertiary hyperthyroidism — hypothalamic TRH excess

E) TSH-secreting pituitary adenoma with suppressed free T4

✓ Correct Answer: C — Primary hyperthyroidism — thyroid gland overproduction of T4

Use the axis interpretation algorithm: Low TSH + High free T4 = Primary hyperthyroidism. The thyroid is overproducing T4 autonomously (e.g., Graves' disease, toxic multinodular goiter, toxic adenoma). The high T4 exerts strong negative feedback on the pituitary → TSH is suppressed. In secondary/tertiary hypothyroidism, both TSH and T4 would be low. In a TSH-secreting pituitary adenoma, TSH is inappropriately normal or elevated in the setting of high T4 (the pituitary is not responding to negative feedback).

Question 12

A patient has cortisol of 35 μg/dL at 8 AM (normal: 6–23) and ACTH of 5 pg/mL (low; normal: 7–50). Which of the following is the most likely cause?

A) Addison disease (primary adrenal insufficiency)

B) Cushing disease (pituitary ACTH-secreting adenoma)

C) Adrenal adenoma autonomously secreting cortisol (Cushing syndrome)

D) Ectopic CRH secretion from a small cell carcinoma

E) Pheochromocytoma secreting cortisol

✓ Correct Answer: C — Adrenal adenoma autonomously secreting cortisol (Cushing syndrome)

High cortisol + low ACTH = ACTH-independent Cushing syndrome = primary adrenal problem (adrenal adenoma/carcinoma, or bilateral adrenal hyperplasia). The adrenal tumor secretes cortisol autonomously → high cortisol exerts negative feedback → pituitary stops making ACTH → ACTH is suppressed. Contrast: Cushing disease (pituitary adenoma) = high cortisol + high ACTH. Ectopic ACTH (small cell) = high cortisol + very high ACTH. Addison = low cortisol + high ACTH. The ACTH level is the key discriminator.

LO 4 — Transport & Free Hormone

Question 13

A 28-year-old pregnant woman (12 weeks) has TSH 1.1 mIU/L (normal) and total T4 of 15 μg/dL (high; normal 4.5–12). She is asymptomatic. Which of the following best explains these findings?

A) She has Graves' disease with autonomous thyroid hormone production

B) Estrogen increases TBG → more T4 bound → total T4 rises; free T4 and TSH remain normal

C) hCG stimulates TSH receptors → increases total and free T4

D) Pregnancy decreases TBG → less T4 bound → total T4 rises

E) Her pituitary has developed a TSH-secreting adenoma

✓ Correct Answer: B — Estrogen increases TBG → more T4 bound → total T4 rises; free T4 and TSH remain normal

Pregnancy ↑ estrogen → liver ↑ TBG synthesis → more binding capacity → initially, free T4 falls slightly → pituitary detects → briefly ↑ TSH → thyroid makes more T4 → new equilibrium: total T4 is high, but free T4 and TSH return to normal. The patient is clinically euthyroid. This is the most classic "transport protein" question on USMLE. Remember: only free hormone is active; total levels can be misleading. Also applies to: OCP (estrogen ↑ TBG), nephrotic syndrome (↓TBG → ↓total T4, normal free T4).

Question 14

A patient with nephrotic syndrome loses significant amounts of protein in the urine, including thyroxine-binding globulin (TBG). Which laboratory pattern would you expect?

A) Low total T4, high free T4, low TSH (hyperthyroid)

B) Low total T4, normal free T4, normal TSH (euthyroid)

C) High total T4, normal free T4, normal TSH (euthyroid)

D) Low total T4, low free T4, high TSH (hypothyroid)

E) Normal total T4, high free T4, normal TSH

✓ Correct Answer: B — Low total T4, normal free T4, normal TSH (euthyroid)

In nephrotic syndrome, protein loss in urine includes TBG. Less TBG → less binding capacity → initially, free T4 would transiently rise → but the hypothalamic-pituitary axis quickly compensates: ↑free T4 → ↓TSH → thyroid reduces production → new equilibrium: total T4 is low (less carrier protein), but free T4 returns to normal, and TSH is normal. Patient is euthyroid. This is the mirror image of the pregnancy question. Contrast with true hypothyroidism: low free T4 + high TSH.

Question 15

A male patient taking anabolic androgens (testosterone) develops gynecomastia. His testosterone is low-normal and his SHBG is markedly decreased. Which of the following best explains the gynecomastia?

A) Androgens directly stimulate breast tissue growth

B) Low SHBG → more free testosterone → more aromatization to estradiol → gynecomastia

C) Exogenous testosterone suppresses GnRH → decreased LH → decreased estrogen production

D) High SHBG binds estrogen, raising free estrogen levels

E) Androgens block the aromatase enzyme → decreased estradiol

✓ Correct Answer: B — Low SHBG → more free testosterone → more aromatization to estradiol → gynecomastia

Exogenous androgens suppress SHBG. With less SHBG, more testosterone is free and available for aromatase-mediated conversion to estradiol in adipose, liver, and skin. Elevated estradiol → stimulates breast tissue → gynecomastia. This is a key mechanism of anabolic steroid-related gynecomastia. Also relevant: obesity → more adipose → more aromatase → more estradiol → gynecomastia in obese males. The free hormone hypothesis explains why SHBG changes can have paradoxical effects despite "normal" total hormone levels.

LO 5 — Synthesis & Secretion

Question 16

A 22-year-old type 1 diabetic injects insulin before dinner but forgets to eat. He develops hypoglycemia. Labs show glucose 38 mg/dL, insulin 85 μIU/mL (high), and C-peptide 0.1 ng/mL (suppressed). Which of the following is the correct interpretation?

A) Insulinoma — endogenous insulin hypersecretion with high C-peptide

B) Exogenous insulin injection — elevated insulin without C-peptide co-secretion

C) Reactive hypoglycemia — elevated insulin post-meal with high C-peptide

D) Sulfonylurea ingestion — stimulates β-cell insulin secretion with high C-peptide

E) Liver failure — decreased insulin clearance with high C-peptide

✓ Correct Answer: B — Exogenous insulin injection — elevated insulin without C-peptide co-secretion

Insulin is synthesized as proinsulin → cleaved into insulin + C-peptide in a 1:1 ratio in the β-cell. Commercial insulin preparations contain only insulin, not C-peptide. So exogenous insulin → high blood insulin but no C-peptide released → C-peptide is suppressed. Furthermore, the high exogenous insulin suppresses the β-cells via feedback → even less endogenous production. Insulinoma and sulfonylurea use both stimulate endogenous β-cell secretion → high insulin + high C-peptide. C-peptide is the key to distinguishing endogenous from exogenous insulin.

Question 17

A patient's cortisol is measured at 6 AM and 6 PM as part of a workup for Cushing syndrome. Normal circadian pattern shows peak cortisol in the morning. In a patient with Cushing syndrome from a pituitary adenoma, which finding is most characteristic?

A) Cortisol 6 AM high, 6 PM appropriately low (preserved diurnal rhythm)

B) Cortisol 6 AM low, 6 PM high (reversed diurnal rhythm)

C) Cortisol 6 AM high, 6 PM also high (loss of diurnal rhythm)

D) Cortisol 6 AM normal, 6 PM normal (normal diurnal rhythm)

E) Cortisol 6 AM high, 6 PM undetectable

✓ Correct Answer: C — Cortisol 6 AM high, 6 PM also high (loss of diurnal rhythm)

Cortisol follows a circadian rhythm: peaks at 6–8 AM (driven by ACTH pulse), nadirs at midnight. In normal physiology, late-night (midnight) cortisol is very low. In Cushing syndrome (any cause), cortisol hypersecretion is autonomous — it no longer follows the normal sleep/wake circadian pattern → loss of diurnal variation. Both AM and PM levels are elevated. The midnight salivary cortisol test exploits this: a normal person has very low midnight cortisol; Cushing patients have elevated midnight cortisol. This is one of the screening tests for Cushing syndrome.

Question 18

Somatostatin (SS) and dopamine (D2 receptor) both inhibit pituitary hormone secretion. A patient with a GH-secreting pituitary adenoma is started on octreotide. What is the mechanism of octreotide's action?

A) Blocks GH receptors in peripheral tissues → prevents IGF-1 production

B) Stimulates dopamine D2 receptors on somatotrophs → inhibits GH release

C) Mimics somatostatin → activates Gi-coupled SSTR receptors → ↓cAMP → inhibits GH secretion

D) Blocks GnRH receptors → reduces pituitary stimulation

E) Activates Gs → ↑cAMP → paradoxically reduces GH via feedback

✓ Correct Answer: C — Mimics somatostatin → activates Gi-coupled SSTR receptors → ↓cAMP → inhibits GH secretion

Octreotide is a somatostatin analog (longer half-life than native SS). It binds somatostatin receptors (SSTRs, primarily SSTR2/5) on somatotrophs, which are Gi-coupled: Gi → inhibits adenylyl cyclase → ↓cAMP → ↓PKA → ↓GH secretion. It also inhibits tumor growth. Octreotide is used for: acromegaly, carcinoid syndrome, VIPoma, glucagonoma, and GI bleeding (variceal). Note: Cabergoline (D2 agonist) is an alternative for GH-secreting adenomas but is more commonly used for prolactinomas. Both work via Gi → ↓cAMP.

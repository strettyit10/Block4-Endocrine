LO 1: Pituitary Adenomas — Classification and Overview

Key Concepts

Most common pituitary tumor; typically benign

Microadenoma: < 10 mm | Macroadenoma: ≥ 10 mm

Most are functional (hormone-secreting); some non-functional (null cell)

Arise from anterior pituitary cells

Classification by Cell of Origin

Adenoma Type Hormone Secreted Frequency Clinical Syndrome
Prolactinoma Prolactin Most common (40%) Galactorrhea, amenorrhea, infertility, hypogonadism
Somatotroph GH 2nd most common Acromegaly (adult), Gigantism (child)
Corticotroph ACTH 3rd Cushing's disease
Thyrotroph TSH Rare Secondary hyperthyroidism
Gonadotroph FSH/LH Rare Usually non-functional
Null cell None Common non-functional Mass effects only

Mass Effect Symptoms (Common to All Large Adenomas)

  1. Bitemporal hemianopsia: compression of optic chiasm (nasal retinal fibers → temporal visual fields). "Tunnel vision" — patients bump into things on sides.
  2. Headache: increased intracranial pressure, dural stretching
  3. Hypopituitarism: compression of normal pituitary tissue — order of GH > LH/FSH > TSH > ACTH
  4. Cavernous sinus invasion: CN III, IV, V1, V2, VI palsy (diplopia, facial numbness)
  5. Hyperprolactinemia from stalk compression: any large tumor, not just prolactinoma (lose dopamine inhibition)
Optic chiasm compression → ?
Bitemporal hemianopsia (crossing nasal fibers)
🧠Mnemonic: "P-G-C-T" = Prolactinoma > GH > Corticotroph > TSH (rare)
🎯 USMLE Summary:
Most common pituitary adenoma is prolactinoma. Mass effect presents with bitemporal hemianopsia (optic chiasm compression). Any large pituitary mass causes stalk compression → secondary hyperprolactinemia. Order of AP hormone loss: GH → LH/FSH → TSH → ACTH.
⚠️ Exam trap: Elevated prolactin doesn't always mean prolactinoma — check for stalk compression or medications.
High-Yield Bullets:
  • Most common pituitary adenoma: Prolactinoma
  • Mass effect: bitemporal hemianopsia (optic chiasm compression)
  • Stalk compression by ANY pituitary mass → ↑prolactin (lose dopamine inhibition)
  • Non-functional adenomas: present with hypopituitarism + mass effects (no hormone excess)
  • Order of AP hormone loss with mass: GH → LH/FSH → TSH → ACTH (GH first to go, ACTH last)

LO 2: Prolactinoma

Overview

Most common pituitary adenoma (40%)

Microadenoma: more common in women | Macroadenoma: more common in men (present later)

Prolactin > 200 ng/mL almost always = prolactinoma (vs stalk compression = usually < 150)

Clinical Features

  • Women: galactorrhea + amenorrhea + infertility (prolactin → ↓GnRH → ↓LH/FSH → anovulation + oligomenorrhea)
  • Men: hypogonadism (low libido, erectile dysfunction, infertility) ± gynecomastia; headache; visual field defect (present later/larger tumors)
  • Both: osteoporosis (from hypogonadism)

Diagnosis

  • Serum prolactin (elevated)
  • MRI pituitary with gadolinium (gold standard for imaging)
  • Rule out: pregnancy, hypothyroidism (↑TRH → ↑prolactin), medications (antipsychotics, metoclopramide, SSRIs)

Treatment

  1. Dopamine agonists (first-line for all prolactinomas):
    • Cabergoline (preferred) — longer-acting, fewer side effects
    • Bromocriptine — D2 agonist → ↓prolactin synthesis → tumor shrinkage
  2. Surgery (transsphenoidal): if medication fails, intolerant of medications, or visual compromise not improving
  3. Observation: microadenoma in postmenopausal women (no fertility concerns, low risk of enlargement)
Prolactin > 200 ng/mL = ?
Prolactinoma until proven otherwise
Hook Effect: Very high prolactin levels can paradoxically read low on immunoassay → if clinical suspicion, dilute the sample and retest.
🎯 USMLE Summary:
Prolactinoma is the #1 pituitary adenoma. Presents with galactorrhea + amenorrhea in women, hypogonadism in men. Prolactin > 200 = prolactinoma. First-line treatment: dopamine agonist (cabergoline > bromocriptine). Surgery only if medical therapy fails.
⚠️ Exam trap: Men present later with larger tumors (no obvious symptom like amenorrhea).
High-Yield Bullets:
  • Prolactinoma: #1 pituitary adenoma; galactorrhea + amenorrhea in women
  • First-line treatment: dopamine agonist (cabergoline > bromocriptine)
  • Prolactin > 200 ng/mL = prolactinoma until proven otherwise
  • Surgery only if medical therapy fails
  • Hook effect: very high prolactin → falsely low lab result; dilute and retest

LO 3: Acromegaly and Gigantism (GH-Secreting Adenoma)

Definitions

GH excess before epiphyseal closure → Gigantism (increased linear height)

GH excess after epiphyseal closure → Acromegaly (bones thicken, soft tissue grows)

Acromegaly Clinical Features

  • Enlarged hands and feet: ring/shoe size increase
  • Coarse facial features: frontal bossing, jaw protrusion (prognathism), enlarged nose/lips/tongue (macroglossia)
  • Organomegaly: cardiomegaly (most common cause of death = cardiomyopathy/CHF), hepatomegaly, splenomegaly
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome: median nerve compression from soft tissue growth
  • Diabetes mellitus: GH is anti-insulin → ↑glucose; "diabetogenic"
  • Hypertension
  • Colon polyps: ↑colorectal cancer risk (need colonoscopy)
  • Sleep apnea: macroglossia, prognathism
  • Arthropathy

Diagnosis of Acromegaly

  1. Serum IGF-1 (insulin-like growth factor-1; stable, best initial test) — elevated
  2. Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT) — GOLD STANDARD: Give 75g glucose → measure GH.
    • Normal: GH suppressed to < 1 ng/mL
    • Acromegaly: GH NOT suppressed (remains > 1 ng/mL — fails to suppress)
  3. MRI pituitary to locate adenoma

Treatment

  1. Surgery (transsphenoidal resection) — first-line if surgically accessible
  2. Somatostatin analogs (octreotide, lanreotide) — suppress GH/IGF-1; first-line if surgery fails or not feasible
  3. Dopamine agonists (cabergoline) — less effective than SS analogs; mainly for small tumors with mild IGF-1 elevation
  4. Pegvisomant — GH receptor antagonist; most effective at normalizing IGF-1; used when SS analogs fail
  5. Radiation (stereotactic) — if all else fails
Most common cause of death in acromegaly?
Cardiovascular disease (cardiomyopathy)
🧠Mnemonic: "ACROMEGALY"
Arthropathy | Carpal tunnel | Ring/shoe enlargement | Organomegaly | Macrognathia | ENT (enlarged tongue, nose) | Glucose↑ (diabetes) | Amenorrhea | Large hands/feet | You get heart disease (cardiomyopathy)
🎯 USMLE Summary:
Acromegaly presents with coarse features, large hands/feet, diabetes, cardiomegaly. Diagnosis: ↑IGF-1; confirm with OGTT (GH fails to suppress). Treatment: surgery first; then octreotide (somatostatin analog); then pegvisomant (GH receptor antagonist). Most common cause of death: cardiovascular disease (cardiomyopathy).
⚠️ Exam trap: GH suppresses normally with glucose in acromegaly patients — wait for the lab to say it FAILS to suppress.
High-Yield Bullets:
  • Acromegaly: coarse features, large hands/feet, diabetes, cardiomegaly
  • Diagnosis: ↑IGF-1; confirm with OGTT (GH fails to suppress)
  • Treatment: surgery first; then octreotide; then pegvisomant (GH receptor antagonist)
  • Most common cause of death: cardiovascular disease (cardiomyopathy)
  • Gigantism: GH excess before epiphyseal closure; linear growth ↑↑

LO 4: Cushing's Disease vs Cushing's Syndrome

Definitions

Cushing's Syndrome: any cause of cortisol excess (umbrella term)

Cushing's Disease: pituitary ACTH-secreting adenoma (corticotroph adenoma) — most common ENDOGENOUS cause

Causes of Cushing's Syndrome

Cause Frequency ACTH Cortisol Key Features
Exogenous steroids Most common overall ↓↓ ↑ Iatrogenic; adrenal atrophy
Cushing's disease (pituitary ACTH adenoma) Most common endogenous (70%) ↑ ↑ Small microadenoma
Ectopic ACTH (SCLC, carcinoid) ~15% ↑↑↑ ↑↑↑ Severe, hypokalemia, hyperpigmentation
Adrenal adenoma/carcinoma ~15% ↓ ↑ Unilateral mass

Clinical Features of Cushing's

  • Central obesity: moon facies, buffalo hump, truncal fat
  • Supraclavicular fat pads
  • Striae: purple/wide (cortisol ↓ collagen → thin skin tears)
  • Proximal muscle weakness: cortisol → proteolysis of muscle
  • Hypertension
  • Hyperglycemia: cortisol → gluconeogenesis
  • Osteoporosis
  • Hypogonadism: cortisol → ↓GnRH
  • Psychiatric: depression, psychosis
  • Immunosuppression → infections
  • Poor wound healing
  • Hyperpigmentation ONLY in ACTH-dependent causes (ACTH/MSH from POMC)

Diagnosis of Cushing's (Step-by-Step)

Step 1: Screen for hypercortisolism
  • 24-hour urinary free cortisol (↑↑)
  • Late-night salivary cortisol (loss of diurnal variation — cortisol should be lowest at midnight)
  • Low-dose dexamethasone suppression test (LDDST): 1 mg at 11 PM → measure 8 AM cortisol
    • Normal: suppresses to < 1.8 μg/dL
    • Cushing's: fails to suppress
Step 2: Measure ACTH
  • ↓ACTH → adrenal cause (adenoma, carcinoma, or exogenous steroids)
  • ↑ACTH → ACTH-dependent (pituitary or ectopic)
Step 3: Differentiate pituitary (disease) from ectopic
  • High-dose dexamethasone suppression test (HDDST, 8 mg):
    • Cushing's disease → SUPPRESSES (pituitary responds to high-dose dex)
    • Ectopic ACTH → does NOT suppress (autonomous secretion)
  • CRH stimulation test: Cushing's disease → ACTH rises; Ectopic → no rise
  • MRI pituitary (may be too small to see, 40-50% not visible!)
  • Inferior petrosal sinus sampling (IPSS): catheterize venous drainage of pituitary; if ACTH ratio petrosal:peripheral > 2 → pituitary source (gold standard)
High-dose dex suppresses ACTH in Cushing's?
Disease (pituitary) YES; Ectopic NO
🧠Mnemonic: "Pituitary disease plays POSSUM"
Pituitary responds to high-dose dex (suppresses) | Ectopic does NOT suppress
Ectopic ACTH (SCLC): Often causes severe hypokalemia (cortisol at mineralocorticoid receptor levels due to extreme elevation), hyperpigmentation, and rapid clinical deterioration. Often more severe than Cushing's disease.
🎯 USMLE Summary:
Most common Cushing's overall = exogenous steroids; most common ENDOGENOUS = Cushing's disease. Workup: elevated cortisol (24h UFC, LDDST) → measure ACTH (↑ = ACTH-dependent) → high-dose dex suppression (disease SUPPRESSES, ectopic does NOT). IPSS is gold standard for localizing pituitary. Ectopic ACTH often presents with severe hypokalemia and hyperpigmentation.
⚠️ Exam trap: 40-50% of pituitary microadenomas are not visible on MRI; IPSS may be needed.
High-Yield Bullets:
  • Most common Cushing's = exogenous steroids (overall); Cushing's disease = most common endogenous
  • ACTH ↓ = adrenal cause; ACTH ↑ = ACTH-dependent
  • High-dose dex suppresses Cushing's disease but NOT ectopic ACTH
  • IPSS: gold standard to localize pituitary Cushing's
  • Ectopic ACTH (SCLC): severe hypokalemia, hyperpigmentation, rapid onset

LO 5: Other Pituitary Pathology

Craniopharyngioma

  • Most common suprasellar tumor in CHILDREN (but also seen in adults, bimodal 5-15 + 45-60 yrs)
  • Arises from Rathke's pouch remnants (squamous epithelium)
  • Pathology: calcified cystic tumor; cholesterol crystals; "machine oil" brown cystic fluid
    • Adamantinomatous type (children) or papillary type (adults)
  • Imaging: calcified suprasellar mass (on X-ray and CT) — CALCIFICATION is the key finding
  • Clinical: bitemporal hemianopsia, hypopituitarism (especially GH deficiency in children → short stature), headache, raised ICP
    • In children: growth failure, delayed puberty
  • Treatment: surgical resection (difficult, often recurs)

Sheehan's Syndrome (Postpartum Pituitary Necrosis)

  • Severe peripartum hemorrhage → hypovolemic shock → ischemic necrosis of anterior pituitary
  • (Pituitary enlarges in pregnancy → more susceptible to ischemia)
  • Presentation (in order of hormone loss — GH > LH/FSH > TSH > ACTH):
    • Failure to lactate post-delivery (no prolactin)
    • Failure to resume menses (no LH/FSH)
    • Fatigue, cold intolerance (no TSH → hypothyroidism)
    • Hypotension, weakness (no ACTH → adrenal insufficiency)
  • Posterior pituitary PRESERVED (has separate blood supply) — no DI unless stalk also damaged
  • Treatment: hormone replacement (hydrocortisone FIRST! before thyroid hormone — to prevent adrenal crisis)
Post-partum hemorrhage → pituitary necrosis → hormone loss order?
GH > LH/FSH > TSH > ACTH (GH first; ACTH last)

Hypopituitarism (Panhypopituitarism)

  • Loss of ALL anterior pituitary hormones
  • Causes: pituitary adenoma (most common), surgical removal, radiation, Sheehan's, hemochromatosis (iron deposition), sarcoidosis, lymphocytic hypophysitis
  • Order of hormone loss: GH (first, most sensitive) → LH/FSH → TSH → ACTH (last — most resistant)
  • Treatment: replace each hormone → GH, sex steroids, thyroid hormone, cortisol
    • CRITICAL: ALWAYS replace cortisol BEFORE thyroid hormone (avoid precipitating adrenal crisis)

Empty Sella Syndrome

  • Herniation of arachnoid/CSF into sella turcica → pituitary compressed against walls → "empty" appearance on MRI
  • Primary: idiopathic (often obese women); pituitary function usually normal
  • Secondary: pituitary infarction or surgery
  • Usually asymptomatic; may have mild hyperprolactinemia (stalk stretching)
  • Key: pituitary function is usually INTACT despite appearance

Lymphocytic Hypophysitis

  • Autoimmune inflammation of pituitary; associated with pregnancy (3rd trimester/postpartum)
  • MRI: diffuse pituitary enlargement (mimics tumor)
  • Often presents like pituitary adenoma but post-partum
  • ACTH cells often affected first → adrenal insufficiency
  • May be associated with other autoimmune conditions (Hashimoto's, Addison's)
Sheehan's syndrome: Remember to replace cortisol BEFORE thyroid hormone. If you give thyroid hormone first, it accelerates cortisol metabolism → adrenal crisis.
🎯 USMLE Summary:
Craniopharyngioma: calcified suprasellar mass in children (Rathke's pouch). Sheehan's: postpartum hemorrhage → ischemic necrosis → failure to lactate + no menses (replace cortisol FIRST). Hypopituitarism order: GH > LH/FSH > TSH > ACTH. Empty sella: normal function despite MRI appearance. Lymphocytic hypophysitis: postpartum autoimmune pituitary inflammation mimicking adenoma.
⭐ KEY: Always replace cortisol before thyroid hormone in hypopituitarism.
High-Yield Bullets:
  • Craniopharyngioma: calcified suprasellar mass, children, Rathke's pouch, "machine oil" cyst, bitemporal hemianopsia
  • Sheehan's: postpartum hemorrhage → ischemic necrosis → failure to lactate + no menses; posterior pituitary spared
  • Hypopituitarism order: GH > LH/FSH > TSH > ACTH (GH first to go, ACTH last)
  • Replace cortisol BEFORE thyroid hormone in hypopituitarism (avoid adrenal crisis)
  • Empty sella: normal function despite MRI appearance; primary type = obese women

Practice Questions: USMLE Vignettes

Question 1: Macroprolactinoma Diagnosis and Treatment

A 42-year-old man presents with headaches and progressive loss of peripheral vision. He reports decreased libido and has not shaved in 2 months. Exam shows temporal visual field loss bilaterally. MRI shows a 2.3 cm pituitary mass. Serum testosterone is 85 ng/dL (low), LH 1.2 IU/L (low), FSH 1.5 IU/L (low), prolactin 280 ng/mL.

What is the diagnosis and treatment?
Answer: Macroprolactinoma with pituitary mass effect

Key diagnostic features:

  • Prolactin 280 ng/mL (> 200) = prolactinoma
  • Large 2.3 cm mass = macroadenoma
  • Bitemporal hemianopsia = optic chiasm compression
  • Hypogonadism (↓testosterone, ↓LH/FSH) = prolactin suppression of GnRH
  • Men present later with larger tumors (no obvious symptom like amenorrhea)

Treatment: Dopamine agonist (cabergoline first-line). Most macroprolactinomas shrink with medical therapy alone, even large ones with visual field effects. Most patients show resolution of headaches and improvement in visual fields within weeks to months. Surgery reserved for medical therapy failure or intolerance.

Question 2: Cushing's Disease Diagnostic Workup

A 35-year-old woman presents with progressive weight gain, purple stretch marks on her abdomen, and proximal muscle weakness (difficulty rising from chair). Her face appears round. BP 158/94. Random cortisol is high. 24-hour UFC is elevated. Overnight 1 mg dexamethasone test: morning cortisol 28 μg/dL (no suppression). ACTH is 38 pg/mL (slightly elevated). 8 mg dexamethasone test: cortisol suppresses to 40% of baseline. MRI shows 6mm pituitary hypointensity.

What is the diagnosis?
Answer: Cushing's Disease (pituitary ACTH-secreting microadenoma)

Diagnostic reasoning:

  1. Elevated cortisol confirmed — elevated 24h UFC, failed LDDST (no suppression at 1 mg dex)
  2. ACTH elevated (38 pg/mL) → ACTH-dependent cause (pituitary or ectopic)
  3. High-dose dex (8 mg) SUPPRESSES cortisol (to 40% of baseline) → pituitary source responds to high-dose dex; ectopic would not suppress
  4. Small pituitary adenoma on MRI (6mm microadenoma)
  5. Clinical features consistent: central obesity, purple striae (cortisol ↓ collagen), proximal weakness, hypertension

Treatment: Transsphenoidal surgery by experienced neurosurgeon (first-line for Cushing's disease). If surgery unsuccessful or not feasible, medical therapy with somatostatin analogs (pasireotide) or bilateral adrenalectomy.

Question 3: Sheehan's Syndrome and Hormone Replacement Order

A woman delivers her baby after a complicated delivery with 2.5 L blood loss requiring transfusions. Postpartum day 2, she fails to produce breast milk. At 6-week follow-up, she still has no milk and her menstrual period has not returned. She complains of fatigue and cold intolerance. TSH is 8.2 (high), free T4 is 0.5 (low), morning cortisol is 2.1 μg/dL (low).

What is the diagnosis and what hormone should be replaced FIRST?
Answer: Sheehan's Syndrome — postpartum pituitary necrosis

Pathophysiology: Severe peripartum hemorrhage (2.5 L loss) → hypovolemic shock → ischemic necrosis of anterior pituitary. Pituitary enlarges in pregnancy, making it more susceptible to ischemia.

Clinical presentation (in order of hormone loss):

  • First: Failure to lactate (no prolactin → no milk production)
  • Second: Failure to resume menses (no LH/FSH → anovulation)
  • Third: Fatigue, cold intolerance (no TSH → hypothyroidism; TSH 8.2, free T4 0.5 are secondary hypothyroidism from loss of pituitary TSH)
  • Fourth: Hypotension, weakness (no ACTH → adrenal insufficiency; cortisol 2.1 is low, representing secondary adrenal insufficiency)

CRITICAL — Hormone replacement order:

1. HYDROCORTISONE FIRST (cortisol replacement) — Cortisol must be replaced BEFORE thyroid hormone. If thyroid hormone is given first, it accelerates cortisol metabolism → precipitate adrenal crisis (hypotension, shock, death).

2. Then levothyroxine (thyroid hormone)

3. Then sex steroids (estrogen/progesterone or testosterone)

4. Then GH (if needed)

Note: Posterior pituitary is PRESERVED (has separate blood supply via inferior hypophyseal arteries) — no diabetes insipidus unless stalk is damaged.

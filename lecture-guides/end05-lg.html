1C21 steroids vs. androgens vs. estrogens

The Gist All steroid hormones share the 4-ring cholesterol-derived backbone but differ by carbon count. C21 = progestins (progesterone) + corticoids (cortisol, aldosterone) — retain the full side chain. C19 androgens = DHEA, androstenedione, testosterone, DHT — lose 2 carbons via 17,20-lyase. C18 estrogens = estrone, estradiol, estriol — lose the C19 methyl group AND gain an aromatic A-ring via aromatase.
📚 Going Deeper will expand on:
  • (1) Carbon count as a fingerprint — C21 / C19 / C18 across the three classes.
  • (2) The two critical conversion enzymes — 17,20-lyase (C21 → C19) and aromatase (C19 → C18).
  • (3) Why the aromatic A-ring matters — changes hormone properties (binding, excretion).

★ HIGH-YIELD for USMLE
  • C21 = corticoids/progestins, C19 = androgens, C18 = estrogens. end05 lecture; FA 3.11 Adrenal steroids and CAH
  • 17,20-lyase bridges C21 → C19 (the sex steroid fork). end05 lecture
  • Aromatase bridges C19 → C18 (androgen → estrogen). end05 lecture
  • Estrogens are aromatic — the A-ring is a benzene ring. Unique among steroid hormones. end05 lecture

2Androgens and estrogens — hormones and receptor affinities

The Gist Androgens (in decreasing AR affinity): DHT > testosterone >> androstenedione > DHEA / DHEA-S. DHEA and androstenedione are weak androgens — they mainly serve as peripheral precursors. Estrogens (in decreasing ER affinity): 17β-estradiol (E2) > estrone (E1) > estriol (E3). There are two ER genes (ERα, ERβ), one AR gene (X-linked), one PR gene; all are cytoplasmic nuclear receptors. Membrane versions also exist for rapid non-genomic effects.
📚 Going Deeper will expand on:
  • (1) Androgen potency ranking — why DHT is 2–5× more potent than testosterone.
  • (2) Estrogen potency ranking — which estrogen dominates at each life stage (estradiol → estrone → estriol).
  • (3) Receptors — ERα / ERβ, AR (X-linked), PR; genomic and non-genomic signaling.
  • (4) Progesterone's receptor — PR; distinct from MR and GR.

3Sources of androgens and estrogens

The Gist Androgens come from THREE sources: (1) testicular Leydig cells (primary source of testosterone in males), (2) ovarian thecal cells (primary source of androstenedione in females), (3) adrenal reticularis (DHEA, DHEA-S, androstenedione). Estrogens come from: (1) ovarian granulosa cells via aromatase on thecal-derived androgens (two-cell / two-gonadotropin model), (2) placenta during pregnancy (estriol), (3) peripheral aromatase in adipose and bone (estrone postmenopausally).
📚 Going Deeper will expand on:
  • (1) Leydig vs thecal cells — gonadal androgen production in males vs females.
  • (2) The two-cell / two-gonadotropin model — LH on theca, FSH on granulosa.
  • (3) Peripheral conversion — adipose tissue, skin, bone express aromatase and 5α-reductase.
  • (4) Placental estriol — pregnancy-specific source; dependent on fetal DHEA-S.
  • (5) Lifetime changes — premenopause → postmenopause shift.

📚 Going Deeper — every source in detail

1 · Gonadal sources — Leydig vs thecal cells

Testis (males)
  • Leydig cells (interstitial):
    • Located between seminiferous tubules.
    • Primary source of testosterone.end05 lecture; B&B 4.02 Male Reproductive Hormones
    • Stimulated by LH (which is Gs-coupled → cAMP).
  • Sertoli cells (seminiferous tubule):
    • Nurse spermatogonia; produce androgen-binding protein (ABP) to concentrate testosterone locally.
    • Produce inhibin B (selective FSH inhibitor) and anti-Müllerian hormone (AMH) in fetal life.
Ovary (females)
  • Thecal cells:
    • Surround each developing follicle.
    • Produce androstenedione primarily (LH-stimulated).end05 lecture; B&B 4.03 Female Reproductive Hormones
    • Most of the androgen diffuses into granulosa cells for estrogen synthesis.
  • Granulosa cells:
    • Inner follicle cells.
    • FSH-stimulated; aromatase converts thecal androstenedione → estrone → estradiol.

2 · Two-cell / two-gonadotropin model

  • Rule:
    • LH stimulates thecal cells to make androstenedione.B&B 4.03 Female Reproductive Hormones; FA 3.11
    • FSH stimulates granulosa cells to aromatize androstenedione → estrogens.
  • Clinical relevance:
    • LH spike at mid-cycle (from the estradiol positive-feedback surge) triggers ovulation.
    • This coordinated 2-cell / 2-hormone dance is the basis of the menstrual cycle.

3 · Adrenal sources

  • Zona reticularis:
    • Primary adrenal source of DHEA, DHEA-S, and some androstenedione.FA 3.11 Adrenal steroids and CAH; end04 lecture
    • Small contribution to circulating testosterone via peripheral conversion.
    • Adrenarche begins at age 6–8, peaks in mid-20s.
  • Clinical:
    • Adrenal androgen excess (CAH, tumors) → virilization.

4 · Peripheral conversion

  • 5α-reductase (skin, prostate, external genitalia, hair follicles):
    • Converts testosterone → DHT (more potent).end05 lecture; Bootcamp 6.14 5-α-reductase Deficiency
    • DHT drives male external genital formation in utero + secondary sexual characteristics at puberty.
  • Aromatase (adipose, bone, brain, gonads):
    • Converts androgens → estrogens.
    • Major contributor to postmenopausal estrogen production.end05 lecture
  • 17β-HSD (tissue-specific isoforms):
    • 17β-HSD3 (androgenic): androstenedione → testosterone in Leydig cells.
    • 17β-HSD1 (estrogenic): estrone → estradiol in granulosa cells.end05 lecture

5 · Placenta and pregnancy

  • Fetal-placental unit:
    • Placenta lacks 17α-hydroxylase; cannot make DHEA on its own.
    • Uses fetal adrenal DHEA-S → placenta desulfates → aromatizes → estriol.AI Generated · obstetrics
  • Clinical:
    • Estriol is a marker of fetal-placental health.
    • Progesterone maintained by placenta through most of pregnancy.

6 · Lifetime changes

  • Premenopausal:
    • 17β-estradiol dominant (from granulosa cells).
  • Postmenopausal:
    • Ovarian output drops drastically.
    • Estrone becomes dominant — produced peripherally from adrenal androstenedione.end05 lecture
    • Obesity increases postmenopausal estrogen (more aromatase in adipose) → risk factor for endometrial hyperplasia and breast cancer.AI Generated · oncology

  • Two-cell / two-gonadotropin model: LH on theca (androstenedione), FSH on granulosa (aromatase → estradiol). B&B 4.03
  • DHT is made peripherally by 5α-reductase; not directly secreted. end05 lecture; Bootcamp 6.14
  • Postmenopausal dominant estrogen = estrone, via adipose aromatase on adrenal androstenedione. end05 lecture
  • Pregnancy dominant estrogen = estriol, from the fetal-placental unit. end05 lecture

4Biosynthesis pathways for androgens and estrogens

The Gist Androgen synthesis begins exactly like adrenal steroidogenesis: cholesterol → pregnenolone → 17-OH-pregnenolone → DHEA → androstenedione → testosterone (17β-HSD3 in Leydig cells). Peripherally, 5α-reductase converts testosterone to the more potent DHT; aromatase converts androgens to estrogens (testosterone → 17β-estradiol; androstenedione → estrone → 17β-estradiol via 17β-HSD1).

  • (2) Ovarian estrogen synthesis — thecal androstenedione → granulosa estrogens.
  • (3) Peripheral conversion — 5α-reductase and aromatase in target tissues.
  • (4) Progesterone — corpus luteum + placental origin.
  • (5) Enzyme deficiencies — 5α-reductase deficiency, 17β-HSD3 deficiency.

1 · Testosterone synthesis — the Leydig cell pathway

Step-by-step
  • Starting material:
    • Cholesterol → pregnenolone via desmolase (as in adrenal steroidogenesis).end05 lecture
  • Key Δ5 pathway steps:
    • Pregnenolone → 17-OH-pregnenolone (17α-hydroxylase).
    • 17-OH-pregnenolone → DHEA (17,20-lyase).
  • Convert to Δ4:
    • DHEA → androstenedione (3β-HSD).
  • Final reduction to testosterone:
    • Androstenedione → testosterone (17β-HSD3, the "androgenic" 17β-HSD).
    • Reduction reaction (ketone → alcohol at C17).end05 lecture

2 · Ovarian estrogen synthesis — the two-cell model

Theca makes precursor
  • LH at theca:
    • LH binds Gs-coupled receptor → ↑ cAMP → ↑ steroidogenesis.B&B 4.03 Female Reproductive Hormones
    • Thecal cells make androstenedione (primary product).
    • Thecal cells LACK aromatase.
Granulosa aromatizes to estrogen
  • FSH at granulosa:
    • FSH binds Gs-coupled receptor → ↑ cAMP → ↑ aromatase.
    • Granulosa cells express aromatase.
  • Aromatase actions:
    • Androstenedione → estrone (E1).
    • Testosterone → 17β-estradiol (E2) directly (via aromatization).
  • 17β-HSD1 (estrogenic):
    • Estrone → 17β-estradiol.
    • Higher affinity for estrogen substrates than 17β-HSD3.end05 lecture

3 · Peripheral conversion

5α-reductase
  • Where expressed:
    • Skin (esp. scrotum, face, scalp), prostate, external genitalia, hair follicles.end05 lecture; Bootcamp 6.14 5-α-reductase Deficiency
  • Function:
    • Testosterone → DHT.
    • DHT binds AR with 2–5× affinity vs. testosterone.
  • Physiologic role:
    • Male external genital differentiation in utero.
    • Secondary sexual characteristics at puberty (facial hair, deepening voice, prostate growth).
  • Pharmacologic inhibition:
    • Finasteride, dutasteride — used for BPH, male-pattern baldness.AI Generated · urology pharm
Aromatase
  • Where expressed:
    • Ovarian granulosa, adipose, skin, bone, brain, placenta.end05 lecture
  • Function:
    • Converts androgens → estrogens.
  • Pharmacologic inhibition:
    • Anastrozole, letrozole (nonsteroidal); exemestane (steroidal).
    • Postmenopausal ER+ breast cancer first-line.AI Generated · oncology pharm

4 · Enzyme deficiencies

5α-reductase deficiency
  • Genetics:
    • Autosomal recessive.Bootcamp 6.14
  • Mechanism:
    • Cannot make DHT → male external genital differentiation fails in utero.
    • Internal male structures (derived from Wolffian ducts, which need testosterone only) form normally.
  • Presentation:
    • 46,XY infants with ambiguous or female-appearing external genitalia.
    • At puberty, rising testosterone triggers virilization ("guevedoces").
  • Treatment:
    • Depends on clinical / social context; DHT can be supplemented.
17β-HSD3 deficiency (rare)
  • Mechanism:
    • Cannot convert androstenedione → testosterone in Leydig cells.AI Generated · endocrine / repro
  • Presentation:
    • Similar to 5α-reductase deficiency: 46,XY ambiguous genitalia at birth.
    • Some virilization at puberty from peripheral conversion.

Final enzyme in testosterone synthesis, and where?
17β-HSD3 (androgenic 17β-HSD) in Leydig cells — converts androstenedione → testosterone.
Two peripheral conversions of testosterone?
5α-reductase → DHT (more potent androgen). Aromatase → 17β-estradiol.

5Circulation and inactivation of sex steroids

The Gist Sex steroids have SHORT half-lives. Most androgens and estrogens: <20 min. Testosterone and androstenedione can last up to an hour. Progesterone: ~5 min (very short). Testosterone carrier distribution: ~60% SHBG, ~38% albumin, ~2% free. Estrogen distribution: ~60% SHBG, ~20% albumin, ~20% free. Inactivated by liver glucuronide/sulfate conjugation → kidney excretion. 17β-estradiol can be converted to weaker estrogens (estrone, estriol).

📚 Going Deeper — carriers and inactivation

1 · SHBG — the main sex steroid carrier

What it binds
  • Preferences:
    • Testosterone and DHT (high affinity).end05 lecture; FA 3.11
    • Estradiol (lower affinity than testosterone).
  • Clinical impact:
    • SHBG level strongly determines free testosterone — the biologically active pool.
SHBG regulation
  • ↑ SHBG (reduces free T):
    • Estrogen (pregnancy, OCPs, hyperthyroidism, cirrhosis, anorexia).AI Generated · end01 carrier concepts + clinical elsewhere
    • Aging (gradual rise in men).
  • ↓ SHBG (raises free T):
    • Androgens, obesity, hyperinsulinemia (PCOS, T2DM), hypothyroidism.
  • Clinical consequence:
    • PCOS: ↓ SHBG → ↑ free testosterone → hirsutism, acne, irregular menses.AI Generated · repro
    • Hyperthyroidism: ↑ SHBG → can reveal low free testosterone in men (gynecomastia).

2 · Short half-lives — why sex steroids don't last

  • Why short:
    • Relatively small free fraction + efficient hepatic uptake and conjugation.end05 lecture
    • Unlike cortisol (~70 min) or DHEA-S (7–10 h), sex steroids cycle fast.
  • Progesterone is the shortest:
    • ~5 min half-life.
    • Weak CBG/albumin binding.
  • Clinical implication:
    • Oral testosterone undergoes extensive first-pass metabolism — IM/transdermal/implantable preferred.AI Generated · urology pharm

3 · Hepatic inactivation

  • Glucuronide / sulfate conjugation:
    • Liver conjugates at various hydroxyl groups → water-soluble → kidney excretion.end05 lecture
  • Estradiol → weaker estrogens:
    • 17β-estradiol → estrone (17β-HSD2, oxidative) → estriol (hydroxylation).
    • Estrone and estriol are weaker than estradiol but can still bind ER.

4 · Pregnancy and lifetime changes

  • Pregnancy:
    • Estrogen ↑↑ → ↑ SHBG, CBG, TBG (all carrier proteins).
    • Total testosterone rises but free testosterone stays normal (feedback maintains it).AI Generated · obstetrics
    • Estriol dominates from the fetal-placental unit.
  • Postmenopause:
    • Estrogen production falls; estrone becomes dominant via peripheral aromatase.
    • FSH and LH rise (loss of negative feedback).

    • Gradual decline in testosterone with rising SHBG → free testosterone falls.

6Mechanisms of sex steroid action

The Gist Sex steroid receptors (AR, ER, PR) use the same canonical mechanism as MR and GR: lipophilic hormone crosses the plasma membrane → binds cytoplasmic receptor chaperoned by HSP90 → receptor dimerizes → translocates to nucleus → binds hormone response elements → modulates transcription. Two ER isoforms (ERα, ERβ); one AR gene (X-linked); one PR gene. Membrane-bound variants also exist for rapid non-genomic effects (seconds–minutes) that are increasingly recognized for estrogen and progesterone.

📚 Going Deeper — receptor mechanisms and clinical modulators

1 · Canonical nuclear receptor mechanism

  • Resting state:
    • AR, ER, PR sit in the cytoplasm bound to HSP90 (like MR and GR).end05 lecture; FA 3.15 Signaling pathways of steroid hormones
  • Activation:
    • Lipophilic hormone crosses plasma membrane → binds receptor → HSPs dissociate.
    • Receptor dimerizes, translocates to nucleus.
    • Dimer binds palindromic hormone response element on DNA (ARE / ERE / PRE).
    • Modulates transcription (up or down depending on coactivators/corepressors).
  • Timescale:
    • Hours for full transcriptional / translational effects.

2 · ERα vs ERβ

  • ERα (ESR1):
    • Dominant in breast, uterus, liver, hypothalamus, bone.end05 lecture; B&B 4.03
    • Drives classic estrogenic effects: breast proliferation, endometrial growth, bone preservation, lipid regulation.
  • ERβ (ESR2):
    • Dominant in prostate, ovary, CNS, cardiovascular tissues.
    • Often opposes or modulates ERα activity.
    • Target of some SERMs.
  • Clinical implication:
    • SERM tissue selectivity reflects differential ERα / ERβ engagement + tissue coregulator context.

3 · AR is X-linked — inheritance of AIS

  • Mechanism:
    • AR gene is on the X chromosome.end05 lecture
    • Loss-of-function mutation in a 46,XY individual → Complete Androgen Insensitivity Syndrome (CAIS).
  • Phenotype:
    • Phenotypic female at birth; no axillary/pubic hair at puberty; primary amenorrhea; blind-ending vagina; testes in abdomen or inguinal canal.AI Generated · genetics / repro
  • Inheritance:
    • X-linked recessive — carrier mothers have unaffected daughters and 50% affected sons.

4 · Non-genomic (rapid) effects

  • Membrane receptors:
    • GPER / GPR30 is a membrane estrogen receptor that triggers rapid MAPK / PI3K signaling.end05 lecture
    • Membrane PR also exists.
  • Effects:
    • Seconds to minutes (too fast for transcription).
    • Examples: estrogen-induced vasodilation, rapid CNS effects.
  • Clinical significance:
    • Less well-characterized than genomic effects but increasingly appreciated in cardiovascular and CNS pharmacology.

5 · Pharmacologic modulators

SERMs (selective estrogen receptor modulators)
  • Tamoxifen:
    • Antagonist in breast; agonist in bone and endometrium.
    • Adjuvant in ER+ breast cancer (pre- and postmenopausal).
    • Increases risk of endometrial cancer and VTE.AI Generated · oncology pharm
  • Raloxifene:
    • Antagonist in breast and endometrium; agonist in bone.
    • Osteoporosis treatment + breast cancer prevention.
    • No endometrial cancer risk.
  • Clomiphene:
    • Antagonist at hypothalamic ER → ↑ GnRH pulsatility → ↑ LH, FSH → ovulation induction.AI Generated · repro pharm
Aromatase inhibitors
  • Nonsteroidal (reversible):
    • Anastrozole, letrozole.
    • Used in postmenopausal ER+ breast cancer.AI Generated · oncology pharm
  • Steroidal (irreversible):
    • Exemestane.
Anti-androgens
  • AR antagonists:
    • Flutamide, bicalutamide, enzalutamide (prostate cancer).AI Generated · oncology pharm
    • Spironolactone (off-target AR antagonism) — PCOS, hirsutism, transgender care.
  • 5α-reductase inhibitors:
    • Finasteride, dutasteride (BPH, male-pattern baldness).
  • GnRH modulation:
    • Leuprolide (continuous) → suppresses LH/FSH → chemical castration (prostate CA, endometriosis).AI Generated · see end02
PR modulators
  • Mifepristone (RU-486):
    • PR antagonist (also GR antagonist).
    • Medical abortion; Cushing syndrome (off-label).AI Generated · repro / endocrine pharm

LO 1 — HPG Axis

GnRH: Pulsatile Release & Signaling

GnRH is a decapeptide from the hypothalamic arcuate nucleus. It is released pulsatile (every 60-90 min) into the hypothalamic-hypophyseal portal blood and binds Gq-coupled receptors on anterior pituitary gonadotrophs.

  • Pulsatile GnRH: Regular pulses → LH and FSH secretion
  • Continuous GnRH: Constant exposure → receptor downregulation → ↓LH/↓FSH (therapeutic use in endometriosis, precocious puberty)
  • Pulse frequency: Slow (1 per 2-3h) favors FSH; fast (1 per 60min) favors LH

LH and FSH: Glycoprotein Hormones with Shared α-Subunit

Both share the same α-subunit (also in TSH and hCG). Their β-subunits confer specificity. Both are Gs-coupled → ↑cAMP.

  • LH: Ovulation trigger, luteinization; Leydig cell testosterone
  • FSH: Follicle growth, granulosa aromatase (estradiol); Sertoli function
  • Inhibin B: From Sertoli (males) and granulosa (females); specifically inhibits FSH

OCP Mechanism

  • Progestin: Suppresses LH surge → prevents ovulation; thickens cervical mucus
  • Estrogen: Suppresses FSH → no follicle development

LO 2 — GH Axis Overview

Dual Control: GHRH and Somatostatin

  • GHRH (arcuate, Gs-coupled): ↑cAMP → ↑GH
  • Somatostatin (periventricular, Gi-coupled): ↓cAMP → ↓GH

GH Secretion Pattern

  • Sleep-related: Peak during slow-wave NREM sleep (stage 3-4)
  • Exercise, fasting, stress, puberty: All ↑GH
  • Inhibited by: Hyperglycemia, obesity, elevated FFAs, IGF-1 negative feedback

GH Direct vs Indirect Effects

Direct (diabetogenic): ↑Lipolysis, ↑gluconeogenesis, ↓glucose uptake (anti-insulin)

Indirect via IGF-1: Liver IGF-1 → linear growth, protein synthesis

GH Signaling: JAK-STAT (NOT cAMP, NOT RTK)

GH receptor (cytokine receptor) → JAK-STAT → transcription

Clinical Monitoring

  • GH levels: Pulsatile, unreliable
  • IGF-1: Stable, preferred for acromegaly monitoring
EXAM TRAP

GH is anti-insulin (raises glucose), NOT insulin-like. Remember catabolic GH mobilizes energy.

LO 3 — HPT Axis Overview

The HPT Cascade: TRH → TSH → T3/T4

TRH (tripeptide, Gq-coupled) from hypothalamus stimulates TWO pituitary hormones:

  • TSH (primary effect)
  • Prolactin (secondary effect — critical in hypothyroidism)

TSH (glycoprotein, Gs-coupled) stimulates all thyroid synthesis steps: iodide uptake, thyroglobulin synthesis, iodination, T3/T4 release, cell growth.

T3 vs T4

  • T4: Abundant, inactive prohormone → T3 by 5'-deiodinase
  • T3: 3-4× more potent, faster onset

TSH Lab Interpretation: 4-Box Table

TSHFree T4Diagnosis
↑↓Primary Hypothyroidism
↓↓Secondary/Tertiary Hypothyroidism
↓↑Primary Hyperthyroidism
↑↑TSH-secreting adenoma or resistance

Key Point: TRH Stimulates Prolactin

In hypothyroidism, ↑TRH → ↑prolactin → galactorrhea. Treat with levothyroxine (NOT dopamine agonist).

HY FACT: Hypothyroidism-related galactorrhea has normal MRI pituitary. Levothyroxine is curative.

LO 4 — Cross-Axis Interactions

Hyperprolactinemia → Amenorrhea

  • Mechanism: Prolactin ↓kisspeptin → ↓GnRH pulsatility → ↓LH/FSH → amenorrhea
  • Causes: Prolactinoma, breastfeeding, dopamine antagonists

Hypothyroidism → Galactorrhea

  • Mechanism: ↑TRH → ↑prolactin → galactorrhea
  • Key: Normal MRI pituitary (not a prolactinoma)
  • Treatment: Levothyroxine (NOT dopamine agonist)

Kisspeptin: Master GnRH Regulator

Kisspeptin neurons (arcuate and anteroventral periventricular nuclei) are the most potent GnRH stimulators:

  • Estrogen positive feedback: Works through kisspeptin to trigger LH surge
  • Leptin signaling: Leptin → ↑kisspeptin; low leptin (fasting, anorexia) → ↓kisspeptin → ↓GnRH
  • Puberty: Increased kisspeptin expression marks puberty onset

Stress & Glucocorticoids

Suppress HPG axis at multiple levels → functional hypogonadism

Female Athlete Triad

  1. Low energy availability (insufficient calories)
  2. Menstrual dysfunction (amenorrhea from ↓leptin→↓kisspeptin→↓GnRH)
  3. Low bone mineral density (estrogen deficiency)

Treatment: Restore energy balance. Menses often resume before ideal BMI.

Kallmann Syndrome

Definition: Idiopathic hypogonadotropic hypogonadism + anosmia.

Pathophysiology: GnRH neurons fail to migrate from olfactory placode → absent/dysfunctional GnRH neurons.

  • Key diagnostic clue: ANOSMIA — absent sense of smell is pathognomonic
  • Imaging: Absent/hypoplastic olfactory bulbs on MRI
  • Males: Delayed puberty, cryptorchidism, micropenis, infertility
  • Females: Primary amenorrhea, absent breast development
  • Treatment: Pulsatile GnRH (fertility) or HRT

Turner Syndrome (45,XO)

  • Short stature, webbed neck, broad chest
  • Streak gonads → hypergonadotropic hypogonadism (high FSH/LH, low estradiol)
  • Primary amenorrhea, infertility (unless donor oocytes)
  • Cardiac: Bicuspid AV valve, coarctation of aorta (most common)
  • Renal: Dysgenesis, horseshoe kidney
  • Autoimmune: Thyroiditis, celiac, type 1 diabetes
  • Treatment: GH + estrogen/progestin HRT; oocyte donation

Klinefelter Syndrome (47,XXY)

  • Tall, gynecomastia, small firm testes
  • Azoospermia → infertility
  • Labs: ↑FSH (Sertoli damage), ↑LH, ↓testosterone
  • Treatment: Testosterone replacement (virilization, bone health; NOT fertility)

Primary Ovarian Insufficiency (POI)

Diagnosis: Amenorrhea ≥4 months in woman <40yo + FSH >40 IU/L on TWO occasions ≥4 weeks apart.

  • Causes: Autoimmune (most common), FMR1 premutation, chemotherapy, radiation
  • Treatment: HRT for symptoms/bone health; oocyte donation for fertility

Functional Hypothalamic Amenorrhea (FHA)

Mechanism: Low body weight/fat → ↓leptin → ↓kisspeptin → ↓GnRH pulsatility → ↓LH/FSH → low estradiol → amenorrhea.

  • Associated with: Excessive exercise, eating disorders, stress, rapid weight loss
  • Labs: Low LH/FSH/estradiol, normal prolactin/TSH (distinguishes from other causes)
  • Treatment: Weight restoration (most important), reduce exercise, nutrition counseling
  • Bone consequences: Chronic estrogen deficiency → low bone density → stress fractures
EXAM TRAP

Kallmann = hypogonadotropic hypogonadism + ANOSMIA. The combination is pathognomonic.

Practice Questions

Q1

A 19-year-old male presents with absent puberty, anosmia, and absent olfactory bulbs on MRI. Labs: low LH, low FSH, low testosterone.

A. Primary hypergonadotropic hypogonadism
B. Kallmann syndrome
C. Constitutional delay of puberty
D. Klinefelter syndrome
Correct: B — Kallmann Syndrome

Hypogonadotropic hypogonadism + anosmia + absent olfactory bulbs on MRI is pathognomonic for Kallmann syndrome (GnRH neuron migration failure from olfactory placode).

Q2

A 28-year-old female marathon runner with 8 months amenorrhea. Labs: LH 3.2 (low), FSH 4.1 (low), estradiol 15 (low), prolactin 12 (normal), TSH 2.1 (normal). T-score: -1.5.

A. Prolactinoma
B. Primary hypothyroidism
C. Functional hypothalamic amenorrhea
D. Primary ovarian insufficiency
Correct: C — Functional Hypothalamic Amenorrhea

Low LH/FSH + low estradiol + normal prolactin/TSH in an athletic woman with low bone density (T-score -1.5) = FHA from ↓leptin→↓kisspeptin→↓GnRH pulsatility. Treatment: weight restoration.

Q3

A 24-year-old female with hypothyroidism presents with galactorrhea and amenorrhea. TSH 12.5, fT4 0.4, prolactin 48. MRI pituitary: normal, no adenoma.

A. Bromocriptine
B. Pituitary surgery
C. Levothyroxine therapy
D. Estrogen replacement
Correct: C — Levothyroxine Therapy

In hypothyroidism, ↑TRH stimulates BOTH TSH and prolactin. The galactorrhea is from TRH overstimulation, NOT a prolactinoma (MRI normal). Levothyroxine normalizes TRH and allows prolactin to fall.

Q4

A 16-year-old male: tall stature, small firm testes, gynecomastia, azoospermia. Labs: FSH 35 (↑), LH 18 (↑), testosterone 150 (↓).

A. 46,XY
B. 47,XXY
C. 45,XO
D. 46,XY with Y microdeletion
Correct: B — 47,XXY (Klinefelter)

Tall stature, small firm testes, gynecomastia, azoospermia, elevated FSH/LH, low testosterone = Klinefelter. FSH is disproportionately elevated (Sertoli cell damage). Treatment: testosterone replacement.

Q5

A 14-year-old female: primary amenorrhea, short stature (130 cm), webbed neck, broad chest, bicuspid AV valve. Labs: FSH 45, LH 20, estradiol 10.

A. 45,XO (Turner syndrome)
B. 46,XY testicular dysgenesis
C. 46,XX with 21-hydroxylase deficiency
D. 47,XXX
Correct: A — 45,XO (Turner)

Short stature, webbed neck, broad chest, primary amenorrhea with high FSH/LH (streak gonads), cardiac defect (bicuspid AV valve, coarctation) = Turner. Management: GH + HRT + cardiac/renal screening.

Q6

On day 21 of a 28-day menstrual cycle, which hormone would you expect at its highest level?

A. FSH
B. LH
C. Progesterone
D. Estradiol
Correct: C — Progesterone

Day 21 is mid-luteal phase. The corpus luteum is maximally functional and produces peak progesterone (~day 21). This is when the endometrium is maximally secretory (ready for implantation).

Q7

A patient receives 6-month depot leuprolide (GnRH agonist) for endometriosis. Initial LH/FSH rise occurs (flare), then suppression. What causes the eventual suppression?

A. Competitive inhibition of GnRH receptor
B. Continuous stimulation leads to receptor desensitization and downregulation
C. Leuprolide converts to GnRH antagonist
D. Negative feedback from elevated sex steroids
Correct: B — Continuous stimulation leads to receptor desensitization and downregulation

GnRH agonists work paradoxically: chronic continuous stimulation causes pituitary GnRH receptor desensitization and downregulation, resulting in suppression of LH/FSH below baseline (hypogonadal state).

Q8

A 45-year-old man with acromegaly is monitored for treatment response. Which laboratory marker is MOST reliable?

A. Random GH level
B. Morning GH level
C. IGF-1 level
D. GH suppression test with glucose
Correct: C — IGF-1 Level

GH is highly pulsatile and unreliable. IGF-1 is stable throughout the day and reflects integrated GH effect over time. IGF-1 is the preferred marker for both diagnosis and monitoring of acromegaly.

Q9

A 22-year-old man with fasting hypoglycemia shows robust GH rise on stimulation test. During hypoglycemia, which regulatory hormones change occurs?

A. ↓GHRH and ↑Somatostatin
B. ↑GHRH and ↓Somatostatin
C. Normal GHRH and normal Somatostatin
D. ↑Somatostatin and ↑GH directly
Correct: B — ↑GHRH and ↓Somatostatin

During hypoglycemia (stress), the hypothalamus increases GHRH and decreases somatostatin → ↑GH. GH then mobilizes glucose via anti-insulin effects (↑lipolysis, ↑gluconeogenesis).

Q10

Which glycoprotein hormones share the same α-subunit?

A. LH and prolactin
B. FSH and GH
C. TSH, LH, FSH, and hCG
D. ACTH and prolactin
Correct: C — TSH, LH, FSH, and hCG

These four glycoprotein hormones share a common α-subunit (chromosome 6). Each has a unique β-subunit conferring specificity. hCG cross-reacts with LH receptors (maintaining corpus luteum in early pregnancy).

Q11

A 35-year-old woman is diagnosed with POI. Which laboratory finding is required for formal diagnosis?

A. Single FSH level >40 IU/L
B. FSH >40 IU/L on two occasions at least 4 weeks apart
C. Estradiol <10 pg/mL on any single draw
D. Elevated antimüllerian hormone (AMH)
Correct: B — FSH >40 IU/L on two occasions at least 4 weeks apart

POI diagnosis requires: (1) Amenorrhea ≥4 months in woman <40yo, AND (2) FSH >40 on two separate occasions ≥4 weeks apart. A single FSH is insufficient; FSH fluctuates and occasional ovulation may occur.

Q12

Which pathway does GH signaling use?

A. Gs-coupled → cAMP → PKA
B. Gq-coupled → IP3/DAG → PKC
C. Receptor tyrosine kinase (RTK)
D. Cytokine receptor → JAK-STAT
Correct: D — Cytokine receptor → JAK-STAT

GH binds a cytokine receptor (not GPCR, not RTK) and signals through JAK-STAT. Activated STAT proteins dimerize, translocate to nucleus, and initiate transcription of growth-related genes.

Q13

A patient develops hypothyroidism and subsequently has galactorrhea and amenorrhea. Prolactin is elevated but MRI pituitary is normal. Which hormone is responsible for elevated prolactin?

A. TSH directly stimulates prolactin
B. Low T4 directly stimulates lactotroph cells
C. TRH stimulates both TSH and prolactin
D. Cortisol from stress increases prolactin
Correct: C — TRH stimulates both TSH and prolactin

In hypothyroidism, low T4 leads to elevated TRH (loss of negative feedback). TRH is Gq-coupled and stimulates BOTH TSH AND prolactin. Levothyroxine normalizes TRH and prolactin drops.

Q14

Inhibin B is produced by which cells and specifically inhibits which gonadotropin?

A. Leydig cells; inhibits LH
B. Sertoli cells (males) and granulosa cells (females); specifically inhibits FSH
C. Theca interna cells; inhibits both LH and FSH
D. Corpus luteum; inhibits GnRH
Correct: B — Sertoli cells (males) and granulosa cells (females); specifically inhibits FSH

Inhibin B from Sertoli cells (males, supporting spermatogenesis) and granulosa cells (females, developing follicles) exerts selective negative feedback on FSH at the pituitary level. LH is controlled by estradiol and progesterone feedback.

Q15

A 22-year-old female athlete: 3 months weight loss after intense training. Labs: LH 2.1 (low), FSH 3.4 (low), estradiol 20 (low), prolactin 8 (normal), TSH 2.3 (normal). Which molecular pathway is most directly disrupted?

A. Dopamine inhibition of prolactin
B. TSH stimulation of thyroid
C. Leptin → Kisspeptin → GnRH pulsatility
D. Estradiol positive feedback on LH
Correct: C — Leptin → Kisspeptin → GnRH pulsatility

Low body weight/fat → reduced leptin → decreased kisspeptin neuron activity → decreased GnRH pulsatility → suppressed LH/FSH → low estradiol. Leptin is the metabolic signal linking energy status to reproduction via kisspeptin.

Q16

Which clinical finding MOST reliably distinguishes Kallmann syndrome from other hypogonadotropic hypogonadism causes?

A. Anosmia (absent sense of smell)
B. Low testosterone
C. Low LH and FSH
D. Absent puberty
Correct: A — Anosmia (absent sense of smell)

Anosmia is pathognomonic for Kallmann because GnRH neurons originate from the olfactory placode and migrate to the hypothalamus. In Kallmann, this migration fails → absent olfactory bulbs (anosmia) and absent GnRH neurons. The combination is diagnostic.

Q17

A woman exclusively breastfeeding her 3-month-old has not menstruated since delivery. Which mechanism explains lactational amenorrhea?

A. Oxytocin directly inhibits GnRH
B. Milk production consumes amino acids for hormone synthesis
C. Suckling → ↑Prolactin → ↓Kisspeptin → ↓GnRH → ↓LH/FSH → ↓Estradiol
D. hCG from residual placenta suppresses FSH
Correct: C — Suckling → ↑Prolactin → ↓Kisspeptin → ↓GnRH → ↓LH/FSH → ↓Estradiol

Breastfeeding stimulates prolactin release, which suppresses kisspeptin neurons. This reduces GnRH pulsatility and suppresses LH/FSH. This is lactational amenorrhea, a natural postpartum contraceptive. Menses resume when breastfeeding stops.

Q18

In males, which gonadotropin primarily supports spermatogenesis, and which cells does it act upon?

A. LH acts on both Leydig and Sertoli cells
B. FSH acts on Sertoli cells; LH acts on Leydig cells for testosterone
C. LH acts on Sertoli cells; FSH acts on Leydig cells
D. Both FSH and LH act on Sertoli cells equally
Correct: B — FSH acts on Sertoli cells; LH acts on Leydig cells for testosterone

FSH stimulates Sertoli cell maturation and supports spermatogenesis directly. LH stimulates Leydig cells to produce testosterone, which is also necessary for spermatogenesis. Both hormones are required for normal male fertility.

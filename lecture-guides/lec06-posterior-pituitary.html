ADH (Antidiuretic Hormone / Vasopressin): Synthesis and Structure

Synthesis and Transport

  • Synthesized in: Supraoptic nucleus (ADH primarily) and paraventricular nucleus of hypothalamus
  • Transport: Down axons to posterior pituitary (neurohypophysis)
  • Storage: Herring bodies (axon terminals in posterior pituitary)
  • Release: Action potentials trigger Ca²⁺ influx → vesicle fusion → ADH release

Structure and Nomenclature

  • Neuropeptide: 9 amino acids (octapeptide with cysteine bridge)
  • Similarity to oxytocin: Differ in only 2 amino acids at positions 3 and 8 (both disulfide-linked)
  • Names: ADH = AVP (arginine vasopressin) = vasopressin
  • Sequence: Cys-Tyr-Phe-Gln-Asn-Cys-Pro-Arg-Gly-NH₂

Receptor Types and Actions

  • V1 (Gq-coupled):
    • Location: Vascular smooth muscle (arteries, arterioles)
    • Action: Vasoconstriction (↑BP)
    • Also on liver: glycogenolysis (↑glucose)
    • Also on platelets: aggregation (procoagulant)
  • V2 (Gs-coupled, ↑cAMP):
    • Location: Principal cells of collecting duct
    • Action: Inserts AQP2 (aquaporin-2) water channels into apical membrane → water reabsorption
    • Also contributes to urea reabsorption in inner medullary collecting duct

Clinical Pharmacology

  • DDAVP (desmopressin): V2-selective agonist
    • Uses: Central diabetes insipidus, hemophilia A, von Willebrand disease (releases vWF from endothelium)
    • Route: Intranasal, oral, IV, IM, SQ
  • Terlipressin: V1 agonist, longer half-life
    • Uses: Esophageal varices, hepatorenal syndrome
ADH is a 9-amino acid neuropeptide synthesized in the hypothalamus and stored in the posterior pituitary. V2 receptors on collecting duct principal cells mediate water reabsorption via AQP2 insertion; V1 receptors on vascular smooth muscle cause vasoconstriction.
ADH and oxytocin are "sister" peptides that differ in only 2 amino acids but have completely different effects. Know the receptor types: V1 = Gq (vasoconstriction), V2 = Gs (water reabsorption).

ADH Regulation and Physiology

Stimuli for ADH Release

  • ↑Plasma osmolarity (most potent): Sensed by osmoreceptors in anterior hypothalamus (around organum vasculosum of lamina terminalis)
    • Threshold: ~280–290 mOsm/kg
    • Linear increase above threshold
  • ↓Blood volume or ↓BP: Sensed by baroreceptors in atria, carotid sinus, aortic arch
    • Requires ~10% decrease (less sensitive than osmolarity)
    • Potent trigger once activated
  • Pain, stress, nausea, surgery, hypoxia
  • Drugs causing SIADH: Nicotine, morphine, tricyclic antidepressants, carbamazepine, cyclophosphamide (SIADH-causing)
  • Angiotensin II (especially if ↓BP/↓volume)

Inhibitors of ADH Release

  • ↓Plasma osmolarity (most important): Suppresses osmoreceptor firing
  • ↑Blood volume or ↑BP: Activates baroreceptors → inhibit ADH
  • Alcohol (ETOH): Directly inhibits ADH release → polyuria (hence "pissing like a horse" after drinking) and dehydration (hangover)
  • ANP/BNP: Increase water excretion via multiple mechanisms
  • Demeclocycline: V2 receptor antagonist (used therapeutically for SIADH)
  • Lithium: Blocks V2 signaling (used therapeutically but also causes nephrogenic DI as side effect)

ADH Physiology in the Collecting Duct

  • Mechanism of water reabsorption:
    1. ADH binds V2 receptor (Gs-coupled) on principal cells
    2. ↑cAMP via adenylyl cyclase
    3. cAMP activates PKA (protein kinase A)
    4. PKA phosphorylates AQP2-containing vesicles
    5. AQP2 vesicles translocate to and insert into apical membrane
    6. Water enters cell from tubular lumen via AQP2 channels (osmotic gradient)
    7. Water exits cell via AQP3/AQP4 on basolateral membrane
  • Without ADH: AQP2 endocytosed from membrane → apical membrane impermeable to water → dilute urine (polyuria)
  • ADH also increases urea reabsorption: In inner medullary collecting duct, contributes to medullary osmotic gradient

ADH release is primarily regulated by plasma osmolarity (osmoreceptors) and secondarily by blood volume/pressure (baroreceptors). In the collecting duct, ADH → V2 → cAMP → PKA → AQP2 insertion → water reabsorption.
Baroreceptor stimulation requires ~10% volume loss, whereas osmoreceptors are exquisitely sensitive to small changes. Hypovolemia is a powerful ADH stimulus even when osmolarity is normal—this is why hemorrhage suppresses urine output despite plasma dilution.

Diabetes Insipidus (DI)

Central (Neurogenic) Diabetes Insipidus

  • Pathophysiology: ADH deficiency (hypothalamus or posterior pituitary damage)
  • Causes:
    • Head trauma (pituitary stalk transection)
    • Pituitary or hypothalamic surgery (transsphenoidal approach most common)
    • Pituitary tumors (craniopharyngioma, prolactinoma, adenomas)
    • Metastatic cancer to pituitary
    • Sheehan syndrome (postpartum pituitary necrosis)
    • Langerhans cell histiocytosis (LCH)
    • Idiopathic (autoimmune destruction ~30% of cases)
  • Clinical presentation: Polydipsia, polyuria (>3 L/day, often 8–12 L/day), nocturia, enuresis
  • Labs:
    • ↑Serum Na⁺ (>145) — hypernatremia
    • ↑Serum osmolarity (>300)
    • ↓Urine osmolarity (<300, typically <200)
    • ↓Urine specific gravity (<1.005)
    • ↓ADH level (if measured)
  • Water deprivation test:
    • Restrict fluids for 8 hours → measure serum osmolarity, urine osmolarity
    • Central DI: Urine osmolarity remains very low (<300) despite ↑serum osmolarity → GIVE DDAVP → urine osmolarity rises (>600) → confirms kidney is responsive, ADH is the problem
    • Discriminates from nephrogenic DI and psychogenic polydipsia
  • Treatment:
    • DDAVP (desmopressin): Intranasal (preferred), oral, IV, IM, or SQ
      • Dose: Titrate to individual need (avoid overcorrection)
      • Monitor serum Na⁺ q1-2h during acute correction to avoid hyponatremia
    • Thiazide diuretics (paradoxical):
      • Cause mild volume depletion → ↓GFR → ↑proximal tubule reabsorption → less water delivered to collecting duct
      • Also ↓hypokalemia → improve sensitivity
    • NSAIDs: Inhibit prostaglandin synthesis → may modestly reduce urine output
  • Triphasic response post-pituitary surgery:
    1. Acute DI (days 1–3): ADH release stops immediately
    2. SIADH (days 3–5): Stored ADH leaks from damaged stalk (paradoxical excess)
    3. Permanent DI (weeks): If stalk transaction is complete and axons don't regenerate

Nephrogenic Diabetes Insipidus

  • Pathophysiology: ADH present but kidney doesn't respond (V2 receptor or AQP2 defect)
  • Causes:
    • Lithium (most common drug cause): Enters principal cells via ENaC → blocks adenylyl cyclase downstream of V2 → ↓cAMP → AQP2 not inserted
    • Demeclocycline: Direct V2 receptor antagonist (same mechanism, intentional for SIADH therapy)
    • Hypercalcemia: ↓cAMP production, damages collecting duct cells
    • Hypokalemia: Impairs concentrating ability
    • Inherited: X-linked V2 receptor mutations (~90% of hereditary cases), rare AQP2 mutations
  • Labs:
    • ↑ADH level (high! kidney not responding)
    • ↑Serum Na⁺ (>145)
    • ↑Serum osmolarity (>300)
    • ↓Urine osmolarity (very low, <300) — kidneys cannot concentrate despite high ADH
  • Water deprivation test:
    • Urine osmolarity remains low even with dehydration → GIVE DDAVP → urine osmolarity does NOT rise (or rises only slightly) → kidney is the problem, not ADH production
  • Treatment:
    • Thiazide diuretics + low-sodium diet:
      • Volume contraction stimulates proximal tubule reabsorption (before water reaches collecting duct)
      • ↓Distal delivery of fluid → less polyuria despite inability to concentrate
      • Low-Na⁺ diet enhances effect by reducing osmotic load
    • Amiloride (for lithium-induced):
      • Blocks ENaC (epithelial sodium channel) → prevents lithium entry into principal cells
      • Preserves V2 signaling even in presence of lithium
      • Also K⁺-sparing (treats concurrent hypokalemia)
    • NSAIDs: ↓Prostaglandin E2 production → ↓adenylyl cyclase antagonism → modest benefit
    • Adequate hydration: Ensure free access to water to prevent hypernatremia

SIADH (Syndrome of Inappropriate ADH)

Pathophysiology

  • Definition: Inappropriately high ADH (relative to serum osmolarity) → excess water retention and dilution of serum → dilutional hyponatremia
  • Water excess without sodium loss: ADH retains water, but kidneys still excrete Na⁺ normally → euvolemic hyponatremia (no edema, no hypotension)
  • ADH continues to be released: Despite ↓serum osmolarity, kidneys concentrate urine and reabsorb water → Na⁺ diluted, hyponatremia worsens

Causes of SIADH

  • Malignancy (SIADH caused by tumor):
    • Small cell lung cancer (SCLC): Most common malignant cause; produces ectopic ADH (secretion happens from neuroendocrine granules)
    • Also: head/neck, breast, pancreas, bladder, GI cancers (but SCLC is #1)
  • Pulmonary disease:
    • Infections: pneumonia, TB, aspergillosis
    • Positive pressure ventilation (mechanical ventilation, CPAP) — ↓atrial stretch → ADH release
  • CNS disease:
    • Meningitis, encephalitis (viral, bacterial, fungal)
    • Subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH)
    • Head trauma (hypothalamic damage → direct ADH release)
    • Stroke, hypoxia
  • Endocrine:
    • Adrenal insufficiency: ↓Cortisol disinhibits CRH → ↑ADH; also cortisol normally suppresses ADH
    • Hypothyroidism: ↓Metabolic rate → ↓free water clearance (?), ↑ADH sensitivity
  • Drugs (SIADH-causing):
    • SSRIs (especially sertraline, fluoxetine): inhibit ADH suppression
    • Tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs)
    • Carbamazepine (anticonvulsant): enhances ADH release
    • Cyclophosphamide (chemotherapy)
    • Vincristine (chemotherapy)
    • Nicotine
    • Morphine
    • Haloperidol
    • Oxytocin (exogenous administration)
  • Post-operative/Post-ICU: Pain, stress, nausea, hypoxia → ADH release
  • Other: HIV/AIDS (opportunistic infections), exercise-induced SIADH (marathon runners)

Diagnosis of SIADH

  • Prerequisites for diagnosis: Rule out hypothyroidism, adrenal insufficiency, diuretic use, heart failure, cirrhosis, renal disease (these cause hyponatremia for other reasons)
  • Labs confirming SIADH:
    • ↓Serum Na⁺ (<135, often 115–125 in symptomatic SIADH)
    • ↓Serum osmolarity (<275, hypoosmolar)
    • ↑Urine osmolarity (>100, often >300) — inappropriate concentration despite low serum osmolarity
    • ↑Urine Na⁺ (>20 mEq/L, often >40) — kidneys still excreting Na despite volume expansion
    • Normal thyroid function, normal cortisol (rule out)
    • Euvolemia: No edema, no hypotension, JVP normal
  • Imaging: Chest CT if SCLC suspected (paraneoplastic syndrome); brain MRI if CNS etiology

Treatment of SIADH

  • Mild to moderate hyponatremia (Na⁺ 120–135, asymptomatic):
    • Fluid restriction (first-line): Restrict to 500–1000 mL/day (hypotonic intake)
    • Usually effective; avoids rapid Na⁺ swings
  • Severe or symptomatic hyponatremia (Na⁺ <120, seizures, altered mental status, coma):
    • Hypertonic saline (3% NaCl): IV infusion
      • Correct SLOWLY: max 8–12 mEq/L per 24 hours (never >12 mEq/L/24h)
      • Formula: Na⁺ deficit = 0.6 × weight(kg) × (desired[Na⁺] − current[Na⁺])
      • Example: 70 kg, current Na⁺ = 115, target Na⁺ = 125: deficit = 0.6 × 70 × (125−115) = 420 mEq
      • 3% saline = 513 mEq/L → need ~820 mL over 24h (~34 mL/h)
    • Correct symptomatic hyponatremia faster initially: ~4–6 mEq/L acutely to stop seizures, then slow down
    • Monitor serum Na⁺ q2–4h during acute correction to avoid overcorrection
  • Chronic SIADH management:
    • Fluid restriction: Long-term first-line; compliance can be difficult
    • Demeclocycline:
      • V2 receptor antagonist → blocks ADH effect on collecting duct
      • Dose: 600–1200 mg/day PO divided
      • Onset slow (5–7 days); used for chronic/recurrent SIADH unresponsive to fluid restriction
      • Cause nephrogenic DI phenotype (intentional therapeutic use)
    • Vaptans (V2/V1 receptor antagonists):
      • Tolvaptan (Samsca): Selective V2 antagonist, oral
      • Conivaptan: Mixed V1/V2 antagonist, IV (hospital use)
      • Fast onset (hours), but expensive; monitor for overcorrection and liver toxicity
    • Treat underlying cause: Remove offending drug, treat infection, manage cancer, etc.

Osmotic Demyelination Syndrome (ODS) / Central Pontine Myelinolysis (CPM)

  • Pathophysiology: Rapid overcorrection of hyponatremia → osmotic gradient shifts water OUT of oligodendrocytes → myelin disruption, demyelination
  • Risk: Correcting >12 mEq/L per 24 hours; especially if Na⁺ was low for >48 hours (brain adapts)
  • Presentation (3–5 days after overcorrection):
    • Flaccid quadriplegia (initially may be spastic)
    • Dysarthria, dysphagia
    • "Locked-in" syndrome (preserved consciousness but can't move/speak)
    • Pseudobulbar palsy
    • Can be irreversible if severe
  • Prevention: Slow, cautious correction; recheck Na⁺ q2–4h; don't exceed 12 mEq/L/24h
  • Treatment: Supportive; if ODS occurs, may lower Na⁺ back down slightly (risky maneuver) to reduce osmotic gradient
ODS Prevention: "Too Fast for Na, Too Slow to Walk"
Overly rapid correction of hyponatremia (>12 mEq/L/24h) → osmotic demyelination syndrome → locked-in syndrome, quadriplegia. Correct SLOWLY.
SIADH is euvolemic hyponatremia (no edema, no dehydration). Don't confuse with hyponatremia from heart failure or cirrhosis (hypervolemic) or from diuretics (hypovolemic). Check urine Na⁺ and osmolarity to distinguish.
Small cell lung cancer is the most common malignancy causing SIADH. Always check Na⁺ in SCLC patients. When correcting severe hyponatremia, aim for 8–12 mEq/L/24h—not 8–12 mEq/L/hour!
SIADH: euvolemic ↓Na⁺, ↓serum osmolarity, ↑urine osmolarity, ↑urine Na⁺. Treat mild cases with fluid restriction, severe cases with slow 3% saline (max 12 mEq/L/24h). Rapid correction causes ODS. Chronic management with demeclocycline or vaptans.

Oxytocin: Synthesis, Actions, and Clinical Uses

Synthesis and Release

  • Synthesized in: Paraventricular nucleus (primarily, ~60–70%) and supraoptic nucleus of hypothalamus
  • Transport and storage: Down axons to posterior pituitary, stored in Herring bodies
  • Release triggers:
    • Nipple stimulation (suckling/breastfeeding): Sensory input from breast → medial forebrain bundle → oxytocin neurons fire → milk let-down reflex
    • Cervical dilation (Ferguson reflex): Cervical stretch receptors → spinal cord → oxytocin release → uterine contractions → more stretch → positive feedback
    • Orgasm, touch, emotional bonding

Structure and Receptor

  • Neuropeptide: 9 amino acids (octapeptide); differs from ADH in positions 3 and 8
    • ADH: ...Phe-Gln...Pro-Arg-Gly-NH₂
    • Oxytocin: ...Ile-Gln...Pro-Leu-Gly-NH₂
  • Receptor (OTR): G-protein coupled receptor, Gq subtype
    • Activation → phospholipase C → IP3/DAG → ↑intracellular Ca²⁺
    • Ca²⁺ triggers smooth muscle contraction (myosin-actin interaction)

Physiological Actions

  • Uterine contractions:
    • Frequency and force ↑ throughout pregnancy (due to ↑OTR expression with progesterone withdrawal)
    • Labor: strong rhythmic contractions → cervical dilation → more stretch → more oxytocin → positive feedback loop
  • Milk let-down (milk ejection reflex):
    • Nipple stimulation → afferent sensory → oxytocin → myoepithelial cells contract → milk expelled from alveoli into ducts
    • Also stimulates alpha-smooth muscle in breast → increases pressure → milk flows
  • CNS effects:
    • Bonding, pair-bonding, maternal behavior, trust, social behavior ("love hormone")
    • May reduce anxiety, promote calm
  • Kidney (minor):
    • Mild V2-like effect (water reabsorption) due to structural similarity with ADH
    • Can cause hyponatremia if given in large doses (rare clinically)

Clinical Pharmacology and Uses

  • Pitocin (synthetic oxytocin):
    • Indications:
      • Labor induction (post-term pregnancy, maternal/fetal indications)
      • Augmentation of labor (strengthen weak contractions)
      • Postpartum hemorrhage (↑uterine tone → compression of vessels → ↓bleeding from uterine atony)
      • Retained placenta
    • Dosing: IV infusion, titrated by uterine response (start 0.5–1 mU/min, increase by 1 mU/min q30min until contractions q2–3min)
    • Adverse effects: Uterine tetany (sustained contraction → placental insufficiency → fetal hypoxia), water intoxication/hyponatremia (if given in hypotonic fluid), arrhythmias
  • Carbetocin:
    • Longer-acting oxytocin analog (half-life ~40 min vs 3 min for oxytocin)
    • Used for postpartum hemorrhage prophylaxis (single dose more effective than repeated oxytocin)

Oxytocin Antagonists (Tocolytics)

  • Atosiban:
    • Selective oxytocin receptor antagonist
    • Use: Preterm labor (tocolytic to slow/stop contractions)
    • Efficacy similar to β2-agonists but fewer systemic side effects
  • Beta-2 agonists (terbutaline, ritodrine):
    • Relax uterine smooth muscle (↑cAMP → ↓Ca²⁺)
    • Also reduce oxytocin receptor expression
    • Side effects: Tachycardia, tremor, hyperglycemia
  • Progesterone:
    • ↓Oxytocin receptor expression during pregnancy → maintains uterine quiescence
    • Withdrawal of progesterone (placental separation) → ↑OTR expression → labor onset

Ferguson Reflex and Labor

  • Ferguson reflex:
    1. Cervical dilation during labor
    2. Stretch receptors in cervix/lower uterus activated
    3. Sensory signal → pelvic plexus → spinal cord (S2–S4) → brainstem → hypothalamus
    4. Oxytocin neurons fire → oxytocin release
    5. Oxytocin → myometrial contraction → further dilation → more oxytocin
    6. Positive feedback loop that augments labor progression
  • Also called "milk let-down reflex" when applied to breastfeeding

Practice Questions: USMLE Vignettes

Question 1
A 55-year-old man with a recent diagnosis of small cell lung cancer presents with confusion, headache, and a serum sodium of 118 mEq/L. Physical exam reveals no edema or signs of volume depletion. His urine sodium is 45 mEq/L and urine osmolarity is 520 mOsm/kg. Serum osmolarity is 245 mOsm/kg. The patient is initially given 3% NaCl to address his symptoms.

What is the diagnosis? And what is the maximum rate at which his sodium should be corrected?
Click to reveal answer
Diagnosis: SIADH from ectopic ADH production by small cell lung cancer.

Key findings:
  • Euvolemic hyponatremia (no edema, no dehydration)
  • ↓Serum Na⁺ (118) and ↓serum osmolarity (245) — inappropriately low
  • ↑Urine osmolarity (520) despite low serum osmolarity — inappropriate concentration
  • ↑Urine Na⁺ (45) — kidneys still excreting sodium
  • Small cell lung cancer is the most common malignancy causing SIADH via ectopic ADH production

Correction rate: Maximum 8–12 mEq/L per 24 hours (never >12 mEq/L/24h).

Rationale: This patient is symptomatic (confusion, headache) from severe hyponatremia (Na⁺ 118), so 3% hypertonic saline is appropriate for symptomatic SIADH. However, correction must be slow and monitored carefully (serum Na⁺ q2–4h initially) to prevent overcorrection. Rapid correction (>12 mEq/L/24h) causes osmotic demyelination syndrome (central pontine myelinolysis) with permanent neurologic damage (flaccid quadriplegia, locked-in syndrome, dysarthria).

Long-term management: Treat the underlying malignancy (chemotherapy for SCLC); fluid restriction; demeclocycline (V2 antagonist) if fluid restriction fails; consider vaptans (tolvaptan) for rapid/refractory correction.
Question 2
A 28-year-old man undergoes transsphenoidal pituitary surgery for a non-functioning adenoma. On postoperative day 1, he produces 8 liters of urine over 12 hours. His serum sodium is 148 mEq/L (normal 135–145), serum osmolarity is 305 mOsm/kg. Urine osmolarity is 65 mOsm/kg. He is given DDAVP intravenously, and his urine osmolarity rises to 620 mOsm/kg within 2 hours.

What is the diagnosis? Explain the mechanism of this complication.
Click to reveal answer
Diagnosis: Central (neurogenic) diabetes insipidus.

Mechanism:
  • Transsphenoidal pituitary surgery damages the pituitary stalk and/or posterior pituitary tissue
  • Loss of ADH synthesis/release from hypothalamus or loss of storage/release from posterior pituitary
  • Kidney loses the signal to reabsorb water despite high serum osmolarity
  • Results: polyuria (8L in 12h), dilute urine (osmolarity 65), hypernatremia (148)

DDAVP response (key finding):
  • Urine osmolarity rises from 65 to 620 mOsm/kg after DDAVP → confirms the kidney IS responsive
  • The collecting duct can concentrate urine when given exogenous ADH replacement
  • Therefore, the problem is ADH deficiency (not kidney resistance), confirming CENTRAL DI

Triphasic response to pituitary surgery (common pattern):
  1. Acute DI (immediate): Stalk transection → ADH release stops → polyuria
  2. SIADH (days 3–5): Stored ADH leaks from damaged stalk → hyponatremia (paradoxically opposite)
  3. Permanent DI (weeks): If stalk transection is complete and axons don't regenerate

Management:
  • DDAVP replacement (intranasal preferred; dose titrated to urine output, typically 10 mcg 1–3×/day)
  • Fluid replacement to match insensible losses
  • Monitor serum Na⁺ closely (q4–6h initially, then daily) to avoid overcorrection
  • Watch for triphasic response: DI → SIADH → permanent DI
Question 3
A 62-year-old woman with bipolar disorder has been on lithium for 15 years. She now presents with severe polydipsia (drinking 6 L/day) and polyuria (voiding 6 L/day). Her serum sodium is 146 mEq/L and serum osmolarity is 304. Urine osmolarity is 80 mOsm/kg. She is given an IV bolus of DDAVP, but her urine osmolarity fails to rise and remains at 85 mOsm/kg.

What is the diagnosis? Explain the molecular mechanism of lithium toxicity. How should this patient be treated?
Click to reveal answer
Diagnosis: Nephrogenic diabetes insipidus secondary to chronic lithium use.

Molecular mechanism of lithium-induced nephrogenic DI:
  • Lithium enters principal cells of the collecting duct via the epithelial sodium channel (ENaC)
  • Inside the cell, lithium inhibits inositol polyphosphate (IP) recycling
  • ↓IP availability → impaired phospholipase C signaling from V2 receptor (Gq → PLC → IP3)
  • Also: lithium may directly inhibit adenylyl cyclase downstream of V2 receptor
  • Result: ↓cAMP → PKA not activated → AQP2 vesicles not inserted → collecting duct impermeable to water
  • Urine remains dilute despite high endogenous ADH and exogenous DDAVP

Why DDAVP fails:
  • DDAVP's mechanism (V2 → cAMP → AQP2 insertion) is blocked by lithium's intracellular effect
  • The kidney is the problem, not ADH production
  • This is ADH-resistant nephrogenic DI

Labs confirming nephrogenic DI:
  • High ADH levels (expected; body trying to compensate) — but NOT directly measured in this case
  • Hypernatremia (146) and ↑osmolarity (304)
  • Dilute urine (osmolarity 80) despite hypernatremia
  • No response to DDAVP (unlike central DI)

Treatment:
  1. Discontinue lithium if possible: Talk to psychiatrist about alternative mood stabilizers (valproate, lamotrigine, etc.). Nephrogenic DI may partially reverse if lithium is stopped early.
  2. Thiazide diuretic + low-sodium diet (first-line for nephrogenic DI):
    • Thiazide (e.g., HCTZ) causes mild volume depletion → ↓GFR → ↑proximal tubule reabsorption of Na⁺ and water
    • Less fluid is delivered to collecting duct → less polyuria despite inability to concentrate
    • Low-Na⁺ diet reduces osmotic load, enhances proximal reabsorption
  3. Amiloride (specific for lithium-induced DI):
    • ENaC blocker → prevents lithium entry into principal cells
    • Allows V2 signaling to work normally despite lithium presence
    • Dose: 5–10 mg PO daily; monitor K⁺ (amiloride is K⁺-sparing)
  4. NSAIDs (modest benefit): Inhibit prostaglandin synthesis; may reduce adenylyl cyclase antagonism
  5. Ensure adequate free water intake: Patient should have ad libitum access to water to maintain hydration and prevent hypernatremia

Prognosis: Nephrogenic DI from lithium is often irreversible if lithium was used for many years, as it can cause permanent renal damage. Early recognition and intervention (discontinuing lithium, starting amiloride + thiazide) improve outcomes.

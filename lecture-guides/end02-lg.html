1Anatomical connections between hypothalamus and pituitary

The Gist The hypothalamus (diencephalon, above the pituitary) and the pituitary gland (in the sella turcica of the sphenoid bone) are linked by the infundibulum (pituitary stalk). Two functional circuits run between them: (1) the hypophyseal portal system carries hypothalamic releasing/inhibiting hormones to the anterior pituitary, and (2) direct axonal projections from hypothalamic neurons deliver ADH and oxytocin to the posterior pituitary.

📚 Going Deeper — anatomy and the two delivery routes

1 · Anatomy at a glance

Bony & Neural Landmarks
  • Hypothalamus:
    • Part of the diencephalon.
    • Forms the floor and lateral walls of the third ventricle.
    • Sits just above the pituitary.Costanzo 6e ch 9
  • Pituitary gland:
    • Resides in the sella turcica ("Turkish saddle") of the sphenoid bone.
    • Covered superiorly by the diaphragma sellae, a dural fold.
    • Cavernous sinuses on either side contain CN III, IV, V1, V2, VI and the internal carotid.FA 2024 Neuro/Endo
  • Optic chiasm:
    • Sits directly above the pituitary.
    • Crossing fibers come from the nasal halves of each retina.
    • Pituitary masses compress these crossing fibers → bitemporal hemianopsia.Pathoma 2024 ch 15; B&B Endo
Orientation Rule (sagittal view)
  • Step 1 for any sagittal diagram:
    • Find the optic chiasm — it marks anterior.
    • The anterior pituitary sits toward the chiasm; the posterior lobe sits behind it.

2 · The hypophyseal portal system

Blood Flow Path
  • Inflow:
    • Superior hypophyseal arteries (branches of the internal carotid) enter the median eminence.
  • Primary capillary plexus:
    • Forms in the median eminence (a circumventricular organ — no BBB here).
    • Hypothalamic releasing/inhibiting hormones are secreted into these capillaries by axon terminals from hypothalamic neurons.Guyton 14e ch 75
  • Portal veins:
    • Carry the hormone-rich blood down the infundibulum.
    • Low-pressure system (venous).
  • Secondary capillary plexus:
    • Located in the anterior pituitary.
    • Delivers hypothalamic hormones at high concentration directly to secretory cells — without systemic dilution.
    • Finally drains into the systemic venous circulation.
Why this matters clinically
  • Low-pressure vulnerability:
    • The portal system is a low-pressure venous loop.
    • During pregnancy the pituitary enlarges but its blood supply doesn't scale proportionally.
    • Peripartum hemorrhage → hypoperfusion → pituitary infarction = Sheehan syndrome.Pathoma 2024 ch 15; FA 2024 Endo
  • Stalk transection:
    • Cutting the infundibulum cuts off dopamine (PIH) from the lactotropes.
    • Result: all anterior pituitary hormones fall EXCEPT prolactin, which rises.

3 · Direct axonal connections to the posterior pituitary

Neurons Involved
  • Magnocellular neurons:
    • Large neurons in the supraoptic nucleus (SON) and paraventricular nucleus (PVN).
    • Synthesize ADH and oxytocin (bound to their carrier protein neurophysin).
    • Axons travel through the infundibulum to the posterior pituitary.Costanzo 6e ch 9
Storage & Release
  • Herring bodies:
    • Eosinophilic axon-terminal swellings in the posterior pituitary.
    • Packed with neurosecretory granules (ADH/oxytocin).Guyton 14e ch 75
  • Pituicytes:
    • Glial support cells of the neurohypophysis.
    • Appear as basophilic nuclei on H&E.
  • Release mechanism:
    • Action potentials travel down the axon → Ca²⁺ entry at terminal → vesicle fusion.
    • ADH and oxytocin diffuse into adjacent fenestrated capillaries → systemic circulation.

4 · Circumventricular organs (CVOs)

Definition
  • CVOs:
    • Areas of the brain near ventricles where the blood-brain barrier is minimal or absent.
    • Allow direct neural sensing of circulating molecules and direct hormone release into blood.B&B Endo; Guyton 14e
Endocrinologically Important CVOs
  • Median eminence:
    • Site of the primary capillary plexus of the hypophyseal portal system.
  • Neurohypophysis (posterior pituitary):
    • ADH and oxytocin released directly into fenestrated capillaries.
  • OVLT (organum vasculosum of the lamina terminalis):
    • Senses plasma osmolarity.
    • Drives ADH release and thirst when plasma is hyperosmotic.
  • Pineal gland:
    • Releases melatonin on a circadian rhythm.
  • Others:
    • Area postrema (vomiting center), subfornical organ (thirst/AngII sensing).

  • Sheehan syndrome: postpartum hypopituitarism from pituitary infarction. First and most common sign is failure to lactate (PRL). AI Generated · see end08
  • Pituitary stalk transection: all anterior pituitary hormones ↓ EXCEPT prolactin ↑ (dopamine no longer inhibits lactotropes). end02 lecture concept; FA 3.04 Prolactin

2Hypothalamic nuclei and the hormones they secrete

The Gist The hypothalamus is organized into nuclei (clusters of neuron cell bodies), each producing specific hormones. Hormones delivered via the portal system to the anterior pituitary come in releasing (RH) and inhibiting (IH) flavors — almost all are peptides except dopamine (the only monoamine in the set, which serves as prolactin-inhibiting hormone). The supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei produce ADH and oxytocin delivered directly to the posterior pituitary via axons.

📚 Going Deeper — the hormones and the nuclei

1 · Releasing vs. inhibiting hormones

Definitions
  • Releasing hormone (-RH):
    • Stimulates secretion of a specific anterior pituitary hormone.
    • Examples: GnRH, TRH, CRH, GHRH.
  • Inhibiting hormone (-IH):
    • Suppresses secretion of a specific anterior pituitary hormone.
    • Examples: somatostatin (GHIH), dopamine (PIH), GnIH.Costanzo 6e ch 9
The dopamine exception
  • Only non-peptide:
    • Dopamine (PIH) is a monoamine (catecholamine) — all other hypothalamic hormones are peptides.
    • Synthesized in the arcuate nucleus (tuberoinfundibular pathway).
    • Tonically inhibits prolactin.FA 2024 Endo
  • Why this matters:
    • Dopamine antagonists (antipsychotics, antiemetics like metoclopramide) → hyperprolactinemia.
    • Dopamine agonists (bromocriptine, cabergoline) are first-line for prolactinoma.

2 · The nuclei map (focused, not every nucleus)

Hormones going to the posterior pituitary
  • Supraoptic (SON):
    • Primary source of ADH.
    • Smaller contribution to oxytocin.
  • Paraventricular (PVN):
    • Primary source of oxytocin.
    • Also makes some ADH, CRH, TRH — "Swiss Army nucleus."Guyton 14e ch 75
Hormones going to the anterior pituitary (portal system)
  • Medial preoptic → GnRH:
    • Master of the reproductive axis.
    • Must be released in pulses (~every 60–90 min) to avoid gonadotrope desensitization.
  • Dorsomedial → GnIH:
    • Directly opposes GnRH.
  • Paraventricular + anterior nuclei → TRH, CRH:
    • Drive the thyroid and adrenal axes.
  • Ventromedial → GHRH:
    • VMN is also the "satiety center" — lesions cause hyperphagia and obesity.
  • Periventricular → somatostatin (GHIH):
    • Opposes GHRH.
    • Also inhibits TSH secretion (mild contribution).
  • Arcuate → dopamine (PIH) + GHRH:
    • Arcuate is the only nucleus that sends a monoamine to the anterior pituitary.
    • Also participates in appetite regulation (NPY/AgRP and POMC neurons live here).

3 · Posterior pituitary hormones (ADH & oxytocin)

Structure
  • Nonapeptides:
    • Both ADH and oxytocin are 9-amino-acid peptides.
    • Differ by only 2 amino acids.Costanzo 6e ch 9
  • Neurophysin carriers:
    • Each hormone is co-packaged with a specific neurophysin.
    • Neurophysin I carries oxytocin; neurophysin II carries ADH.
ADH — functions
  • V2 receptor (renal collecting duct, principal cells):
    • Gs → cAMP → inserts aquaporin-2 channels into apical membrane.
    • Increases water reabsorption → concentrated urine.
  • V1 receptor (vascular smooth muscle):
    • Gq → IP3/Ca²⁺ → vasoconstriction.
    • Supports BP in hypovolemia.FA 2024 Pharm
  • Stimuli for ADH release:
    • Increased plasma osmolarity (sensed by OVLT, subfornical organ).
    • Decreased blood volume or pressure (sensed by baroreceptors).
Oxytocin — functions
  • Uterine contraction:
    • Triggers labor contractions.
    • Ferguson reflex: cervical stretch → more oxytocin → more contraction (positive feedback).
  • Milk let-down:
    • Suckling → spinal afferents → oxytocin release → contraction of myoepithelial cells → milk ejection.
  • Other effects:
    • Social bonding, pair bonding, stress modulation.

4 · Hypothalamic hormone structure summary

  • Peptide sizes (approximate):
    • TRH = 3 AA (tripeptide) — smallest.
    • GnIH ≈ 8 AA.
    • Oxytocin / ADH = 9 AA (nonapeptides).
    • GnRH = 10 AA (decapeptide).
    • Somatostatin = 14 or 28 AA (cyclic).
    • CRH = 41 AA.
    • GHRH = 44 AA — largest of this set.Guyton 14e ch 75
  • Dopamine:
    • Not a peptide. Catecholamine synthesized from tyrosine.

5 · The pulsatility rule

  • Pulsatile GnRH:
    • Normal physiologic release every 60–90 minutes.
    • Pulses maintain gonadotrope responsiveness.
  • Continuous exposure = paradoxical suppression:
    • Receptor desensitization and down-regulation.
    • GnRH agonists (leuprolide) used CONTINUOUSLY suppress LH/FSH → chemical castration (prostate cancer, endometriosis).Lippincott Pharm 7e
    • The same drug given PULSATILELY can treat hypogonadism (e.g., Kallmann) by mimicking physiology.
★ HIGH-YIELD for USMLE
  • Dopamine = PIH and the only non-peptide hypothalamic hormone. Everything else is a peptide. end02 lecture
  • TRH stimulates BOTH TSH and PRL — primary hypothyroidism can cause galactorrhea. FA 3.04 Prolactin; HPT clinical in end10/11
  • GnRH must be pulsatile. Continuous GnRH agonist (leuprolide) chemically castrates. end02 concept; leuprolide AI Generated · see end13
  • Stalk section → PRL rises (everything else falls) — loss of tonic dopamine. FA 3.04 Prolactin
  • Kallmann syndrome — failed migration of GnRH neurons from olfactory placode → anosmia + hypogonadotropic hypogonadism. AI Generated · see end05/repro
  • SON = ADH-dominant; PVN = oxytocin-dominant — but both produce both. end02 lecture

3Pituitary anatomy and histology

The Gist The pituitary has two embryologic origins: the adenohypophysis (anterior) comes from oral ectoderm (Rathke's pouch), and the neurohypophysis (posterior) comes from neuroectoderm. The adenohypophysis is populated by basophils (FSH, LH, ACTH, TSH), acidophils (GH, PRL), and non-secretory chromophobes. The neurohypophysis contains axon terminals with Herring bodies and supporting pituicytes.

📚 Going Deeper — every part, every cell

1 · The three parts of each lobe

Adenohypophysis
  • Pars distalis:
    • Largest portion.
    • Houses the 5 secretory cell types (somatotropes, lactotropes, gonadotropes, corticotropes, thyrotropes).Costanzo 6e ch 9
  • Pars intermedia:
    • Thin layer between adeno- and neurohypophysis.
    • Contains densely packed basophilic cells and characteristic colloid-filled cysts.
    • Vestigial in adults; active in fetuses, young children.
    • Makes MSH (via POMC cleavage).
  • Pars tuberalis:
    • Sleeve of tissue that wraps around the infundibular stalk.
    • Mainly gonadotropes.
Neurohypophysis
  • Pars nervosa:
    • Main posterior lobe.
    • Packed with axon terminals from SON and PVN.
    • Herring bodies visible as eosinophilic swellings.
    • Pituicytes (glia) fill the stroma.
  • Median eminence:
    • Not a part of the anterior pituitary, but the site of primary capillary plexus.
    • Where hypothalamic hormones enter the portal system.
  • Infundibular stalk:
    • Connects hypothalamus to pituitary.
    • Carries both portal veins (to adenohypophysis) and axons (to neurohypophysis).

2 · Embryology

Two-origin theory
  • Adenohypophysis:
    • Originates from Rathke's pouch — an outpouching of oral ectoderm.
    • Migrates upward to meet the descending neuroectoderm.FA 2024 Embryo; B&B Endo
  • Neurohypophysis:
    • Originates from the floor of the diencephalon (neuroectoderm).
    • Descends to meet Rathke's pouch.
  • Fusion:
    • Rathke's pouch closes, leaving the pars intermedia at the junction.
    • Residual cells of Rathke's pouch can persist → craniopharyngioma in children.

3 · Cell staining and identification

Acidophils
  • Appearance:
    • Eosin-staining (pink) cytoplasmic granules.
    • Packed with hormone.
  • Hormones:
    • GH (somatotrope).
    • PRL (lactotrope).
    • You can't distinguish somatotrope from lactotrope on H&E alone — immunohistochemistry required.
Basophils
  • Appearance:
    • Hematoxylin-staining (purple/blue) granules.
    • Often PAS-positive due to heavy glycosylation (glycoprotein hormones).Pathoma 2024 ch 15
  • Hormones:
    • FSH, LH (gonadotropes).
    • ACTH (corticotropes) — also generates MSH, β-endorphin, β-lipotropin from POMC.
    • TSH (thyrotropes).
Chromophobes
  • What they are:
    • Pale/colorless cells with few granules.
    • Include non-secretory support cells and "exhausted" secretory cells.
  • Clinical note:
    • Most pituitary adenomas appear chromophobic on H&E (functional or non-functional).

4 · Pars intermedia & POMC

POMC (pro-opiomelanocortin)
  • One precursor, many products:
    • ACTH, MSH (α, β, γ), β-endorphin, β-lipotropin.FA 2024 Endo
  • Cleavage pattern varies:
    • Anterior pituitary corticotropes cleave POMC mainly to ACTH.
    • Pars intermedia cleaves POMC mainly to MSH (in species that have an active intermedia).
Hyperpigmentation connection
  • Primary adrenal insufficiency (Addison):
    • Low cortisol → no negative feedback → ↑ CRH, ↑ POMC.
    • ↑ POMC → ↑ ACTH AND ↑ MSH → cutaneous hyperpigmentation.
    • Classic skin darkening of palmar creases, scars, buccal mucosa.

5 · Clinical pairing — cells to tumors

  • Prolactinoma (lactotrope adenoma):
    • Most common functional pituitary adenoma.
    • Galactorrhea, amenorrhea, infertility, ED.
    • Treat with dopamine agonists (bromocriptine, cabergoline).Pathoma 2024 ch 15
  • Somatotrope adenoma:
    • Before epiphyseal closure → gigantism (very tall).
    • After epiphyseal closure → acromegaly (enlarging hands/feet/jaw, coarse features, DM, cardiomegaly).
  • Corticotrope adenoma:
    • Cushing disease — high ACTH, high cortisol.
  • Thyrotrope / gonadotrope adenoma:
    • Rare functional tumors.
  • Non-functional adenomas:
    • Often chromophobic; present with mass effect (headaches, bitemporal hemianopsia).
  • Craniopharyngioma:
    • Remnant of Rathke's pouch in children.
    • Suprasellar, calcified, "motor oil" cyst fluid.
    • Bitemporal hemianopsia + endocrine dysfunction (commonly GH deficiency, central DI).

Who makes what (by cell class)

2 · GH axis — the growth hormone story

Regulation
  • Positive regulators:
    • GHRH (pulsatile from ventromedial / arcuate nuclei).
    • Ghrelin (from stomach).
    • Deep sleep (stages 3/4).
    • Stress, exercise, hypoglycemia.Costanzo 6e ch 9
  • Negative regulators:
    • Somatostatin (periventricular nucleus).
    • IGF-1 (long-loop feedback).
    • GH itself (short loop) and hyperglycemia.
Mechanism
  • Receptor type:
    • GH binds a JAK-STAT receptor on liver.
    • Induces transcription of IGF-1.FA 2024 Biochem
  • Dual effects:
    • Direct: lipolysis, insulin resistance (diabetogenic).
    • Indirect via IGF-1: linear bone growth, muscle/organ growth.
Clinical testing
  • IGF-1 level:
    • Stable marker (unlike pulsatile GH).
    • First-line screening test for acromegaly.
  • Oral glucose suppression test:
    • Normally glucose suppresses GH.
    • In acromegaly, GH fails to suppress → confirms diagnosis.B&B Endo — Acromegaly
  • GH deficiency testing:
    • Insulin tolerance test (gold standard) — hypoglycemia should stimulate GH.
    • Arginine or glucagon stimulation test.

3 · PRL axis — the dopamine exception

Tonic dopamine inhibition
  • Why it's unique:
    • Prolactin is the only anterior pituitary hormone primarily controlled by inhibition rather than stimulation.
    • Tuberoinfundibular dopamine neurons (arcuate nucleus) continuously suppress lactotropes.FA 2024 Endo
  • Predictable consequences:
    • Stalk section → no dopamine → ↑ PRL.
    • D2 antagonists (antipsychotics, metoclopramide) → ↑ PRL.
    • Primary hypothyroidism → ↑ TRH → ↑ PRL (TRH is a mild PRL stimulator).
Normal triggers for PRL release
  • Suckling:
    • Afferent signal to hypothalamus → ↓ dopamine → ↑ PRL.
  • Estrogen:
    • Pregnancy → high estrogen → lactotrope hyperplasia.
    • Postpartum drop in estrogen + suckling → robust milk production.
PRL's reproductive effect
  • Inhibition of GnRH:
    • High PRL suppresses GnRH → ↓ LH/FSH → amenorrhea and infertility (lactational amenorrhea or prolactinoma).

4 · HPT axis — thyroid

  • Cascade:
    • TRH (hypothalamus) → TSH (thyrotropes) → thyroid → T3/T4.
    • T3/T4 feed back negatively on pituitary and hypothalamus.Guyton 14e ch 77
  • Somatostatin:
    • Also mildly suppresses TSH (not just GH).
  • Clinical:
    • Primary hypothyroidism (Hashimoto): low T4 → high TSH.
    • Central hypothyroidism (pituitary or hypothalamic): low T4 with low or inappropriately normal TSH.

5 · HPA axis — cortisol Clinical detail primarily end19 / 20 / 21

  • Cascade:
    • CRH → ACTH → adrenal cortex (zona fasciculata) → cortisol.
    • Cortisol feeds back on pituitary and hypothalamus.
  • Diurnal variation:
    • Cortisol peaks ~6 am, troughs ~midnight.
    • Used diagnostically (midnight salivary cortisol for Cushing screen).
  • ADH synergy:
    • ADH potentiates CRH-driven ACTH release during stress.

6 · HPG axis — gonads

  • Cascade:
    • GnRH (pulsatile) → LH + FSH → gonads → sex steroids + inhibin.
  • Feedback pattern:
    • Sex steroids usually provide NEGATIVE feedback.
    • Mid-cycle: rising estradiol for ~48 h switches to POSITIVE feedback → LH surge → ovulation.FA 2024 Repro; Costanzo 6e ch 10
  • Inhibin B:
    • Released by Sertoli (males) and granulosa (females).
    • Selectively inhibits FSH.

7 · Posterior pituitary — ADH & oxytocin in depth

ADH Receptors
  • V1 (vascular smooth muscle):
    • Gq → IP3/Ca²⁺ → vasoconstriction.
    • Important in hypovolemic shock.
  • V2 (renal collecting duct principal cells):
    • Gs → cAMP → aquaporin-2 insertion → water reabsorption.FA 2024 Renal
  • V3 (anterior pituitary):
    • Potentiates CRH-induced ACTH release.

    • Central DI: low ADH (posterior pituitary lesion).
    • Nephrogenic DI: kidney unresponsive (V2 or AQP-2 mutation; lithium).

  • SIADH:
    • Excess ADH → dilutional hyponatremia.
    • Causes: small-cell lung CA, CNS lesions, drugs (SSRI, carbamazepine), pulmonary dz.
Oxytocin
  • Receptor:
    • OT-R (Gq) on uterine myometrium and mammary myoepithelial cells.
  • Labor physiology:
    • Cervical stretch → spinal afferents → oxytocin release → stronger contraction (Ferguson reflex, positive feedback).
  • Lactation:
    • Suckling → oxytocin release → myoepithelial contraction → milk ejection ("let-down").

5Feedback loops that regulate hormone secretion

The Gist Three negative-feedback loops regulate hypothalamic-pituitary hormone output: ultrashort (hypothalamic hormones inhibit themselves via axon collaterals — creates pulsatility), short (pituitary hormones feed back on the hypothalamus), and long (target-organ hormones feed back on both the pituitary and the hypothalamus). A few positive feedback loops exist too — most famously the estradiol → LH surge that triggers ovulation and the oxytocin → uterine contraction loop of labor.

📚 Going Deeper — every feedback loop with clinical applications

1 · Ultrashort loop — hypothalamic autoinhibition

Mechanism
  • Axon collaterals:
    • Hypothalamic neurons send collateral branches back onto themselves (and neighboring neurons).
    • These collaterals deliver an inhibitory signal.Guyton 14e ch 75
  • Consequence:
    • Once a neuron fires, it temporarily inhibits itself.
    • Creates a pulsatile secretion pattern.
Why pulsatility matters
  • Prevents target-cell desensitization:
    • Continuous exposure → receptor down-regulation and loss of responsiveness.
    • Pulses give receptors time to recycle/re-sensitize.
  • Clinical application — GnRH:
    • Physiologic GnRH pulses every 60–90 minutes → normal LH/FSH output.
    • Continuous GnRH agonist (leuprolide) → chemical castration (prostate CA, endometriosis).
    • Pulsatile GnRH (via pump) → fertility treatment in Kallmann syndrome.FA 2024 Pharm; Lippincott Pharm 7e

2 · Short loop — pituitary on hypothalamus

  • Mechanism:
    • The pituitary hormone itself is sensed by the hypothalamus.
    • Reduces further hypothalamic releasing-hormone output.
  • Examples:
    • ACTH suppresses CRH.
    • GH suppresses GHRH (and stimulates somatostatin).
    • PRL increases dopamine secretion from tuberoinfundibular neurons (self-inhibition).Costanzo 6e ch 9

3 · Long loop — target organ on hypothalamus + pituitary

The dominant regulatory loop
  • Pattern:
    • The peripheral hormone (the "output" of the axis) feeds back on both the pituitary and the hypothalamus.
  • Canonical examples:
    • Cortisol → inhibits CRH and ACTH (HPA).
    • T3/T4 → inhibit TRH and TSH (HPT).
    • Testosterone/estradiol → inhibit GnRH and LH/FSH (HPG, usually negative).
    • IGF-1 → inhibits GHRH and GH.
  • Clinical relevance:
    • Exogenous cortisol → suppresses CRH + ACTH → adrenal atrophy.
    • Exogenous thyroid hormone (e.g., surreptitious levothyroxine use) → suppresses TSH.
    • Anabolic steroid abuse → suppresses LH → testicular atrophy.FA 2024 Endo

4 · Positive feedback — rare but critical

The estradiol → LH surge
  • Normally negative:
    • Estradiol normally suppresses GnRH and LH/FSH.
  • The switch:
    • Late follicular phase: sustained high estradiol (>200 pg/mL for ~48 h) switches feedback to POSITIVE.
    • Triggers a massive LH surge → ovulation.Costanzo 6e ch 10
  • After ovulation:
    • Corpus luteum produces progesterone and estradiol.
    • Negative feedback re-establishes; LH and FSH fall.
Oxytocin in labor (Ferguson reflex)
  • Mechanism:
    • Cervical stretch → spinal afferents → oxytocin release → stronger uterine contraction → more stretch.
    • Self-amplifying cascade that terminates with delivery.
Oxytocin in lactation
  • Mechanism:
    • Suckling → afferent spinal signal → oxytocin release → myoepithelial contraction → milk ejection.
    • Conditioned response (baby's cry can trigger let-down).

5 · Diurnal and cyclic patterns

  • Cortisol:
    • Peak ~6 am, trough near midnight.
    • Midnight salivary cortisol — key Cushing screening test.B&B Endo — Cushing
  • GH:
    • Largest pulses during deep (slow-wave) sleep.
    • Why chronic sleep deprivation impairs growth.
  • TSH:
    • Mild nocturnal peak.
  • Menstrual cycle:
    • Estradiol, progesterone, LH, FSH — cyclical, with mid-cycle LH surge.

6 · Testing feedback clinically

  • Dexamethasone suppression test:
    • Low-dose: suppresses ACTH in normals; fails in any Cushing.
    • High-dose: suppresses only in pituitary-origin Cushing DISEASE; fails in adrenal tumor or ectopic ACTH.FA 2024 Endo
  • ACTH stimulation test (cosyntropin):
    • Tests adrenal response. Normal: cortisol rises. Primary adrenal insufficiency: cortisol fails to rise.
  • GnRH stimulation test:
    • Tests pituitary gonadotrope function — LH/FSH should rise after IV GnRH.
  • Glucose suppression test for acromegaly:
    • Normally GH suppresses. Fails to suppress in acromegaly.

1 · The predict-it-all framework

4-step method
  • Step 1 — Identify the axis:
    • HPA (cortisol), HPT (thyroid), HPG (gonads), or GH?
  • Step 2 — Locate the broken link:
    • Target organ = primary.
    • Pituitary = secondary.
    • Hypothalamus = tertiary.
  • Step 3 — Apply feedback logic:
    • If the target hormone is LOW → loss of negative feedback → upstream hormones RISE (unless the upstream is also broken).
    • If the target hormone is HIGH → strong negative feedback → upstream hormones FALL.
  • Step 4 — Sanity check downstream:
    • If the lesion is upstream, the downstream hormones follow the driver.
    • E.g., hypothalamic failure → low TRH → low TSH → low T4 (tertiary hypothyroidism).FA 2024 Endo; Costanzo 6e ch 9

Primary hypothyroidism (Hashimoto)

    • No feedback on pituitary/hypothalamus → ↑ TRH, ↑ TSH.

    • High TRH stimulates lactotropes → ↑ PRL → galactorrhea, amenorrhea possible.
Secondary hypothyroidism
  • Cascade:
    • Pituitary fails → ↓ TSH → ↓ T4.
    • TRH RISES (loss of T4 feedback).
  • Common cause:
    • Pituitary adenoma compressing normal pituitary, Sheehan, apoplexy.
Tertiary hypothyroidism
  • Cascade:
    • Hypothalamus fails → ↓ TRH → ↓ TSH → ↓ T4.
    • All three are low.
Graves disease (primary hyperthyroidism)
  • Mechanism:
    • Stimulating TSH-receptor antibodies → ↑ T3/T4.
    • T3/T4 feedback → ↓ TSH, ↓ TRH.
TSHoma (secondary hyperthyroidism)

Tertiary adrenal insufficiency

"PST ladder": Primary (gland), Secondary (pituitary), Tertiary (top — hypothalamus).

Rule of thumb: "If it's broken upstream, everything downstream falls. If it's broken at the output, upstream rises."

Objective-by-objective bottom line

#One-line answer to lock in
1Hypothalamus (diencephalon) + pituitary (sella turcica of sphenoid) connect via the infundibulum. Two circuits: portal system → anterior pituitary; axonal projections → posterior pituitary.
2Releasing/inhibiting hormones are peptides EXCEPT dopamine (PIH). SON = ADH; PVN = oxytocin. Arcuate nucleus makes dopamine + GHRH. GnRH must be pulsatile.
3Adeno (oral ectoderm / Rathke) vs. neurohypophysis (neuroectoderm). Basophils = B-FLAT (FSH, LH, ACTH, TSH); acidophils = GPA (GH, PRL).
4Anterior: FLAT PiG. Prolactin uniquely under tonic dopamine inhibition. GH via JAK-STAT → IGF-1. Posterior: ADH (V1 vasoconstrict, V2 aquaporin-2) + oxytocin.
5Three negative loops: ultrashort, short, long. Positive feedback: estradiol → LH surge; oxytocin in labor/let-down.
6Primary (gland fails) / secondary (pituitary) / tertiary (hypothalamus). Map all 6 permutations for each axis.

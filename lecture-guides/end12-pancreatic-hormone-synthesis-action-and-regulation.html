LO1: HPT Axis Overview and TRH-TSH-T4/T3 Loop

▶

Three-Tier Cascade: Hypothalamus → Pituitary → Thyroid

Hypothalamus: Paraventricular nucleus secretes TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone) — a tripeptide into hypothalamic-hypophyseal portal blood.

Anterior Pituitary: TSH-secreting thyrotrophs receive TRH → stimulated to synthesize and secrete TSH (glycoprotein with α and β subunits).

Thyroid Gland: Follicular cells express TSH receptors → ↑ iodine uptake, hormone synthesis, coupling, and secretion of T4 and T3.

TRH: Receptor Mechanism & Effects

TRH Receptor: Gq-coupled; activates PIP2 → IP3 + DAG → ↑ intracellular Ca²⁺ → TSH synthesis and secretion.

Clinical Pearl — Prolactin Link: TRH also stimulates PROLACTIN release from lactotrophs. In hypothyroidism, ↑ TRH → ↑ prolactin → galactorrhea can occur (test-taking trap: remember the "triple effect" of TRH).

TSH: Molecular Actions on Thyroid

TSH Receptor: Gs-coupled on follicular cells → ↑ cAMP → PKA activation.

TSH Actions:

  • ↑ NIS (sodium-iodide symporter) expression → ↑ iodine uptake
  • ↑ TPO (thyroid peroxidase) → ↑ iodine coupling to Tg
  • ↑ Thyroglobulin synthesis
  • ↑ Endocytosis of colloid and proteolysis → hormone release
  • ↑ Thyroid follicular cell growth (goitrogenic effect)

Negative Feedback: T3 & T4 as the "Brakes"

Dual-Site Inhibition: T4 and T3 inhibit BOTH:

  • Hypothalamus: Suppress TRH release
  • Pituitary (Primary): Directly inhibit TSH secretion — this is the dominant feedback site

Why T3 is Special: T4 is converted to T3 IN thyrotroph cells by type-2 deiodinase (D2). T3 is the actual active inhibitor in the pituitary. This explains why T3 is so potent at suppressing TSH.

Log-Linear Relationship: The pituitary is exquisitely sensitive to small changes in free T4. TSH can fluctuate 10-fold with modest changes in T4 — this makes TSH the MOST SENSITIVE test for primary thyroid dysfunction.

"TSH is the BOSS: When T4 is low, TSH rises (BOSS is upset trying to fix it). When T4 is high, TSH falls (BOSS is satisfied and relaxes)."
🎯 TESTED CONCEPT: TSH is the most sensitive first-line test for thyroid disease. Small changes in T4 cause large inverse changes in TSH. Know the log-linear relationship.

LO2: Factors That Modulate the HPT Axis

▶

Stimulators of TRH & TSH

  • Cold Exposure: Important in neonates/animals; modest effect in adult humans.
  • Estrogen: Mild upregulation of TRH/TSH (explains higher TSH in women; test-taking hint: pregnancy may increase TSH).
  • Hypothyroidism itself: Loss of negative feedback → axis tries to compensate by ↑ TRH/TSH.

Inhibitors of TRH & TSH

  • T3/T4 (dominant): Negative feedback — strongest inhibitor.
  • Somatostatin: Hypothalamic inhibition (similar to effect on GH secretion).
  • Dopamine: Clinical use: dopamine infusions in ICU suppress TSH → can create lab confusion (see NTIS below).
  • Glucocorticoids: Suppress TSH secretion; high-dose steroids → ↓ TSH.
  • Fasting/Severe Illness: Central suppression of TRH/TSH.

Non-Thyroidal Illness Syndrome (NTIS) = "Sick Euthyroid Syndrome"

Where: Severe systemic illness (ICU, major surgery, sepsis, starvation, trauma).

Mechanism:

  • ↓ 5'-Deiodinase (D1) activity → ↓ conversion of T4 → T3
  • ↑ Reverse T3 (rT3) production (inactive metabolite)
  • ↓ TBG (thyroxine-binding globulin)
  • ↓ TSH (from dopamine, glucocorticoids, fasting)

Lab Pattern (Classic): ↓ T3, ↓/normal T4, ↑ rT3, low/normal TSH → can MIMIC central hypothyroidism (trap!).

KEY RULE: DO NOT TREAT with thyroid hormone replacement. NTIS improves when underlying illness resolves. Treating with levothyroxine worsens outcomes (paradoxically).

"SICK patients get LOW T3 + HIGH rT3 — that's NORMAL for sickness. DON'T TREAT THE THYROID; treat the underlying illness."

Iodine Status & Axis

  • Iodine Deficiency: ↑ TSH (axis tries to drive thyroid to increase uptake) → goiter development.
  • Iodine Excess (Wolff-Chaikoff Effect): Transiently ↓ thyroid hormone production (TPO inhibition) → TSH rises → goiter if chronic excess.
⚠️ TRAP: NTIS labs can look like central hypothyroidism. Key differentiator: NTIS is self-limiting and improves with treatment of underlying disease. DO NOT give thyroid hormone in NTIS.

LO3: Primary vs Secondary vs Tertiary Hypothyroidism

▶

Primary Hypothyroidism

Pathology: Problem in the thyroid gland itself.

Labs: ↓ T4/T3, ↑↑ TSH (axis compensates).

Most Common Causes:

  • Worldwide: Iodine deficiency (largest preventable cause of intellectual disability)
  • US: Hashimoto's thyroiditis (autoimmune; anti-TPO and anti-thyroglobulin antibodies)
  • Other: Post-thyroidectomy, post-radioiodine ablation, medications (amiodarone, lithium, methimazole), congenital hypothyroidism

Monitoring: Use TSH (most sensitive). Once on levothyroxine, titrate dose to achieve TSH in target range (typically 0.5–2.5 mIU/L).

Secondary (Central) Hypothyroidism

Pathology: Problem in the anterior pituitary → ↓ TSH → ↓ T4.

Labs: ↓ T4, LOW or INAPPROPRIATELY NORMAL TSH (TSH fails to compensate).

Causes: Pituitary adenoma, Sheehan's syndrome, radiation, infiltrative disease (sarcoidosis, hemochromatosis), hypopituitarism.

CRITICAL DIFFERENCE: TSH is unreliable for monitoring because the pituitary is broken. Monitor using FREE T4 (not TSH). Target: Free T4 in upper-normal to high-normal range (to drive whatever TSH the pituitary can muster).

Tertiary Hypothyroidism

Pathology: Problem in the hypothalamus → ↓ TRH → ↓ TSH → ↓ T4.

Labs: ↓ T4, low/normal TSH (similar to secondary on routine labs).

Causes: Hypothalamic tumors, radiation, infiltration.

How to Differentiate Secondary vs Tertiary: TRH stimulation test.

  • IV TRH given; measure TSH at 30 minutes
  • Normal: TSH rises appropriately (intact pituitary & hypothalamus)
  • Secondary (pituitary broken): Blunted or absent TSH response
  • Tertiary (hypothalamus broken): Delayed/prolonged TSH response (pituitary is intact but slow to respond without TRH stimulation)

Subclinical Hypothyroidism

Labs: ↑ TSH, normal T4/T3 (earliest stage of compensation).

Significance: May progress to overt hypothyroidism.

Treatment: Consider treatment if:

  • TSH > 10 mIU/L
  • Patient is pregnant (risk of fetal hypothyroidism)
  • Symptomatic or high cardiac risk
"1° = Gland broken (↑ TSH, ↓ T4). 2° = Pituitary broken (↓ TSH, ↓ T4). 3° = Hypothalamus broken (↓ TSH, ↓ T4). Monitor 2°/3° with FREE T4, not TSH."
🎯 TESTED CONCEPT: Secondary hypothyroidism: TSH is LOW or normal despite low T4. Students often miss this. Use FREE T4 to monitor, not TSH.
⚠️ TRAP: Assuming TSH will always be high in hypothyroidism. In secondary/tertiary, TSH is LOW. Always check free T4 if central cause suspected.

LO4: Primary vs Secondary vs Tertiary Hyperthyroidism

▶

Primary Hyperthyroidism

Labs: ↑ T4/T3, ↓↓ TSH (suppressed by high hormone).

Most Common Causes:

  • Graves' Disease (most common): Autoimmune; TSH-receptor antibodies (TSI, TSHRAb) act as TSH agonist → unregulated hormone secretion. Look for: exophthalmos, pretibial myxedema, diffuse goiter.
  • Toxic Multinodular Goiter (Plummer's disease): Autonomous follicular nodules with functioning tissue.
  • Toxic Adenoma: Single autonomously functioning adenoma.
  • Thyroiditis (destructive phase): Hashimoto's thyrotoxicosis, subacute (De Quervain's) thyroiditis, postpartum thyroiditis → inflammatory release of stored hormone.
  • Exogenous Thyroid Hormone: Factitious hyperthyroidism (patient taking too much levothyroxine or intentional overdose).
  • Struma Ovarii: Ectopic thyroid tissue in ovarian teratoma → produces thyroid hormone.
  • hCG-Mediated (Pregnancy): Molar pregnancy or severe hyperemesis gravidarum → hCG cross-reacts with TSH receptor → transient suppression of TSH.

Secondary Hyperthyroidism (TSH-secreting Adenoma)

Labs: ↑↑ TSH, ↑↑ T4 (BOTH high — loss of normal feedback).

KEY TRAP: This is VERY RARE but HIGH-YIELD. Students expect TSH to be low when T4 is high. This adenoma breaks that rule.

Diagnosis: MRI pituitary; elevated α-subunit (common to TSH, LH, FSH, hCG).

Treatment: Transsphenoidal adenomectomy; may need somatostatin analogs (octreotide) to suppress TSH pre-operatively.

Subclinical Hyperthyroidism

Labs: ↓ TSH, normal T4/T3.

Significance: Increased risk of atrial fibrillation and osteoporosis (especially in elderly).

Common Causes: Over-replacement with levothyroxine, thyroid hormone secretion from nodules, early Graves'.

Thyroid Storm: Life-Threatening Emergency

Presentation: Extreme hyperthyroidism with:

  • Fever > 38.5°C (typically 39–40°C)
  • Heart rate > 140 bpm
  • Altered mental status (agitation, delirium, coma)
  • Precipitant (infection, surgery, stress, abrupt levothyroxine withdrawal)

Treatment (Mnemonic: "PTI&Dex"):

  • PTU (propylthiouracil) — blocks TPO and peripheral conversion of T4→T3
  • Topical cooling, IV fluids, supportive care
  • Iodine (SSKI, Lugol's solution) — MUST give AFTER PTU (iodine alone can worsen if PTU not given first)
  • Dexamethasone — blocks peripheral conversion, reduces inflammatory cytokine release
  • Propranolol — β-blocker for symptom control (tachycardia, tremor)
"TSH-oma = HIGH TSH + HIGH T4 (both elevated, feedback broken). Rare but high-yield trap on boards."
⚠️ TRAP: Seeing both TSH and T4 elevated = think TSH-secreting adenoma. Most students expect inverse relationship.
⭐ HY BULLET: Thyroid storm: PTU + propranolol + iodine (AFTER PTU) + dexamethasone + cooling. Do not delay treatment.

LO5: TSH Lab Interpretation in Clinical Scenarios

▶

TSH as Screening Test

Gold Standard: TSH is the BEST first screening test for thyroid disease in outpatient populations with intact pituitary-thyroid axis.

Reference Range: ~0.4–4.0 mIU/L (varies by lab; always check your lab's range).

Circadian Rhythm: TSH peaks at midnight, lowest in afternoon → sample in AM if possible for consistency.

When TSH Alone Misleads

Central Hypothyroidism: TSH low or normal despite low T4. Always check free T4 if central pathology suspected (pituitary/hypothalamic disease).

TSH-secreting Adenoma: TSH elevated + T4 high (doesn't suppress as expected). Rare but trap.

Euthyroid Sick Syndrome (NTIS): TSH may be low from dopamine/glucocorticoids in ICU despite mild T4 reduction. Check free T3 and rT3 to differentiate from true hypothyroidism.

Recovery from Hypothyroidism: After starting levothyroxine, free T4 normalizes first; TSH lags behind and stays elevated for weeks. Don't over-treat based on delayed TSH normalization.

Pregnancy: Lower TSH normal range, especially 1st trimester (hCG cross-reactivity with TSH-R). Know trimester-specific ranges.

Amiodarone Effect: Initially may ↑ TSH (Wolff-Chaikoff), later can normalize or shift unpredictably due to drug effects on metabolism.

Anti-TSH Antibodies (Heterophile Abs): Rare but can cause falsely elevated TSH on older assay platforms (lab artifact).

Second-Generation TSH Assays

Sensitivity: Modern assays are sensitive enough to detect suppressed TSH (e.g., in hyperthyroidism or over-replacement). Important for detecting subclinical hyper and for monitoring thyroid cancer suppression therapy.

Diagnostic Algorithm

  • Step 1: Check TSH
  • Step 2 (if abnormal): Add free T4
  • Step 3 (if T4 abnormal too): Consider free T3 and clinical exam
  • Step 4: Determine etiology (autoimmune, nodular, central, etc.)
🎯 TESTED CONCEPT: TSH normal does NOT always mean euthyroid. Must check free T4 in suspected central disease. Also, TSH lags in early treatment — don't judge adequacy by TSH alone initially.
⚠️ TRAP: Pregnancy 1st trimester = low TSH is NORMAL (hCG effect). Don't over-treat. Know trimester-specific reference ranges.

LO6: Thyroid Function in Special Populations

▶

Pregnancy

TBG Changes: Estrogen ↑ TBG → ↑ total T4 (but free T4 NORMAL) → euthyroid state. This is normal, not hyperthyroidism.

hCG Cross-Reactivity: hCG (especially high in 1st trimester) cross-reacts with TSH receptor → mild ↓ TSH. Physiologic, not pathologic.

TSH Reference Range by Trimester:

  • 1st trimester: 0.1–2.5 mIU/L (lower due to hCG)
  • 2nd trimester: 0.2–3.0 mIU/L
  • 3rd trimester: 0.3–3.5 mIU/L

Maternal Hypothyroidism: SEVERE risk to fetus (impairs brain development/IQ). Treat aggressively with levothyroxine. Maternal needs INCREASE 25–30% during pregnancy (due to ↑ TBG, ↑ renal clearance, placental transfer). Increase dose proactively or check TSH more frequently.

Postpartum Thyroiditis: Autoimmune condition; biphasic: hyperthyroid phase (4–8 weeks post-delivery, from inflammatory release of stored hormone) → hypothyroid phase → recovery in most (but recurs in future pregnancies). May need temporary T4 during hypo phase, or beta-blockers during hyper phase.

Neonates

TSH Surge at Birth: Physiologic rise in TSH after birth (adaptive response to extrauterine life and increased metabolic demands). Normalizes within 1–5 days.

Neonatal Screening: All newborns screened (dried blood spot) for elevated TSH (hypothyroidism) and low T4. Critical for detecting congenital hypothyroidism early to prevent intellectual disability.

Elderly

Subclinical Hypothyroidism (SCH) Common: Prevalence increases with age. Risk of progression to overt hypothyroidism ~2–5% per year (higher if TSH > 10).

Cardiovascular Risks of Hyperthyroidism Higher: Elderly more susceptible to atrial fibrillation and ischemic heart disease from excess thyroid hormone. Avoid over-treatment; aim for slightly higher TSH targets in this group.

Assay Interference: Biotin Supplementation

Issue: High-dose biotin (used for hair/nail health) can interfere with competitive immunoassay platforms.

Effect: Falsely LOW TSH and falsely HIGH T4 on same assay (opposite pattern — think "biotin breaks the inverse relationship").

Clinical Pearl: Ask patients about supplements, especially if TSH/T4 pattern seems odd. May need different assay platform or discontinue biotin 48 hours before testing.

"Pregnancy = ↑ TBG → ↑ total T4 but FREE T4 normal. hCG suppresses TSH in 1st trim → don't panic about low TSH. Increase levothyroxine dose 25–30% in pregnancy."
"Biotin breaks the axis — falsely LOW TSH + falsely HIGH T4 despite normal free T4. Ask about supplements."
🎯 TESTED CONCEPT: Pregnant patient with low TSH in 1st trimester = normal (hCG effect), not hyperthyroid. Don't treat. Increase levothyroxine dose 25–30% proactively.
⚠️ TRAP: Biotin supplements can falsely lower TSH and raise T4. Always ask about supplements if labs seem discordant.
⭐ HY BULLET: Postpartum thyroiditis: hyperthyroid phase → hypothyroid phase → recovery. Recurs in future pregnancies. Counsel patients appropriately.

Practice MCQs: 18 USMLE-Style Questions

▶
1. A 35-year-old woman presents with fatigue, weight gain, and cold intolerance. Labs: TSH 12.5 mIU/L, free T4 0.6 ng/dL (low). Which scenario best fits?
Correct: B) Primary hypothyroidism
Key: ↑ TSH + ↓ T4 = primary. The gland is broken; pituitary correctly compensates by ↑ TSH. Most common cause: Hashimoto's thyroiditis in US or iodine deficiency globally. Secondary/tertiary would show ↓ TSH despite low T4.
2. A 50-year-old man with known pituitary adenoma (resected 2 years ago) now has low energy. Labs: free T4 0.5 ng/dL (low), TSH 0.2 mIU/L (low). Which monitoring strategy is CORRECT?
Correct: C) Monitor with free T4; target upper-normal to high-normal
Critical concept: In secondary hypothyroidism (broken pituitary), TSH is unreliable because the pituitary itself is broken. Use FREE T4 to guide therapy. TSH will remain low/normal even with adequate replacement because the pituitary cannot respond normally. Trap: Don't use TSH for monitoring.
3. A 28-year-old woman in 1st trimester pregnancy (8 weeks) has routine labs: TSH 1.2 mIU/L, free T4 1.0 ng/dL (both normal). She's worried her TSH is "too low." What is the BEST response?
Correct: C) Reassure her; this is physiologic. hCG cross-reacts with TSH receptor in 1st trimester
High-yield trap: Low TSH in early pregnancy is NORMAL, not hyperthyroidism. hCG is a weak TSH-R agonist. Don't treat. Know trimester-specific TSH ranges: 1st trim 0.1–2.5, 2nd trim 0.2–3.0, 3rd trim 0.3–3.5.
4. A 60-year-old patient in the ICU on mechanical ventilation for sepsis has labs: T3 low, T4 low-normal, rT3 HIGH, TSH low. What is the BEST interpretation?
Correct: C) Euthyroid sick syndrome (NTIS); do NOT treat with thyroid hormone
Pathognomonic labs for NTIS: ↓ T3, ↑ rT3, low/normal TSH in critical illness. Caused by ↓ D1 activity (can't convert T4→T3). Treating with thyroid hormone worsens outcomes. Let the underlying illness resolve. This mimics central hypo on routine labs but is NOT true hypothyroidism.
5. A 45-year-old woman presents with tremor, palpitations, and weight loss. Labs: TSH 0.1 mIU/L (suppressed), free T4 3.2 ng/dL (elevated). Which is the MOST likely diagnosis?
Correct: B) Primary hyperthyroidism
The normal inverse relationship: ↑ T4 → suppressed TSH. This is primary hyper. Most common cause is Graves' (look for TSI antibodies). Toxic multinodular goiter and toxic adenoma also present similarly. TSH-oma is rare but would show BOTH TSH and T4 high (not suppressed).
6. A 52-year-old man has labs: TSH 8.5 mIU/L, free T4 3.8 ng/dL (BOTH elevated). Imaging shows a pituitary adenoma. What is the diagnosis?
Correct: B) TSH-secreting pituitary adenoma
HIGH-YIELD TRAP: TSH-oma is rare but board-tested. Key: BOTH TSH and T4 are elevated (breaks the normal inverse relationship). The adenoma secretes TSH independent of feedback inhibition. Check α-subunit (elevated). Treat with transsphenoidal surgery ± somatostatin analogs (octreotide).
7. A woman taking 50 mcg biotin daily for hair growth has labs checked: TSH 0.3 mIU/L (LOW), free T4 2.0 ng/dL (HIGH). She's clinically euthyroid. What is the MOST likely explanation?
Correct: B) Biotin assay interference
Biotin at high doses interferes with competitive immunoassays → falsely low TSH and falsely high T4 (opposite of what you'd expect). Clinical picture trumps labs: if she's euthyroid, suspect assay artifact. Recommend stopping biotin 48 hours before retesting or using alternative assay platform.
8. A patient started on levothyroxine for primary hypothyroidism 3 weeks ago has labs: free T4 normal, TSH still 8.5 mIU/L (high). Patient is clinically improved. What is the BEST next step?
Correct: B) Recheck in 6 weeks; TSH lags behind T4 normalization
After starting levothyroxine, free T4 normalizes first (2–4 weeks), but TSH takes weeks/months to fall (it lags). This is normal biology due to slow pituitary sensitivity resetting. Don't over-treat based on slow TSH decline. Recheck in 6 weeks.
9. A 30-year-old woman presents with tremor, sweating, and diffuse goiter. Labs: TSH 0.05 mIU/L, free T4 2.8 ng/dL. TSH-receptor antibodies (TSI) are positive. What is the diagnosis and expected eye finding?
Correct: B) Graves' disease; expect exophthalmos and pretibial myxedema
Graves' is the ONLY primary hyperthyroid condition with extrathyroidal manifestations (TSH-R antibodies attack orbital and skin tissues). Positive TSI confirms autoimmunity. Exophthalmos (orbitopathy), pretibial myxedema (glycosaminoglycan deposition), and thyroid acropachy are pathognomonic.
10. A patient in thyroid storm presents with fever (39.8°C), HR 160, altered mental status. Temperature will not come down with acetaminophen alone. Which treatment combination is ESSENTIAL?
Correct: B) PTU + propranolol + SSKI iodine (AFTER PTU) + dexamethasone
Mnemonic "PTI&Dex": PTU (blocks TPO + peripheral conversion), propranolol (symptom relief), iodine AFTER PTU (else worsens), dexamethasone (blocks conversion + immune). Never give iodine first (without PTU) or you'd supply substrate for more hormone synthesis. Cooling measures and supportive care essential.
11. A pregnant woman (16 weeks) has hypothyroidism. Before pregnancy, her levothyroxine dose was 75 mcg daily and TSH was 1.2 mIU/L. She continued 75 mcg during pregnancy. Recent labs: TSH 5.8 mIU/L, free T4 0.7 ng/dL (low). What is the BEST management?
Correct: B) Increase levothyroxine by 25–30%; check TSH every 4 weeks
Pregnancy increases T4 requirements by 25–30% due to ↑ TBG, ↑ renal clearance, and placental transfer. Maternal hypothyroidism risks fetal brain development. Increase dose proactively or check TSH frequently. Target TSH: 1st trim 0.1–2.5, 2nd trim 0.2–3.0. This is critical obstetric management.
12. A woman 6 weeks postpartum presents with transient hyperthyroid symptoms (tremor, anxiety, palpitations), then hypothyroid symptoms (fatigue, weight gain) over next 8 weeks, now recovering. What is the diagnosis?
Correct: B) Postpartum thyroiditis (autoimmune, biphasic)
Postpartum thyroiditis: transient hyperthyroid phase (weeks 4–8 postpartum from inflammatory release of stored hormone) → hypothyroid phase → recovery in 80% (20% progress to permanent hypo). Recurs in future pregnancies. Check TPO/thyroglobulin antibodies. Supportive treatment during phases.
13. A TRH stimulation test is performed. Normal response: TSH rises at 30 minutes. In a patient with a hypothalamic lesion (tertiary hypo), what is the EXPECTED response?
Correct: B) Delayed/prolonged TSH response
TRH stim differentiates secondary from tertiary: Blunted/absent = secondary (pituitary is broken). Delayed = tertiary (hypothalamic problem; pituitary is intact but starved of TRH, so slower to respond). Normal = intact axis. Key to know for boards.
14. A hypothyroid patient on levothyroxine develops galactorrhea. What is the MOST likely mechanism?
Correct: B) Inadequate T4 replacement; ↑ TRH → ↑ prolactin
Ongoing hypothyroidism = persistent ↑ TRH. TRH is not thyroid-specific; it ALSO stimulates prolactin release from lactotrophs. This is why hypothyroid patients can have elevated prolactin and galactorrhea. Increase T4 dose to suppress TRH → prolactin normalizes.
15. A 55-year-old man with diabetes and hypertension is found to have subclinical hypothyroidism (TSH 7.2 mIU/L, normal free T4). He is asymptomatic. What is the BEST approach?
Correct: B) Recheck TSH in 6–12 weeks; treat only if TSH > 10 or symptoms develop
Subclinical hypothyroidism (↑ TSH, normal free T4) is early compensation. Not all progress. Treatment threshold: TSH > 10 mIU/L OR symptomatic OR pregnant. Recheck to confirm trend; ~2–5% per year progress to overt. No need to treat empirically at TSH 7.
16. A 70-year-old woman is discovered to have subclinical hyperthyroidism (TSH 0.2 mIU/L, normal free T4). She has atrial fibrillation and osteoporosis. What is the PRIMARY concern?
Correct: B) Low TSH increases risk of atrial fibrillation and bone loss (especially in elderly)
Subclinical hyperthyroidism (low TSH, normal T4) carries cardiovascular and skeletal risks, especially in elderly. Excess thyroid hormone worsens A-fib rate and accelerates osteoporosis. If she's over-replaced on levothyroxine, reduce dose. If nodular disease, may need treatment.
17. A 40-year-old man on dopamine infusion for septic shock has labs: TSH 0.3 mIU/L (low), free T4 0.9 ng/dL (low-normal), rT3 normal. Which is the BEST interpretation?
Correct: B) TSH suppressed by dopamine (expected in ICU); not true hypothyroidism; do NOT treat
Dopamine infusions suppress TSH. In ICU, low TSH + low-normal T4 is often from dopamine, not true hypothyroidism. Resting normal rT3 (vs elevated in NTIS) supports this. Do NOT treat. If dopamine is stopped, TSH should recover.
18. A patient with known Hashimoto's hypothyroidism is well-controlled on levothyroxine. A new provider starts high-dose supplements containing biotin, iodine, and selenium. Next labs: TSH 2.1 mIU/L (still normal), but free T4 measured as 2.5 ng/dL (elevated, different from previous 1.2). Patient reports no new symptoms. What happened?
Correct: C) Biotin causing falsely HIGH free T4 assay result; clinical status unchanged (euthyroid); reassess off biotin
Biotin interferes with immunoassays: falsely low TSH AND falsely high free T4 (opposite directions). TSH remained normal because biotin doesn't typically lower TSH as much as it raises measured T4. Clinical picture: patient euthyroid. Stop biotin, recheck in 48 hours. Iodine would supply substrate (less relevant in Hashimoto's where gland is fibrotic).

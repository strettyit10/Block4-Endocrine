Cushing's, Addison's, CAH, tumors, hyperaldosteronism & incidentalomas

LO1: Cushing's Syndrome — Pathology and Diagnosis

Definition & Etiologies ▼

Cushing's Syndrome Definition

  • Chronic glucocorticoid excess (endogenous or exogenous)
  • Most common cause: EXOGENOUS glucocorticoids (iatrogenic) — most common overall
  • Endogenous Cushing's rarer (~15-20% of all cases)

Endogenous Causes

  • Cushing's disease (pituitary ACTH adenoma): ~70% of endogenous cases; basophilic adenoma; bilateral adrenal hyperplasia
  • Ectopic ACTH: ~15%; small cell lung cancer (most common), bronchial carcinoid, pancreatic NETs; VERY high ACTH → severe hypokalemia + alkalosis
  • Adrenal adenoma: ~10%; unilateral; autonomous cortisol → ↓ACTH → contralateral atrophy
  • Adrenal carcinoma: ~5%; large tumors; mixed cortisol + androgens
  • PPNAD (Carney complex association): pigmented nodular disease
  • AIMAH: aberrant receptor expression (GIP, LH, vasopressin)
Clinical Features & Pathology ▼

Clinical Presentation

  • Central obesity (buffalo hump, moon face, supraclavicular fat pads)
  • Proximal muscle weakness (protein catabolism)
  • Wide purple striae (>1cm) — distinguishes from simple obesity
  • Easy bruising, thin skin (collagen breakdown)
  • Hirsutism, acne (androgens); virilization in women with carcinoma
  • Hypertension, hyperglycemia/DM, osteoporosis
  • Mood disturbance (depression, psychosis), poor wound healing
  • Immunosuppression → infections

Adrenal Pathology

  • Cushing's disease: bilateral adrenal hyperplasia (diffuse or nodular)
  • Adrenal adenoma: single gland enlarged, lipid-rich cells (zona fasciculata); contralateral atrophy
  • Adrenal carcinoma: usually >6cm; necrosis, vascular invasion; Weiss criteria (>3 of 9 features)
  • Ectopic ACTH adrenals: hyperplastic from continuous ACTH stimulation
Key Diagnostic Testing ▼

Screening Tests

  • 24-hour urine free cortisol (most common initial test)
  • 1mg overnight DST (dexamethasone suppression test) — cortisol should suppress to <1.8 mcg/dL
  • Late-night salivary cortisol (high specificity)

Localization Tests (After Confirmed Cushing's)

  • ACTH level: if ↓ ACTH = adrenal source; if ↑ ACTH = pituitary or ectopic
  • High-dose DST (8mg): if cortisol suppresses → pituitary (Cushing's disease); if doesn't suppress → adrenal or ectopic
  • CRH stimulation test: ↑ ACTH if pituitary; no response if adrenal or ectopic
  • CT/MRI adrenal: adenoma vs hyperplasia vs carcinoma
  • Chest CT if ectopic suspected (lung primary most common)
TRAP: Exogenous is Most Common!
  • Iatrogenic steroid use = #1 cause of Cushing's overall
  • Exam question: "Most common cause of Cushing's syndrome?" = Exogenous glucocorticoids
  • Don't default to pituitary adenoma — that's #1 endogenous
Mnemonic: CUSHING'S
  • Central fat (buffalo hump, moon face)
  • Ugly striae (purple, wide)
  • Sugar ↑ (hyperglycemia)
  • Hypertension
  • Infections ↑
  • Neurasthenia (mood changes)
  • Growth stunted (in children)
Quick Recall Flashcards
Ectopic ACTH → ? lab finding
Very HIGH ACTH (>200), severe hypokalemia + alkalosis, no suppress on high-dose DST
Adrenal adenoma → contralateral gland?
Atrophied (autonomous cortisol suppresses contralateral ACTH)
Cushing's disease = Bilateral adrenal hyperplasia via ?
Pituitary ACTH adenoma driving chronic stimulation

LO2: Adrenal Insufficiency (Primary — Addison's Disease)

Pathology & Etiology ▼

Addison's Disease Definition

  • PRIMARY adrenal insufficiency = destruction of ALL 3 cortical zones
  • Results: ↓ cortisol + ↓ aldosterone + ↓ adrenal androgens
  • Unlike secondary (pituitary) insufficiency: aldosterone preserved (renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system intact)

Major Etiologies

  • Autoimmune adrenalitis (most common in developed world): anti-21-hydroxylase antibodies; bilateral cortical destruction; lymphocytic infiltrate; ACTH very high → MSH-like activity → hyperpigmentation
  • TB adrenalitis (historically #1 worldwide): bilateral; caseous necrosis → calcification on CT
  • Fungal (histoplasma, coccidioides): granulomatous destruction
  • Metastatic cancer (lung, breast most common): bilateral infiltration
  • Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome: meningococcemia → bilateral hemorrhagic infarction → acute crisis with petechiae, purpura, DIC, shock
Autoimmune Polyendocrine Syndromes (APS) ▼

APS-1 (APECED)

  • Addison's + hypoparathyroidism + mucocutaneous candidiasis
  • AIRE gene mutation (rare)

APS-2 (Schmidt Syndrome)

  • Addison's + autoimmune thyroiditis (Hashimoto's or Graves') + Type 1 DM
  • Most common form; HLA-DR3/DR4 association
Clinical & Labs ▼

Clinical Features

  • Hyperpigmentation (ACTH → MSH): skin creases, buccal mucosa, scars, nipples, pressure areas
  • Fatigue, weakness, anorexia, nausea, weight loss (insidious onset)
  • Abdominal pain, diarrhea
  • Orthostatic hypotension (aldosterone loss → volume depletion)
  • Acute crisis (Addisonian crisis): hypotension, shock, abdominal pain (can be triggered by infection, surgery, stress)

Key Lab Findings

  • ↓ Na+ (volume loss from ↓ aldosterone + SIADH-like cortisol deficiency effect)
  • ↑ K+ (↓ aldosterone → no Na/K exchange)
  • ↓ glucose (cortisol deficiency → ↓ gluconeogenesis)
  • ↑ ACTH (loss of cortisol feedback suppression)
  • ↑ renin (volume depletion + ↓ aldosterone)
  • ↓ cortisol (diagnostic)
  • Eosinophilia (cortisol normally suppresses eosinophils)

Histology

  • Atrophied cortex with lymphocytic infiltrate (autoimmune)
  • Collapsed reticular fibers; medulla preserved
  • TB: granulomas with caseating necrosis
  • Calcification may be visible on imaging (TB)
TRAP: Eosinophilia in Addison's!
  • Cortisol is an immunosuppressant
  • When cortisol is LOW → eosinophils ↑ (not ↓)
  • This is pathognomonic for primary adrenal insufficiency
Mnemonic: ADDISON'S AKA
  • ACTH ↑
  • K ↑ (hyperkalemia)
  • Aldosterone ↓
  • Skin Ashy (hyperpigmentation)
  • Na ↓ (hyponatremia)
  • Adrenal atrophy/destruction
Quick Recall Flashcards
Waterhouse-Friderichsen = ?
Meningococcemia with bilateral adrenal hemorrhage; acute crisis, DIC, petechiae/purpura
Hyperpigmentation in Addison's via ?
High ACTH → MSH-like activity from POMC processing
Addison's crisis can be triggered by?
Infection, surgery, stress (cortisol reserve depleted)

LO3: Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (CAH)

CAH Overview & Biochemistry ▼

CAH Definition

  • Autosomal recessive disorders of adrenal steroidogenesis
  • Cortisol deficiency → ↑↑ ACTH → bilateral adrenal hyperplasia
  • Substrate accumulation → shunted to alternate pathways (androgens)

21-Hydroxylase Deficiency (~90% of CAH)

  • Enzyme: CYP21A2 converts progesterone → 11-deoxycorticosterone (aldosterone path) AND 17-OH-progesterone → 11-deoxycortisol (cortisol path)
  • Blockage result: ↑ 17-OH-progesterone (diagnostic), ↓ cortisol, shunt to androgens → virilization
  • Severity spectrum: Classic salt-wasting → Classic simple virilizing → Non-classic (mild)
21-Hydroxylase: Clinical Forms ▼

Classic Salt-Wasting Form (Severe)

  • ↓ cortisol + ↓ aldosterone
  • Girls: VIRILIZED genitalia at birth (clitoromegaly, labial fusion) — virilization in utero from excess androgens
  • Boys: appear NORMAL at birth (already have penis) → salt-wasting crisis in first 2-3 weeks (hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, shock) if untreated
  • Triggers: neonatal screening, salt-wasting crisis brings to medical attention

Classic Simple Virilizing (Partial)

  • ↓ cortisol, normal/adequate aldosterone
  • Girls: virilized genitalia
  • Boys: true precocious puberty (accelerated growth, advanced bone age, early penile enlargement)
  • No salt loss

Non-Classic (Mild/Late-Onset)

  • Normal cortisol and aldosterone at baseline
  • Presentation: young women (teens to 30s) with hirsutism, oligomenorrhea, acne, infertility
  • Diagnosis: elevated 17-OH-progesterone (especially after ACTH stimulation test)
  • NO genital ambiguity (cortisol usually adequate)
11β-Hydroxylase & 17α-Hydroxylase Deficiency ▼

11β-Hydroxylase Deficiency (2nd Most Common, ~5-8%)

  • Blocks: 11-deoxycortisol → cortisol AND 11-deoxycorticosterone (DOC) → corticosterone
  • Accumulation: DOC (mineralocorticoid) → HYPERTENSION + hypokalemia (OPPOSITE of salt-wasting!)
  • Androgens: ↑ → virilization in girls
  • Diagnosis: ↑ 11-deoxycortisol (Compound S), ↑ DOC, virilization + HYPERTENSION

17α-Hydroxylase Deficiency (Rare)

  • Blocks: sex steroid synthesis AND cortisol (17-position needed for both)
  • Aldosterone: pathway intact (doesn't require 17α-hydroxylase)
  • Result: ↓ sex steroids (hypogonadism, no puberty), ↑ DOC/corticosterone → HYPERTENSION + hypokalemia
  • Phenotype: 46XX: phenotypically female, primary amenorrhea; 46XY: phenotypically female or intersex (NO virilization)
  • Diagnostic clue: Hypertension + hypokalemia + NO virilization + absent secondary sexual characteristics
Diagnosis & Management ▼

Diagnosis

  • ↑ ACTH (from cortisol deficiency feedback)
  • ↑ androgens (testosterone, DHEA)
  • Elevated precursor based on enzyme defect (17-OH-progesterone for 21-OH; 11-deoxycortisol for 11β-OH)
  • ACTH stimulation test may be needed for non-classic forms
  • Bilateral adrenal hyperplasia on imaging

Treatment (21-Hydroxylase)

  • Hydrocortisone: replaces cortisol + suppresses ACTH → reduces androgen overproduction
  • Fludrocortisone: added in salt-wasting forms (replaces aldosterone function)
  • Stress dosing education: patients taught to increase hydrocortisone during illness, surgery, trauma
  • Goal: suppress ACTH to normal, normalize androgens, allow normal growth/development
TRAP: Boys with Salt-Wasting
  • Boys born with normal appearance (have penis, normal scrotum) — easy to miss diagnosis at birth
  • Salt-wasting crisis in days 1-3 of life → vomiting, shock, hyperkalemia, hyponatremia
  • Neonatal screening detects elevated 17-OH-progesterone BEFORE crisis occurs
21-OH vs 11β-OH vs 17α-OH
  • 21-OH: ↑17-OH-prog, salt-wasting OR virilization
  • 11β-OH: ↑11-deoxycortisol, HTN + virilization (DOC effect)
  • 17α-OH: ↑DOC, HTN + NO sex steroids (hypogonadism)
Quick Recall Flashcards
Most sensitive/specific test for 21-OH?
Elevated 17-OH-progesterone (baseline or after ACTH stimulation)
11β-OH deficiency = HTN + ? + virilization
Hypokalemia (from DOC mineralocorticoid activity)
17α-OH = HTN + no sex steroids, phenotype?
Phenotypically female (46XX or 46XY), absent/absent puberty, primary amenorrhea

LO4: Adrenal Tumors — Adenoma, Carcinoma, Pheochromocytoma

Adrenocortical Adenoma ▼

General Features

  • Common: found in 2-10% of autopsies
  • Usually <4cm; typically smooth, homogeneous
  • Most are NON-FUNCTIONING (no hormone excess)
  • Presents as "adrenal incidentaloma" on imaging for other reason

Workup of Incidental Adenoma

  • 1mg overnight DST (rule out subclinical Cushing's)
  • Plasma free metanephrines or 24-hr urine metanephrines (rule out pheo)
  • K+ and aldosterone/renin ratio (rule out Conn's)
  • DHEA-S if concern for carcinoma

Imaging Features

  • CT: homogeneous, smooth, <10 HU (lipid-rich = benign) → observe
  • >10 HU → suspicious → consider washout protocol (contrast washout to assess lipid content)
  • If functioning: CT shows unilateral mass

Management

  • If non-functioning and <4cm: observe (repeat imaging in 6-12 months)
  • If functioning: adrenalectomy
  • If >4cm or growing: adrenalectomy
  • Adrenalectomy MUST be preceded by pheo workup (metanephrines!) to prevent intraoperative crisis
Adrenocortical Carcinoma ▼

Epidemiology & Presentation

  • Rare; bimodal age distribution: children <5 years AND adults 40-50
  • Very poor prognosis: 5-year survival ~30%
  • Usually large (>6cm) at diagnosis
  • Left side more common
  • May produce mixed cortisol + androgens → Cushing's + virilization features together

Histology: Weiss Criteria (>3 = malignant)

  • High mitotic rate (>5 mitoses per 50 high-power fields)
  • Atypical mitoses
  • Necrosis
  • Venous invasion
  • Sinusoidal invasion
  • Capsular invasion
  • Diffuse (non-nodular) architecture
  • Eosinophilic cytoplasm
  • Nuclear pleomorphism

Molecular Features

  • TP53 mutations (often)
  • IGF-2 overexpression
  • β-catenin mutations
  • Immunohistochemistry: inhibin A/B (+), SF-1 (+), melan-A (+); CK7/CK20 (-)

Treatment

  • Adrenalectomy (partial resection if possible to preserve function)
  • Mitotane: adrenolytic agent; inhibits steroidogenesis; used as adjuvant
  • Multimodal: surgery + chemotherapy + radiation + immunotherapy depending on stage
Pheochromocytoma ▼

Basics

  • Chromaffin cell tumor of adrenal medulla
  • Secretes catecholamines (epi, norepi) → episodic HTN, sweating, palpitations
  • Triad: headache + profuse sweating + palpitations (80% have this)
  • Hypertension (sustained in ~50%, paroxysmal in ~50%)
  • Rule of 10s: ~10% bilateral, ~10% extra-adrenal (paraganglioma), ~25% malignant (updated from old 10%)

Diagnosis

  • 24-hour urine catecholamines and metanephrines (most sensitive traditional test)
  • Plasma free metanephrines (most sensitive for HEREDITARY pheo — use this in young patients or familial cases)
  • CT/MRI: large, heterogeneous, enhancing mass; MRI T2 "light bulb" sign
  • 123I-MIBG scintigraphy or PET/CT for metastatic disease

Hereditary Syndromes (Test Mandatory in ALL Pheo)

  • MEN2A (RET): medullary thyroid cancer (MTC) + pheochromocytoma + hyperparathyroidism
  • MEN2B (RET): MTC + pheochromocytoma + marfanoid habitus + mucosal neuromas
  • VHL (von Hippel-Lindau): hemangioblastomas + renal cell carcinoma (RCC) + pheochromocytoma
  • NF1: neurofibromatosis type 1
  • SDH mutations (SDHB/SDHC/SDHD): mostly paragangliomas; SDHB → HIGH malignancy risk, metastatic disease
  • Overall: 25-30% of pheo/paraganglioma patients have germline mutations → genetic testing recommended for ALL

Pre-Op Management (CRITICAL)

  • ALPHA-BLOCKER FIRST (phenoxybenzamine preferred — non-selective, irreversible; or doxazosin)
  • Phenoxybenzamine: non-selective alpha blocker; irreversible; best choice for perioperative control
  • NEVER give beta-blocker FIRST or ALONE → unopposed alpha effect → hypertensive crisis
  • Beta-blocker ONLY after adequate alpha blockade (nifedipine, labetalol, or metoprolol after phenoxybenzamine)
  • High salt + fluid intake (expand chronically vasoconstricted volume)
  • Goal: normalize BP, prevent intraoperative catecholamine surge
TRAP: Beta-Blocker FIRST = DEATH
  • Blocking beta-mediated vasodilation ONLY → unopposed alpha vasoconstriction
  • Result: hypertensive crisis, MI, stroke, arrhythmias
  • ALWAYS alpha-block FIRST, then beta-block
  • This is a classic USMLE trap
Pheochromocytoma Mnemonic
  • Paroxysmal HTN
  • Headache
  • Episodic Sweating
  • Orthostatic hypotension (paradoxical)
  • Metanephrines (diagnostic test)
  • Alpha FIRST
Quick Recall Flashcards
ACC >6cm = must have done what first?
Metanephrine testing (rule out pheochromocytoma before biopsy/surgery)
Best test for hereditary pheochromocytoma?
Plasma free metanephrines (highest sensitivity for hereditary cases)
SDHB mutation → ? risk in paraganglioma
HIGH malignancy + metastatic disease (lymph nodes, liver, bone)

LO5: Hyperaldosteronism (Conn's Syndrome)

Primary Hyperaldosteronism Overview ▼

Conn's Syndrome Definition

  • PRIMARY hyperaldosteronism: autonomous aldosterone secretion → suppressed renin
  • Most common surgically CORRECTABLE cause of hypertension
  • Prevalence: 5-10% of all hypertensive patients (underdiagnosed)

Etiologies

  • Aldosterone-producing adenoma (APA/Conn's adenoma): ~35%
  • Bilateral idiopathic hyperplasia (IHA): ~60% (most common overall)
  • Unilateral adrenal hyperplasia: ~5%
  • Familial hyperaldosteronism (FH)
  • Adrenocortical carcinoma: <1%
Clinical & Laboratory Findings ▼

Clinical Features

  • Hypertension (often resistant to 3+ drugs)
  • Hypokalemia (may be normal — only ~50% present with low K)
  • Metabolic alkalosis (↑ HCO3-, ↑ pH)
  • Muscle weakness, cramps, tetany (from hypokalemia)
  • Polyuria, polydipsia (↓ aldosterone sensitivity → ADH resistance)
  • Headache

Key Labs

  • ↑ aldosterone (morning recumbent or 24-hr urine)
  • ↓↓ renin (suppressed by high aldosterone)
  • Aldosterone:renin ratio (ARR) — SCREENING test; >30 = suspicious; >50 = highly suspicious
  • Hypokalemia (K <3.0 in severe cases, but often normal!)
  • Metabolic alkalosis (HCO3- ↑, pH ↑)
Diagnostic Algorithm ▼

Step 1: Screening

  • Aldosterone:renin ratio (ARR) during morning recumbency, off spironolactone/ACE-I/ARB if possible
  • ARR >30 = suspicious; >50 = very suspicious

Step 2: Confirmatory Test (If ARR Elevated)

  • Oral sodium loading test (best in primary care): high Na+ diet 5 days, 24-hr urine Na >200 mEq/day → if aldosterone not suppressed = PHA
  • Saline infusion test: IV normal saline → if aldosterone doesn't suppress <4 ng/dL = PHA
  • Fludrocortisone suppression test: IV fludrocortisone → no aldosterone suppression = PHA
  • Captopril test: ACE inhibitor → renin ↑ in normal; aldosterone unchanged in PHA

Step 3: Localization (CT/MRI Adrenal)

  • Small unilateral nodule (<1cm) = likely APA
  • Bilateral nodules or no clear nodule = IHA

Step 4: Adrenal Vein Sampling (AVS)

  • REQUIRED before surgery if >40 years old to lateralize (which gland is responsible)
  • Distinguishes unilateral APA (surgery candidate) from bilateral IHA (medical therapy)
  • If AVS shows >4:1 aldosterone gradient (affected side:IVC) = unilateral
Management & Special Forms ▼

Treatment

  • Unilateral APA: adrenalectomy (cures HTN in 35-50% of cases, improves in most others)
  • Bilateral IHA: aldosterone antagonist — spironolactone (Aldactone) or eplerenone (Inspra)
  • Spironolactone side effects: gynecomastia, menstrual irregularities (androgen antagonism)
  • Eplerenone: more selective (fewer sex hormone side effects) but more expensive

Familial Hyperaldosteronism Type 1 (FH-1/GRA)

  • Glucocorticoid-remediable aldosteronism (GRA)
  • Chimeric gene: ACTH-sensitive aldosterone synthase (normally aldosterone synthase is not ACTH-responsive)
  • Treatment: dexamethasone (suppresses ACTH → ↓ aldosterone) — unique to this form!
  • Young age, family history, early HTN should raise suspicion

Adrenocortical Adenoma Pathology

  • Small (<2cm), bright yellow (lipid-rich)
  • Composed of zona glomerulosa cells hyperplasia
  • "Spironolactone bodies": lamellar inclusions seen in treated cases
TRAP: Normal K+ in Conn's!
  • 50% of patients with primary hyperaldosteronism have normal serum K+
  • Don't rule out PHA based on "normal potassium"
  • Use aldosterone:renin ratio regardless of K+ level
Conn's Mnemonic
  • Can't keep K+ (hypokalemia)
  • Opposing renin (suppressed)
  • Needs ARR (aldosterone:renin ratio)
  • Needs AVS (adrenal vein sampling)
  • 'S
Quick Recall Flashcards
ARR >30 means? Step next?
Suspicious for PHA → do confirmatory test (saline load, oral Na, fludrocortisone, or captopril)
AVS shows 4:1 aldosterone gradient = ?
Unilateral (affected:IVC) → surgery candidate

LO6: Adrenal Incidentaloma and Adrenal Medulla Pathology

Adrenal Incidentaloma Management ▼

Definition & Prevalence

  • Adrenal mass found incidentally on imaging (CT, MRI, PET) done for unrelated reason
  • Found in 2-4% of abdominal CTs; increases with age
  • Most are benign non-functioning adenomas

Mandatory Hormonal Workup (ALL Incidentalomas)

  • 1mg overnight DST (rule out subclinical Cushing's)
  • Plasma free metanephrines or 24-hr urine catecholamines/metanephrines (rule out pheochromocytoma — DO THIS BEFORE ANY SURGERY OR CONTRAST INJECTION!)
  • Aldosterone/renin ratio (if hypertensive)
  • DHEA-S (if concern for carcinoma)

Imaging Interpretation

  • Size: <4cm = generally benign; >6cm = consider adrenalectomy (malignancy risk)
  • Hounsfield Units (HU): <10 HU = lipid-rich (benign adenoma) → observe; >10 HU = suspicious → need washout protocol
  • Washout protocol: non-contrast baseline HU → 15-min delayed post-contrast HU; benign adenomas retain contrast poorly (high washout %); malignancy retains contrast (low washout %)
  • Morphology: smooth homogeneous = benign; heterogeneous with enhancement = suspicious

Management Algorithm

  • Non-functioning, <4cm, HU <10: observe with repeat imaging at 6-12 months, 1-2 years
  • Non-functioning, >4cm OR HU >10: consider adrenalectomy (malignancy risk)
  • Functioning (any size): adrenalectomy
  • >6cm: adrenalectomy (high carcinoma risk)
  • ANY incidentaloma before surgery/biopsy: metanephrine test MANDATORY (pheo can cause intraoperative crisis)
Paraganglioma ▼

Definition & Locations

  • Extra-adrenal pheochromocytoma; arise from sympathetic or parasympathetic ganglia
  • Rule of 10s applies (updated: ~25% malignant)

Sympathetic Paraganglioma

  • Retroperitoneal (most common site)
  • Usually catecholamine-secreting (hypertension, epistaxis)
  • Organ of Zuckerkandl: paraaortic sympathetic paraganglia (regress in infancy normally)

Parasympathetic Paraganglioma

  • Head and neck location (carotid body, jugulotympanic, vagal)
  • Usually NON-secreting (no catecholamine symptoms)
  • Presents as pulsatile neck mass or cranial nerve deficits

Genetics & Malignancy

  • SDHB mutation: HIGH malignancy risk, metastatic potential (lymph nodes, liver, bone, lung)
  • SDHC, SDHD, SDH-others: typically non-malignant
  • Genetic testing recommended (SDH mutations ~30-40% of malignant paragangliomas)
Neuroblastoma ▼

Epidemiology

  • Most common solid EXTRACRANIAL tumor of children
  • Median age at diagnosis: <2 years
  • Arises from neural crest cells in adrenal medulla or sympathetic chain

Clinical Presentation

  • Abdominal mass (often incidental on imaging)
  • May be asymptomatic or present with abdominal distension/pain
  • Catecholamine secretion: hypertension, VMA/HVA elevated in urine
  • Paraneoplastic syndromes: opsoclonus-myoclonus (antibody-mediated)
  • Metastatic disease: bone, liver, lymph nodes

Imaging & Pathology

  • Abdominal mass that CROSSES MIDLINE (vs Wilms' tumor of kidney, which does NOT cross midline)
  • Heterogeneous, may have calcification
  • Histology: small round blue cells; Homer-Wright pseudorosettes (neuroblasts surrounding neuropil without true rosette)
  • Ganglioneuroma: mature, benign end of spectrum (differentiated ganglion cells + schwann cells); no treatment if asymptomatic

Prognostic Factors & Treatment

  • MYCN amplification: poor prognosis (proto-oncogene amplification)
  • Age <2 years and diploid DNA: better prognosis
  • Histology: favorable (well-differentiated) vs unfavorable (undifferentiated)
  • Stage: depends on degree of metastasis
  • Low-risk disease: surgery alone; intermediate/high-risk: multimodal chemotherapy + surgery + radiation + immunotherapy (anti-GD2 monoclonal antibody)
TRAP: Pheo Before Surgery!
  • Must exclude pheochromocytoma BEFORE any adrenal biopsy, surgery, or contrast injection
  • Can trigger hypertensive crisis, MI, arrhythmias
  • Metanephrines FIRST, imaging second
Wilms' vs Neuroblastoma
  • Wilms' (renal): from kidney, does NOT cross midline
  • Neuroblastoma (adrenal): from adrenal/sympathetic, CROSSES midline
  • Both most common childhood tumors; age <5 years
Quick Recall Flashcards
Incidentaloma <4cm, HU <10, non-functioning → ?
Observe with repeat imaging at 6-12 months, 1-2 years (low malignancy risk)
Neuroblastoma crosses midline, so what's different from Wilms'?
Neuroblastoma = adrenal origin (crosses); Wilms' = renal origin (confined to kidney)
SDHB mutation → paraganglioma malignancy risk?
HIGH; metastatic disease to lymph nodes, liver, bone (genetic testing needed)

Practice MCQs — USMLE-Style (3 per LO)

1. A 45-year-old woman presents with hypertension, central obesity, easy bruising, and proximal muscle weakness. Labs show 24-hour urine free cortisol 2x normal. 1mg overnight DST shows cortisol 8 mcg/dL (does NOT suppress). Which is the MOST COMMON overall cause of her Cushing's syndrome?
ANSWER: B
Exogenous glucocorticoids (iatrogenic from steroids used for various conditions) is the MOST COMMON cause of Cushing's syndrome overall. While pituitary adenoma (Cushing's disease) is the most common ENDOGENOUS cause (~70% of endogenous), exogenous far exceeds endogenous in prevalence. This is a classic USMLE trap.
2. A 35-year-old male with small cell lung cancer develops rapidly progressive hypertension, severe hypokalemia (K+ 2.1 mEq/L), and metabolic alkalosis. ACTH is 156 pg/mL (very elevated). High-dose DST shows no cortisol suppression. Which is the most likely diagnosis?
ANSWER: C
Ectopic ACTH from small cell lung cancer is the classic presentation: VERY HIGH ACTH (>200 typical), severe hypokalemia + alkalosis from intense glucocorticoid effect, and no suppression on high-dose DST (because tumor produces ACTH independently, not responsive to dexamethasone). The combination of small cell lung cancer + these findings = ectopic ACTH.
3. A 28-year-old woman with hyperpigmented skin, fatigue, anorexia, and hyponatremia (Na+ 128 mEq/L) is found to have ↑ K+ (5.8 mEq/L), ↑ ACTH (200 pg/mL), ↓ cortisol, and ↓ aldosterone. CBC shows eosinophilia. Which lab finding is PATHOGNOMONIC for primary adrenal insufficiency?
ANSWER: B
EOSINOPHILIA is pathognomonic for primary adrenal insufficiency. Cortisol is a potent immunosuppressant, and when cortisol levels are LOW, eosinophils are not suppressed and rise. This finding distinguishes primary adrenal insufficiency from secondary (pituitary) insufficiency, which would also have low cortisol but normal-to-low eosinophil counts (because other pituitary hormones are intact). The other findings (hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, high ACTH) can occur in both primary and secondary deficiency.
4. A newborn girl is found to have ambiguous genitalia (clitoromegaly, labial fusion). Labs: ↑↑ ACTH, ↓ cortisol, ↓ aldosterone, ↑ 17-OH-progesterone. What is the most likely diagnosis and expected presentation in an UNTREATED boy with the same condition?
ANSWER: B
Girl with ambiguous genitalia + very elevated 17-OH-progesterone + low aldosterone = 21-hydroxylase deficiency, salt-wasting form (most severe). The key point: boys with salt-wasting CAH appear NORMAL at birth (they have a penis already!). The diagnosis is often missed until days 1-3 of life when the salt-wasting crisis occurs (hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, shock, vomiting). This is why newborn screening for elevated 17-OH-progesterone is critical — to catch boys BEFORE crisis. Girls are diagnosed earlier due to obvious virilization.
5. An 8-year-old boy presents with rapid growth acceleration, advanced bone age, and early penile enlargement. Labs: ↑ ACTH, ↑ testosterone, ↑ DHEA, normal cortisol at baseline, ↑ 17-OH-progesterone. Which form of CAH is this, and why is aldosterone normal?
ANSWER: B
Classic SIMPLE VIRILIZING form of 21-hydroxylase deficiency: cortisol is adequate (modest enzyme deficiency allowing some cortisol synthesis), but androgen shunting is prominent. Aldosterone pathway is preserved (partial block, not complete). Boy presents with precocious puberty (true precocious puberty, not pseudo), rapid growth, advanced bone age. No salt-wasting (aldosterone intact). Girls would show virilization; boys show early development.
6. A 16-year-old girl with hirsutism, oligomenorrhea, and acne undergoes evaluation. Cortisol and aldosterone are normal at baseline. ACTH stimulation test shows ↑ 17-OH-progesterone (elevation only after ACTH, not at baseline). Which diagnosis is most consistent with NON-CLASSIC presentation?
ANSWER: A
Non-classic (late-onset) CAH: normal baseline cortisol/aldosterone, elevated 17-OH-progesterone AFTER ACTH stimulation (not at baseline). Presents in young women (teens to 30s) with hirsutism, oligomenorrhea, acne, infertility — mild adrenal androgen excess without cortisol/aldosterone deficiency. No genital ambiguity. ACTH stimulation test required for diagnosis.
7. A 42-year-old man is found to have a 3cm left adrenal mass on CT done for abdominal pain (HU 5). He is hypertensive but asymptomatic. Labs: cortisol suppresses on 1mg DST, metanephrines normal, aldosterone/renin ratio normal. CT washout protocol: 80% washout at 15 minutes. What is the most appropriate next step?
ANSWER: B
Non-functioning incidentaloma <4cm, low HU (5), benign imaging features (smooth, 80% washout = typical of adenoma). All hormonal tests normal. Management: observe with repeat imaging at 6-12 months, then 1-2 years. High washout % indicates lipid-rich benign adenoma. No surgery needed. If it was >4cm or HU >10, adrenalectomy would be considered.
8. A 38-year-old woman with resistant hypertension (on 4 antihypertensives), hypokalemia (K+ 3.0), and hyponatremia is evaluated. Aldosterone 22 ng/dL, renin <0.5 ng/mL/hr, ARR 44. Oral sodium loading test shows aldosterone DOES NOT suppress. What is the most likely diagnosis?
ANSWER: B
Primary hyperaldosteronism (Conn's syndrome): elevated aldosterone, suppressed renin, ARR >30 (here 44), and FAILURE to suppress aldosterone on oral sodium loading = diagnostic. Resistant hypertension, hypokalemia (not always present), suppressed renin are classic. Secondary hyperaldosteronism would SUPPRESS aldosterone on salt loading (appropriate response to volume expansion).
9. A 55-year-old man with primary hyperaldosteronism on imaging has a 1.5cm left adrenal nodule and normal right gland. Before recommending left adrenalectomy, which test should be MANDATORY to confirm unilateral disease?
ANSWER: C
Adrenal vein sampling (AVS) is MANDATORY before surgery in patients >40 years old to confirm unilateral (APA) vs bilateral (IHA) aldosterone excess. A 1.5cm nodule on one side doesn't prove it's the source — could be bilateral IHA with an incidental nodule. AVS showing >4:1 aldosterone gradient (affected side:IVC) confirms unilateral and indicates surgery will help. Without AVS, there's risk of operating on the wrong gland.
10. A 54-year-old man presents with episodic severe headaches, profuse sweating, and palpitations over 3 months. BP 168/104 mmHg. 24-hour urine metanephrines: 1.8x normal (normal <100 mcg/24hr). CT shows 4cm right adrenal mass. Before imaging localization, what is the SINGLE most critical next step in pre-operative management?
ANSWER: E
PHENOXYBENZAMINE (non-selective irreversible alpha-blocker) FIRST and ONLY. Beta-blockers are FORBIDDEN first (would cause unopposed alpha → hypertensive crisis). Doxazosin/prazosin are acceptable but short-acting. Phenoxybenzamine is preferred for perioperative control (irreversible). Then after adequate alpha blockade (3-5 days), add beta-blocker if needed. High salt/fluid intake to expand depleted volume. This is a classic USMLE trap: many choose metoprolol (wrong!), but beta-first = death.
11. A 50-year-old woman has an adrenal mass that is 6.5cm, heterogeneous on CT with areas of necrosis, HU 35. Labs: cortisol 45 mcg/dL (elevated), DHEA-S elevated. Imaging shows metastatic lymph nodes. Adrenalectomy reveals high mitotic rate, atypical mitoses, tumor necrosis, venous invasion, and capsular invasion on histology. What is the diagnosis?
ANSWER: B
Adrenocortical carcinoma: large (>6cm), mixed endocrine production (cortisol + androgens), imaging findings of necrosis and metastatic disease, and Weiss criteria met (>3: high mitotic rate, atypical mitoses, necrosis, venous invasion, capsular invasion = 5/9 satisfied). Prognosis poor (5-year survival ~30%). Treatment: adrenalectomy + mitotane ± chemotherapy.
12. A 28-year-old man is diagnosed with pheochromocytoma. Genetic testing is recommended. Which mutation carries the HIGHEST risk of malignancy and metastatic disease?
ANSWER: C
SDHB (succinate dehydrogenase B) mutation carriers have the HIGHEST malignancy risk in pheochromocytoma and especially paraganglioma — up to 60-70% malignant with metastatic disease to lymph nodes, liver, bone. SDHC/SDHD typically non-malignant. Patients with SDHB mutations need aggressive surveillance and genetic counseling. This is why genetic testing is done for all pheochromocytoma patients (25-30% hereditary).
13. A 3-year-old boy presents with an abdominal mass found on imaging. The mass is noted to CROSS THE MIDLINE. Urine shows elevated VMA and HVA. Histology shows small round blue cells with Homer-Wright pseudorosettes. What is the diagnosis?
ANSWER: B
NEUROBLASTOMA: most common solid extracranial tumor of children. Key features: CROSSES MIDLINE (from adrenal/sympathetic chain), small round blue cells (undifferentiated), Homer-Wright pseudorosettes (not true rosettes), elevated urinary catecholamines (VMA, HVA). Wilms' does NOT cross midline (confined to kidney). Ganglioneuroma = mature/benign end of spectrum.
14. A 2-year-old girl with neuroblastoma is found to have MYCN amplification. What is the prognostic significance of this finding?
ANSWER: C
MYCN amplification (proto-oncogene) = POOR prognosis in neuroblastoma. High-risk disease. Requires aggressive multimodal therapy: chemotherapy + surgery + radiation + immunotherapy (anti-GD2 monoclonal antibody). Conversely, age <2 years, diploid DNA, and well-differentiated histology = favorable prognosis.
15. A 7-year-old boy with 21-hydroxylase deficiency (simple virilizing form) on hydrocortisone 10mg daily presents to the ED after a minor viral infection (fever 38.5°C). Which adjustment is MOST appropriate?
ANSWER: C
STRESS DOSING: CAH patients require 2-3x their usual hydrocortisone dose during illness, surgery, or trauma. Cortisol requirements ↑ during stress. A minor viral illness warrants stress dosing (3x daily dose, or if severe/vomiting, 25mg IV). This is part of patient education — all CAH patients need to know stress dosing protocol to prevent adrenal crisis.
16. A 45-year-old woman with resistant hypertension is found to have an 8cm left adrenal mass with HU 22 and no cortisol/androgen excess. CT washout: 40% washout at 15 minutes. What is the most appropriate management?
ANSWER: B
Large mass (8cm >6cm threshold), high HU (22 >10 = suspicious), poor washout (40% <50% = washout insufficient, suggests malignancy). Management: adrenalectomy. Before surgery, MUST rule out pheochromocytoma with metanephrines. Size >6cm alone warrants adrenalectomy due to malignancy risk.
17. A 14-year-old girl presents with primary amenorrhea and absent secondary sexual characteristics. Labs: ↑ ACTH, ↑ DOC (11-deoxycorticosterone), ↓ cortisol, ↑ testosterone (despite phenotypically female), HTN, hypokalemia. Which enzyme deficiency does this represent?
ANSWER: C
17α-HYDROXYLASE deficiency: blocks BOTH sex steroid synthesis AND cortisol, but aldosterone pathway intact. Result: ↓ sex steroids (hypogonadism, amenorrhea, no secondary sexual development), ↑ DOC/corticosterone (mineralocorticoid activity → HTN + hypokalemia), phenotypically female (46XX or 46XY). Elevated testosterone is from shunting toward other pathways but not converted to active androgens. This is the ONLY CAH where hypogonadism is the primary presentation.
18. A 65-year-old man with an incidental 3.8cm left adrenal mass (HU 8) is found to have subclinical Cushing's (cortisol 5.2 mcg/dL on 1mg DST, mildly elevated but suppresses). Metanephrines, aldosterone/renin ratio, and DHEA-S are all normal. What is the most appropriate management?
ANSWER: B
Subclinical Cushing's: mild cortisol elevation on DST (5.2, borderline) but patient asymptomatic, no signs/symptoms of Cushing's. Guidelines recommend conservative management: observe with imaging follow-up (6-12 months) and repeat DST. Adrenalectomy reserved for overt hypercortisolism (cortisol >10-15 mcg/dL on DST) or significant clinical/lab abnormalities. <4cm, benign imaging features support observation.

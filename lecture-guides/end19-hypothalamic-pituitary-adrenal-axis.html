Lecture 15: Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal Axis

Physiology of the HPA axis, cortisol effects, diagnostic testing, and clinical management of adrenal disorders

LO1: HPA Axis Overview — CRH, ACTH, Cortisol

▼

Three-Tier Axis Architecture

Hypothalamus (PVN) → Anterior Pituitary (corticotrophs) → Adrenal cortex (zona fasciculata)

CRH: Corticotropin-Releasing Hormone

  • 41-amino acid peptide from paraventricular nucleus (PVN)
  • Receptor: Gs-coupled (↑ cAMP) on corticotrophs
  • Effect: stimulates POMC synthesis and ACTH secretion

POMC: Pro-Opiomelanocortin

Precursor peptide cleaved into:

  • ACTH: main product from anterior pituitary
  • β-endorphin: opioid
  • MSH (melanocyte-stimulating hormone): shares N-terminal sequence with ACTH; ↑ melanin → hyperpigmentation in primary adrenal insufficiency (↑↑ ACTH → ↑ MSH-like activity)
  • Note: intermediate lobe in humans is vestigial; POMC cleavage mainly gives ACTH

ACTH: Adrenocorticotropic Hormone

  • 39 amino acids; first 13 AAs = α-MSH
  • Receptor: MC2R (Gs-coupled) on zona fasciculata/reticularis
  • Stimulates cortisol synthesis: ↑ StAR protein (rate-limiting), ↑ CYP11A1, ↑ CYP11B1
  • Trophic effect: maintains adrenal gland size; without ACTH → adrenal atrophy

Cortisol: Main Glucocorticoid

  • Circadian rhythm: peaks 6-8 AM (cortisol awakening response); nadir at midnight
  • Pulsatile secretion

Negative Feedback

Cortisol → ↓ CRH (hypothalamus), ↓ ACTH (pituitary)

  • Fast feedback: seconds-minutes, non-genomic
  • Slow feedback: hours-days, genomic
💡 "CRH → ACTH → Cortisol; Cortisol feeds BACK to crush CRH and ACTH"
⭐ HY Bullets:
• POMC → ACTH + β-endorphin + MSH
• MSH-like activity causes hyperpigmentation in PRIMARY adrenal insufficiency
• ACTH receptor = MC2R (Gs-coupled)
• Cortisol negative feedback suppresses both CRH and ACTH

LO2: Cortisol Physiology — Mechanism and Effects

▼

Mechanism of Action

Cortisol (lipophilic steroid) → binds GR (glucocorticoid receptor) in cytoplasm → GR translocates to nucleus → affects gene transcription

Transport in Blood

  • ~90% bound to cortisol-binding globulin (CBG) + albumin
  • ~10% free (biologically active)
  • CBG increases with estrogen (pregnancy) → ↑ total cortisol, normal free cortisol

Key Physiologic Effects

Cortisol Effects on Metabolism
↑ Gluconeogenesis (liver): PEPCK, G6Pase
↑ Proteolysis (muscle): amino acids for gluconeogenesis
↑ Lipolysis (fat): FFAs → triglycerides
Net: HYPERGLYCEMIA + peripheral wasting + central obesity
Immune & Anti-Inflammatory
Inhibits NF-κB → ↓ cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α)
↓ COX-2, ↓ phospholipase A2
↓ Lymphocytes, ↓ Eosinophils, ↑ Neutrophils

ELIN mnemonic (Cushing's): Eosinophils ↓, Lymphocytes ↓, Increased neutrophils, iN (neutrophilia)

Cardiovascular Effects
↑ Sensitivity to catecholamines (↑ α-receptor expression)
Maintains vascular tone/BP
Addison's → hypotension
Cushing's → hypertension
Bone & Growth Effects
Bone: ↓ Osteoblasts, ↑ Osteoclasts → osteoporosis
↓ Intestinal Ca absorption + ↑ Renal Ca loss → 2° HPT
Growth: ↓ GH, ↓ IGF-1 → growth suppression
Renal & Mineralocorticoid
Cortisol has mild MR activity (normally protected by 11β-HSD2)
Excess cortisol overwhelms 11β-HSD2
→ Hypokalemia + hypertension (apparent mineralocorticoid excess)
CNS Effects
High doses → euphoria then depression
Chronic excess → hippocampus damage
→ Memory ↓, HPA dysregulation
💡 "CORTISOL = Catabolic, Opposes insulin, Raises glucose, Thins skin/bones, Immune suppression, Stimulates catecholamines, Opposes growth, Lipid redistribution"
🎯 Tested Concepts:
• Free cortisol (not total) is biologically active
• CBG ↑ with estrogen → total cortisol ↑, but free cortisol normal
• Cortisol causes central obesity (redistribution to visceral fat)
• Chronic glucocorticoids → osteoporosis + avascular necrosis
⚠️ Traps:
• Hypokalemia + hypertension can occur from EXCESS cortisol's MR activity
• "Cortisol suppresses immune" = may have normal WBC count but reduced function
• Cortisol ↑ lipolysis (mobilizes fat) but redistributes to central depot

LO3: Circadian Rhythm and HPA Axis Regulation

▼

Cortisol Circadian Rhythm

Driver: SCN (suprachiasmatic nucleus) → PVN → CRH pulse amplitude varies

  • Peak: 6-8 AM (before waking) — cortisol awakening response (CAR)
  • Nadir: midnight (12 AM) — late-night salivary cortisol used diagnostically (normally very low; elevated in Cushing's)
  • Light/dark entrainment; disrupted by shift work, jet lag → cortisol dysregulation

Stressors That Activate HPA

Physical: hypoglycemia (most potent), surgery, trauma, infection/sepsis, pain, hemorrhage, exercise, cold

Psychological: fear, anxiety, anticipatory stress

Other stimulants: IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α (immune-neuroendocrine link), vasopressin (AVP/ADH — synergizes with CRH)

Dexamethasone Suppression Test (DST)

Principle: synthetic glucocorticoid → suppresses ACTH (pituitary feedback) → ↓ cortisol in normal

LOW-DOSE DST
1mg overnight or 2-day regimen
Screens for Cushing's syndrome
Normal: cortisol suppresses to <1.8 μg/dL
Cushing's: FAILS to suppress
HIGH-DOSE DST
8mg regimen
Distinguishes pituitary from ectopic/adrenal
Cushing's disease: SUPPRESSES
Ectopic/adrenal: does NOT suppress
💡 "Low dose = Rule OUT Cushing's (normal = suppresses); High dose = Rule IN pituitary (pituitary suppresses, ectopic doesn't)"

Other Diagnostic Tests

  • Midnight salivary cortisol: elevated in Cushing's (loss of circadian nadir)
  • 24-hour UFC (urine free cortisol): integrated cortisol exposure; elevated in Cushing's
  • CRH stimulation test: differentiates pituitary (responds → ↑ ACTH + cortisol) from ectopic (no response) and adrenal (↓ ACTH baseline, no response) causes
🎯 Tested Concepts:
• Midnight salivary cortisol = BEST initial screening for Cushing's (non-invasive, loss of normal nadir)
• Low-dose DST: normal person suppresses cortisol; Cushing's patient does NOT
• High-dose DST: if suppresses → pituitary; if doesn't suppress → ectopic or adrenal
⚠️ Traps:
• Dexamethasone doesn't cross-react with cortisol assay (critical for DST)
• Midnight salivary cortisol is MORE specific than 24-hr UFC
• DST unreliable if cortisol-binding abnormalities (pregnancy, OCPs, liver disease)

LO4: ACTH Measurement and the Step-Down Diagnostic Approach

▼

Principle: ACTH Tells the Story

Measure ACTH to determine if pituitary-driven or autonomous

ACTH-DEPENDENT Hypercortisolism (↑ ACTH)

  • Cushing's disease (pituitary adenoma — ~70% of endogenous Cushing's)
  • Ectopic ACTH syndrome (small cell lung, carcinoid, pancreatic NETs, medullary thyroid)
  • CRH-secreting tumor (very rare)

ACTH-INDEPENDENT Hypercortisolism (↓ ACTH — adrenal autonomous)

  • Adrenal adenoma (~10%)
  • Adrenal carcinoma (~5%)
  • Bilateral adrenal hyperplasia (macronodular, PPNAD)

Diagnostic Algorithm for Confirmed Cushing's

  1. Measure plasma ACTH
    • ACTH LOW (<10 pg/mL) → adrenal source → CT adrenal
    • ACTH NORMAL or HIGH (>20 pg/mL) → ACTH-dependent → proceed
  2. High-dose DST or CRH stimulation
    • Suppresses → likely Cushing's disease → MRI pituitary
    • Doesn't suppress → likely ectopic → CT/MRI chest/abdomen for source
  3. IPSS (inferior petrosal sinus sampling): gold standard for pituitary vs ectopic distinction
    • Measure ACTH in IPS (drains pituitary) vs peripheral blood
    • IPS:peripheral ratio >2 basal OR >3 after CRH = Cushing's disease
    • Ratio <2 = ectopic source

Adrenal Insufficiency Classification by ACTH

  • Primary adrenal insufficiency: ↓ cortisol → ↑ ACTH → hyperpigmentation (MSH-like)
  • Secondary adrenal insufficiency: ↓ ACTH → ↓ cortisol; NO hyperpigmentation; NO aldosterone deficiency (aldosterone regulated by RAAS)

ACTH Stimulation Test (Cosyntropin Test)

Gives synthetic ACTH (cosyntropin) → measures cortisol at 30/60 min

  • Normal: cortisol rises to >18-20 μg/dL
  • Failure: adrenal insufficiency
🎯 Tested Concepts:
• ACTH <10 in hypercortisolism = adrenal source
• ACTH-dependent = pituitary, ectopic, or rarely CRH source
• IPSS: ratio >2 or >3 post-CRH = pituitary
• Cosyntropin test: measures adrenal responsiveness
⚠️ Traps:
• ACTH in "normal range" (10-20) with elevated cortisol = still abnormal (suppressed)
• Ectopic ACTH can be VERY high (200+ pg/mL) — often alkalosis, hypokalemia
• IPSS requires specialized center; technical sample can be unreliable

LO5: Adrenal Insufficiency — Primary (Addison's) vs Secondary

▼

Primary Adrenal Insufficiency (Addison's Disease)

Pathology: destruction of adrenal cortex → ↓ cortisol + ↓ aldosterone + ↓ adrenal androgens; ↑ ACTH + ↑ renin

Causes:

  • Autoimmune (most common in developed world — anti-21-hydroxylase antibodies)
  • TB (worldwide leading cause historically)
  • Meningococcemia (Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome — bilateral adrenal hemorrhage in sepsis)
  • Fungal, metastatic cancer, hemorrhage, drugs (ketoconazole, etomidate)

Features of Primary Adrenal Insufficiency:

  • Fatigue, weakness, weight loss, nausea/vomiting, abdominal pain
  • Hypotension, salt craving
  • Hyperpigmentation (ACTH/MSH on melanocytes)
  • Hyponatremia (↓ aldosterone + cortisol → ↓ free water clearance)
  • Hyperkalemia (↓ aldosterone)
  • Hypoglycemia (↓ cortisol)
  • Eosinophilia (↑ from loss of cortisol's suppression)
Waterhouse-Friderichsen Syndrome
Bilateral adrenal hemorrhage from septicemia
Especially N. meningitidis
DIC → hemorrhagic infarction
Acute adrenal crisis: severe hypotension, altered mental status

Secondary Adrenal Insufficiency

Pathology: ↓ ACTH → ↓ cortisol; aldosterone PRESERVED (RAAS intact)

Most Common Cause:

Chronic exogenous glucocorticoid use → HPA axis suppression → sudden withdrawal → adrenal crisis

Other Causes:

Pituitary destruction (Sheehan's, tumors, trauma, radiation)

Features of Secondary Adrenal Insufficiency:

  • Similar to primary BUT:
  • NO hyperpigmentation (↓ ACTH/MSH)
  • NO hyperkalemia (aldosterone OK)
  • NO salt craving
  • Hyponatremia CAN still occur (cortisol deficiency → SIADH-like via ↑ ADH)

Adrenal Crisis: Life-Threatening Emergency

Definition: acute cortisol deficiency; precipitated by stress (illness, surgery, trauma)

Features:

  • Severe hypotension, nausea/vomiting, abdominal pain
  • Altered consciousness, fever
  • Shock, cardiovascular collapse if untreated

Treatment (DO NOT DELAY FOR TESTING):

  • IV hydrocortisone 100mg bolus (then 50-100mg q6-8h)
  • IV 0.9% NaCl + dextrose
  • Treat underlying cause
💡 "Addison's 3 A's: Adrenal (primary), ACTH up, Aldosterone down (Addison's = missing ALL 3 cortical products)"
🎯 Tested Concepts:
• PRIMARY adrenal insufficiency: ↑ ACTH, ↓ Na, ↑ K, hyperpigmentation
• SECONDARY adrenal insufficiency: ↓ ACTH, ↓ Na (SIADH), normal K, NO pigmentation
• Waterhouse-Friderichsen = meningococcal sepsis + bilateral adrenal hemorrhage
• Adrenal crisis: IV hydrocortisone STAT — don't wait for labs
⚠️ Traps:
• Hyperpigmentation = PRIMARY insufficiency ONLY (↑ ACTH → ↑ MSH)
• Hyperkalemia = PRIMARY only (aldosterone deficiency)
• Secondary insufficiency: aldosterone still works (RAAS independent)
• Hyponatremia in secondary = from cortisol ↓ (→ ↑ ADH), NOT aldosterone deficiency
• Eosinophilia in primary (↓ cortisol no longer suppresses eosinophils)

LO6: Glucocorticoid Pharmacology — Synthetic Steroids

▼

Glucocorticoid Potency Comparison (relative to cortisol = 1)

Drug GC Activity MC Activity Notes
Hydrocortisone 1× 1× Replacement therapy
Prednisone 4× 0.8× Oral; converted to prednisolone in liver
Methylprednisolone 5× 0.5× IV (status asthmaticus, SCI)
Dexamethasone 25-30× 0× No MC; DST, meningitis, brain edema
Fludrocortisone 0.1× 125× Pure MC replacement
Betamethasone 25× — Fetal lung maturity (crosses placenta)

Half-Lives and Clinical Implications

  • Hydrocortisone: short (8-12h)
  • Prednisone: intermediate (12-36h)
  • Dexamethasone: long (36-72h) → more potent HPA suppression risk

Clinical Uses of Glucocorticoids

  • Autoimmune/inflammatory (RA, lupus, asthma, IBD)
  • Allergic reactions
  • Adrenal insufficiency replacement
  • CAH (congenital adrenal hyperplasia)
  • Immunosuppression (transplant)
  • Malignancy (lymphoma, edema)
  • ARDS, meningitis (dexamethasone reduces inflammation)

Side Effects of Chronic Glucocorticoid Use

Metabolic & Musculoskeletal
Hyperglycemia, dyslipidemia, central obesity
Osteoporosis (↓ Ca absorption + ↑ resorption)
Proximal myopathy
Avascular necrosis (femoral head most common)
Immunologic & Dermatologic
Susceptibility to infection (especially opportunistic)
Impaired wound healing
Thin skin, striae, easy bruising
Ophthalmic & GI
Cataracts (posterior subcapsular)
Glaucoma (↑ IOP)
Peptic ulcer disease (especially + NSAIDs)
Pancreatitis
CNS & HPA Suppression
Mood changes, insomnia, psychosis
Pseudotumor cerebri (children on withdrawal)
HPA suppression: >5-7.5mg prednisone for >3 weeks
Must taper slowly (don't stop abruptly)
💡 "GIVING steroids: Glucose ↑, Infections ↑, Vision (cataracts/glaucoma), Infections ↑, Necrosis (avascular), Growth ↓"

HPA Suppression & Tapering

  • Trigger: doses >5-7.5mg prednisone equivalent for >3 weeks → suppress HPA
  • Consequence: sudden withdrawal → adrenal crisis
  • Solution: must taper slowly (gradual dose reduction)

Stress Dosing

For patients on chronic steroids undergoing physiologic stress:

  • "Sick day rules": double/triple dose during illness
  • IV hydrocortisone 100mg before surgery
🎯 Tested Concepts:
• Dexamethasone: 25-30× more potent than cortisol, zero MC activity
• Hydrocortisone: replacement drug (1:1 activity ratio)
• >3 weeks at moderate-high dose → HPA suppression → taper slowly
• Avascular necrosis of femoral head: most common serious MSK complication
• Stress dosing: double/triple during illness; IV hydrocortisone before surgery
⚠️ Traps:
• Abrupt steroid withdrawal = adrenal crisis (patient may seem fine at first)
• "Posterior subcapsular cataracts" = typical steroid cataract
• Proximal myopathy from steroids (not distal)
• Avascular necrosis: femoral head > humeral head > tibia
• Pseudotumor cerebri in children: especially on withdrawal

MCQs & Practice Questions

▼

Click on an answer to reveal the explanation.

1. A 45-year-old woman presents with hypertension, central obesity, proximal muscle weakness, and purple striae. Labs show elevated 24-hour urine free cortisol and midnight salivary cortisol. ACTH is 35 pg/mL (normal 5-25). Which of the following best localizes the source?
Answer: B) Pituitary microadenoma
ACTH is elevated/normal (35 is at upper limit), indicating ACTH-dependent Cushing's. Adrenal adenoma (A) would have LOW ACTH (<10). Both ectopic ACTH (C, D) and pituitary disease cause elevated ACTH, but the constellation of classic Cushing's features (proximal weakness, striae, central obesity) is most common with pituitary disease, which represents ~70% of endogenous Cushing's. CRH-secreting tumors (E) are extremely rare. MRI pituitary would localize to microadenoma.
2. A patient with newly diagnosed primary adrenal insufficiency presents with hypotension, hyperkalemia, and hyponatremia. Which finding would NOT be present?
Answer: D) Normal aldosterone level
In PRIMARY adrenal insufficiency, the adrenal cortex is destroyed, so aldosterone IS low (not normal). Elevated ACTH (A) occurs from loss of cortisol feedback. Hyperpigmentation (B) results from high ACTH/MSH-like activity. Renin (C) and eosinophils (E) are elevated (RAAS activated from low aldosterone; cortisol suppression allows eosinophil survival). The key trap: primary adrenal insufficiency = low aldosterone + low cortisol.
3. A 55-year-old man on long-term prednisone (10mg daily for 8 months) for rheumatoid arthritis develops hip pain and cannot walk. X-ray shows collapse of the femoral head. What is the most likely diagnosis?
Answer: B) Avascular necrosis of femoral head
Chronic glucocorticoid use is a well-known cause of avascular necrosis (also called osteonecrosis). The femoral head is the most common site, followed by the humeral head and tibia. Hip pain with collapse on imaging is classic. While osteoporosis (C) also occurs, it typically causes compression fractures of the spine, not femoral head collapse. The timing (8 months of high-dose prednisone) fits avascular necrosis risk.
4. A 35-year-old woman undergoes low-dose dexamethasone suppression test. Her midnight cortisol is 22 μg/dL (normal <1.8 after 1mg dexamethasone). What is the next diagnostic step?
Answer: C) Measure plasma ACTH level
Low-dose DST failed to suppress (cortisol 22 >> 1.8), confirming autonomous hypercortisolism (Cushing's syndrome). The NEXT step is ACTH measurement to determine if it's ACTH-dependent (elevated/normal ACTH) vs. ACTH-independent (low ACTH). If ACTH is low, proceed to adrenal CT. If ACTH is elevated, proceed to high-dose DST (B) to distinguish pituitary from ectopic. Answer (C) is the logical next step in the algorithm before imaging.
5. A 42-year-old man with Cushing's disease (pituitary microadenoma) undergoes high-dose dexamethasone suppression test. His cortisol falls from 35 μg/dL to 8 μg/dL after 8mg dexamethasone. What does this indicate?
Answer: B) Confirms pituitary source (maintains some ACTH feedback suppression)
HIGH-DOSE DST: cortisol SUPPRESSES → indicates pituitary source. Pituitary adenomas retain partial feedback sensitivity to high-dose dexamethasone, so cortisol falls. In ectopic ACTH (C) or adrenal tumors, cortisol does NOT suppress (fully autonomous, no feedback). The rule: "High dose suppresses = pituitary; High dose doesn't suppress = ectopic/adrenal". IPSS (A) is reserved when DST/CRH testing is inconclusive or equivocal.
6. A patient on chronic exogenous glucocorticoids suddenly stops taking his prednisone. Within 24-48 hours, he presents with severe hypotension, altered mental status, and nausea. What is the mechanism?
Answer: B) HPA axis suppressed; adrenal glands atrophied → cannot produce cortisol acutely
SECONDARY ADRENAL INSUFFICIENCY from chronic exogenous steroids. Chronic glucocorticoid exposure suppresses the HPA axis (negative feedback on CRH and ACTH), so the adrenal cortex atrophies. Sudden withdrawal leaves the patient without any cortisol source (exogenous supply gone, endogenous production cannot recover immediately). This causes ADRENAL CRISIS. Recovery of HPA axis takes weeks to months even after tapering. Answer (B) is the mechanism. Always taper steroids slowly!
7. A 28-year-old woman presents with fatigue, weight loss, hypotension, and oral hyperpigmentation. Labs show low cortisol (3 μg/dL), elevated ACTH (85 pg/mL), hyponatremia, and hyperkalemia. A cosyntropin stimulation test is performed, and cortisol remains low (fails to rise above 18 μg/dL). What is the most likely diagnosis?
Answer: B) Primary adrenal insufficiency (Addison's disease)
Classic presentation: ↓ cortisol + ↑ ACTH + hyperpigmentation + hyponatremia + hyperkalemia. The high ACTH is the key finding (excludes secondary insufficiency, where ACTH would be low). Cosyntropin test fails because the adrenal glands themselves are destroyed and cannot respond to ACTH stimulus. Answer (A) would show low ACTH. Addison's disease (most commonly autoimmune or TB) causes all 3 cortical hormone deficiencies: cortisol, aldosterone, and androgens.
8. A 50-year-old man with small cell lung cancer presents with severe hypokalemia (K+ 2.2), metabolic alkalosis, and very elevated ACTH (280 pg/mL). His cortisol is 55 μg/dL. High-dose dexamethasone suppression test shows cortisol remains at 52 μg/dL. What is the mechanism of his hypokalemia?
Answer: A) ACTH stimulates aldosterone → sodium reabsorption + potassium wasting
This is ECTOPIC ACTH SYNDROME from small cell lung cancer. The very high ACTH (280) stimulates aldosterone secretion (ACTH has some cross-reactivity with MC receptors on adrenal zona glomerulosa). High cortisol ALSO has mineralocorticoid activity (overwhelms 11β-HSD2). Result: severe hypokalemia + metabolic alkalosis (classic for ectopic ACTH). High-dose DST fails to suppress (proves ectopic source, not pituitary). This is a medical emergency requiring treatment of the underlying cancer + hypokalemia replacement + possibly metyrapone/ketoconazole to block cortisol synthesis.
9. A 38-year-old woman on OCPs (oral contraceptive pills) has total cortisol level of 40 μg/dL (high) but free cortisol is normal. What is the explanation?
Answer: B) OCPs increase estrogen → ↑ CBG → ↑ total cortisol, normal free cortisol
KEY CONCEPT: Estrogen increases CBG (cortisol-binding globulin). OCPs contain ethinyl estradiol, which increases CBG synthesis in the liver. More CBG = more cortisol bound (and less free). But the TOTAL cortisol is elevated because the hormone is bound but still present. Free cortisol (the biologically active form) remains normal. This is why measuring free cortisol (not total) is better in patients on estrogen therapy. Same phenomenon occurs in pregnancy.
10. A 64-year-old woman with meningococcal sepsis rapidly develops shock, DIC, and bilateral adrenal hemorrhage. What is this syndrome called?
Answer: A) Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome
This is the classic eponymous syndrome: bilateral adrenal hemorrhage during fulminant meningococcemia (N. meningitidis). DIC leads to bleeding into the adrenal glands → hemorrhagic infarction. Patient develops acute adrenal crisis (severe hypotension, shock) on top of septic shock. Mortality is very high even with treatment. Requires immediate IV hydrocortisone + antibiotics + supportive care. This is a medical emergency and a classic board question.
11. Which of the following statements about CRH is correct?
Answer: D) CRH is released from the paraventricular nucleus
CRH (41 amino acids, not 39 — that's ACTH) is produced by hypothalamic neurons in the PVN. It binds a GS-coupled receptor (A is wrong type of peptide; B is wrong coupling; C is wrong location; E is wrong statement). Answer (D) is correct.
12. A 45-year-old man on hydrocortisone replacement for primary adrenal insufficiency develops an acute surgical abdomen requiring emergency surgery. How should his hydrocortisone be dosed perioperatively?
Answer: B) IV hydrocortisone 100mg immediately, then 50-100mg q6-8h perioperatively
STRESS DOSING for adrenal insufficiency patients. During surgery (physiologic stress), HPA is activated and needs cortisol. Patients with primary or secondary insufficiency cannot mount an adequate response. Standard approach: IV hydrocortisone 100mg bolus before anesthesia induction, then 50-100mg q6-8h for the duration of stress. Taper back to baseline as recovery progresses. Answers (A), (C), (D), (E) are incorrect or suboptimal.
13. In the IPSS (inferior petrosal sinus sampling) test for distinguishing pituitary from ectopic ACTH, which of the following is the diagnostic criterion for pituitary source?
Answer: B) IPS ACTH / peripheral ACTH ratio >2 basal or >3 after CRH stimulation
IPSS compares ACTH from the inferior petrosal sinus (which drains the pituitary) to peripheral blood ACTH. A HIGH ratio indicates that the pituitary is producing the excess ACTH (Cushing's disease). Cutoff: >2:1 basal or >3:1 after CRH stimulation = pituitary. Ratio <2 = ectopic source (ACTH not coming from pituitary). Answer (B) is the established criterion.
14. A 55-year-old man with newly diagnosed Cushing's syndrome has an ACTH of 8 pg/mL and cortisol of 35 μg/dL. What is the source of his hypercortisolism?
Answer: C) Adrenal adenoma or carcinoma
ACTH is LOW (8 pg/mL, normal 5-25) with elevated cortisol. This is ACTH-INDEPENDENT hypercortisolism = primary adrenal pathology. The low ACTH indicates the pituitary "knows" there's too much cortisol and is appropriately suppressing CRH/ACTH. Differential: adrenal adenoma (~10% of Cushing's), adrenal carcinoma (~5%), or bilateral adrenal hyperplasia. Answer (C) is correct.
15. A patient with secondary adrenal insufficiency (from pituitary radiation) presents with hyponatremia. Which mechanism explains the hyponatremia?
Answer: B) Cortisol deficiency → ↑ ADH (SIADH-like effect) → free water retention
In SECONDARY adrenal insufficiency, aldosterone production is INTACT (RAAS independent). So hyponatremia is NOT from aldosterone deficiency. Instead, cortisol normally suppresses ADH/vasopressin. When cortisol is low, ADH rises → free water retention → dilutional hyponatremia (SIADH-like). Key difference from primary insufficiency where hyponatremia comes from BOTH aldosterone and cortisol deficiency. Answer (B) is correct for secondary insufficiency mechanism.
16. Which of the following synthetic glucocorticoids has ZERO mineralocorticoid activity and is therefore preferred for dexamethasone suppression testing?
Answer: D) Dexamethasone
Dexamethasone has 25-30× the glucocorticoid activity of cortisol and ZERO mineralocorticoid activity. This makes it ideal for DST because: (1) doesn't bind to aldosterone receptors, (2) doesn't interfere with cortisol assay, (3) maximally suppresses ACTH/CRH. Hydrocortisone (A) has both GC and MC activity. Prednisone and methylprednisolone (B, C) have some MC activity. Fludrocortisone (E) is a pure mineralocorticoid. Answer (D) is correct.
17. A 40-year-old woman on long-term corticosteroids for polymyalgia rheumatica gradually develops proximal muscle weakness. Which type of myopathy is this?
Answer: B) Proximal myopathy from glucocorticoid effects on type II muscle fibers
Chronic glucocorticoid use causes PROXIMAL myopathy (hip, shoulder girdle weakness), not distal. Mechanism: glucocorticoids inhibit protein synthesis and promote proteolysis in type II (fast-twitch) muscle fibers. This is well-established in medical literature and a classic board question. Elevated CK and EMG show myopathic pattern. Answer (B) is correct. Note: separate from Cushing's cortisol ↑ causing peripheral wasting — this is direct steroid drug effect.
18. A 50-year-old patient on chronic prednisone develops cataracts. Which type of cataract is most typical of glucocorticoid-induced cataracts?
Answer: C) Posterior subcapsular cataracts
Glucocorticoid-induced cataracts classically present as POSTERIOR SUBCAPSULAR cataracts. This is a pathognomonic finding in patients on chronic steroids. Present with visual symptoms, often earlier than age-related nuclear sclerotic or cortical cataracts. Screening ophthalmology exams are recommended for patients on prolonged high-dose steroid therapy. Answer (C) is the classic board answer.

Mind Map: HPA Axis Integration

▼
🎯 Mind Map Summary:
The HPA axis is a three-tier endocrine system: hypothalamic CRH stimulates pituitary ACTH, which drives adrenal cortisol secretion. Cortisol provides negative feedback at hypothalamus and pituitary. Circadian rhythm drives ACTH pulsatility, peaking at 6-8 AM. Clinical disorders range from Cushing's (excess) to Addison's (deficiency), diagnosed by cortisol levels, ACTH measurements, and DST paradigms. Pharmacologic understanding of synthetic glucocorticoids is essential for both replacement and therapeutic use.

1Endocrine signaling vs. other intercellular communication

The Gist Cells talk in several modes. Endocrine = hormone rides the bloodstream to distant cells. Hormones physically reach almost every cell in the body, but only cells with the matching receptor respond. That receptor expression is what creates hormonal specificity.
📚 Going Deeper will expand on:
  • (1) What defines "endocrine" — unpacks the "ductless, into the bloodstream" rule and contrasts endocrine with exocrine.
  • (2) Autocrine vs. paracrine — explains how the same molecule can be a hormone, paracrine, or autocrine depending on delivery.
  • (3) Neuroendocrine — shows how the hypothalamus turns neurons into hormone-secreting cells (posterior pituitary, hypophyseal portal).
  • (4) Specificity — details why receptor expression is necessary but not sufficient (isoform, machinery, concentration, timing).

📚 Going Deeper — the details behind the gist

1 · What defines "endocrine" (the one-line rule)

Core Definition
  • Hormone =
    • A molecule secreted by a ductless gland into the bloodstream to act on a distant target cell.FA 2024 Endo
  • Endocrine vs. exocrine:
    • Rules out exocrine glands (pancreatic acini → duodenum via duct, sweat glands, salivary glands).
Unique Organ — The Pancreas
  • Dual role:
    • Endocrine: islets of Langerhans → blood.
    • Exocrine: acini → duct of Wirsung → duodenum.
    • Same organ, two modes.Costanzo Physiology 6e, ch 9
Clinical Implication
  • Why endocrinopathies are systemic:
    • "Ductless" delivery means the hormone goes everywhere → multi-organ symptoms.

2 · Autocrine vs. paracrine — the tricky pair

Autocrine — cell signals itself
  • Core feature:
    • Receptor and ligand on the SAME cell.
  • Tumor biology examples:
    • Glioblastomas often have PDGF autocrine loops.
    • Some breast cancers have IGF-1 autocrine loops.
  • Immunology examples:
    • Activated T cells produce IL-2 AND express IL-2R → autocrine proliferation.FA 2024 Immuno
Paracrine — neighbor-to-neighbor diffusion
  • Mechanism:
    • Signal diffuses to NEIGHBORING cells through interstitial fluid.
  • Classic examples:
    • Somatostatin from pancreatic δ-cells inhibits adjacent α and β cells (paracrine) — same molecule from the hypothalamus is endocrine on the anterior pituitary.
    • Histamine from ECL cells → neighboring parietal cells → HCl secretion. Blocked by H2-blockers (famotidine).Guyton & Hall 14e ch 78
Key Insight
  • Mode is defined by delivery, not the molecule:
    • A single molecule can be autocrine, paracrine, OR endocrine depending on which cell makes it and which cell listens.

3 · Neuroendocrine — the hypothalamus is the master regulator

Definition
  • Neuroendocrine neuron:
    • A neuron whose axon terminal releases hormone into the bloodstream instead of across a synapse.
Two Hypothalamic Neuron Classes
  • Magnocellular neurons:
    • Located in supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei.
    • Produce ADH & oxytocin.
    • Stored/released from the posterior pituitary → systemic circulation.
  • Parvocellular neurons:
    • Produce TRH, CRH, GnRH, GHRH, dopamine (PIH), somatostatin.
    • Released into the hypophyseal portal system → anterior pituitary (local, concentrated delivery).Costanzo 6e ch 9; B&B Endo
Anatomical Details
  • Hypophyseal portal network:
    • A capillary → vein → capillary system that delivers hypothalamic hormones to the anterior pituitary.
    • Achieves ~1000× the concentration hormones would reach via systemic circulation.
    • Why lesions of the pituitary stalk cause anterior pituitary dysfunction.Guyton 14e ch 75
  • Adrenal medulla:
    • A modified sympathetic ganglion.
    • Chromaffin cells are post-ganglionic sympathetic neurons that release Epi/NE into the blood.
    • Functions as a neuroendocrine organ.

4 · Specificity — receptor expression + local factors

Core Rule
  • Receptor expression alone:
    • Necessary but not sufficient for hormonal response.
Additional Factors That Shape Specificity
  • Receptor isoform affinity:
    • α1 vs β1 both bind NE, but produce opposite vascular effects.
  • Intracellular signaling machinery:
    • A cell with GPCR but no adenylyl cyclase won't respond to Gs-coupled signaling.
  • Local concentration:
    • Paracrine signals achieve higher [hormone] than endocrine.
    • Explains why paracrine somatostatin is potent locally despite low systemic levels.
  • Timing:
    • Some receptors are expressed only during certain cell-cycle or developmental windows.
★ HIGH-YIELD for USMLE
  • Endocrine vs. exocrine: endocrine = ductless, into blood. Exocrine = into a duct (sweat, saliva, pancreatic enzymes).
  • A "hormone" is defined by endocrine delivery. The same molecule can act as neurotransmitter, paracrine, or hormone depending on context (dopamine, somatostatin, gastrin).
  • Specificity = receptor expression, not hormone distribution.
🧠 MEMORY TRICK

PANE-J: Paracrine, Autocrine, Neural, Endocrine, Juxtacrine.

Distance mantra: "Autocrine whispers to itself, paracrine gossips to neighbors, endocrine shouts across the body."

Neuroendocrine = "the neuron that hijacks the bloodstream" (posterior pituitary, hypothalamic releasing hormones).

🏥 CLINICAL PEARL
  • Pheochromocytoma dumps catecholamines systemically → endocrine effect (episodic HTN, palpitations).
  • IGF-1 from liver = endocrine. IGF-1 locally = paracrine/autocrine. Same molecule, different modes.
  • Gastrinoma (Zollinger-Ellison): gastrin normally paracrine, but a tumor makes it endocrine → systemic acid hypersecretion, refractory ulcers.

2Classify hormones by structure, solubility & storability

The Gist Three structural classes: amino acid derivatives (amines), peptides/proteins, and steroids. Eicosanoids are a minor 4th category (usually paracrine). The single most useful split is water-loving vs. fat-loving, because solubility determines storage, carriers, receptor location, onset, and duration.

📚 Going Deeper — each class in detail

1 · Amino acid derivatives (amines)

From Tyrosine
  • Catecholamines:
    • Epinephrine, norepinephrine, dopamine.
    • Hydrophilic; short half-life (~1–2 min); stored in vesicles.
  • Thyroid hormones T3/T4:
    • Made by coupling two iodinated tyrosines.
    • Iodination + ring structure = lipophilic.
    • Long half-life (T4 ~7 days).Costanzo 6e ch 9
From Tryptophan
  • Serotonin (5-HT):
    • Mostly neurotransmitter.
    • Acts as paracrine/endocrine from enterochromaffin cells in GI tract (carcinoid syndrome).Pathoma 2024 ch 15
  • Melatonin:
    • From pineal gland; peaks at night.
    • Acts via MT1/MT2 GPCRs for circadian rhythm.
From Histidine
  • Histamine:
    • Mostly paracrine (gastric ECL cells, mast cells).

2 · Peptides & proteins — the synthesis pipeline

Synthesis Steps
  • Step 1 — Translation:
    • Transcribed → translated on ribosomes of the rough ER as a larger precursor (pre-pro-hormone).
  • Step 2 — Signal cleavage:
    • Pre-sequence (signal peptide) targets the protein to the ER lumen → cleaved → pro-hormone.
  • Step 3 — Packaging & processing:
    • Pro-hormone travels Golgi → packaged in secretory vesicles → proteolytically processed inside the vesicle to the final active form.
Classic example — Insulin
  • Processing pathway:
    • Pre-pro-insulin (110 AA) → pro-insulin (86 AA with A and B chains connected by C-peptide) → insulin (A + B chains, disulfide-linked) + C-peptide (equimolar).FA 2024 Endo
  • Why C-peptide matters clinically:
    • C-peptide is inert but clinically valuable.
    • It rises with endogenous insulin and is ABSENT with exogenous insulin.
    • Distinguishes insulinoma / endogenous hyperinsulinism from factitious (injected) insulin use.B&B Endo — Insulinoma
Peptide Hormone Characteristics
  • Size range:
    • 3 AAs (TRH) to 200+ AAs (glycoprotein hormones like FSH).
Types of Peptide Hormones — Glycoprotein Subfamily
  • Members:
    • TSH, LH, FSH, hCG.
  • Subunit structure:
    • Share a common α-subunit (encoded by a single gene — CGA).
    • Unique β-subunit confers receptor specificity.
  • Why hCG is ideal for pregnancy testing:
    • Glycosylation patterns extend half-life and modulate affinity.
    • hCG has extra sialic acid → longer half-life than LH → detectable at low concentrations in urine.Guyton 14e ch 76

3 · Steroids — the cholesterol family

Core Principle
  • Universal precursor:
    • All 5 steroid hormone classes (progestins, gluco/mineralocorticoids, androgens, estrogens) + vitamin D are built from cholesterol (27 carbons).
Cholesterol Sources (in order of preference)
  • Primary — LDL-receptor-mediated uptake:
    • Highest contribution in the adrenal gland.
  • Secondary — de novo synthesis:
    • From acetyl-CoA via HMG-CoA reductase.
  • Tertiary — stored cholesterol esters:
    • Held in lipid droplets, mobilized acutely during stimulation.Costanzo 6e ch 9
Steroid Characteristics
  • Why steroids cannot be stored:
    • The lipid backbone diffuses through ANY membrane, including vesicle membranes.
    • Cells make steroids on demand and release them immediately.Guyton 14e ch 74
  • Why steroids have long half-lives:
    • Carrier protein binding (CBG, SHBG, albumin) protects from hepatic metabolism.
    • Bound hormone cannot be filtered by the kidney, extending circulating time.
  • Elimination:
    • Hepatic conjugation (glucuronide or sulfate) → excreted in bile or urine.
    • Liver disease can markedly prolong steroid half-life.Lippincott Pharm 7e

4 · Eicosanoids (lipid derivatives) — brief

Source & Release
  • Precursor:
    • Arachidonic acid (20-C polyunsaturated fatty acid).
    • Released from membrane phospholipids by phospholipase A2 (PLA2).
Two Main Enzymatic Pathways
  • COX-1 / COX-2 pathway:
    • Produces prostaglandins (PGE2, PGI2) and thromboxane (TxA2).
    • Blocked by NSAIDs.
  • 5-lipoxygenase (LOX) pathway:
    • Produces leukotrienes (LTB4, LTC4).
    • Enzyme blocked by zileuton.
    • Receptor blocked by montelukast (used in asthma).FA 2024 Pharm — NSAIDs
Kinetic Profile
  • Half-life:
    • Seconds.
    • Act as paracrine/autocrine — NOT classic endocrine.

5 · The thyroid rebel — why T3/T4 are lipophilic

Structural Basis
  • How T3 and T4 are built:
    • T4 = two DIT (di-iodotyrosine) residues coupled.
    • T3 = MIT + DIT.
    • Coupling + iodination creates a bulky, non-polar aromatic structure.
Net Properties — Behaves Like a Steroid
  • Functional profile:
    • Fat-soluble; crosses membranes freely.
    • Carrier-bound in plasma (TBG).
    • Nuclear receptor (transcription factor).
    • Long half-life (T4 ~7 days).
T4 vs. T3 — Prohormone and Active Form
  • Circulating form:
    • T4 is the primary circulating form (~80% of thyroid output).
  • Active form:
    • T3 is ~5× more potent at the TRβ receptor.
  • Peripheral conversion:
    • 5'-deiodinase converts T4 → T3 in target tissues (liver, kidney, muscle).
    • Why a patient on levothyroxine still ends up with active T3 systemically.Guyton 14e ch 77; Pathoma ch 15
Clinical Trap — Euthyroid Sick Syndrome
  • Reverse T3 (rT3):
    • In severe illness / starvation, T4 is preferentially converted to reverse T3 — an inactive metabolite.
    • Patient appears hypothyroid biochemically but is NOT truly hypothyroid — no treatment needed. Classic USMLE trap.
★ HIGH-YIELD for USMLE

THE REBEL: Thyroid hormone (T3/T4). Amino-acid-derived but behaves like a steroid — lipophilic, TBG-bound, nuclear receptor, long half-life (T4 ≈ 7 days). Don't be caught on this one.

  • Glycoprotein hormones (TSH, FSH, LH, hCG) share a common α-subunit; the β-subunit gives receptor specificity.
  • Peptide hormones are cleaved from pre-pro forms (pre-pro-insulin → pro-insulin → insulin + C-peptide). C-peptide distinguishes endogenous from exogenous insulin.
🧠 MEMORY TRICK

"Steroids and Thyroid are Siblings" (S.T.S.) — both lipophilic, carrier-bound, nuclear receptors, slow + long-lasting.

"CAT PEP" = hydrophilic: CATecholamines + PEPtides. Stored, short-lived, membrane receptors.

"TFL-hC" = glycoprotein α-subunit sharers: TSH, FSH, LH, hCG.

🏥 CLINICAL PEARL
  • C-peptide measurement distinguishes insulinoma / endogenous insulin (↑ C-peptide) from surreptitious insulin injection (↓ C-peptide).
  • Pregnancy → ↑TBG → ↑ total T4, normal free T4. Patient is euthyroid — classic trap.
  • hCG cross-reacts with TSH receptor (shared α) → gestational hyperthyroidism in hyperemesis gravidarum.

📚 Going Deeper — the three must-knows in detail

1 · The order of the pathway (why each step matters)

Step 0 — Phe → Tyr (phenylalanine hydroxylase, liver)
  • Context:
    • Not strictly part of catecholamine synthesis, but clinically important.
  • Clinical relevance:
    • Deficiency = PKU (phenylketonuria) → intellectual disability, musty body odor, seizures.
    • Screened at birth in all US states.FA 2024 Biochem
    • Tyrosine becomes an "essential" AA in PKU patients — must be supplied in diet.
Step 1 — Tyr → L-DOPA (tyrosine hydroxylase) · ★ RATE-LIMITING
  • Location & cofactors:
    • Cytoplasmic.
    • Requires BH4 (tetrahydrobiopterin) and O₂.
    • Same cofactor as phenylalanine hydroxylase — BH4 deficiency breaks multiple pathways.Costanzo 6e ch 9
  • Regulation:
    • Feedback-inhibited by end products (DA, NE, Epi) binding an allosteric site.
    • Phosphorylation by PKA or CaMKII during sympathetic activation INCREASES activity (relieves feedback).
Step 2 — L-DOPA → Dopamine (DOPA decarboxylase / AADC)
  • Location & cofactors:
    • Cytoplasmic.
    • Requires vitamin B6 (PLP).
  • Activity level:
    • High baseline activity — rarely rate-limiting.
    • This is why L-DOPA is effective in Parkinson's: the enzyme is already waiting to convert it.
  • Pharmacologic target:
    • Blocked peripherally by carbidopa in Parkinson's treatment.FA 2024 Pharm — Parkinson's
Step 3 — Dopamine → NE (dopamine β-hydroxylase / DBH)
  • Unique location:
    • Occurs INSIDE the chromaffin granule / synaptic vesicle.
    • Dopamine is first pumped into the vesicle by VMAT (vesicular monoamine transporter), then hydroxylated.
  • Cofactors:
    • Vitamin C (ascorbate).
    • Copper.
  • Pharmacologic target:
    • Reserpine inhibits VMAT → blocks vesicular uptake → depletes catecholamines (old antihypertensive).Lippincott Pharm 7e
Step 4 — NE → Epi (PNMT — phenylethanolamine N-methyltransferase)
  • Location & cofactor:
    • Cytoplasmic.
    • Requires SAM (S-adenosyl methionine) as methyl donor.
  • Special requirement:
    • Only active where cortisol levels are high — see section 3 below.

2 · The rate-limiting step: tyrosine hydroxylase in depth

Why it's rate-limiting
  • Enzyme kinetics:
    • Slowest Vmax in the pathway and highly regulated.
    • Any change in catecholamine output requires a change in this enzyme's activity.
Short-term regulation (seconds–minutes)
  • Feedback control:
    • Allosteric end-product feedback by NE, Epi, and DA.
    • Phosphorylation by PKA (cAMP-dependent) and CaMKII during stress → relieves end-product inhibition → more NE/Epi produced.B&B Endo — Catecholamines
Long-term regulation (hours–days)
  • Transcriptional upregulation:
    • Induced by chronic stress, glucocorticoids, and nerve growth factor (NGF).
    • This is why chronic stress or steroid excess can lead to elevated sympathetic tone.
Clinical Inhibition
  • Metyrosine (α-methyl-p-tyrosine):
    • Competitive tyrosine hydroxylase inhibitor.
    • Used preoperatively in pheochromocytoma when α-blockade alone isn't enough.Lippincott Pharm 7e; UWorld Endo Qbank
    • Reduces catecholamine stores by 50–80% → helps prevent intraoperative hypertensive crisis.
Why NOT DOPA decarboxylase?
  • Reasoning:
    • DOPA decarboxylase has HIGH baseline activity and rapidly consumes any DOPA produced.
    • This is why L-DOPA therapy works — you give the substrate and the enzyme is ready and waiting.

3 · Why only the adrenal medulla makes epinephrine

Adrenal Anatomy
  • Gross structure:
    • Adrenal gland = cortex (outer) + medulla (inner).
  • Cortical zones (outer → inner):
    • Zona Glomerulosa → aldosterone.
    • Zona Fasciculata → cortisol.
    • Zona Reticularis → adrenal androgens (DHEA).
  • Medulla:
    • Modified post-ganglionic sympathetic neurons = chromaffin cells.
    • Innervated directly by pre-ganglionic sympathetic fibers (splanchnic nerves).Pathoma 2024 ch 15; Guyton 14e ch 78
The Blood Supply Trick — The Key Anatomical Insight
  • Path of blood flow:
    • Blood enters the adrenal cortex first via superficial capsular arteries.
    • Cortical capillaries load up with cortisol from the zona fasciculata.
    • Those cortisol-rich capillaries drain CENTRIPETALLY through the medulla before leaving via the central adrenal vein.
  • Consequence:
    • The adrenal medulla bathes in cortisol at 10–100× systemic plasma concentration.
    • A concentration seen NOWHERE else in the body.Guyton 14e ch 78; Costanzo 6e ch 9
Why Cortisol Matters Molecularly
  • Mechanism:
    • Cortisol binds glucocorticoid receptors in chromaffin cells → induces PNMT gene transcription.
    • Without high local cortisol, PNMT is not expressed → NE cannot be methylated to Epi.
  • Why nerves elsewhere can't make Epi:
    • Sympathetic nerve terminals elsewhere (heart, vessels, bronchi) never see this cortisol concentration.
    • No PNMT → they release NE only.FA 2024 Endo
Clinical Consequences
  • Addison's disease (primary adrenal insufficiency):
    • ↓ cortisol → ↓ PNMT expression → impaired epinephrine synthesis.
    • Contributes to fatigue, hypoglycemia, and poor stress response.
  • Pheochromocytoma (chromaffin cell tumor):
    • Releases a mix of Epi + NE.
  • Paraganglioma (extra-adrenal sympathetic tumor):
    • Releases mostly NE — no local cortisol to induce PNMT.
    • HY distinction between pheo and paraganglioma.Pathoma 2024 ch 15
  • Chronic exogenous glucocorticoid use:
    • May experience adrenal medullary atrophy AND HPA-axis suppression.
    • But the adrenal cortisol–medulla anatomy means patients often still make some Epi.
Output Ratios
  • Adrenal medulla:
    • ~80% Epi / ~20% NE.
  • Sympathetic nerve terminals:
    • ~0% Epi / 100% NE.FA 2024 Endo

4 · Catecholamine degradation and lab markers

Degradation Enzymes
  • MAO (monoamine oxidase):
    • Mitochondrial, intracellular.
    • MAO-A preferentially acts on NE and serotonin.
    • MAO-B preferentially acts on dopamine.
  • COMT (catechol-O-methyltransferase):
    • Cytoplasmic, extracellular.
    • Uses SAM as methyl donor.Lippincott Pharm 7e
Major Metabolites
  • Norepinephrine pathway:
    • NE → normetanephrine (COMT) → VMA (MAO).
  • Epinephrine pathway:
    • Epi → metanephrine (COMT) → VMA (MAO).
  • Dopamine pathway:
    • Dopamine → HVA (homovanillic acid).
Diagnostic Tests for Pheochromocytoma
  • Best initial test:
    • Plasma free metanephrines — most sensitive (~99%).
  • Confirmatory:
    • 24-hour urine metanephrines and VMA — specific.UWorld Endo
Drug Interaction Pearl
  • MAOI + tyramine foods:
    • Hypertensive crisis.
    • MAO normally degrades dietary tyramine; without MAO, tyramine reaches nerve terminals and displaces stored NE.
★ HIGH-YIELD for USMLE

Rate-limiting enzyme = Tyrosine Hydroxylase. Regulated by end-product feedback and phosphorylation during sympathetic activation.

Why only the medulla makes Epi: PNMT needs HIGH cortisol. Cortisol drains from the adrenal cortex through the medulla via portal venous drainage. Post-ganglionic sympathetic neurons release NE only.

  • Adrenal medulla output ≈ 80% Epi / 20% NE.
  • Sympathetic nerve terminals ≈ 100% NE.
  • Degradation by MAO + COMT → VMA (urine) and metanephrines (plasma/urine — more sensitive for pheochromocytoma).

1 · The chemistry: cholesterol → pregnenolone

The Reaction
  • Carbon count change:
    • Removes 6 carbons from cholesterol's side chain (C27 → C21).
    • Hence the name "side-chain cleavage" (P450scc = CYP11A1 = desmolase — four names, same enzyme).Costanzo 6e ch 9
  • Mechanism:
    • Two sequential hydroxylations at C20 and C22.
    • Followed by C-C bond cleavage.
Cofactors & Location
  • Required cofactors:
    • NADPH.
    • O₂.
    • Fe (as P450 heme cofactor).
  • Location:
    • Inner mitochondrial membrane — so cholesterol must be imported first (see StAR, below).

2 · The real bottleneck: StAR and mitochondrial cholesterol import

  • What StAR does:
    • Shuttles cholesterol from the OUTER to the INNER mitochondrial membrane.
    • This transport is the actual rate-limiting step of steroidogenesis.
  • Why desmolase isn't the bottleneck:
    • Desmolase is constitutively active — it simply waits for substrate.
    • Block StAR and the downstream enzyme is idle.

  • Preferred (most to least):
    • LDL-receptor uptake.

    • Mutations in StAR.

    • Only FREE hormone is biologically active. It crosses membranes and binds receptors.

📚 Going Deeper — the kinetics and the clinical chaos

1 · Binding kinetics — the Kd concept

The Equilibrium
  • Dissociation constant:
    • The binding of hormone to carrier is reversible and governed by Kd.
    • Formula: Kd = [Hormone]free × [Carrier]free / [Complex]
  • Interpretation:
    • LOW Kd = tight binding.
    • HIGH Kd = loose binding.
    • TBG has low Kd for T4 — very tight.
Free Fractions at Steady State
  • Thyroid hormones:
    • T4: ~0.03% free.
    • T3: ~0.3% free.
  • Steroids:
    • Cortisol: ~5% free.
    • Testosterone: ~2% truly free; ~40% is loosely albumin-bound and bioavailable.Costanzo 6e ch 9
Why Feedback Keeps Free Stable
  • Homeostatic adjustment:
    • When carrier level changes, total hormone shifts but free hormone returns to baseline via negative feedback.
    • This is why a pregnant euthyroid patient has ↑ total T4 but normal free T4 and normal TSH.

1 · GPCR biology in depth

Receptor Architecture
  • Structure:
    • 7 transmembrane α-helices, single polypeptide chain.
    • Extracellular N-terminus, intracellular C-terminus.
    • Ligand binding pocket sits among the TM helices.
  • Resting state:
    • Gα (bound to GDP) + Gβγ form a trimer.
    • Trimer attached to the inner membrane, loosely associated with the receptor.
Activation Cycle
  • Steps:
    • Hormone binds → conformational change in receptor → receptor acts as a GEF (guanine exchange factor).
    • Gα exchanges GDP for GTP → dissociates from Gβγ.
    • Gα-GTP (and sometimes Gβγ) activates or inhibits downstream effectors.
    • Gα's intrinsic GTPase activity hydrolyzes GTP → GDP, terminating the signal and reassembling the trimer.Costanzo 6e ch 1
Gs Cascade
  • Pathway:
    • Gs-α activates adenylyl cyclase → ATP → cAMP → activates PKA.
    • PKA phosphorylates substrates (enzymes, channels, CREB).
  • Termination & modulation:
    • Terminated by phosphodiesterases (PDEs) that hydrolyze cAMP → AMP.
    • Caffeine inhibits PDEs non-selectively → ↑ cAMP duration → stimulation.
  • Toxin disruption:
    • Cholera toxin ADP-ribosylates Gs-α → permanently "on" → ↑↑ cAMP → Cl⁻/water secretion → rice-water diarrhea.FA 2024 Micro — Vibrio cholerae
Gq Cascade
  • Pathway:
    • Gq-α activates phospholipase C-β (PLC) → cleaves PIP2 → IP3 + DAG.
    • IP3 → opens ER calcium channel (IP3R) → ↑ cytoplasmic Ca²⁺.
    • DAG + Ca²⁺ → activate PKC → phosphorylate targets.
  • Classic clinical example:
    • α1 agonist on vascular smooth muscle → Gq → Ca²⁺ → contraction → vasoconstriction.
Gi Cascade
  • Pathway:
    • Gi-α inhibits adenylyl cyclase → ↓ cAMP.
    • Gβγ directly opens cardiac GIRK K⁺ channels — why M2 agonists slow the heart.
  • Toxin disruption:
    • Pertussis toxin ADP-ribosylates Gi → permanently "off" → cAMP un-inhibited → ↑ cAMP → lymphocytosis, whooping cough.FA 2024 Micro — Bordetella
Desensitization
  • Mechanism:
    • GRKs (G-protein-coupled receptor kinases) phosphorylate the activated receptor.
    • β-arrestin binds → uncouples receptor from G-protein.
    • Receptor internalized via clathrin-coated pits.

    • Members: GR (cortisol), MR (aldo), AR, PR, ER.
    • Resting location: cytoplasm, bound to HSP90 and HSP70.
    • On hormone binding: HSPs dissociate → receptor dimerizes → nuclear translocation → binds palindromic half-site HREs.
  • Type II — "thyroid / vit D / retinoid" receptors:
    • Members: TR, VDR, RAR, PPAR.
    • Resting location: nucleus, pre-bound to DNA as heterodimers with RXR, holding a corepressor.
    • On hormone binding: swaps corepressor for a coactivator → transcription.Guyton 14e ch 74
Timing of Effects
  • Classical "genomic" effects:
    • Minutes for transcription initiation.
    • Hours for full protein-level effects.
  • Non-genomic effects:
    • Some steroid effects are rapid (seconds–minutes) via membrane-associated receptors.
    • Less well-characterized, but HY for vascular effects of aldosterone and estrogen.

6 · Quick decision tree — pick the receptor family

  • Is the hormone lipid-soluble (steroid, thyroid, vit D, retinoid)?
    • → Intracellular receptor.
    • Cytoplasm for steroids; nucleus for thyroid / vit D / retinoid.
  • Is it insulin, a growth factor, or a cancer-driver ligand?
    • → Receptor tyrosine kinase (RTK).
  • Is it GH, prolactin, EPO, TPO, or a cytokine?
    • → JAK-STAT.
  • Is it ANP, BNP, or NO?
    • → Guanylyl cyclase / cGMP pathway.
  • Otherwise (most peptide hormones, most neurotransmitters):
    • → GPCR — then figure out Gs / Gi / Gq.

  • Acidophiles use JAK-STAT: GH and Prolactin (from pituitary acidophils). Also EPO, TPO.
  • Insulin receptor is an RTK (α₂β₂ tetramer). Many cancer drug targets too (HER2, EGFR).
  • ANP/BNP → membrane guanylyl cyclase → cGMP → natriuresis, vasodilation. BNP ↑ in heart failure.

The Gist A target cell's response depends on (1) how much receptor it has, (2) how tightly each receptor binds the hormone, and (3) how well the downstream machinery converts binding into a physiologic effect. Together these shape the dose–response curve (EC50 measures sensitivity).

Reading a dose–response curve

  • X: log[hormone]. Y: % max response.
  • EC50 = [hormone] producing half-max response → sensitivity measure.
  • LEFT shift (↓EC50) = more sensitive. RIGHT shift (↑EC50) = less sensitive.
  • Lower plateau (↓Emax) = ↓ efficacy (fewer functional receptors or broken downstream).
📚 Going Deeper — curves, regulation, and resistance syndromes

1 · Dose-response curves — the math you need

The Hill Equation
  • Mathematical form:
    • Response = (Emax × [H]n) / (EC50n + [H]n)
Key Parameters
  • EC50:
    • The [hormone] producing 50% of maximal response.
    • Lower EC50 = more sensitive (curve shifted left).
  • Emax:
    • The maximum achievable response (efficacy).
    • Drops when receptor machinery is globally impaired (few receptors OR broken downstream).
  • Hill coefficient (n):
    • Steepness of the curve.
    • n>1 = positive cooperativity (classic example: hemoglobin–O₂).
    • Hormone-receptor binding is usually n≈1 (single-site binding).Rang & Dale Pharmacology 9e; Costanzo 6e ch 1
Spare Receptors Concept
  • Core idea:
    • Many tissues have more receptors than needed for Emax.
    • This creates high sensitivity even at low receptor occupancy.
  • Practical consequence:
    • A tissue with 90% spare receptors can lose 80% of its receptors and still reach Emax — just with a right-shifted curve (↑EC50).
    • Explains why receptor down-regulation causes decreased sensitivity (EC50 shift) BEFORE decreased efficacy (Emax drop).Lippincott Pharm 7e

2 · Receptor regulation — multiple mechanisms, different timescales

Short-term Desensitization (seconds to minutes)
  • Mechanism:
    • GRKs phosphorylate activated receptors on C-terminal serine/threonine residues.
    • β-arrestin binds phosphorylated receptor → uncouples it from G-proteins.
  • Classic example:
    • β2-receptor tachyphylaxis to albuterol in severe asthma overuse.FA 2024 Pharm — β-agonists
Internalization (minutes to hours)
  • Mechanism:
    • β-arrestin recruits clathrin adaptor proteins → receptor taken into endosomes.
    • Fate: recycled to plasma membrane (re-sensitized) OR trafficked to lysosome (degraded = down-regulated).
  • Classic example:
    • Insulin receptor is classically internalized together with bound insulin → both degraded in lysosome.
Transcriptional Changes (hours to days)
  • Direction of change:
    • Chronic hormone excess → ↓ receptor gene transcription → fewer new receptors made.
    • Chronic hormone deficit → ↑ receptor gene transcription → more receptors made.
  • Classic example:
    • β-blocker withdrawal tachycardia: chronic β-blockade up-regulates β1 → sudden discontinuation → catecholamines meet a supersensitive receptor population.

3 · Down-regulation vs. resistance — clarify

Definitions
  • Down-regulation:
    • Fewer receptors on the cell surface.
  • Resistance:
    • Decreased biologic response overall.
    • Can be from fewer receptors, impaired signal transduction, impaired downstream effector machinery, or all three.B&B Endo — Diabetes
Type 2 DM — Insulin Resistance is Primarily POST-Receptor
  • Initiating trigger:
    • Obesity / inflammation → ↑ TNF-α and free fatty acids.
  • Molecular block:
    • Serine phosphorylation of IRS-1 (insulin receptor substrate-1).
    • Serine-phosphorylated IRS-1 cannot be tyrosine-phosphorylated properly → impaired PI3K/AKT signaling.
  • Downstream consequence:
    • ↓ GLUT4 translocation in muscle.
    • ↓ Suppression of hepatic gluconeogenesis.
  • Late compounding:
    • Chronic hyperinsulinemia eventually ALSO down-regulates receptors, compounding the problem.FA 2024 Endo; Pathoma ch 15
Drug Targets
  • Metformin:
    • Activates AMPK, partially bypasses insulin resistance.
    • Reduces hepatic gluconeogenesis.
  • Thiazolidinediones (pioglitazone):
    • PPAR-γ agonists.
    • Improve insulin sensitivity at adipose tissue.
  • GLP-1 agonists (semaglutide):
    • Enhance glucose-dependent insulin secretion.
    • Promote weight loss.Lippincott Pharm 7e

4 · Permissive, synergistic, antagonistic — used precisely

Permissive
  • Definition:
    • Hormone A is required for hormone B's effect, but A alone at physiologic levels has little direct effect.
  • Cortisol permits catecholamine vasoconstriction:
    • Addisonian crisis patients don't respond to norepinephrine until hydrocortisone is given.
    • Cortisol up-regulates α1 receptor expression and preserves vascular responsiveness.Guyton 14e ch 78
  • Thyroid hormone permits GH and catecholamines:
    • Permits GH effects on growth.
    • Permits catecholamine cardiac effects (↑ β-adrenergic receptor density in myocardium).
Synergistic
  • Definition:
    • Two hormones together produce MORE than the sum of individual effects.
  • Examples:
    • Glucagon + Epi + cortisol → hyperglycemia during stress (counter-regulatory response, more than additive).
    • FSH + estrogen in follicular development.
Antagonistic
  • Definition:
    • Opposing actions.
  • Examples:
    • Insulin vs. glucagon on hepatic glucose output.
    • PTH vs. calcitonin on bone (PTH resorbs; calcitonin inhibits osteoclasts — clinically minor in humans).

5 · Hormone resistance syndromes — the "receptor is broken" diseases

Sex Hormone / Growth Resistance
  • Complete androgen insensitivity (AIS):
    • Cause: loss-of-function mutation in androgen receptor (AR).
    • Presentation: 46,XY phenotypic female with testes, no axillary/pubic hair, blind-ending vagina, primary amenorrhea.FA 2024 Repro; Pathoma ch 15
  • Laron syndrome:
    • Cause: GH receptor mutation.
    • Labs: ↑ GH (no negative feedback), ↓ IGF-1.
    • Clinical: short stature; paradoxically protective against cancer and diabetes.
GPCR / Gsα Defects
  • Pseudohypoparathyroidism type 1A (Albright hereditary osteodystrophy):
    • Cause: Gsα mutation → PTH doesn't signal despite high PTH levels.
    • Features: hypocalcemia + short 4th/5th metacarpals + round facies + mental disability.
  • Nephrogenic diabetes insipidus:
    • Cause: V2 receptor mutation (X-linked) OR aquaporin-2 mutation.
    • Clinical: kidney cannot respond to ADH → polyuria, dilute urine.
Nuclear Receptor Defects
  • Thyroid hormone resistance:
    • Cause: TRβ mutation.
    • Labs: ↑ T3/T4 with inappropriately normal or ↑ TSH.
    • Clinical: often presents with goiter and ADHD-like features.
Metabolic Receptor Defects
  • Leptin receptor deficiency:
    • Cause: loss-of-function mutation in leptin receptor.
    • Clinical: severe early-onset obesity despite high leptin levels (no satiety signaling).

6 · Tachyphylaxis (rapid tolerance) — how and why

Classic Drug-Level Examples
  • β2-agonist overuse (albuterol) in asthma:
    • Receptor desensitization by GRK / β-arrestin.
    • Receptor internalization.
    • Net effect: diminished bronchodilation despite more puffs.
  • Opioid tolerance:
    • μ-opioid receptor down-regulation plus post-receptor adaptations.
    • Requires higher doses over time for equivalent analgesia.
  • Nitrate tolerance:
    • Depletion of intracellular sulfhydryl groups needed to convert nitroglycerin to NO.
    • Requires a nitrate-free interval each day to restore efficacy.Lippincott Pharm 7e — cardiovascular
Unifying Theme
  • Big picture:
    • These mechanisms are variations of the same theme: receptors (and the systems behind them) adapt to keep a cell's response within a useful physiologic range.
★ HIGH-YIELD for USMLE

Insulin resistance = RIGHT shift (↑EC50). Higher insulin needed for same effect. Advanced T2DM also drops Emax (β-cell failure + receptor down-reg + post-receptor defects).

  • Chronic hormone excess → down-regulation (β-agonist tolerance, insulin resistance).
  • Chronic hormone deficit → up-regulation (denervation hypersensitivity).

Beyond the target cell: 3 more modifiers

Permissive effects

  • Definition:
    • One hormone at baseline is required for another to work.
    • The permissive hormone doesn't cause the effect on its own — it just licenses it.
  • Classic examples:
    • Cortisol is permissive for catecholamine vasoconstriction → Addisonian crisis = refractory hypotension until you give hydrocortisone.
    • Thyroid hormone is permissive for GH and catecholamines → cardiac output.

Synergy vs. antagonism

  • Synergy:
    • Glucagon + epinephrine + cortisol → additive (often more-than-additive) hyperglycemia during stress.
  • Antagonism:
    • Insulin vs. glucagon on liver glucose output.

Free-fraction and clearance

  • Core idea:
    • Even perfect receptors can only see FREE hormone.
    • Carrier proteins and clearance (liver / kidney) set the "input" the cell experiences.

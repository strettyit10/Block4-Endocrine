LO 1 — Adrenal Zones & Steroidogenesis

▼ Adrenal Zones & Hormones

⭐ The adrenal cortex has three concentric zones, each producing different steroid hormones. Zona glomerulosa makes aldosterone; zona fasciculata makes cortisol; zona reticularis makes androgens. This is why they're called the GFR zones.

🔬 Zona Glomerulosa (Outer)

  • Produces: Aldosterone (mineralocorticoid)
  • Enzyme: Aldosterone synthase (key enzyme found ONLY here)
  • Stimulus: Angiotensin II, high K+, low Na+
  • Regulates: Blood volume and K+ balance

🔬 Zona Fasciculata (Middle)

  • Produces: Cortisol (glucocorticoid)
  • Enzyme: 17α-hydroxylase and 11β-hydroxylase
  • Stimulus: ACTH from anterior pituitary
  • Effects: Stress response, glucose homeostasis, anti-inflammation

🔬 Zona Reticularis (Inner)

  • Produces: DHEA and androstenedione (weak androgens)
  • Stimulus: ACTH
  • Clinical note: Minimal contribution to virilization in health; but becomes important in CAH
⚠️ TRAP: GFR Zones — What Gets Stimulated by What ▼
  • Zona glomerulosa = ONLY responds to Ang II and K+ (NOT ACTH)
  • Zona fasciculata & reticularis = respond to ACTH (NOT Ang II)
  • Students mix these up. Remember: aldosterone is unique—it's regulated by RAAS, not pituitary

▼ Steroidogenesis Pathway

⭐ All steroids start from cholesterol. The rate-limiting step is the movement of cholesterol into the mitochondria via StAR protein. Know this enzyme—it's how every adrenal disorder shows ↓cortisol or ↓aldosterone.

🔬 The Full Pathway

Cholesterol (via StAR protein) → Pregnenolone → DHEA → Androstenedione → Testosterone (androgens)

Cholesterol → Pregnenolone → 17-OH-Pregnenolone → 17-OH-Progesterone → 11-Deoxycortisol → Cortisol

Cholesterol → Pregnenolone → Progesterone → Deoxycorticosterone → Corticosterone → Aldosterone

🔑 Rate-Limiting Step & Key Enzymes

  • StAR protein (P450scc complex): Cholesterol import into mitochondria — THIS IS THE RATE-LIMITING STEP. If StAR is blocked, ALL steroid production stops.
  • 21α-hydroxylase: Most common enzyme defect (CAH). Converts 17-OH-progesterone → 11-deoxycortisol (cortisol pathway) and progesterone → deoxycorticosterone (aldosterone pathway).
  • 11β-hydroxylase: Final step to cortisol. Defect causes ↓cortisol, ↑11-deoxycortisol, and HTN (deoxycorticosterone is salt-retaining).
  • 17α-hydroxylase: Needed for cortisol synthesis but NOT for aldosterone. Defect = no cortisol but ↑aldosterone and corticosterone (HTN, hypokalemia, but no virilization).
⚠️ TRAP: StAR Protein vs. Enzyme Defects ▼
  • StAR blocks = NO hormones at all (total adrenal failure)
  • 21α-hydroxylase blocks = shunt toward androgens (↑17-OH-progesterone, ↑DHEA, virilization)
  • 11β-hydroxylase blocks = ↑11-deoxycortisol + HTN (salt-retaining precursor backs up)
  • These cause DIFFERENT labs. Know which enzyme defect causes which pattern.

▼ Why Zones Specialize

⭐ Each zone expresses different enzymes because they develop from different embryologic tissue layers and are exposed to different hormonal signals.

🔬 Enzyme Expression by Zone

ZoneKey Enzyme(s)Why It Matters
GlomerulosaAldosterone synthaseOnly place aldo is made; ONLY zone that responds to Ang II
Fasciculata17α-hydroxylase, 11β-hydroxylaseMakes cortisol (glucocorticoid); responds to ACTH
Reticularis17α-hydroxylase, 17,20-lyaseShunted toward androgens; responds to ACTH (especially in aging)

LO 2–3 — HPA Axis & Cortisol Physiology

▼ HPA Axis Overview

⭐ The HPA axis is a three-level cascade: hypothalamus releases CRH, anterior pituitary releases ACTH, adrenal cortex releases cortisol. Cortisol feeds back to suppress CRH and ACTH. This is THE most important endocrine axis to understand.

🔬 Three Levels of the Axis

  • Hypothalamus: Secretes CRH (corticotropin-releasing hormone) in response to stress, time of day, or low cortisol
  • Anterior Pituitary: CRH stimulates corticotroph cells to secrete ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone). ACTH is a 39-amino-acid peptide derived from POMC.
  • Adrenal Cortex (zona fasciculata): ACTH stimulates cortisol synthesis and release. Cortisol takes ~15–30 min to rise after ACTH spike.

🔬 The Pulsatile Pattern

  • CRH and ACTH are not constant; they pulse in bursts every 60–90 minutes throughout the day
  • Cortisol follows these pulses but also accumulates and stays relatively high during the day
  • This pulsatile pattern is essential for maintaining HPA function; constant cortisol suppresses ACTH
  • Step 1 loves asking: if you measure cortisol at random times, you'll get variable results (unlike TSH/free T4 which are more stable)
⚠️ TRAP: Single Cortisol Measurement vs. 24-h Urine Free Cortisol ▼
  • A single blood cortisol is USELESS for diagnosis because of pulsatile secretion
  • Midnight salivary cortisol or 24-h urine free cortisol are better because they reflect integrated production
  • In Cushing syndrome, the normal circadian suppression is lost—cortisol stays high even at midnight

▼ Circadian Rhythm

⭐ Cortisol peaks in the early morning (6–8 AM) and nadir at midnight. This is a circadian clock-driven rhythm, not just a response to waking up. Loss of this rhythm is pathognomonic for Cushing syndrome.

🔬 Normal Circadian Pattern

  • ~4 AM: CRH surge (suprachiasmatic nucleus [SCN] initiated)
  • ~6–8 AM: Cortisol peak (triggered by CRH/ACTH surge)
  • Throughout day: Cortisol gradually declines with CRH/ACTH pulses
  • ~Midnight: Cortisol nadir (suppressed by circadian clock)
  • Deep sleep: Cortisol suppressed (so cortisol is lowest when you sleep)

🔬 Why Does Cortisol Peak Early Morning?

  • The SCN (master circadian clock) drives CRH secretion at ~4 AM
  • This prepares the body for the stress of waking and activity
  • Cortisol mobilizes glucose and mental alertness before you get out of bed
  • NOT just "stress from waking"—the rhythm persists in sleeping subjects and shift workers
⚠️ TRAP: Midnight Salivary Cortisol in Cushing ▼
  • Normal people: cortisol suppresses to ~30% of morning value by midnight
  • Cushing syndrome: cortisol stays HIGH at midnight (no suppression)
  • Midnight salivary cortisol >5 ng/mL = highly suggestive of Cushing. Step 1 LOVES this screening test.

▼ Cortisol's Physiologic Effects

⭐ Cortisol is a catabolic hormone that mobilizes energy stores and suppresses immunity. Its effects are via glucocorticoid receptors (GR) expressed widely in the body. High cortisol = immunosuppression + hyperglycemia + protein breakdown.

🔬 Metabolic Effects (Mobilization)

  • Gluconeogenesis: Cortisol stimulates the liver to make glucose from amino acids and glycerol. High cortisol = ↑blood glucose (hyperglycemia).
  • Protein catabolism: Cortisol breaks down muscle protein to supply amino acids for gluconeogenesis. This is why Cushing patients have proximal muscle weakness.
  • Lipolysis (selective): Cortisol increases lipolysis in limbs (↓subcutaneous fat = skinny arms/legs) but increases lipogenesis in face and upper back (moon facies, buffalo hump in Cushing).
  • Insulin antagonism: Cortisol is diabetogenic (opposes insulin). High cortisol → insulin resistance.

🔬 Immune/Inflammatory Effects (Suppression)

  • Lymphocyte apoptosis: Cortisol triggers T and B cell death. High cortisol = ↓white cells, especially lymphocytes.
  • Anti-inflammatory: Suppresses IL-2, TNF-α, and other pro-inflammatory cytokines. This is why cortisol is used as a drug for autoimmune diseases.
  • Shifts immune response: Skews toward Th2 (humoral) and away from Th1 (cellular) immunity. High cortisol = ↓cell-mediated immunity, increased susceptibility to intracellular pathogens (TB, fungi).

🔬 Other Effects

  • Bone: Cortisol inhibits osteoblasts and ↑osteoclasts → osteoporosis (especially in Cushing)
  • CNS: Mood, cognition, memory. Chronic high cortisol → depression, memory impairment
  • Hemodynamics: Cortisol permissive for catecholamine effects; increases vascular tone and BP
⚡ High-Yield:
  • The mnemonic for cortisol effects: SHAM = Sugar (↑glucose), H20 (Na+ retention), Andro (permissive for catecholamines), Muscle (catabolism)
  • Cortisol is catabolic in excess but anabolic in physiologic amounts (cortisol deficiency = weight loss, weakness)

▼ Negative Feedback Loop

⭐ Cortisol inhibits both CRH (at hypothalamus) and ACTH (at pituitary). This is negative feedback. Short-loop feedback (ACTH → CRH) also exists. Disruption of this feedback is how you get Cushing syndrome.

🔬 How Feedback Works

  • Low cortisol: Pituitary and hypothalamus sense low cortisol → ↑CRH → ↑ACTH → ↑cortisol
  • High cortisol: Inhibits CRH at hypothalamus (slow feedback) and ACTH at pituitary (fast feedback) → cortisol production stops
  • Negative feedback is NOT immediate: Takes 30+ minutes for cortisol to affect ACTH because feedback is via the GR in the pituitary

🔬 Dexamethasone Suppression Test (Diagnostic)

  • Dexamethasone is a synthetic GR agonist that mimics cortisol feedback
  • Low-dose dex (1 mg overnight): Suppresses cortisol in healthy people. If cortisol doesn't suppress = Cushing syndrome
  • High-dose dex (8 mg): Suppresses cortisol in Cushing disease (pituitary tumor) but NOT in ectopic ACTH or adrenal tumors. Diagnostic test to differentiate.
⚠️ TRAP: Low-Dose vs. High-Dose Dex Suppression ▼
  • LOW-dose dex = screening test for Cushing (separates normal from hypercortisolism)
  • HIGH-dose dex = differentiating test (separates Cushing disease from ectopic ACTH/adrenal tumor)
  • Cushing disease = pituitary tumor that produces ACTH → responsive to high-dose dex suppression
  • Ectopic ACTH or adrenal tumor = NOT responsive to dex (independent of pituitary feedback)

▼ Aldosterone Synthesis

⭐ Aldosterone is a mineralocorticoid made ONLY in zona glomerulosa. Its primary regulators are angiotensin II and K+, NOT ACTH. This is the critical difference from cortisol.

🔬 Synthesis Pathway

  • Cholesterol → (via StAR) → Pregnenolone → Progesterone → Deoxycorticosterone → Corticosterone → Aldosterone (final step via aldosterone synthase, unique to zona glomerulosa)

🔬 Primary Regulators

  • Angiotensin II (strongest): Directly stimulates aldosterone synthase in zona glomerulosa. Low blood pressure/volume → ↑renin → ↑Ang II → ↑aldosterone
  • High K+: Directly stimulates zona glomerulosa cells to release aldosterone. High K+ is a more direct signal than Ang II.
  • ACTH: Can stimulate aldo slightly but NOT the primary regulator. This is why corticosteroid excess doesn't cause severe hyperaldosteronism.
⚠️ TRAP: Aldosterone Is Independent of ACTH ▼
  • Secondary adrenal insufficiency (low ACTH) = preserved aldosterone (because glomerulosa doesn't need ACTH)
  • Primary adrenal insufficiency (Addison disease) = BOTH low cortisol and low aldosterone (glomerulosa is destroyed)
  • This is why secondary AI doesn't cause hyponatremia as severe as primary AI

▼ RAAS Regulation

⭐ The renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system is a volume-sensing and Na+ -conserving cascade. When blood volume or renal perfusion pressure drops, the kidney releases renin. Renin activates the cascade.

🔬 The RAAS Cascade

  1. Juxtaglomerular (JG) cells in kidney sense low blood pressure and low Na+ → release renin (a protease)
  2. Renin cleaves angiotensinogen (made by liver) → angiotensin I
  3. ACE (angiotensin-converting enzyme, in lung capillaries) converts Ang I → angiotensin II
  4. Angiotensin II has three effects:
    • Stimulates aldosterone release from zona glomerulosa → ↑Na+ and H2O reabsorption → ↑BP and ↑blood volume
    • Vasoconstriction → ↑BP directly
    • Stimulates ADH release → ↑H2O reabsorption
  5. Result: Na+, H2O, and BP restored → renin suppressed (negative feedback)

🔬 Physiologic Triggers for RAAS Activation

  • ↓BP or ↓blood volume (JG cell sensor)
  • ↓Na+ delivery to macula densa (sensor in kidney)
  • Sympathetic activation (β1 adrenergic stimulation of JG cells)

🔬 Negative Feedback on Renin

  • High BP: Decreases renin release (mechanoreceptor feedback)
  • ↑Angiotensin II: Suppresses renin (paracrine feedback in kidney)
  • ↑Na+: Suppresses renin (decreased macula densa signal)
⚡ High-Yield:
  • ACE inhibitors (lisinopril) and ARBs (losartan) both block RAAS at different points → ↓aldosterone, ↓BP, ↑K+ (hyperkalemia)
  • The distinction: ACE-I block Ang II formation; ARBs block Ang II receptor

▼ Aldosterone's Mechanism on Collecting Duct

⭐ Aldosterone binds to mineralocorticoid receptors (MR) in collecting duct principal cells. It increases expression of ENaC (epithelial Na+ channel) and Na-K-ATPase → Na+ reabsorption and K+ secretion.

🔬 Cellular Mechanism

  • Aldosterone binds to mineralocorticoid receptor (MR) in cytoplasm
  • MR-aldo complex enters nucleus → increases transcription of ENaC (epithelial Na+ channel) and Na-K-ATPase
  • ENaC in apical membrane: increases Na+ reabsorption from urine
  • Na-K-ATPase in basolateral membrane: pumps Na+ into blood, pumps K+ into cell
  • Result: ↑Na+ reabsorption (↑BP, hypernatremia if water intake is restricted) and ↑K+ secretion (hypokalemia)

🔬 Classic Aldosterone Effects

  • ↑Na+ and H2O reabsorption (↑BP, ↑plasma volume)
  • ↑K+ secretion (↓serum K+, hypokalemia)
  • ↑H+ secretion (metabolic alkalosis)
  • Hypokalemia + metabolic alkalosis = classic lab pattern of hyperaldosteronism
⚠️ TRAP: Aldosterone Doesn't Cause Hyponatremia ▼
  • Pure hyperaldosteronism = ↑Na+ (hypernatremia if fluids restricted) + ↑BP
  • Hyponatremia + high aldo occurs ONLY if there's concurrent water retention (e.g., SIADH co-existing with aldo excess)
  • Most hyperaldosteronism = HTN + hypokalemia + metabolic alkalosis (NOT hyponatremia)

▼ Primary vs. Secondary Hyperaldosteronism

⭐ The key difference: PRIMARY aldo excess = ↓renin (autonomous aldo suppresses RAAS). SECONDARY aldo excess = ↑renin (responding to decreased perfusion). Use renin level to distinguish.

🔬 Primary Hyperaldosteronism (Conn Syndrome)

  • Cause: Adrenal adenoma (~65%) or bilateral adrenal hyperplasia (30%) produces aldosterone autonomously
  • Key feature: Aldo is HIGH but renin is LOW (autonomous production suppresses renin)
  • Lab pattern: ↑aldo, ↓renin, ↑Na+, ↓K+, ↑BP, metabolic alkalosis
  • Classic presentation: Young HTN patient (20–40 y/o) with hypokalemia that's resistant to diuretics
  • Diagnostic test: Plasma aldo/renin ratio >20–30 is suggestive. Saline suppression test: aldo doesn't suppress (autonomous)

🔬 Secondary Hyperaldosteronism

▼Renal Artery Stenosis
  • Cause: Decreased renal perfusion (atherosclerotic narrowing or fibromuscular dysplasia) → juxtaglomerular cells sense ↓pressure → ↑renin
  • Lab: ↑aldo AND ↑renin (appropriate response to low renal perfusion)
  • Presentation: HTN + renal insufficiency (or flash pulmonary edema if bilateral stenosis)
▼Heart Failure
  • Cause: Decreased cardiac output → ↓renal perfusion → ↑renin → ↑aldo (compensatory)
  • Lab: ↑aldo AND ↑renin (trying to maintain perfusion)
  • Problem: In HF, aldosterone activation is maladaptive—worsens fluid retention and HTN
  • Treatment: ACE-I or ARB to block RAAS; aldosterone antagonists (spironolactone, eplerenone) to reduce aldo effect
▼Cirrhosis, Nephrotic Syndrome
  • Cause: ↓plasma volume (protein loss, portal HTN) → JG cells sense low pressure → ↑renin → ↑aldo
  • Lab: ↑aldo AND ↑renin (appropriate to volume depletion)
🎯 USMLE Pearl: The Key Test is Renin
  • ↑Aldo + ↓Renin = PRIMARY hyperaldosteronism (Conn syndrome)
  • ↑Aldo + ↑Renin = SECONDARY hyperaldosteronism (RAS, HF, cirrhosis, nephrotic)

▼ Cushing Syndrome Overview

⭐ Cushing syndrome = sustained hypercortisolism from ANY cause. Cushing disease = Cushing syndrome caused by a pituitary ACTH-secreting tumor. These terms are NOT synonymous. The cause determines treatment.

🔬 Terminology

  • Cushing syndrome: Clinical entity of cortisol excess (from any source)
  • Cushing disease: Subset of Cushing syndrome caused by a pituitary ACTH-secreting tumor (corticotroph adenoma)
  • Ectopic ACTH syndrome: ACTH secreted from non-pituitary tumor (usually lung cancer)
  • Primary adrenal tumor: Adrenal adenoma or carcinoma producing cortisol independently (ACTH is suppressed)

🔬 Clinical Features (All Forms)

  • Hypertension (high cortisol increases vascular tone)
  • Proximal muscle weakness (protein catabolism)
  • Diabetes/hyperglycemia (antagonism of insulin)
  • Mood changes (depression, anxiety, psychosis in severe cases)
  • Osteoporosis (cortisol inhibits osteoblasts)
  • Easy bruising (collagen breakdown, thinned skin)
  • Hirsutism/virilization (if adrenal androgens also elevated)

🔬 Physical Exam Findings (when severe)

  • Moon facies: Rounded face from lipogenesis in face (not everywhere)
  • Buffalo hump: Dorsocervical fat pad (lipid redistribution)
  • Proximal weakness: Can't rise from squatting (muscle atrophy)
  • Purple stretch marks: Striae from collagen breakdown + subcutaneous bleeding
  • Thin skin: Fragile, bruises easily
⚠️ TRAP: Not All Cushing Is from Pituitary ▼
  • Common mistake: equating "Cushing" with "pituitary tumor"
  • Only 70% of Cushing syndrome is from pituitary (Cushing disease)
  • 15% is ectopic ACTH (small cell lung cancer is #1 source)
  • 15% is primary adrenal (adenoma or carcinoma)

▼ Cushing Disease (Pituitary ACTH Tumor)

⭐ Cushing disease is a microadenoma in the anterior pituitary that produces ACTH. It's responsive to feedback but at an abnormally high set point. This is why high-dose dex suppresses it (restores feedback loop).

🔬 Pathophysiology

  • Corticotroph adenoma (usually microadenoma <1 cm) produces ACTH → ↑cortisol
  • The tumor is STILL feedback-responsive (unlike ectopic ACTH or adrenal tumor) but at a higher set point
  • Result: ↑ACTH, ↑cortisol, with an abnormal circadian rhythm (flat, no suppression at midnight)

🔬 Lab Pattern

  • Cortisol: Elevated (24-h UFC ↑, midnight salivary cortisol ↑)
  • ACTH: Elevated (15–100 pg/mL, typically 50–90 in Cushing disease)
  • Dexamethasone suppression: Low-dose dex (1 mg) does NOT suppress; HIGH-dose dex (8 mg) DOES suppress (this is the key test to identify pituitary source)
  • CRH stimulation test: Cushing disease responds with ↑ACTH and ↑cortisol (pituitary tumor still responds to CRH)

🔬 Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Diagnosis: Elevated cortisol + ↑ACTH + suppression to high-dose dex (>50% reduction) = Cushing disease
  • Imaging: MRI pituitary (microadenoma may not be visible, so negative MRI doesn't rule out Cushing disease)
  • Treatment: Transsphenoidal pituitary surgery (trans-sphenoidal hypophysectomy). Medical therapy (ketoconazole, mitotane) if surgery fails or patient refuses.

▼ Ectopic ACTH Syndrome

⭐ A non-pituitary tumor produces ACTH. #1 source is small-cell lung cancer. The ectopic ACTH is NOT feedback-responsive (independent of cortisol suppression), so dexamethasone doesn't suppress.

🔬 Common Ectopic Sources

  • Small-cell lung cancer (SCLC): #1 cause of ectopic ACTH (40–50% of cases)
  • Other lung tumors: Bronchial carcinoid, large-cell carcinoma
  • Neuroendocrine tumors: Carcinoid (bronchial, thymic), gastrinoma, pheochromocytoma
  • Other: Renal cell carcinoma, pancreatic cancer, medullary thyroid carcinoma

🔬 Lab Pattern

  • Cortisol: VERY high (often >50 μg/dL, higher than Cushing disease)
  • ACTH: VERY high (often >200 pg/mL, much higher than Cushing disease)
  • Hypokalemia: Severe (cortisol excess + mineralocorticoid effects from 11-deoxycortisol)
  • Metabolic alkalosis: From cortisol and potassium loss
  • Dexamethasone suppression: Low-dose dex does NOT suppress; HIGH-dose dex also does NOT suppress (this is the key difference from Cushing disease)

🔬 Diagnosis

  • High ACTH + cortisol + NO suppression to high-dose dex = Ectopic ACTH (NOT pituitary)
  • CRH stimulation test: Ectopic ACTH source does NOT respond (no CRH receptor on lung cancer cells)
  • Imaging: Search for occult malignancy (CT chest for SCLC, CT abdomen, endoscopy)

🔬 Clinical Pearl

Ectopic ACTH presents more acutely and severely than Cushing disease. The cortisol and ACTH are often VERY high. Watch for hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis + severe HTN.

▼ Primary Adrenal Tumor

⭐ Adrenal adenoma or carcinoma produces cortisol autonomously. ACTH is suppressed by cortisol feedback (pituitary shuts down). This is why ACTH is LOW and cortisol is HIGH.

🔬 Lab Pattern

  • Cortisol: Elevated
  • ACTH: LOW (suppressed by cortisol feedback). This is the key differentiating lab.
  • Dexamethasone suppression: Does NOT suppress (autonomous tumor, doesn't respond to dex)

🔬 Types & Diagnosis

  • Adenoma: Usually benign, smaller, older patients. Imaging shows low-density lesion.
  • Carcinoma: Rare, large (>4 cm), aggressive. Often with virilization (androgen excess).
  • Imaging: CT/MRI adrenal shows mass. Low Hounsfield units (<10) suggest adenoma; high density suggests malignancy.
  • Treatment: Surgical resection (adrenalectomy)
⚠️ TRAP: Low ACTH + High Cortisol = Adrenal Source (Not Pituitary) ▼
  • If ACTH is LOW in a Cushing patient, the source is ADRENAL (suppressed pituitary from high cortisol feedback)
  • If ACTH is HIGH, the source is either PITUITARY or ECTOPIC

▼ Diagnostic Algorithm

⭐ Step-by-step approach to differentiate Cushing syndrome causes.

🔬 Algorithm

  1. Confirm hypercortisolism: 24-h urine free cortisol, midnight salivary cortisol, late-night cortisol. (OR low-dose dexamethasone suppression test).
  2. Measure ACTH: This is the KEY test.
    • If ACTH is <5 pg/mL (LOW) → PRIMARY ADRENAL tumor. Do adrenal imaging (CT).
    • If ACTH is >15 pg/mL (HIGH or normal-high) → Go to step 3.
  3. High-dose dexamethasone suppression test (8 mg overnight):
    • If cortisol suppresses >50% → CUSHING DISEASE (pituitary). Do pituitary MRI, consider CRH stim test.
    • If cortisol does NOT suppress → ECTOPIC ACTH or primary adrenal (but ACTH was high, so ectopic). Search for malignancy (chest CT for lung cancer).

LO 7 — Adrenal Insufficiency

Primary (Addison) vs. secondary insufficiency; pathophysiology and labs · HoyaDocs Endocrine Block

Learning Objectives Covered

On This Page
  1. Primary Adrenal Insufficiency (Addison)
  2. Secondary Adrenal Insufficiency
  3. Hyperpigmentation Mechanism
  4. Acute Adrenal Crisis

▼ Primary Adrenal Insufficiency (Addison Disease)

⭐ Addison disease is destruction/dysfunction of the adrenal cortex. Both cortisol AND aldosterone are low. Pituitary tries to compensate with ↑ACTH (loss of negative feedback), but the adrenal can't respond. Result: ↑ACTH + ↓cortisol + ↓aldosterone.

🔬 Causes

▼Autoimmune (Most Common in Developed Countries, 60%)
  • Autoimmune destruction of adrenocortical cells (21-hydroxylase antibodies)
  • Associated with other autoimmune conditions (thyroiditis, type 1 DM, pernicious anemia, vitiligo)
▼Tuberculosis (Most Common Worldwide, 25%)
  • TB infiltrates and destroys adrenal gland (caseous necrosis)
  • Bilateral involvement destroys cortex
▼Other Causes
  • Adrenal hemorrhage (sepsis, anticoagulation, thrombosis)
  • Metastatic cancer (lung, breast, lymphoma)
  • Infiltrative diseases (sarcoidosis, histoplasmosis, HIV/AIDS)

🔬 Lab Pattern (Most Important)

  • Cortisol: LOW (<5 μg/dL, normal 5–25)
  • ACTH: HIGH (>100 pg/mL, trying to stimulate dead gland). This is the key lab that distinguishes PRIMARY from secondary.
  • Aldosterone: LOW (glomerulosa destroyed)
  • Renin: HIGH (sensor detects low Na+ and BP from ↓aldo)
  • Na+: LOW (hyponatremia from ↓aldo)
  • K+: HIGH (hyperkalemia from ↓aldo)

🔬 Clinical Features

  • Symptoms of cortisol deficiency: Weakness, fatigue, weight loss, anorexia, hypoglycemia (especially in stress)
  • Symptoms of aldo deficiency: Hypotension (orthostatic), syncope
  • Hyperpigmentation: (See next section)
  • GI symptoms: Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain (may be confused with appendicitis)

🔬 Diagnosis

  • Screening: 8 AM cortisol <5 μg/dL (or 24-h UFC <20 μg) + ACTH >100 = likely Addison
  • Confirmatory test: ACTH (cosyntropin) stimulation test. Normal people and secondary AI respond with ↑cortisol (>18 μg/dL at 60 min). In primary AI, cortisol does NOT rise (adrenal is destroyed).
⚠️ TRAP: Addison Causes HYPER- (not HYPOnatremia) Because of Low Aldo ▼
  • Actually: Addison causes BOTH hyponatremia AND hyposmolality (confusing!)
  • The mechanism: ↓aldo → ↓Na+ retention → hyponatremia. Also, ↓cortisol → ↑ADH (cortisol normally suppresses ADH) → more water retention → further dilution of Na+
  • So Addison = ↓Na+, ↓K+ (wait, K+ is HIGH), ↓BP. The hypokalemia comes from urine K+ loss with ↓aldo.
  • Wait, that's wrong. Let me correct: Addison = ↓Na+ (from ↓aldo), ↑K+ (from ↓aldo), ↓BP (from ↓aldo), ↓blood glucose

▼ Secondary Adrenal Insufficiency

⭐ Secondary AI = pituitary or hypothalamic damage. ACTH production is lost, so cortisol is low. BUT ACTH level is low (not high like Addison). Aldosterone is PRESERVED (glomerulosa is intact and responds to RAAS).

🔬 Causes

  • Pituitary tumor or surgery: Resection of corticotrophs destroys ACTH production
  • Hypothalamic injury: Trauma, surgery, radiation
  • Chronic corticosteroid use: Exogenous cortisol suppresses CRH and ACTH → when you stop steroids, pituitary doesn't immediately restart ACTH

🔬 Lab Pattern

  • Cortisol: LOW (same as Addison)
  • ACTH: LOW (this is the KEY difference from primary AI). Pituitary is dead/damaged.
  • Aldosterone: NORMAL (glomerulosa isn't affected; RAAS still works)
  • Renin: Normal-high (RAAS is functional)
  • Na+: Normal or mildly low (mild hyponatremia only if severe water retention from ↓cortisol)
  • K+: Normal (aldo is preserved, so K+ handling is normal)

🔬 ACTH Stimulation Test

  • Give cosyntropin (synthetic ACTH) → In secondary AI, the adrenal CAN respond if stimulated (gland is intact). Cortisol rises (>18 μg/dL at 60 min).
  • This distinguishes secondary (cortisol rises) from primary (cortisol doesn't rise).
⚠️ TRAP: Secondary AI Preserves Aldosterone (So No Severe Hyponatremia or Hyperkalemia) ▼
  • Primary AI (Addison): ↓cortisol + ↓aldo → hyponatremia + hyperkalemia + orthostasis
  • Secondary AI: ↓cortisol + normal aldo → mild hyponatremia (from ↓cortisol, not ↓aldo) + normal K+ + may have less hypotension

▼ Why Addison Causes Hyperpigmentation (Not Secondary)

⭐ USMLE loves this question: "Why does Addison have hyperpigmentation but secondary AI doesn't?" The answer: loss of cortisol's negative feedback → ↑ACTH → ACTH acts on melanocortin receptors in melanocytes → more melanin.

🔬 Mechanism

  • ACTH: Derived from POMC (pro-opiomelanocortin), a peptide that also produces MSH (melanocyte-stimulating hormone)
  • In Addison: High ACTH binds melanocortin-1 receptors on melanocytes → increases melanin synthesis → brown/black pigmentation (especially in creases, areola, palms, lips)
  • In secondary AI: ACTH is LOW (pituitary damaged) → no MSH effect → no hyperpigmentation

🔬 Classic Sites of Hyperpigmentation in Addison

  • Palms and soles (normally non-pigmented)
  • Areola and genitalia
  • Skin creases, scars (especially old scars become darker)
  • Oral mucosa (buccal mucosa becomes dark)
⚡ High-Yield:
  • Addison = Primary AI = ↑ACTH = Hyperpigmentation (dark skin)
  • Secondary AI = ↓ACTH = No hyperpigmentation (normal/pale skin)
  • This single clinical finding differentiates the two. Step 1 LOVES testing this.

▼ Acute Adrenal Crisis

⭐ Adrenal crisis is acute decompensation from lack of cortisol (and aldo). It's a medical emergency. Patients on chronic corticosteroids can develop crisis if they suddenly stop their meds or encounter severe stress without dose increase.

🔬 Triggers

  • Sudden cessation of corticosteroids (especially after prolonged use)
  • Severe stress/infection (surgery, sepsis, MI) without cortisol dose adjustment
  • Undiagnosed Addison disease + stress

🔬 Presentation

  • Sudden hypotension, shock
  • Severe hyponatremia (↓Na+ <120 mEq/L)
  • Fever, delirium
  • Hypoglycemia (cortisol is absent)
  • Abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting

🔬 Emergency Treatment

  • Immediate IV hydrocortisone 50–100 mg (NOT prednisone or dexamethasone; need fast-acting cortisol)
  • Repeat every 6–8 hours until stable
  • IV fluids (normal saline) for hypotension and hyponatremia
  • Treat underlying infection if present

▼ CAH Overview

⭐ Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH) is an autosomal recessive enzyme deficiency that impairs steroid synthesis. The substrate backs up and is shunted toward androgens. 21α-hydroxylase deficiency accounts for 90% of all CAH. It's the most common cause of ambiguous genitalia in genetic females.

🔬 Key Concept: Enzyme Defect → Substrate Backup → Androgen Shunt

  • 21α-hydroxylase blocks conversion of 17-OH-progesterone → 11-deoxycortisol (cortisol pathway)
  • Blocked substrate backs up as 17-OH-progesterone (diagnostic)
  • Precursors are diverted to androgen synthesis (↑DHEA, ↑androstenedione, ↑testosterone)
  • High ACTH (from low cortisol) drives more steroid synthesis, worsening the block

▼ 21-Hydroxylase Deficiency

⭐ 21α-hydroxylase is needed for BOTH cortisol synthesis (via 17-OH-progesterone pathway) AND aldosterone synthesis (via progesterone pathway). Deficiency blocks both pathways.

🔬 Enzyme Block Location

  • 21α-hydroxylase blocks: 17-OH-progesterone → 11-deoxycortisol (stops cortisol synthesis)
  • 21α-hydroxylase also blocks: Progesterone → Deoxycorticosterone (stops aldosterone synthesis)

🔬 Consequences

  • ↓↓ Cortisol (synthesis blocked at 21α-hydroxylase step)
  • ↓↓ Aldosterone (synthesis blocked at 21α-hydroxylase step)
  • ↑↑ 17-OH-progesterone (precursor backs up) — diagnostic marker
  • ↑↑ DHEA, androstenedione, testosterone (substrate diverted to androgens)

▼ Lab Pattern

⭐ The diagnostic labs for 21-hydroxylase deficiency CAH are: ↑17-OH-progesterone (diagnostic), ↓cortisol, ↓aldosterone, ↑androgens, ↑ACTH (trying to drive cortisol synthesis, but blocked).

🔬 Newborn Screening

  • 17-OH-progesterone in dried blood spot is the screening test
  • Abnormal result → confirmatory testing (17-OH-prog level, ACTH stimulation, DNA)
  • Early diagnosis allows treatment to prevent virilization in females

▼ Clinical Forms: Classic vs. Salt-Wasting vs. Non-Classic

⭐ 21-hydroxylase deficiency has three clinical presentations depending on enzyme residual activity: classic (with or without salt-wasting) and non-classic (mild).

🔬 Classic CAH (90% of cases, ~0% enzyme activity)

▼Simple Virilizing Form
  • Presentation: Normal newborn screening; virilization in female infants (ambiguous genitalia, clitoromegaly) OR precocious puberty in males (early pubic hair, advanced bone age)
  • Na+/K+: Usually normal (some residual aldosterone)
  • Why "simple virilizing": High androgens cause virilization but aldo is not completely gone
▼Salt-Wasting Form (Most Severe)
  • Presentation: Virilization + acute salt-wasting at 1–4 weeks of life (hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, shock, metabolic acidosis)
  • Why: COMPLETE loss of aldosterone synthesis (21α-hydroxylase is needed for both cortisol AND aldo pathways)
  • Clinical emergency: Shock from hypovolemia + hyponatremia can be fatal if not treated
  • Treatment: Salt replacement, fludrocortisone (mineralocorticoid), hydrocortisone (glucocorticoid)

🔬 Non-Classic CAH (Attenuated, 10% of cases, ~20–30% enzyme activity)

  • Presentation: Later in childhood or adolescence with premature adrenarche (pubic hair before age 8), irregular menses in girls, or acne/hirsutism
  • Lab: 17-OH-progesterone may be borderline (100–200 ng/dL); ACTH stimulation test shows ↑17-OH-prog (diagnostic)
  • No salt-wasting: Residual enzyme activity is enough for some aldo synthesis

⚠️ TRAP: Genetic XX Female with Virilization = CAH (Not True Androgen-Secreting Tumor) ▼
  • Most common cause of ambiguous genitalia in genetic females (XX) is CAH (21-OH def)
  • Androgen-secreting tumors are rare; always consider CAH first
  • Prenatal treatment: mothers of affected females can receive dexamethasone in early pregnancy to suppress ACTH and reduce virilization (controversial but used in some centers)

▼ Management

⭐ Treatment goals: (1) Replace cortisol and aldosterone to normalize hormones, (2) Suppress ACTH (which drives androgen synthesis), (3) Prevent virilization and maintain normal growth.

🔬 Treatment Regimen

  • Hydrocortisone (glucocorticoid): Replaces cortisol. Dose is adjusted to suppress ACTH and androgens while avoiding overtreatment (which causes Cushing-like effects).
  • Fludrocortisone (mineralocorticoid): Replaces aldosterone, especially needed in salt-wasting form. Prevents hyponatremia and hyperkalemia.
  • Salt supplementation: Extra Na+ in diet, especially in infants with salt-wasting
  • Monitoring: Check ACTH, 17-OH-progesterone, androgens, Na+/K+ at regular intervals to titrate doses

🔬 Surgical Considerations (for Virilized Females)

  • Clitoroplasty/vaginoplasty: Surgical feminization if desired (timing depends on severity and family preferences)
  • Counseling: Gender identity issues; prenatal diagnosis and potential early dex treatment; reproductive counseling
⚡ High-Yield:
  • CAH 21-OH deficiency = most common cause of ambiguous genitalia in genetic females
  • Diagnostic test = ↑17-OH-progesterone
  • Treatment = hydrocortisone + fludrocortisone (replaces both cortisol and aldo)

Very high ACTH + very high cortisol + NO suppression to high-dose dex = ectopic ACTH syndrome (lung cancer is classic source). Cushing disease would suppress to high-dose dex.

Question 11

TB is the #1 global cause of Addison disease (TB granulomas destroy adrenal cortex). Very high ACTH + low cortisol + poor response to ACTH stimulation = primary AI. Salt-wasting CAH loses BOTH glucocorticoids (cortisol) AND mineralocorticoids (aldosterone) due to complete 21-OH block. Results in hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, and elevated renin (RAAS compensates for low BP). Virilizing form has some residual aldosterone function.

Question 20

A mother of a child with CAH asks about future pregnancies. If she is a carrier (heterozygous) and the father is unaffected, what is the risk of having an affected female child?

A) 0%

B) 12.5%

C) 25%

D) 50%

✓ Correct Answer: B

CAH is autosomal recessive. Carrier mother (Aa) × unaffected father (AA) = 50% AA (unaffected) + 50% Aa (carrier). Of that 50% carriers, only half will be female. So 50% × 50% = 25% chance of carrier child, but only ~12.5% chance of affected child overall (as affected is 1:4 = 25%, but only if both parents are Aa, which is not the case here).

Learning Objectives Completed
LO1: Hypothyroidism — Causes, Features, Diagnosis ▼
SLOWED everything is the key concept. Symptoms include:
  • Fatigue and low energy
  • Weight gain despite reduced appetite
  • Cold intolerance — patient always cold
  • Constipation — slowed GI motility
  • Bradycardia — slow heart rate
  • Dry skin, brittle hair
  • Hair loss (outer 1/3 eyebrow) = Queen Anne's sign
  • Myxedema — non-pitting edema from glycosaminoglycan deposition (puffy face, lips)
  • Delayed DTR relaxation = "hung reflexes"
  • Menorrhagia — heavy menses
  • Depression, cognitive slowing — mood changes
  • Hyperlipidemia — ↑ LDL (reduced catabolism)
  • Macrocytic/normocytic anemia
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome — myxedema in tunnel
Click to reveal: Queen Anne's sign
Loss of outer 1/3 of eyebrows due to hair loss in hypothyroidism
Main Causes:
  • Hashimoto's thyroiditis (most common in iodine-sufficient regions)
    • Autoimmune; anti-TPO and anti-thyroglobulin antibodies
    • Histology: Hürthle cell change, lymphocytic infiltrate + germinal centers
    • Initial thyrotoxicosis phase (hashitoxicosis) → then hypothyroidism
    • Associated with: DM type 1, pernicious anemia, vitiligo, Addison's
  • Iodine deficiency (most common worldwide)
  • Post-thyroidectomy/radioiodine ablation — iatrogenic
  • Medications: amiodarone, lithium, methimazole, interferon-alpha
  • Congenital (cretinism): thyroid dysgenesis (most common)
  • Riedel's thyroiditis: rare, fibrous replacement ("iron-hard" thyroid); associated with IgG4-related disease; can cause compressive symptoms
🎯 Key Labs
Primary hypothyroidism:
↑ TSH, ↓ free T4 (thyroid is failing)
Check anti-TPO antibodies to confirm Hashimoto's
SLOWS mnemonic: Sluggish (bradycardia/constipation), Low temp (cold intolerance), Over-weight (weight gain), Waterlogged (myxedema), Sad (depression)
⚠️ Common Trap
Myxedema is NON-PITTING edema (glycosaminoglycans don't indent). Distinguish from pitting edema of other causes.
Myxedema Coma: Life-threatening extreme hypothyroidism
  • Precipitated by: cold exposure, infection, medications, stress
  • Features: hypothermia, bradycardia, hypoventilation, decreased consciousness
  • Treatment: IV levothyroxine + hydrocortisone (rule out concurrent adrenal insufficiency)
LO2: Hyperthyroidism — Causes, Features, Diagnosis ▼
SPEEDED everything is the key concept. Symptoms include:
  • Weight loss despite ↑ appetite — ↑ metabolism
  • Heat intolerance — always hot
  • Sweating, palpitations — sympathetic activation
  • Tachycardia, A-fib — risk of stroke
  • Tremor — fine, rapid
  • Anxiety, irritability, emotional lability
  • Lid lag, exophthalmos (Graves' only) — from retro-orbital GAG deposition, NOT from T4 level, persists after treatment
  • Diarrhea — ↑ GI motility
  • Proximal muscle weakness — thyrotoxic myopathy
  • Osteoporosis — ↑ bone turnover
  • Amenorrhea/oligomenorrhea
  • ↓ LDL cholesterol — ↑ LDL receptor expression
  • ↑ sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG)
Why does lid lag occur in Graves'?
Sympathetic overactivity + orbital tissue inflammation causing proptosis (exophthalmos). NOT purely from high T4 — persists after thyroid ablation.
Causes of Hyperthyroidism:
  • Graves' disease (60-80%, most common)
    • Autoimmune; TSI (thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulin) = IgG antibody that ACTIVATES TSH-R
    • Diffuse goiter, bruit from vascularity
    • Ophthalmopathy (exophthalmos) — retro-orbital GAG deposition
    • Pretibial myxedema (infiltrative dermopathy) — rare but pathognomonic
    • Acropachy (clubbing, bone remodeling) — rare
  • Toxic multinodular goiter (Plummer's): autonomous nodules; older patients; no autoimmune features
  • Toxic adenoma: single hot nodule; suppresses rest of thyroid
  • Subacute thyroiditis (De Quervain's): painful goiter post-viral; ↑ ESR; self-limited; low radioiodine uptake
  • Hashitoxicosis: transient hyper phase of Hashimoto's
  • Postpartum thyroiditis: within 1 year post-delivery; hyper → hypo → recovery
  • Factitious thyrotoxicosis: exogenous T4/T3; low thyroglobulin, low uptake
  • Struma ovarii: ectopic thyroid in ovarian teratoma; low neck uptake, uptake in pelvis
🎯 Key Lab Pattern: Graves' vs Thyroiditis
Graves'/Toxic MNG/Adenoma (autonomous synthesis):
↓ TSH, ↑ free T4, ↑ free T3, HIGH radioiodine uptake

Thyroiditis/Factitious/Struma (no synthesis, just release):
↓ TSH, ↑ free T4, ↑ free T3, LOW radioiodine uptake
Radioiodine uptake: High vs Low?
HIGH = active thyroid synthesis (Graves', toxic MNG, adenoma) | LOW = thyroid not taking up iodine (thyroiditis, factitious, struma ovarii)
⭐ High-Yield: Graves' Features
The GIFT: Goiter (diffuse) + Graves' eyes (exophthalmos) + Graves' skin (pretibial myxedema) = autoimmune thyroid disease
⚠️ Critical Trap
Graves' ophthalmopathy persists after successful treatment (thyroidectomy, radioiodine, antithyroid drugs) because it's caused by retro-orbital TSH receptor activation, not T4 levels. Treatment with glucocorticoids or radiation may be needed for eye symptoms.
LO3: Thyroid Cancers — Classification and Pathology ▼
Mnemonic: PTF-MA helps remember thyroid cancers: PTC (lymphatics), FTC (blood), MTC (calcitonin), Anaplastic (worst)
⭐ Papillary Thyroid Carcinoma (PTC)
Most common (~85% of thyroid cancers)
Spreads via LYMPHATICS → cervical lymph nodes
Excellent prognosis (5-year survival >95%)
"Cold nodule" on scan (doesn't take up radioiodine until ablated)
PTC Histology:
  • Papillary fronds — branching pattern
  • Orphan Annie eye nuclei — ground-glass, overlapping nuclei with grooves (pathognomonic)
  • Psamomma bodies — concentric calcifications that look like "dead" papillae
What are "Orphan Annie eye nuclei"?
Ground-glass appearance, oval or round nuclei with fine chromatin, nuclear grooves, and pseudoinclusions — pathognomonic finding in PTC
PTC Molecular & Risk:
  • BRAF V600E mutation (most common)
  • RET/PTC rearrangements
  • RAS mutations
  • Risk factors: radiation exposure (especially childhood); Chernobyl association
  • Spreads to lung ("snow storm" pattern on CXR) and bone via hematogenous routes
⭐ Follicular Thyroid Carcinoma (FTC)
~10% of thyroid cancers
Spreads via BLOOD → bone, lung (hematogenous)
Rarely to lymph nodes
⚠️ CRITICAL TRAP: FTC Diagnosis
Cytology CANNOT distinguish FTC from follicular adenoma. Requires CAPSULAR INVASION or VASCULAR INVASION on histology. Must excise to diagnose.
  • FTC Molecular: RAS mutations, PAX8-PPARγ rearrangement
  • Treatment: total thyroidectomy + radioiodine ablation
⭐ Medullary Thyroid Carcinoma (MTC)
~5% of thyroid cancers
From parafollicular C cells → secretes CALCITONIN (use as tumor marker)
  • Histology: amyloid deposits (calcitonin-derived; Congo red → apple-green birefringence), spindle/polygonal cells
  • Sporadic (70%) vs Hereditary (30%):
    • MEN2A: MTC + pheochromocytoma + hyperparathyroidism
    • MEN2B: MTC + pheochromocytoma + marfanoid habitus + mucosal neuromas
  • Molecular: RET proto-oncogene mutation (germline in hereditary, somatic in sporadic)
  • CRITICAL: Treat RET mutation carriers prophylactically with total thyroidectomy BEFORE cancer develops
  • Does NOT take up radioiodine (C cells lack NIS transporter)
⚠️ MTC vs Other Thyroid Cancers
MTC is the only thyroid cancer that does NOT respond to radioiodine therapy. Treatment is surgical resection. Calcitonin levels are used for surveillance.
🎯 Anaplastic (Undifferentiated) Thyroid Carcinoma
<2% but WORST prognosis (months median survival)
Usually elderly, rapidly growing mass, invasion of adjacent structures
Histology: bizarre giant cells, pleomorphism, necrosis
Does NOT take up radioiodine; TP53 mutations; may have BRAF V600E (evolution from PTC)
LO4: Parathyroid Pathology — Hyper and Hypoparathyroidism ▼
Primary Hyperparathyroidism (1° HPT): Most common cause of asymptomatic hypercalcemia in outpatients
  • Labs: ↑ PTH, ↑ Ca²⁺, ↓ phosphate, ↑ urine Ca²⁺, ↑ 1,25-OH vitamin D
  • Cause: Single parathyroid adenoma (>80%), chief cell hyperplasia (~15%), parathyroid carcinoma (1%)
  • Associations: MEN1, MEN2A
  • Symptoms: "Bones, Stones, Groans, Moans, Psychic Overtones"
    • Bones: osteitis fibrosa cystica (brown tumors) — lytic lesions
    • Stones: nephrolithiasis (calcium oxalate/phosphate)
    • Groans: GI (nausea, constipation, pancreatitis)
    • Moans: neuropsych (depression, confusion, anxiety)
    • Psychic Overtones: polyuria/polydipsia (hypercalcemia-induced nephrogenic DI)
  • Treatment: parathyroidectomy for symptomatic OR when Ca²⁺ >1 mg/dL above upper limit
What is osteitis fibrosa cystica?
Brown tumors — focal areas of bone resorption and fibrosis from excessive PTH activity in primary hyperparathyroidism. Appear as lytic lesions on X-ray.
🎯 Secondary Hyperparathyroidism
Compensatory ↑ PTH due to low calcium (usually CKD)
Mechanism: CKD → ↓ 1α-hydroxylase → ↓ 1,25-OH D → ↓ Ca²⁺ absorption + ↑ phosphate retention → ↑ PTH
Labs: ↑ PTH, ↓/normal Ca²⁺, ↑ phosphate
Treatment: phosphate binders, vitamin D, cinacalcet (calcimimetic)
⚠️ Secondary vs Tertiary HPT
In 2° HPT, calcium is LOW (TSH feedback works).
In 3° HPT, calcium is HIGH (autonomous secretion after kidney transplant).
Tertiary Hyperparathyroidism: Autonomous PTH after prolonged secondary HPT (e.g., post-kidney transplant)
  • Labs: ↑↑ PTH, ↑ Ca²⁺ (glands no longer respond to feedback)
  • Treatment: parathyroidectomy
⭐ Hypoparathyroidism
↓ PTH → ↓ Ca²⁺, ↑ phosphate, ↓ 1,25-OH D
  • Most common cause: iatrogenic (surgical removal during thyroidectomy)
  • Other causes:
    • Autoimmune (DiGeorge = 22q11.2 deletion → absent parathyroids + T cell deficiency + cardiac defects)
    • Hypomagnesemia (↓ Mg impairs PTH secretion AND action)
  • Symptoms of hypocalcemia:
    • Chvostek sign — facial twitch with tapping on cheek (CN VII)
    • Trousseau sign — carpal spasm with BP cuff inflation
    • Tetany, seizures, QT prolongation
    • Perioral/fingertip tingling (paresthesias)
  • Treatment: calcium + active vitamin D (calcitriol); recombinant PTH (natpara) for chronic
Chvostek vs Trousseau sign?
Chvostek = tap facial nerve → twitch. Trousseau = inflate BP cuff → carpal spasm. Both indicate hypocalcemia.
⚠️ Pseudohypoparathyroidism (PHP)
PTH levels are NORMAL or HIGH but target organs don't respond (resistance).
Labs: ↓ Ca²⁺, ↑ phosphate, ↑↑ PTH
Features (type 1a): short stature, round face, short 4th metacarpals, obesity, cognitive impairment (Albright hereditary osteodystrophy)
Mnemonic: PHP = Plenty of PTH but Problem with Peripheral response
LO5: Calcium Regulation — PTH, Vitamin D, Calcitonin (Physiology) ▼
Normal serum Ca²⁺: 8.5-10.5 mg/dL; ionized Ca²⁺ 4.5-5.0 mg/dL
⭐ PTH (Parathyroid Hormone)
84-amino acid peptide; active fragment = first 34 AAs
Released when ionized Ca²⁺ FALLS (sensed by CaSR = calcium-sensing receptor)
PTH Actions (↑ Ca²⁺, ↓ phosphate):
  • Bone: ↑ osteoclast activity (via RANKL on osteoblasts) → ↑ Ca + phosphate release
    • NOTE: Intermittent PTH → anabolic (stimulates osteoblasts) — basis for teriparatide (osteoporosis treatment)
  • Kidney:
    • ↑ Ca²⁺ reabsorption in distal tubule
    • ↓ phosphate reabsorption in proximal tubule = phosphaturia
    • ↑ 1α-hydroxylase → ↑ 1,25-OH D (active vitamin D)
  • GI: Indirect (via ↑ 1,25-OH D) → ↑ Ca + phosphate absorption
Why is INTERMITTENT PTH anabolic for bone?
Continuous PTH → osteoclasts (bone loss). Intermittent PTH → preferentially stimulates osteoblasts (bone formation). This is why teriparatide treats osteoporosis.
⭐ Vitamin D Metabolism
D3 (cholecalciferol) from skin (UV B) or diet → liver (25-hydroxylation) → 25-OH D (storage form) → kidney (1α-hydroxylation, stimulated by PTH/low phosphate) → 1,25-OH D (active = calcitriol)
1,25-OH D Actions:
  • ↑ Ca + phosphate absorption in GI (MAIN action)
  • ↑ Ca + phosphate reabsorption in kidney
  • At bone: supports mineralization
  • Immunomodulatory effects
Vitamin D Deficiency:
  • Rickets (children): soft bones, bow legs, rachitic rosary (beading of costochondral junctions), craniotabes (softening of skull)
  • Osteomalacia (adults): bone pain, ↑ alkaline phosphatase, looser zones on X-ray
  • Labs: ↓ Ca²⁺, ↑ PTH (secondary HPT), ↓ phosphate initially
🎯 Calcitonin
From C cells; released when Ca²⁺ RISES
Actions: ↓ osteoclast activity → ↓ Ca²⁺ and phosphate; ↑ renal Ca²⁺ and phosphate excretion
Minor role in humans (calcitonin-deficient patients have normal Ca regulation)
Clinical use: salmon calcitonin for Paget's disease, osteoporosis, acute hypercalcemia
PTH mnemonic: "PTH = Phosphate Trashed Here" (↓ phosphate); "Vitamin D = Duo absorption" (Ca + phosphate ↑ in GI)
Hypercalcemia Causes:
CHIMPANZEES mnemonic for hypercalcemia
Cancer, Hyperparathyroidism, Intoxication (Vit D/A), Milk-alkali, Paget's, Addison's, Neoplasm, Zollinger-Ellison, Excessive antacids, Endocrine (hyperthyroid/acromegaly), Sarcoidosis
LO6: Goiter and Thyroid Nodules ▼
Goiter: Enlarged thyroid; can be diffuse or nodular; associated with hyper-, hypo-, or euthyroidism
🎯 Types of Goiter
Simple/Diffuse: Iodine deficiency (most common worldwide) → ↑ TSH → thyroid hypertrophy; also goitrogens (cassava, soy, brassica)

Multinodular: Years of TSH stimulation → heterogeneous growth; some nodules become autonomous (toxic MNG)

Graves': Diffuse, smooth goiter with bruit

Hashimoto's: Firm, irregular → eventual atrophy

Riedel's: Woody-hard fibrous
Thyroid Nodules: Very common (50-70% on ultrasound); >95% benign
Evaluation approach: TSH first or ultrasound first?
TSH FIRST. If suppressed (indicating hot nodule on scan), no biopsy needed (hot nodules are rarely malignant). If normal/elevated, then ultrasound + FNA if indicated.
Bethesda Classification of FNA Cytology:
  • I: Non-diagnostic
  • II: Benign
  • III: Atypia of undetermined significance (AUS)
  • IV: Follicular neoplasm (concern for FTC)
  • V: Suspicious for malignancy
  • VI: Malignant
⚠️ Nodule Risk Stratification
Concerning for malignancy:
• Single, hard, fixed nodule
• Lymphadenopathy
• Hoarseness (RLN invasion)
• Rapid growth
• Male sex
• Prior radiation
• Age <20 or >70
• Family history of MEN2/PTC
⭐ Hot vs Cold Nodules
HOT nodule: Takes up iodine, usually benign (autonomous, functional) — suppresses rest of thyroid

COLD nodule: Doesn't take up iodine, more likely malignant (but still <5% of cold nodules are cancer)
COLD nodule = potentially COLD cancer; HOT nodule = HOT mess but not cancer

Mind Map: Thyroid & Parathyroid Pathology

USMLE-Style MCQs

1. A 52-year-old woman presents with fatigue, weight gain, and cold intolerance. Labs show TSH 8.2 mIU/L (normal 0.5-4) and free T4 0.6 ng/dL (normal 0.8-1.8). Anti-TPO antibody is positive. What is the PRIMARY mechanism of this patient's thyroid dysfunction?
  • A) Destruction of thyroid follicles by CD8+ T cells and antibodies
  • B) TSH receptor blockade by inhibitory antibodies
  • C) Thyroid hormone sequestration by circulating antibodies
  • D) Decreased hepatic production of T4-binding protein
  • E) Iodine deficiency in the diet
Correct Answer: A
The elevated TSH and decreased free T4 with positive anti-TPO antibodies indicate Hashimoto's thyroiditis. This is an autoimmune destruction of thyroid follicles by lymphocytes and antibodies against TPO and thyroglobulin. B is wrong (TSH-R blocking occurs in atrophic hypothyroidism, not typically with anti-TPO+). C, D, E are mechanism mismatches.
2. A 35-year-old man with Graves' disease achieves euthyroidism after radioiodine ablation. However, he continues to have exophthalmos (eye protrusion) and lid lag. What is the BEST explanation for the persistence of these ocular findings?
  • A) Residual thyroid hormone elevation from incomplete radioiodine ablation
  • B) TSH receptor activation in orbital fibroblasts by TSI (thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulin)
  • C) Thyroid hormone-independent sympathetic overactivity
  • D) Contracture of extraocular muscles from previous thyroid hormone excess
  • E) Calcification of the levator palpebrae superioris muscle
Correct Answer: B
Graves' ophthalmopathy is caused by TSI binding to TSH receptors on orbital fibroblasts, causing GAG deposition and inflammation. This persists AFTER successful thyroid ablation because TSI is still present and orbital tissue is still targeted. A is wrong (patient is euthyroid post-ablation). C and D describe mechanisms that don't explain the pathology. E is incorrect.
3. A 58-year-old man with history of exposure to nuclear radiation in childhood presents with a 2 cm thyroid nodule. Fine needle aspiration (FNA) cytology shows follicular cells with ground-glass nuclei and nuclear grooves. What is the MOST likely diagnosis?
  • A) Follicular thyroid carcinoma
  • B) Papillary thyroid carcinoma
  • C) Medullary thyroid carcinoma
  • D) Anaplastic thyroid carcinoma
  • E) Thyroid lymphoma
Correct Answer: B
"Ground-glass nuclei" = Orphan Annie eye nuclei, pathognomonic for PTC. Nuclear grooves are also characteristic. PTC is the most common thyroid cancer and is associated with childhood radiation exposure. FTC cannot be diagnosed by cytology alone (requires invasion). MTC shows amyloid/calcitonin, not Orphan Annie nuclei.
4. A 45-year-old woman presenting with palpitations and weight loss is found to have TSH 0.1 mIU/L and free T4 2.5 ng/dL (normal). Radioiodine uptake scan shows diffuse, high uptake throughout the thyroid. What is the BEST next diagnostic step?
  • A) Measure thyroglobulin level
  • B) Check TSI (TSH receptor antibody)
  • C) Perform thyroid ultrasound
  • D) Obtain CT chest for lung metastases
  • E) Check anti-TPO antibodies
Correct Answer: B
The high diffuse uptake indicates autonomous thyroid synthesis consistent with Graves' disease or toxic multinodular goiter. TSI (TSH receptor antibodies) will distinguish Graves' (TSI+) from toxic MNG (TSI-). Thyroglobulin would be elevated in Graves' due to active synthesis, but TSI is more specific. Anti-TPO is part of Hashimoto's, not hyperthyroidism evaluation.
5. A 28-year-old woman with acute-onset neck pain, fever, and mild hyperthyroidism has labs showing TSH 0.2 (low), free T4 2.8 (elevated), very elevated CRP and ESR. Radioiodine uptake scan shows LOW uptake. Which diagnosis BEST fits this presentation?
  • A) Graves' disease
  • B) Factitious thyrotoxicosis
  • C) Subacute (De Quervain's) thyroiditis
  • D) Toxic adenoma
  • E) Postpartum thyroiditis
Correct Answer: C
Subacute thyroiditis classically presents with neck pain, fever, elevated inflammatory markers (↑ ESR/CRP), transient hyperthyroidism (from thyroid hormone release, NOT synthesis), and LOW radioiodine uptake (because follicles are destroyed and not taking up iodine). A would have HIGH uptake. B has low thyroglobulin. D would have normal inflammatory markers. E is possible but less acute presentation.
6. A 52-year-old man undergoes fine needle aspiration of a thyroid nodule. Cytology shows follicular cells WITHOUT capsular or vascular invasion. The pathologist reports this as "suspicious for follicular neoplasm." Why cannot the diagnosis of follicular carcinoma be made at this time?
  • A) Follicular adenoma and follicular carcinoma cannot be distinguished by cytology alone; invasion must be confirmed on histology
  • B) The patient's TSH level is normal, so malignancy is excluded
  • C) FNA cannot sample deep portions of the nodule where invasion occurs
  • D) Medullary carcinoma must be ruled out first by calcitonin staining
  • E) Molecular testing for BRAF V600E mutation is required first
Correct Answer: A
This is a critical concept: FTC diagnosis REQUIRES either capsular or vascular invasion on histology. FNA cytology showing follicular cells alone CANNOT distinguish FTC from adenoma. The nodule must be excised (thyroidectomy or lobectomy) to assess for invasion. B, C, D, E are distractors or incorrect mechanisms.
7. A 42-year-old man is found to have elevated serum calcitonin (450 pg/mL, normal <10) and a thyroid nodule. Genetic testing identifies a RET proto-oncogene mutation. Which of the following is MOST critical in this patient's management?
  • A) Radioiodine ablation of the thyroid nodule
  • B) Prophylactic total thyroidectomy before cancer develops
  • C) Chemotherapy with tyrosine kinase inhibitors
  • D) Observation with calcitonin surveillance
  • E) TSH suppression with high-dose levothyroxine
Correct Answer: B
RET mutations are associated with hereditary MTC (MEN2A/2B). Carriers with RET mutations MUST undergo prophylactic total thyroidectomy BEFORE cancer develops. This is curative in early disease. A is wrong (MTC doesn't take up radioiodine). C and E are wrong (not prophylactic). D delays necessary surgery.
8. A 68-year-old woman presents to the ER with confusion, lethargy, and hypothermia (core temp 32°C). Labs show TSH 28 mIU/L and free T4 0.4 ng/dL. She has a history of hypothyroidism treated with levothyroxine. What is the MOST appropriate INITIAL treatment?
  • A) Oral levothyroxine 100 mcg daily
  • B) IV levothyroxine + hydrocortisone
  • C) Warming measures only until stable, then levothyroxine
  • D) Liothyronine (T3) IV given faster onset
  • E) Supportive care only; levothyroxine will be absorbed poorly in shock
Correct Answer: B
This is myxedema coma — a medical emergency. IV levothyroxine plus hydrocortisone (to rule out concurrent adrenal insufficiency) is standard initial management. A uses oral route (too slow). C delays treatment. D may be used adjunctively but levothyroxine is standard. E is dangerous (delays necessary therapy).
9. A 58-year-old man is found to have serum calcium 10.8 mg/dL (normal 8.5-10.5), PTH 75 pg/mL (normal 15-65), phosphate 2.8 mg/dL (normal 2.5-4.5), and 1,25-OH vitamin D 65 pg/mL (normal 19-67). What is the BEST diagnosis?
  • A) Secondary hyperparathyroidism from chronic kidney disease
  • B) Primary hyperparathyroidism
  • C) Tertiary hyperparathyroidism
  • D) Vitamin D intoxication
  • E) Pseudohypoparathyroidism
Correct Answer: B
Primary HPT has ↑ PTH AND ↑ calcium (organs responding to PTH). The elevated 1,25-OH D is because PTH stimulates 1α-hydroxylase. A would have LOW-normal calcium (compensatory HPT in CKD). C would follow secondary HPT (post-transplant). D would suppress PTH. E would have HIGH PTH but LOW calcium (resistance).
10. A 35-year-old woman with chronic kidney disease stage 4 has labs: serum calcium 7.9 mg/dL (low), phosphate 5.2 mg/dL (high), PTH 420 pg/mL (very high), 1,25-OH vitamin D 12 pg/mL (low). Which pathophysiologic mechanism is PRIMARY in her hyperparathyroidism?
  • A) Parathyroid adenoma autonomous secretion
  • B) Decreased renal 1α-hydroxylase activity leading to low 1,25-OH D and hypocalcemia stimulating PTH
  • C) Phosphate retention stimulating direct PTH secretion
  • D) Tertiary HPT from previous parathyroidectomy
  • E) Sarcoidosis causing macrophage 1α-hydroxylase activity
Correct Answer: B
Secondary HPT in CKD: renal dysfunction → loss of 1α-hydroxylase → ↓ 1,25-OH D → ↓ intestinal Ca absorption → ↓ serum Ca²⁺ → ↑ PTH (compensatory). High phosphate is also a stimulus but low calcium and low 1,25-OH D are the PRIMARY drivers. A would be primary HPT (normal kidney function). C is partially true but not primary. D requires prior parathyroidectomy. E is sarcoidosis mechanism.
11. A 48-year-old woman presents with perioral numbness, paresthesias, and a positive Chvostek sign. Labs show serum calcium 6.8 mg/dL (low), phosphate 5.4 mg/dL (high), PTH 12 pg/mL (low), and 1,25-OH vitamin D 8 pg/mL (low). She reports undergoing thyroid surgery 6 months ago. What is the MOST likely diagnosis?
  • A) Vitamin D deficiency rickets
  • B) Hypoparathyroidism from parathyroid damage during surgery
  • C) Acute kidney injury
  • D) Pseudohypoparathyroidism
  • E) Secondary hyperparathyroidism with paradoxical hypocalcemia
Correct Answer: B
The low calcium, high phosphate, LOW PTH, and LOW 1,25-OH D following thyroid surgery indicate iatrogenic hypoparathyroidism (inadvertent parathyroid gland removal/damage). The positive Chvostek sign confirms hypocalcemia. A would have ↑ PTH (compensatory). C is possible but less likely with normal creatinine assumed. D would have HIGH PTH. E is contradiction.
12. A 42-year-old man with a family history of short stature and developmental delay presents with serum calcium 7.5 mg/dL (low), phosphate 5.8 mg/dL (high), and PTH 95 pg/mL (HIGH, not low). Physical exam reveals short 4th metacarpals and a round face. What is the MOST likely diagnosis?
  • A) Hypoparathyroidism with secondary hyperparathyroidism
  • B) Pseudohypoparathyroidism type 1a
  • C) Primary hyperparathyroidism
  • D) DiGeorge syndrome
  • E) Vitamin D dependent rickets type 1
Correct Answer: B
PHP type 1a: PTH receptor signaling defect (GNAS mutation) → target organs don't respond to PTH → LOW calcium, HIGH phosphate, HIGH PTH (compensation). Features: short stature, round face, short 4th metacarpals, cognitive impairment (Albright hereditary osteodystrophy). A is wrong (true hypo would have LOW PTH). C would have HIGH calcium. D has absent parathyroids (LOW PTH). E would typically show other features.
13. A 64-year-old woman is admitted with serum calcium 13.2 mg/dL, PTH 8 pg/mL (suppressed), and 1,25-OH vitamin D 35 pg/mL (normal). Imaging reveals multiple bone lytic lesions. Which is the MOST likely cause of her hypercalcemia?
  • A) Hyperparathyroidism
  • B) Granulomatous disease (sarcoidosis) with excessive 1α-hydroxylase
  • C) Malignancy-related hypercalcemia (PTHrP secretion)
  • D) Vitamin D intoxication
  • E) Hyperthyroidism with increased bone turnover
Correct Answer: C
Suppressed PTH + hypercalcemia + bone lesions = malignancy-related hypercalcemia. PTHrP (PTH-related peptide) secreted by tumor cells mimics PTH effects (hypercalcemia, bone resorption, phosphate wasting) but suppresses normal PTH secretion. A would have elevated PTH. B would have elevated 1,25-OH D. D would have suppressed PTH but usually no lytic lesions. E would show other thyroid signs.
14. A 52-year-old man with end-stage renal disease on hemodialysis has been treated for secondary hyperparathyroidism for 3 years. He now undergoes successful kidney transplant and becomes normocalcemic with normal renal function. However, after 6 months, he develops hypercalcemia (11.2 mg/dL) with PTH persistently elevated at 145 pg/mL despite normal serum phosphate. What is the BEST diagnosis?
  • A) Recurrent secondary hyperparathyroidism from transplant rejection
  • B) Tertiary hyperparathyroidism with autonomous PTH secretion
  • C) Primary hyperparathyroidism (de novo)
  • D) Vitamin D intoxication from immunosuppressant metabolism
  • E) Posttransplant lymphoproliferative disorder with PTHrP secretion
Correct Answer: B
Tertiary HPT: after prolonged secondary HPT, parathyroid glands become autonomous. When kidney function is restored (transplant), PTH feedback no longer works, resulting in HIGH PTH + HIGH calcium. This is the classic progression. A would have renal dysfunction. C would not explain the prior secondary HPT. D and E are less likely mechanistically.
15. A 45-year-old woman with vitamin D deficiency presents with bone pain, myalgias, and lab abnormalities. Which of the following is a PRIMARY consequence of her vitamin D deficiency?
  • A) Hypercalcemia from unchecked vitamin D-independent calcium absorption
  • B) Secondary hyperparathyroidism from decreased intestinal calcium absorption
  • C) Primary hypoparathyroidism from lack of vitamin D effect on PTH secretion
  • D) Hypophosphatemia from vitamin D-independent phosphate wasting
  • E) Thyroid hormone resistance from vitamin D receptor defects
Correct Answer: B
Vitamin D is essential for intestinal Ca²⁺ absorption. Deficiency → ↓ Ca absorption → hypocalcemia → secondary ↑ PTH (compensatory). This leads to osteomalacia (adults) or rickets (children). A is wrong (Ca would be low, not high). C is wrong (PTH is elevated, not depressed). D is opposite (phosphate retention). E is unrelated.
16. A 28-year-old woman with anxiety and weight loss is found to have a solitary 1.5 cm thyroid nodule on ultrasound with "cold" appearance on radioiodine uptake scan. TSH is suppressed at 0.1 mIU/L. Which of the following is the MOST appropriate next step?
  • A) Observation with repeat ultrasound in 6 months
  • B) Fine needle aspiration (FNA) cytology
  • C) Immediate total thyroidectomy based on cold nodule
  • D) Radioiodine ablation therapy
  • E) TSH suppression therapy with levothyroxine
Correct Answer: B
Cold nodules have higher cancer risk (~5%) but >95% are benign. FNA is indicated for cytologic evaluation (Bethesda classification). A delays diagnosis. C is premature without cytology. D is not appropriate for cold nodules. E may be added after diagnosis based on findings.
17. A 65-year-old man has been treated with amiodarone for atrial fibrillation for 4 years. He now presents with fatigue and weight gain. Labs show TSH 6.8 mIU/L, free T4 0.5 ng/dL (low), and normal free T3. Thyroid ultrasound shows diffuse gland enlargement. What is the MECHANISM of his hypothyroidism?
  • A) Autoimmune destruction of thyroid follicles (drug-induced Hashimoto's)
  • B) Direct toxic effects of amiodarone on thyroid follicular cells
  • C) Iodine overload from amiodarone (high iodine content) triggering the Wolff-Chaikoff effect
  • D) Impaired conversion of T4 to T3 from amiodarone inhibition of deiodinase
  • E) Central hypothyroidism from amiodarone effects on the pituitary
Correct Answer: C
Amiodarone is 37% iodine by weight. High iodine → Wolff-Chaikoff effect (initial inhibition of thyroid hormone synthesis) → hypothyroidism in susceptible patients. The normal free T3 argues against impaired conversion (D). A and B don't explain the acute onset with drug exposure. E would show suppressed TSH (central hypo).
18. A 34-year-old woman at 6 weeks postpartum presents with palpitations, anxiety, and heat intolerance. Labs show TSH 0.05, free T4 2.8 (elevated), and free T3 elevated. Radioiodine uptake scan shows LOW uptake. Anti-TPO antibodies are positive. What is the MOST likely diagnosis?
  • A) Graves' disease (postpartum flare)
  • B) Postpartum thyroiditis (thyrotoxic phase)
  • C) Factitious thyrotoxicosis from thyroid hormone ingestion
  • D) TSI-positive autoimmune hyperthyroidism
  • E) Toxic nodular goiter presenting postpartum
Correct Answer: B
Postpartum thyroiditis is an autoimmune condition that occurs within 1 year postpartum. Phase 1 is thyrotoxic (↑ T4, LOW uptake) from thyroid hormone release, NOT synthesis. Anti-TPO+ supports autoimmunity. The low uptake distinguishes this from Graves' (which would have HIGH uptake). Low uptake also argues against C (factitious wouldn't have anti-TPO+).

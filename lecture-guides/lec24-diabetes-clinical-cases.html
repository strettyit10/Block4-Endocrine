LEC 24: New-Onset Type 1 Diabetes

CASE 1 — 16-Year-Old with DKA

Polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss × 2 weeks. Glucose 380, HbA1c 11.2%, large ketones, pH 7.32, HCO3 18, AG 14

Clinical Presentation & Diagnosis ▼

Symptoms

Polyuria (osmotic, glucose >180), polydipsia (thirst), weight loss (4–6 kg), fatigue, nausea. Rapid onset over 1–2 weeks suggests autoimmune destruction.

DKA Confirmation

ParameterPatientDKA Criterion
pH7.32<7.30 mild; <7.25 moderate; <7.15 severe
HCO318<18 mild; <15 moderate; <10 severe
AG14>12 elevated
KetonesLargePositive (serum β-hydroxybutyrate preferred)

Diagnosis: Mild DKA (pH 7.32, HCO3 18, AG 14, large ketones).

T1DM Confirmation

  • C-peptide: Undetectable (pancreatic β-cell destruction)
  • GAD65 antibody: Positive (most common autoantigen)
  • IA-2 antibody: Positive
  • HLA typing: DR3/DR4 (highest risk genotype)
Management Protocol ▼

Fluid Resuscitation

  • Initial: 0.9% NS bolus 10–20 mL/kg IV (e.g., 800–1600 mL over 1 hour)
  • Goal: Hemodynamic stabilization, urine output ≥0.5 mL/kg/hr
  • Switch: After initial bolus, 0.45% NaCl at 250–500 mL/hr (slower correction prevents cerebral edema)

Electrolyte Management

CRITICAL: Check serum K+ BEFORE insulin!

  • If K+ <3.5 mEq/L: HOLD insulin, give K+ 20–40 mEq IV (diluted)
  • If K+ 3.5–5.5: Can start insulin (monitor K+ q1–2h)
  • If K+ >5.5: Monitor, may need insulin + D5W to shift K+ intracellularly

Phosphate & magnesium: Also deplete with acidosis; replace if symptomatic.

Insulin Therapy

  • Dose: IV regular insulin 0.1 U/kg/hr (e.g., 8–10 U/hr for 80–100 kg patient)
  • Target: Glucose drop 50–75 mg/dL/hr
  • Dextrose threshold: Add D5W when glucose <250 mg/dL
  • CRITICAL: Continue insulin until anion gap closes (AG <12), even if glucose normalizes. Ketosis resolves slower than hyperglycemia.
  • Transition: When eating and gap closed → basal-bolus insulin (e.g., glargine + lispro)

Monitoring

  • Glucose, VBG, electrolytes q1–2h initially
  • Phosphate, Mg, Ca q4–6h
  • Urine ketones q4h until negative
Patient Education & Counseling ▼

Honeymoon Period

After initial DKA treatment, partial remission may occur (insulin requirements drop 20–50% over weeks). CRITICAL: Educate that this is temporary due to β-cell recovery, NOT cure. Continue insulin—do NOT stop.

Self-Management

  • Carb counting: Teach ratio (e.g., 1 U per 10–15 g carbs)
  • SMBG: 4× daily (fasting, before meals, bedtime, 3 AM spot check initially)
  • Glucagon kit: Prescribe; teach family how to use (hypoglycemia seizure/unconsciousness)
  • Sick day rules: NEVER stop insulin, increase monitoring, drink sugar-free fluids, check ketones if glucose >240

Associated Autoimmune Screening

  • Thyroid: Anti-TPO, TSH (10–30% autoimmune thyroiditis)
  • Celiac: TTG-IgA, total IgA (3–5% celiac disease)
  • Addison: 21-OH antibody (rare, <1%)

Annual Monitoring

  • HbA1c: Target <7%; check q3 months initially, then q6 months
  • Microalbumin/Cr ratio: Baseline, then annually after 5 years duration
  • Dilated eye exam: Baseline (rule out background retinopathy), then annually
  • Foot exam: Annual monofilament + vibration testing
  • Lipids: Baseline, then q5 years if normal (after age 10 with >5 yr T1DM: annual)
TEST INSIGHT: Always confirm DKA (AG >12, pH <7.30, HCO3 <18, ketones) AND T1DM (C-peptide undetectable, ≥2 autoantibodies, HLA-DR3/4). DKA severity based on pH, not glucose level.
TRAP: Starting insulin before checking K+ can cause fatal hyperkalemia shift. Always check K+ first. If <3.5, HOLD insulin and replicate K+.
HYPER-FOCUS: Honeymoon period is temporary. Educate families that insulin must continue despite remission. This misunderstanding leads to DKA relapse.
MEMORY ANCHOR: "DKA triad = Ketones + Acidosis + Insulin Lack." For T1DM: "3 C's = C-peptide undetectable, Cluster of antibodies, Crash presentation."
When do you add D5W in DKA insulin drip?
At glucose <250 mg/dL. Continue insulin until AG closed (usually 12–24 hrs), not when glucose normalizes.

LEC 24: Type 2 Diabetes with CVD & CKD

CASE 2 — 58F with T2DM, CAD, CKD Stage 3a

T2DM 8 years, HbA1c 8.4% on metformin, recent MI, eGFR 47, BMI 34

Baseline Assessment ▼

Current Therapy & Limits

  • Metformin: Safe at eGFR 47 (threshold 30 mL/min/1.73m²; absolute CI <30)
  • HbA1c 8.4%: Above goal; second agent needed

Comorbidities Guiding Drug Choice

ConditionPreferred AgentMechanism
ASCVD (CAD + MI)GLP-1 RA or SGLT2iCV mortality reduction
CKD Stage 3aSGLT2iRenoprotective (eGFR >30 effective)
Obesity (BMI 34)GLP-1 RAWeight loss 3–5 kg, improves CV risk

Evidence Base

  • EMPA-REG OUTCOME (SGLT2i): ↓ CV death 38%, ↓ HF hospitalization 35%
  • LEADER (GLP-1 RA liraglutide): ↓ MACE 13%, ↓ CV death 22%
  • DAPA-HF (SGLT2i dapagliflozin): Benefits in HFrEF regardless of DM status
Drug Selection & Monitoring ▼

Recommended Choice: Empagliflozin (SGLT2i)

Why: Addresses all three issues (CVD, CKD, glycemia) in one agent.

SGLT2i Mechanism & Benefits

  • Mechanism: Blocks glucose reabsorption in proximal tubule → urinary glucose loss
  • Glycemic: ↓ HbA1c 0.5–1%
  • CV: ↓ CV death, HF hospitalization (especially dapagliflozin, empagliflozin, canagliflozin)
  • Renal: ↓ eGFR decline, proteinuria reduction (effective down to eGFR ~30, some benefit at 20–30)
  • Weight: ↓ 2–3 kg (less than GLP-1 but additive)
  • BP: ↓ 3–5 mmHg

SGLT2i Adverse Effects

Genital mycotic infection: 8–13% (educate on hygiene, risk factors). Assess baseline; counsel on symptoms (itching, discharge).

Euglycemic DKA: Rare but serious. Risk factors: major surgery, acute illness, SGLT2i use. Present with normal glucose (<200) but elevated AG + ketones + altered mental status.

  • Prevention: Hold SGLT2i 3–5 days before elective surgery, during acute illness
  • Recognition: If patient on SGLT2i + AMS + AG elevated but glucose <200 → check β-hydroxybutyrate immediately

Dosing & Monitoring

  • Empagliflozin: 10 mg daily (max 25 mg/day; higher doses no additional glycemic benefit)
  • Baseline labs: eGFR, urine ACR, lipid panel
  • Ongoing: eGFR q6–12 months, urine ACR annually, BP target <130/80 on RAAS inhibitor
Comprehensive CVD/CKD Management ▼

RAAS Inhibition

ACE-I or ARB is foundational for CKD + proteinuria + ASCVD. If on ACE-I, monitor K+ and Cr after initiation.

Lipid Management

High-intensity statin required: ASCVD diagnosis mandates atorvastatin 40–80 mg or rosuvastatin 20–40 mg daily.

  • Goal LDL: <70 mg/dL (ASCVD)
  • Consider ezetimibe or PCSK9i: If LDL not at goal on high-intensity statin

BP Control

  • Goal: <130/80 mmHg (ADA/ACC 2017)
  • First-line: RAAS inhibitor + diuretic ± CCB
  • Avoid: Thiazides (hyperglycemia, hypokalemia); α-blockers (HF risk)

HbA1c Target

  • General goal: <7% (reduces microvascular complications)
  • With CKD/CVD: 7–8% acceptable (hypoglycemia risk, limited life expectancy)
  • Avoid intensive glycemic control: No mortality benefit, ↑ hypoglycemia

Add-On Therapy If Needed

If HbA1c remains >8% on metformin + SGLT2i, consider:

  • GLP-1 RA: Additional weight loss, CV benefit, MACE reduction
  • Finerenone (FIDELITY): Non-steroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist; CKD progression ↓
  • Avoid: DPP-4i (no CV benefit), sulfonylureas (hypoglycemia, weight gain)
TEST INSIGHT: In T2DM + ASCVD + CKD, SGLT2i and GLP-1 RA are preferred for CV and renal benefits. Metformin safe down to eGFR 30.
TRAP: Don't use DPP-4i or sulfonylureas for CV risk reduction—they have no mortality benefit. SGLT2i and GLP-1 RA are evidence-based.
HYPER-FOCUS: Euglycemic DKA with SGLT2i: glucose <200 but HIGH AG + ketones. Hold SGLT2i before surgery; restart after oral intake/stable illness.
MEMORY ANCHOR: "SGLT2i = CV Kidney Win." GLP-1 = "Weight & Macrovascular." Both trump older agents in ASCVD/CKD context.
Euglycemic DKA: What glucose range triggers suspicion?
<200 mg/dL with elevated AG + positive ketones + patient on SGLT2i. Normal glucose can mask severe metabolic acidosis.

LEC 24: Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS)

CASE 3 — 78M Unresponsive, Glucose 980

Nursing home resident. BP 88/60, Na 152, HCO3 22, pH 7.38, osm 378, NO ketones

Diagnosis & Differential ▼

HHS Diagnostic Criteria

ParameterHHS CriterionThis Patient
Glucose>600 mg/dL980 ✓
Osmolality>320 mOsm/kg378 ✓
pH>7.30 (minimal acidosis)7.38 ✓
HCO3>18 (minimal acidosis)22 ✓
KetonesSmall/negativeNegative ✓
AGNormal or lowNot elevated ✓

Diagnosis: CONFIRMED HHS

Why No DKA?

T2DM patients retain enough endogenous insulin (insulins can't reach zero in T2DM) to suppress ketogenesis, even though glucose isn't controlled. DKA requires absolute or near-absolute insulin deficiency (T1DM). The residual insulin is insufficient for glucose uptake but enough to prevent lipolysis and ketogenesis.

Precipitating Factors

  • Infection: UTI, pneumonia (most common)
  • Acute illness: Stroke, MI, sepsis
  • Medications: Steroids (hyperglycemia), thiazides (volume loss)
  • Non-compliance: Stopped DM medications
  • Dehydration: Inadequate fluid intake (elderly, limited access)
Management Protocol ▼

Fluid Resuscitation (PRIMARY TREATMENT)

  • Initial: 0.9% NS 1–1.5 L IV over 1 hour (hemodynamic stabilization, Na repletion)
  • Rationale: Severe hypovolemia (often 8–10 L deficit); restore circulating volume first
  • Monitor: Urine output goal >0.5 mL/kg/hr
  • Switch to 0.45% NaCl: After initial bolus, if serum Na >155 (correct free water deficit slowly)
  • Add D5W: When glucose <300 mg/dL to prevent hypoglycemia (don't stop fluid replacement)
  • Avoid rapid osmolality correction: Risk of cerebral edema. Target 3–8 mOsm/kg/hr decrease

Insulin Therapy (SECONDARY)

  • Dose: IV regular insulin 0.05–0.1 U/kg/hr (lower than DKA, e.g., 4–8 U/hr)
  • Rationale: Insulin is less critical in HHS (fluids are primary); aggressive insulin causes hypoglycemia
  • Glucose correction rate: 50–75 mg/dL/hr (avoid rapid drops)
  • Target: Normalize osmolality, not just glucose

Electrolyte Management

  • K+: Often total body depleted (osmotic diuresis); check before insulin. Fluid alone may increase K+ initially.
  • Phosphate & Mg: Also depleted; may need supplementation
  • Na: Correct slowly (3–8 mEq/L decrease per 24 hr) to avoid cerebral edema

Identify & Treat Precipitant

  • Obtain blood cultures, urinalysis, CXR (infection)
  • EKG (MI)
  • Imaging if altered mental status persistent (stroke)
Prognosis & Complications ▼

Mortality

HHS mortality ~15% (vs. DKA 1–2%)

Why higher? Elderly population, delayed presentation (can be insidious), CNS complications (thrombosis, cerebral edema), underlying acute illness (infection, MI).

Common Complications

  • Cerebral edema: From rapid osmolality correction; present with altered mental status, seizure, coma
  • Thromboembolism: Hypercoagulability from hemoconcentration; VTE, CVA
  • Acute renal injury: Pre-renal from dehydration
  • Rhabdomyolysis: Rare; muscle damage from osmotic stress

ICU Monitoring

  • Glucose, electrolytes q1–2h initially
  • Osmolality q4–6h
  • Neurologic checks (mental status)
  • Urine output, vital signs
TEST INSIGHT: HHS = glucose >600 + osmolality >320 + NO acidosis/minimal ketones. Fluids are primary treatment; insulin secondary. Mortality ~15%.
TRAP: Using aggressive insulin in HHS (like DKA protocol) causes hypoglycemia and cerebral edema. Use LOW-dose insulin + gentle fluid correction.
HYPER-FOCUS: Rapid osmolality correction risks cerebral edema. Target 3–8 mOsm/kg/hr decrease. Slow correction = slower recovery but safer.
MEMORY ANCHOR: "HHS = Huge glucose, Huge osmolality, Hypo-acidosis (no acidosis)." "Fluids FIRST, insulin gentle."
Why no DKA in T2DM-induced HHS?
T2DM retains residual endogenous insulin—enough to suppress ketogenesis but NOT enough to lower glucose. DKA needs absolute insulin deficiency (T1DM).

LEC 24: Gestational Diabetes Mellitus

CASE 4 — 32F at 26 Weeks Gestation

BMI 29, 1-hr 50g GCT 168 (positive). 3-hr 100g OGTT: fasting 96 / 1hr 192 / 2hr 165 / 3hr 155

Screening & Diagnosis ▼

Two-Step Screening (Traditional USA)

Step 1: 1-hr 50g glucose challenge test (GCT) at 24–28 weeks (non-fasting, no prior diet restriction).

  • Threshold ≥140 mg/dL → ~80% sensitivity; ≥135 mg/dL → ~90% sensitivity (higher false positive)
  • This patient: 168 → POSITIVE, proceed to OGTT

Step 2: 3-hr 100g OGTT (fasting, after 3-day unrestricted diet).

Carpenter-Coustan Criteria (3-hr OGTT)

≥2 abnormal values = GDM CONFIRMED (This patient has ALL 4 abnormal = definite GDM)

Alternative: 1-Step IADPSG (75g OGTT)

Increasingly adopted. Single 75g OGTT (fasting). ANY 1 abnormal = GDM:

  • Fasting ≥92
  • 1-hour ≥180
  • 2-hour ≥153
Management: Lifestyle & Monitoring ▼

First-Line: Dietary Modification

  • Carbohydrate distribution: 40–45% of calories (vs. standard 45–65%); avoid simple sugars
  • Meal composition: Include protein and fiber to slow absorption
  • Example: 3 meals + 2–3 snacks; smaller, frequent meals
  • Dietitian referral: Strongly recommended

Physical Activity

  • 30 min moderate activity ≥5 days/week (walking, stationary cycling)
  • Avoid high-impact or strenuous exercise (premature labor risk)

Self-Monitoring Blood Glucose (SMBG)

  • Target: Fasting <95, 1-hr postprandial <120, 2-hr <100 mg/dL (some use <140 for 2-hr)
  • Frequency: Fasting + 1-hr or 2-hr postprandial after each meal (4 times daily minimum)
  • Log & review: Weekly trends; adjust diet based on pattern (e.g., post-breakfast high → reduce carbs at breakfast)

Initiate Insulin When Lifestyle Fails

Criteria to start insulin:

  • Fasting glucose persistently >95 mg/dL despite diet (1–2 weeks trial)
  • 1-hr postprandial >120 mg/dL (some guidelines >140)
  • 2-hr postprandial >100 mg/dL (some >120)

Insulin is preferred in pregnancy (safest). Metformin and glyburide cross placenta; long-term fetal effects unknown.

Insulin Therapy & Fetal Risks ▼

Insulin Initiation

  • Type: NPH (intermediate-acting) + rapid-acting analogs (lispro, aspart) safest in pregnancy
  • Dosing: Start 0.5–1 U/kg/day based on fasting glucose; NPH at bedtime ± NPH in morning ± premeal rapid-acting
  • Monitoring: Increase by 2 U every 3–4 days until targets met
  • Hypoglycemia precautions: Teach recognition, glucagon kit, avoid driving alone

Fetal Risks of Maternal Hyperglycemia

Mechanism: Maternal glucose crosses placenta freely. High glucose → fetal hyperglycemia → fetal hyperinsulinism → growth stimulation.

  • Macrosomia (LGA): Birth weight >4000 g; risk of shoulder dystocia, clavicle fracture, brachial plexus injury
  • Neonatal hypoglycemia: Fetal hyperinsulinism → abrupt postnatal drop in glucose; seizure risk
  • Hyperbilirubinemia: Increased RBC turnover
  • Respiratory distress syndrome: Hyperinsulinemia delays fetal lung maturity
  • Fetal death: Rare with modern management but historically associated with poor glycemic control

Obstetric Monitoring

  • Monthly office visits: SMBG review, weight gain, blood pressure
  • Ultrasound: Estimated fetal weight >90th percentile → risk of macrosomia
  • Fetal heart rate tracing: NST after 32 weeks (assess fetal well-being)
  • Delivery timing: Aim 39 weeks (avoid RDS); earlier if preeclampsia, poor glycemic control
Postpartum & Pre-Existing T1DM in Pregnancy ▼

Postpartum Glucose Tolerance

  • 75g OGTT at 6–8 weeks postpartum: Reassess glucose tolerance (not during lactation if breastfeeding)
  • Most revert to normal; ~7–10% remain diabetic (overt T2DM)
  • Long-term risk: ~50% develop T2DM within 5–10 years; varies by ethnicity/BMI

Postpartum Counseling

  • Repeat OGTT q1–3 years if normal at 6–8 weeks
  • Lifestyle modification (weight loss, exercise) reduces T2DM progression by ~58% (DPP trial)
  • Screening in offspring (genetic risk)

Pre-Existing T1DM in Pregnancy (If Applicable)

Different scenario—higher risk.

  • Insulin requirements: ↑ 2nd and 3rd trimester (up to 2–3× prepregnancy dose)
  • Tight glycemic control crucial: Target HbA1c 6–6.5% (intensive, risk of hypoglycemia)
  • Congenital anomalies: 2–3× risk (neural tube defects, cardiac, renal) if poor glycemic control in 1st trimester organogenesis
  • Preeclampsia risk: ↑↑ (15–20% vs. 5% general population)
  • Macrosomia risk: Still high even with good control
  • Pre-conception counseling: Optimize glycemic control before pregnancy to reduce birth defect risk
TEST INSIGHT: GDM diagnosed by ≥2 abnormal values on 3-hr OGTT or ANY 1 abnormal on 1-step IADPSG 75g. First-line is diet + SMBG; insulin if lifestyle fails.
TRAP: Metformin and glyburide cross placenta. Insulin is safest in pregnancy. Do NOT use sulfonylureas or other agents without strong justification.
HYPER-FOCUS: Macrosomia mechanism: maternal hyperglycemia → fetal hyperinsulinism → growth. Neonatal complications include shoulder dystocia, RDS, hypoglycemia.
MEMORY ANCHOR: "GDM = 2+ abnormal on OGTT. Insulin preferred. Fetal risk = hyperinsulinism → LGA + neonatal hypoglycemia."
What is the postpartum glucose tolerance test for GDM?
75g OGTT at 6–8 weeks. ~50% develop T2DM within 5–10 years. Lifestyle modification reduces risk by ~58%.

LEC 24: Insulinoma vs. Factitious Hypoglycemia

CASE 5 — 42F with Recurrent Hypoglycemia

Morning altered mental status, diaphoresis, glucose 38. Insulin 45 µIU/mL (HIGH), C-peptide 4.2 ng/mL (HIGH), proinsulin elevated

Whipple's Triad & Initial Workup ▼

Whipple's Triad (Required for Hypoglycemia)

  1. Documented low glucose: <55 mg/dL at time of symptoms (this patient: 38)
  2. Symptoms: Neuroglycopenic (AMS, confusion) or adrenergic (diaphoresis, tremor, palpitations)
  3. Relief with glucose: Symptoms resolve after glucose/glucagon administration

This patient: All three present → hypoglycemia confirmed.

Differential Diagnosis

  • Insulinoma: Endogenous insulin secretion (β-cell tumor)
  • Factitious hypoglycemia: Exogenous insulin or sulfonylurea injection
  • Post-bariatric hypoglycemia: Reactive hyperinsulinism
  • Other rare: Autoimmune insulin syndrome, anti-insulin antibodies

Distinguishing Insulinoma from Sulfonylurea ▼

72-Hour Supervised Fast

Gold standard diagnostic test. Patient fasts while closely monitored; glucose and insulin measured serially.

  • Insulinoma: Glucose falls below 55 mg/dL, insulin remains inappropriately HIGH (detectable), C-peptide HIGH → test POSITIVE
  • Sulfonylurea: Glucose falls, but SU wears off during fast → glucose may not drop as far, or if does, insulin level normalizes by 72 hr → pattern different

Proinsulin Percentage

  • Insulinoma: Proinsulin >25% of total insulin (tumors secrete unprocessed proinsulin)
  • Normal/Exogenous: Proinsulin <10%
  • This patient: Proinsulin elevated → consistent with insulinoma

Urine/Serum Sulfonylurea Screen

Direct drug screen for SU metabolites. If positive → factitious hypoglycemia (SU ingestion).

This case: If screen negative + proinsulin >25% + 72-hr fast positive → insulinoma diagnosis confirmed.

  1. CT/MRI as first-line to identify mass
  2. EUS if CT/MRI negative but high suspicion (small insulinomas <2 cm often missed on CT)
  3. Octreoscan if thinking about metastatic disease or multiple endocrine neoplasia (MEN-1 associated)
Treatment & Management ▼

Surgical Resection (Curative)

  • Indications: Confirmed insulinoma with localization
  • Success rate: ~90% cure rate
  • Benign vs. malignant: ~90% benign; check for MEN-1, metastases preoperatively

Medical Management (If Surgical Delay/Unresectable)

  • Diazoxide: K+-ATP opener → inhibits insulin secretion. Dose 5–15 mg/kg/day in 2–3 divided doses. Monitor for hypokalemia, salt retention, hyperglycemia.
  • Octreotide: Somatostatin analog → inhibits insulin (as well as GH, glucagon). SC/IM 50–100 µg BID/TID. Useful for somatostatinomas (long-acting formulations available). Watch for diarrhea, steatorrhea, gallstones.
  • Frequent meals & complex carbs: Bridge therapy during diagnostic workup

LEC 24: Perioperative Diabetes Management

CASE 6 — 65M with T2DM Undergoing Elective CABG

T2DM (HbA1c 9.2%), on metformin + sitagliptin + glargine 28U QHS. Pre-op glucose 248

Pre-Operative Optimization ▼

Pre-Operative Assessment

  • HbA1c 9.2%: Suboptimal (target <8% before elective surgery to reduce wound infection risk). Consider delaying surgery if not urgent to optimize glycemic control (2–3 months).
  • Fasting glucose 248: Elevated; continue current regimen with adjustments
  • Screen for complications: Nephropathy (eGFR, ACR), neuropathy, retinopathy, cardiac (ECG, stress test for CAD)

Pre-Operative Glucose Target

Goal: HbA1c <8% (reduces surgical site infection by ~0.6% per 1% HbA1c reduction).

If elective surgery planned: Optimize DM control 2–3 months preoperatively.

Day of Surgery: Medication Management ▼

Antidiabetic Drug Protocol

Example Protocol

  • NPO midnight: Metformin, sitagliptin held
  • Glargine night before surgery: Give 80% of 28 U = 22.4 U (~22 U)
  • Morning of surgery (NPO): NO short-acting insulin, NO metformin
  • Intraop: IV insulin drip (see below)
Intraoperative Glucose Management ▼

IV Insulin Infusion

  • Setup: Regular insulin IV drip (e.g., 100 U in 100 mL NS = 1 U/mL)
  • Initial infusion: 0.5–1 U/hr (titrate based on glucose)
  • Glucose monitoring: q15–30 min intraop, q1h postop
  • Target glucose: 140–180 mg/dL intraop (NICE-SUGAR trial: intensive control <140 → worse outcomes in ICU)

Why NOT Tight Control Intraop?

NICE-SUGAR trial (2009): Intensive glucose control (80–110 mg/dL) in ICU → ↑ mortality vs. conventional (140–180 mg/dL). Likely due to hypoglycemia episodes and impaired immune function. Avoid <140 intraop.

Fluid Management

  • IV fluids: Use 0.9% NaCl (avoid D5W intraop to prevent hyperglycemia)
  • Add dextrose: Only if glucose <100 mg/dL (rare intraop)
Postoperative Management ▼

Stress Hyperglycemia

Expected: Post-op glucose often ↑ due to cortisol, catecholamine surge, inflammatory cytokines. This patient may see glucose 250–350 despite pre-op being 248.

  • Mechanism: Stress hormones → hepatic glucose production, insulin resistance
  • Duration: Usually resolves in 48–72 hr as inflammation subsides

Perioperative Steroid Effect

If patient received methylprednisolone (common for cardiac surgery): Hyperglycemia peaks in afternoon/evening.

  • Management: Supplement with NPH in AM + PM, or increase IV insulin infusion
  • Avoid correction-only dosing; anticipate steroid effect

ICU Glucose Protocol

  • Continue IV insulin: Until eating reliably
  • Target: 140–180 mg/dL (NICE-SUGAR)
  • Monitoring: q1–2h initially, q4h when stable
  • Transition to home regimen: When tolerating diet, restart basal-bolus ± prior agents

When to Restart Metformin

  • Criteria: Eating, eGFR stable (no acute kidney injury), no hemodynamic instability
  • Timing: Usually postop day 2–3

When to Restart SGLT2i

  • Timing: Not immediately postop (euglycemic DKA risk in acute illness)
  • Restart: After stabilization, no acute illness, good oral intake (~postop week 2–3)
Special Scenarios: Euglycemic DKA ▼

Euglycemic DKA Risk in Perioperative Setting

Rare but serious complication with SGLT2i.

  • Presentation: Glucose <200 (or even <150), elevated AG, positive ketones, AMS
  • Risk factors: SGLT2i on board + acute illness/surgery + dehydration + reduced carb intake

Prevention

  • HOLD SGLT2i 3–5 days before major surgery
  • Do not restart until postop recovery established, normal oral intake, no acute illness

Recognition & Diagnosis

Clue: Low glucose + elevated AG + elevated ketones + SGLT2i on board = euglycemic DKA until proven otherwise.

  • Labs: Serum β-hydroxybutyrate (>3 mmol/L diagnostic)
  • Treatment: IV insulin (0.1 U/kg/hr), fluids (0.9% NaCl), stop SGLT2i, supportive care

1. A 16-year-old presents with polyuria, polydipsia, and DKA confirmed (pH 7.32, HCO3 18, AG 14, glucose 380). What is the FIRST fluid choice?
A) D5W bolus
B) 0.9% NaCl bolus 10–20 mL/kg
C) 0.45% NaCl bolus
D) Hypertonic saline
ANSWER: B — 0.9% NaCl bolus 10–20 mL/kg initially for hemodynamic stabilization. Switch to 0.45% NaCl after stabilization to correct free water deficit.
2. In DKA with IV insulin drip, at what glucose level do you add dextrose, and why do you CONTINUE insulin?
A) <100, to prevent hypoglycemia
B) <250, to continue insulin until anion gap closes
C) <150, to allow continued metabolism
D) <180, per NICE-SUGAR
ANSWER: B — Add D5W when glucose <250 to prevent hypoglycemia, but CONTINUE insulin until AG closes (usually 12–24 hr). Glucose normalizes faster than ketosis resolves.
3. HHS and DKA: Which finding BEST distinguishes HHS?
A) Glucose >600, osmolality >320, minimal ketones, no acidosis
B) Elevated anion gap with positive ketones
C) pH <7.15 with HCO3 <10
D) High sodium, low potassium
ANSWER: A — HHS: glucose >600 + osmolality >320 + minimal ketones + pH >7.30 (no acidosis). DKA has acidosis and elevated AG.
4. In T1DM, which autoantibody is the MOST COMMON?
A) IA-2
B) GAD65
C) Anti-TPO
D) Zinc transporter 8
ANSWER: B — GAD65 (glutamic acid decarboxylase) is the most common autoantigen in T1DM (~80% of autoimmune T1DM). IA-2 and ZnT8 also present.
5. A 32-year-old at 26 weeks is diagnosed with GDM (≥2 abnormal OGTT values). What is FIRST-LINE treatment?
A) Start metformin immediately
B) Dietary modification + physical activity + SMBG
C) Start insulin NPH
D) Glyburide for rapid control
ANSWER: B — First-line is lifestyle: carb distribution 40–45%, physical activity, SMBG 4× daily (fasting + postprandial). Insulin started only if lifestyle fails after 1–2 weeks.
6. In GDM, when do you START insulin therapy?
A) If any glucose reading >100
B) If fasting >95 or postprandial >120 (1-hr) or >100 (2-hr) DESPITE lifestyle
C) Automatically at diagnosis
D) Only in 3rd trimester
ANSWER: B — Start insulin if fasting >95 or postprandial >120 (1-hr) or >100 (2-hr) despite dietary modification (1–2 week trial).
7. In GDM, how does maternal hyperglycemia lead to macrosomia (LGA)?
A) Reduced placental nutrient transfer
B) Maternal hyperglycemia → fetal hyperglycemia → fetal hyperinsulinism → growth stimulation
C) Enhanced oxygen delivery to fetus
D) Maternal weight gain
ANSWER: B — Maternal glucose crosses placenta. High glucose → fetal hyperglycemia → fetal hyperinsulinism → increased growth and macrosomia.
8. What is the RECOMMENDED postpartum glucose tolerance test for women with GDM?
A) Fasting glucose at 2 weeks
B) 75g OGTT at 6–8 weeks
C) Random glucose at 3 months
D) HbA1c at 12 weeks
ANSWER: B — 75g OGTT at 6–8 weeks postpartum. ~7–10% remain diabetic; ~50% develop T2DM within 5–10 years.
9. A 42-year-old has recurrent hypoglycemia (glucose 38, Whipple's triad confirmed). What is the GOLD STANDARD diagnostic test for insulinoma?
A) Random insulin and glucose
B) 72-hour supervised fast
C) CT abdomen
D) Insulin stimulation test
ANSWER: B — 72-hour supervised fast: glucose falls <55, insulin remains HIGH, C-peptide HIGH, proinsulin >25% = insulinoma positive.
10. In FACTITIOUS hypoglycemia from exogenous insulin injection, what is the C-peptide level?
A) High (endogenous production)
B) Low/Suppressed (exogenous insulin shuts off β-cells)
C) Normal
D) Cannot be measured
ANSWER: B — C-peptide LOW/SUPPRESSED in exogenous insulin because injected insulin suppresses endogenous β-cell secretion. KEY DISCRIMINATOR from insulinoma.
11. How do you distinguish insulinoma from sulfonylurea ingestion (both present with HIGH glucose + HIGH insulin + HIGH C-peptide)?
A) HbA1c level
B) Imaging (CT/MRI)
C) Urine/serum sulfonylurea drug screen + proinsulin percentage (>25% = insulinoma)
D) Family history
ANSWER: C — Drug screen for SU metabolites. Insulinoma has proinsulin >25% of total insulin (tumors secrete unprocessed proinsulin); SU has normal proinsulin.
12. A 65-year-old with T2DM is scheduled for elective CABG. Which DM drug should be HELD the day of surgery?
A) Basal insulin (glargine)
B) Metformin
C) ACE inhibitor
D) B and C
ANSWER: B — Hold metformin day of surgery (NPO, contrast risk, hemodynamic instability, acute kidney injury risk). Continue basal insulin at 80% dose.
13. Before elective surgery, when should SGLT2i be HELD to prevent euglycemic DKA?
A) Day of surgery only
B) 3–5 days before major surgery
C) 1 week before
D) Continue throughout
ANSWER: B — Hold SGLT2i 3–5 days before major surgery. Euglycemic DKA risk (glucose <200 but HIGH AG + ketones) in acute illness/surgery setting.
14. Per NICE-SUGAR trial, what is the recommended intraoperative glucose target?
A) <100 mg/dL
B) 80–110 mg/dL
C) 140–180 mg/dL
D) >200 mg/dL
ANSWER: C — 140–180 mg/dL. Intensive control (<140) increased hypoglycemia and worsened ICU outcomes. Moderate control is safer.
15. A post-CABG patient on SGLT2i presents with glucose 180, AMS, AG 18, and positive ketones. What is this?
A) Typical postop hyperglycemia
B) Euglycemic DKA (glucose <200 but HIGH AG + ketones + SGLT2i)
C) DKA (high glucose)
D) Hyperglycemic hyperosmolar state
ANSWER: B — Euglycemic DKA: glucose <200 (or even normal in this case 180, still mild) + AG 18 + positive ketones + patient on SGLT2i = emergency. Check β-hydroxybutyrate; stop SGLT2i; start IV insulin.
16. What is the mortality rate of HHS compared to DKA?
A) HHS ~2%, DKA ~15%
B) HHS ~5%, DKA ~10%
C) HHS ~15%, DKA ~1–2%
D) Both ~25%
ANSWER: C — HHS mortality ~15% (elderly, delayed presentation, thromboembolism, cerebral edema). DKA mortality ~1–2% with modern management.
17. In the honeymoon period after T1DM DKA, what is the CORRECT management?
A) Stop insulin; monitor glucose
B) Continue insulin; educate that remission is temporary
C) Switch to oral agents
D) Double insulin dose
ANSWER: B — Honeymoon period (partial remission): continue insulin despite ↓ requirements. Educate that remission is temporary (weeks to months); diabetes will return. Stopping insulin = DKA relapse.
18. What HbA1c goal before elective surgery reduces wound infection risk?
A) <6%
B) <7%
C) <8%
D) <9%
ANSWER: C — HbA1c <8% before elective surgery reduces surgical site infection and improves wound healing. Consider delaying non-urgent surgery to optimize control (2–3 months).

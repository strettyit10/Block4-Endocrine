1Steroid structure and nomenclature

The Gist All steroid hormones share the same 4-ring planar backbone derived from cholesterol (cyclopentanoperhydrophenanthrene). They differ by: carbon count, the functional groups attached, and double-bond position. C21 = progestins & corticoids; C19 = androgens; C18 = estrogens. Knowing the ring labels (A, B, C, D), carbon numbering, and double-bond notation (Δ4, Δ5) makes enzyme actions and structural differences readable at a glance.

  • (4) Reading enzyme names — hydroxylases (add -OH), HSD (oxidize/reduce OH), lyases (cleave bonds), aromatase (convert A-ring).

1 · The 4-ring backbone

Shared architecture
  • Structure:
    • All steroids have 4 fused rings (A, B, C, D) — "cyclopentanoperhydrophenanthrene."end04 lecture
    • Derived from cholesterol, which has 27 carbons.
  • Why it matters:
    • The shared backbone means steroid hormones are all lipophilic.
    • Hence they bind carrier proteins (CBG, albumin), cross plasma membranes, and act on intracellular receptors.

2 · Carbon count tells the class

  • C21 — progestins and corticoids:
    • Cholesterol (C27) loses 6 carbons in the side-chain cleavage step → pregnenolone (C21).end04 lecture
    • All mineralocorticoids and glucocorticoids retain the C21 scaffold.
  • C19 — androgens:
    • 17,20-lyase removes a 2-carbon side chain → androstenedione / DHEA (C19).
  • C18 — estrogens:
    • Aromatase removes the C19 methyl group and aromatizes the A-ring → estrone / estradiol (C18).FA 3.11 Adrenal steroids and CAH; Bootcamp 6.10

3 · Double-bond (Δ) nomenclature

  • Δ5 pathway:
    • Double bond between C5 and C6 (in the B ring).
    • Pregnenolone, 17-OH-pregnenolone, DHEA are Δ5 compounds.end04 lecture; FA 3.11
  • Δ4 pathway:
    • Double bond between C4 and C5 (in the A ring).
    • Progesterone, 17-OH-progesterone, androstenedione are Δ4 compounds.
  • 3β-HSD converts Δ5 → Δ4:
    • 3β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase moves the double bond from position 5 → 4 and oxidizes the C3 hydroxyl to a ketone.
    • This is the "gate" that converts precursors to the Δ4 pathway.

4 · Reading enzyme names

  • Hydroxylases (-hydroxylase):
    • Add an -OH group at a specific carbon (e.g., 21-hydroxylase adds -OH at C21).FA 3.11 Adrenal steroids
  • HSDs (hydroxysteroid dehydrogenases):
    • Oxidize/reduce an -OH group at a specific carbon.
    • 3β-HSD (Δ5 → Δ4) and 17β-HSD (androstenedione → testosterone).
  • Lyases:
    • Cleave C-C bonds (e.g., 17,20-lyase cleaves between C17 and C20 to make C19 steroids).
  • Aromatase:
    • Converts C19 androgens to C18 estrogens by aromatizing the A-ring (removes the C19 methyl).FA 3.11; Bootcamp 6.10

2Synthesis steps shared by all steroid hormones

The Gist Every steroid hormone — whether made in the adrenal cortex, gonads, or placenta — begins with the same four shared steps. (1) Cholesterol is liberated from cytoplasmic lipid droplets by hormone-sensitive lipase (HSL). (2) StAR protein transfers cholesterol from the outer to inner mitochondrial membrane — the rate-limiting step. (3) Mitochondrial cholesterol desmolase (CYP11A1 / P450scc) cleaves cholesterol's side chain → pregnenolone (C21), the universal steroid precursor. (4) The entire process is coordinated by cAMP generated by hormone-responsive GPCRs (ACTH in the cortex, AngII in zona glomerulosa).

1 · Cholesterol sources

Three origins (in order of use)
  • Primary — LDL-receptor-mediated uptake:
    • Hepatocytes and steroidogenic cells express high levels of LDL-R.
    • ACTH upregulates LDL-R expression with some delay.end04 lecture

2 · HSL (hormone-sensitive lipase)

  • Function:
    • Hydrolyzes cholesterol ester bonds, releasing free cholesterol.end04 lecture
  • Activation:
    • PKA-mediated phosphorylation (downstream of cAMP from ACTH or AngII).
  • Clinical relevance:
    • Acute steroidogenic response requires HSL + StAR together.

3 · StAR (steroidogenic acute regulatory protein)

The real bottleneck
  • Function:
    • Shuttles cholesterol from the outer to the inner mitochondrial membrane.end04 lecture
    • Without StAR, desmolase sits idle with no substrate — so this transport is the true rate-limiting step.
  • Regulation:
    • Transcription upregulated rapidly by cAMP-PKA (in minutes).
    • ACTH (adrenal cortex), LH (gonads), AngII + K⁺ (zona glomerulosa) all drive StAR expression.FA 3.11 Adrenal steroids and CAH
  • Classic disease:
    • StAR mutation → congenital lipoid adrenal hyperplasia.
    • Lipid-engorged adrenals, profound deficiency of ALL steroid hormones.AI Generated · see end20 / end06

4 · Desmolase / P450scc / CYP11A1

The four-names-one-enzyme step
  • Names (all the same enzyme):
    • Cholesterol desmolase.
    • 20,22-desmolase.
    • CYP11A1.
    • P450scc (scc = side-chain cleavage).end04 lecture
  • Reaction:
    • Two sequential hydroxylations (C20, C22) followed by C–C bond cleavage.
    • Removes 6 carbons (C27 → C21).
  • Location:
    • Inner mitochondrial membrane.
  • Cofactors:
    • NADPH, O₂, Fe (P450 heme).
  • Pharmacologic inhibitors:
    • Ketoconazole (antifungal; inhibits desmolase + several CYPs).
    • Aminoglutethimide (historical).AI Generated · see end21

5 · cAMP coordinates everything

  • Hormonal drivers:
    • ACTH at the adrenal cortex (binds MC2R Gs-coupled GPCR).end04 lecture
    • AngII + K⁺ at zona glomerulosa (binds AT1R Gq; raises intracellular Ca²⁺).
    • LH at gonads.
  • Downstream actions:
    • ↑ HSL phosphorylation → more free cholesterol.
    • ↑ StAR transcription → more mitochondrial cholesterol import.
    • ↑ LDLR and P450scc expression (with some delay).end04 lecture

6 · Acute vs. chronic stimulation

  • Acute response (minutes):
    • HSL activation + StAR transcription → rapid cortisol release.
  • Chronic response (hours–days):
    • Upregulates LDLR for more cholesterol uptake.
    • Upregulates P450scc and downstream enzymes.
    • Causes adrenal cortex hyperplasia (e.g., in Cushing disease).AI Generated · see end19/20

3Layers of the adrenal cortex, their enzymes, and their hormones

The Gist The adrenal cortex has three zones (outer → inner): zona Glomerulosa (mineralocorticoids — aldosterone), zona Fasciculata (glucocorticoids — cortisol), zona Reticularis (androgens — DHEA). Each zone expresses a different enzyme pattern, which dictates the hormone produced. The medulla (center) is a separate neuroendocrine organ that makes catecholamines (mostly epinephrine).

1 · Histologic and functional zonation

Outside → inside of the cortex
  • Zona Glomerulosa (15% of cortex):
    • Outermost, just deep to the capsule.
    • Cells in small nests ("glomerular" cluster appearance).end04 lecture; Physeo 6.07 Adrenal Glands
    • Primary stimulus: angiotensin II and K⁺.
  • Zona Fasciculata (~75% of cortex, largest):
    • Middle zone, cells in long vertical cords ("fascicles").
    • Cells are lipid-rich ("spongiocytes") with a foamy cytoplasm.
    • Primary stimulus: ACTH.B&B 2.01 Adrenal Glands
  • Zona Reticularis (~10% of cortex):
    • Innermost cortical layer, next to the medulla.
    • Cells in a branching reticular network; pigmented (lipofuscin).
    • Primary stimulus: ACTH (same as ZF but different enzyme suite).
  • Adrenal medulla:
    • Center of the gland — a modified sympathetic ganglion.
    • Chromaffin cells (postganglionic sympathetic neurons) secrete catecholamines.end04 lecture (brief)

2 · Enzyme fingerprints — which enzymes each zone has

Zona Glomerulosa
  • Has:
    • All early steroidogenic enzymes (desmolase, 3β-HSD, 21-hydroxylase).
    • Aldosterone synthase (CYP11B2) — unique to ZG; catalyzes the final 3 steps to aldosterone.end04 lecture; FA 3.11
  • Lacks:
    • 17α-hydroxylase → can't make cortisol or sex steroids.
  • Makes:
    • Aldosterone (primary).
    • Traces of other mineralocorticoids (DOC, corticosterone).
Zona Fasciculata
  • Has:
    • 17α-hydroxylase (activates glucocorticoid pathway).
    • CYP11B1 (11β-hydroxylase, the ZF version of 11β-hydroxylase).end04 lecture
  • Has but at low levels:
    • 17,20-lyase — limited androgen output.
  • Lacks (vs ZG):
    • Aldosterone synthase → cannot make aldosterone.
  • Makes:
    • Cortisol (primary).
    • Corticosterone (minor).
    • Small amounts of adrenal androgens.
Zona Reticularis
  • Has:
    • Enhanced 17,20-lyase activity.
    • Sulfotransferase (converts DHEA → DHEA-S).end04 lecture
  • Lacks / low:
    • NO 21-hydroxylase (can't make corticoids).
    • LOW 3β-HSD activity (stays in Δ5 pathway).
  • Makes:
    • DHEA (primary) and DHEA-S.
    • Androstenedione (minor).
    • Small amounts of testosterone.FA 3.11 Adrenal steroids

3 · Development and lifetime changes in each zone

  • Zona Reticularis (adrenarche):
    • Develops and begins secreting DHEA between ages 6–8 (adrenarche).end04 lecture
    • DHEA output peaks in mid-20s.
    • Declines steadily thereafter.
  • Aging changes:
    • ZR thins with age → falling DHEA with preserved cortisol/aldosterone.
    • Contributes to age-related decline in adrenal androgen output.

4Biosynthesis pathways — aldosterone, cortisol, DHEA

The Gist All three pathways start from cholesterol → pregnenolone. Then they diverge based on each zone's enzyme fingerprint. Aldosterone (ZG): pregnenolone → progesterone → DOC → corticosterone → aldosterone (aldosterone synthase, CYP11B2). Cortisol (ZF): pregnenolone → 17-OH-pregnenolone → 17-OH-progesterone → 11-deoxycortisol → cortisol (11β-hydroxylase, CYP11B1). DHEA (ZR): pregnenolone → 17-OH-pregnenolone → DHEA (17,20-lyase) → DHEA-S (sulfotransferase). Memorize the "grid" of enzymes and you can reason through every CAH.

📚 Going Deeper — pathways and their enzyme forks

1 · The three critical "forks" in steroidogenesis

17α-hydroxylase (the "sex/sugar fork")
  • If 17α-hydroxylase is present:
    • Pregnenolone → 17-OH-pregnenolone → continues to cortisol or sex steroids.end04 lecture; FA 3.11
  • If absent (ZG):
    • Pregnenolone → progesterone → mineralocorticoid pathway only.
17,20-lyase (the "sex steroid fork")
  • When active:
    • 17-OH-pregnenolone → DHEA (or 17-OH-progesterone → androstenedione).
  • Tissue distribution:
    • High in ZR → mostly DHEA.

5Circulation and inactivation — carrier proteins and half-life

The Gist All adrenal steroids travel via carrier proteins but with different ratios. Aldosterone: only ~35% free; binds CBG/albumin with low affinity; T½ ~20 min. Cortisol: ~90% bound to CBG (transcortin), ~6% albumin, ~4% free; T½ ~70 min. DHEA: albumin (low affinity), T½ 15–30 min. Only the free fraction is active and can cross cell membranes.

6Mechanisms of action — aldosterone and cortisol

The Gist Both aldosterone and cortisol are lipophilic steroids that cross the plasma membrane and bind cytoplasmic nuclear receptors (MR and GR, respectively). Receptors are chaperoned by HSP90; hormone binding releases the chaperones, letting the receptor dimerize and translocate to the nucleus. Dimeric receptors bind hormone response elements (MRE or GRE) on chromatin and modulate transcription. Aldosterone: acts on collecting duct principal cells → ↑ ENaC, ↑ Na/K-ATPase, ↑ ROMK → Na⁺ reabsorption, K⁺ and H⁺ secretion. Cortisol: broad effects — gluconeogenesis, lipolysis, protein catabolism, immunosuppression, vascular permissiveness. Cortisol has the same affinity for MR as aldosterone, so kidney protects MR via 11β-HSD2.

1 · Nuclear receptor mechanism — Type I (steroid) receptors

Resting state
  • Receptor location:
    • MR and GR sit in the cytoplasm, bound to HSP90 and HSP70 chaperones.end04 lecture; FA 3.15 Signaling pathways of steroid hormones
Activation
  • Steps:
    • Steroid crosses the plasma membrane (lipophilic).
    • Binds receptor → HSPs dissociate.
    • Receptor dimerizes and translocates to nucleus.
    • Dimeric receptor binds palindromic hormone response elements (HRE) on DNA.
    • Transcription is upregulated or repressed (depending on coactivators/corepressors present).
Timescale
  • Genomic effects:
    • Require transcription + translation → take hours.end04 lecture; FA 3.15

2 · Aldosterone effects on the collecting duct

Principal cell targets

  • Basolateral membrane:
    • ↑ Na/K-ATPase — drives the Na⁺ gradient; moves Na⁺ into blood.

    • ↑ H⁺-ATPase → secretes H⁺ into tubule.

LEC 22

Stress Hormones

LO1: Fight-or-Flight Response & Acute Stress Physiology

Overview

The body mounts rapid hormonal responses to stress through two coordinated systems: the sympathetic nervous system (SNS) for immediate effects and the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis for sustained responses.

Memory Aid

SNS = Fast (seconds to minutes), HPA = Slow (minutes to hours). Both ensure energy mobilization and cardiovascular support.

Neural Pathway: Hypothalamus to SNS

Stressor detected → Hypothalamus activates SNS → Preganglionic fibers to adrenal medulla → Chromaffin cells (neural crest origin) release catecholamines within seconds.

Adrenal Medulla Hormone Synthesis & Release

Composition:

  • 80% Epinephrine (Epi) — acts on β1, β2, α1 receptors
  • 20% Norepinephrine (NE) — predominantly α1 activity

Adrenergic Receptor Effects

Receptor Primary Ligand Key Effects
β1 (Heart) Epi > NE ↑ HR, ↑ contractility (inotrope)
β2 (Airways, vessels) Epi >> NE Bronchodilation, peripheral vasodilation, ↑ glucose
α1 (Skin, gut, splanchnic) NE > Epi Vasoconstriction, shunting blood to muscles
Why is epinephrine 80% of adrenal medulla output?
PNMT (phenylethanolamine N-methyltransferase) converts NE → Epi, requiring cortisol as cofactor. High cortisol in adrenal cortex → high Epi production.

Metabolic Effects of Catecholamines

  • Glycogenolysis (hepatic): β2 → ↑ glucose release (minutes)
  • Lipolysis (adipose): β3-mediated → ↑ free fatty acids
  • Gluconeogenesis: Supported by epi + cortisol + glucagon

HPA Axis: Slower Component

CRH (hypothalamus) → ACTH (pituitary) → Cortisol (adrenal cortex)

Peak cortisol: 15-30 minutes after acute stress (slower than catecholamines but more sustained).

Hyperglycemia Hormone Axis

Epi + Cortisol + Glucagon + GH all ↑ glucose. Epi is fastest (glycogenolysis), cortisol is most sustained (gluconeogenesis + proteolysis).

Other Stress Hormones

  • ADH (Vasopressin): ↑ in stress, maintains vascular tone + osmolality
  • Renin-Aldosterone: Activated by ↓ volume/BP, promotes Na+ retention + vasoconstriction
  • GH: Anti-insulin effect; mobilizes energy substrates
Test Concept

Cortisol is "permissive" for catecholamine action. Low cortisol → cardiovascular collapse despite high catecholamines (Addisonian crisis).

LO2: Catecholamine Biochemistry & Pheochromocytoma

Catecholamine Synthesis Pathway

Tyrosine → (Tyrosine Hydroxylase: RATE-LIMITING) → L-DOPA → (DOPA Decarboxylase) → Dopamine → (Dopamine β-Hydroxylase) → Norepinephrine → (PNMT: requires cortisol as cofactor) → Epinephrine

Key Fact: Tyrosine Hydroxylase

Tyrosine hydroxylase is the rate-limiting enzyme. This step controls overall catecholamine production. It requires tetrahydrofolate (BH4) as cofactor.

Why is PNMT activity only high in the adrenal medulla?
PNMT (phenylethanolamine N-methyltransferase) requires cortisol as a cofactor. The adrenal cortex sits atop the medulla, providing high local cortisol concentrations → abundant Epi production.

Catecholamine Metabolism

  • MAO (monoamine oxidase): mitochondrial enzyme; breaks down catecholamines
  • COMT (catechol-O-methyltransferase): methylates catecholamines
  • Final urinary metabolite: VMA (vanillylmandelic acid) — detects catecholamine excess
  • Most specific plasma test: Plasma free metanephrines (higher sensitivity/specificity than VMA)

Pheochromocytoma: Rule of 10s

  • 10% bilateral (catecholamine-secreting tumors in both adrenals)
  • 10% extra-adrenal (paragangliomas along sympathetic chain)
  • 10% malignant (can metastasize)
  • 10% in children
  • 10% familial (VHL, RET, NF1, SDH mutations)
Hereditary Syndromes

~40% of pheos are hereditary: VHL (von Hippel-Lindau), RET (MEN2A/B), NF1 (neurofibromatosis), SDH (succinate dehydrogenase). Screen first-degree relatives.

Clinical Presentation: "5 P's"

  • Palpitations (tachycardia, pounding chest)
  • Perspiration (profuse sweating, often nocturnal)
  • Pressure (hypertension, often paroxysmal)
  • Pain (headache, chest/abdominal pain)
  • Pallor (vasoconstriction, then flushing)
Common Pitfall

Pheo attacks are episodic and spontaneous. Between attacks, BP and HR may be normal. Don't miss diagnosis because a single office BP is normal.

Diagnosis Algorithm

  1. Plasma free metanephrines (lying 30 min) — most sensitive & specific screening test
  2. If elevated: CT abdomen/pelvis or MRI to localize tumor
  3. If imaging negative: MIBG scan (I-123 metaiodobenzylguanidine) — detects extra-adrenal/ectopic pheos
  4. Confirm MIBG positivity with PET (F-18 DOPA or F-18 FDG) if needed

Management: Alpha-BEFORE-Beta Rule

CRITICAL RULE

ALWAYS start alpha-blockers FIRST. Phenoxybenzamine is traditional choice. THEN add beta-blocker after alpha-blockade established. Never beta-block first (unopposed alpha → hypertensive crisis, MI, stroke).

  • Alpha-blocker: Phenoxybenzamine (non-selective, irreversible; preferred for preop) or Doxazosin (reversible, shorter half-life)
  • Beta-blocker: Propranolol or Labetalol (after alpha-blockade); prevents tachycardia/arrhythmias from unopposed beta effects
  • Surgical resection: Definitive cure after adequate medical prep
Why must you give alpha-blocker BEFORE beta-blocker in pheo?
Beta-blockers without alpha blockade leave α1-mediated vasoconstriction unopposed → severe hypertension, MI, stroke. Alpha blockade first allows vasodilation; then beta-blocker prevents reflex tachycardia.

LO3: Cortisol in Stress Response & Cushing's Syndrome

Cortisol's Permissive Role in Acute Stress

Critical concept: Cortisol is not the primary driver of acute stress responses but is absolutely required for catecholamine effectiveness.

  • Cortisol ↑ adrenergic receptor expression on target tissues (heart, blood vessels, adipose)
  • Without cortisol: Catecholamines present but ineffective → cardiovascular collapse (Addisonian crisis)
  • Cortisol also mobilizes substrates (glucose, FFA) to support prolonged stress response
Test Pearl

A patient in Addisonian crisis may have high catecholamines but low cortisol → cannot mount adequate cardiovascular response. Cortisol replacement → restoration of hemodynamics.

Chronic Stress: Allostatic Load

Prolonged HPA activation leads to allostatic load — chronic elevation of cortisol and catecholamines causes end-organ damage:

  • Hypertension: Cortisol + NE → Na+ retention, vasoconstriction
  • Hyperglycemia/T2DM: Cortisol → ↑ gluconeogenesis, ↓ insulin sensitivity
  • Immune dysfunction: Chronic cortisol → ↓ cell-mediated immunity, ↑ infections
  • Hippocampal atrophy: Hippocampus rich in glucocorticoid receptors (GR). Chronic cortisol → neurotoxicity → ↓ memory, mood regulation, depression risk
Why does chronic stress cause hippocampal atrophy?
The hippocampus has high density of glucocorticoid receptors. Chronic elevated cortisol → excitotoxicity → neuronal death → atrophy. This impairs memory and mood regulation (depression).

Cushing's Syndrome: Classic Features

Result of excessive glucocorticoid exposure (from any source).

Cushing's Classic Presentation

Central obesity + buffalo hump + moon face + proximal myopathy + violaceous striae + easy bruising + hypertension + hyperglycemia + osteoporosis + psychiatric symptoms

  • Central obesity/Buffalo hump: Redistribution from cortisol's lipogenic effects
  • Moon facies: Facial fat deposition + mild edema
  • Proximal myopathy: Cortisol → ↑ muscle protein breakdown
  • Violaceous striae: Thinned skin from collagen loss; striae >1cm wide = Cushing's
  • Easy bruising/skin fragility: Collagen breakdown
  • Hypertension: Mineralocorticoid effect (especially ectopic ACTH)
  • Hyperglycemia/new-onset DM: Anti-insulin effects
  • Osteoporosis: ↓ bone formation, ↑ resorption
  • Psychiatric: Depression, anxiety, mood lability, psychosis in severe cases

Diagnosis Algorithm

  1. 1-mg overnight dexamethasone suppression test (DST): Screening test. Normal cortisol suppresses to <1.8 ng/dL. If ≥1.8 → likely Cushing's.
  2. Confirm excess: 24-hour urinary free cortisol (UFC) + late-night salivary cortisol (most physiologic). Both elevated = ↑↑ specificity
  3. Determine etiology: Measure ACTH
    • ACTH high/normal: ACTH-dependent (pituitary or ectopic)
    • ACTH low (<5): ACTH-independent (adrenal tumor)
  4. Localize source:
    • High ACTH → CRH test (pituitary Cushing's: ACTH↑; ectopic: no response) or IPSS (inferior petrosal sinus sampling)
    • Low ACTH → CT adomen/pelvis to image tumor
Diagnostic Pitfall

Mild cortisol elevations in depressed patients: depression itself can cause mild HPA activation and abnormal DST. Not all abnormal DST = Cushing's. Ensure clinical features support diagnosis.

LO4: Metabolic Integration & Glucose Counterregulation

Fuel Mobilization Timeline in Fasting/Stress

Timeframe Primary Hormone Mechanism
Minutes Epinephrine Hepatic glycogenolysis (breaks down stored glucose)
Hours Glucagon Hepatic gluconeogenesis + ketogenesis (makes new glucose + ketones)
Hours-Days Cortisol + GH ↑ Gluconeogenesis + proteolysis + lipolysis; GH provides anti-insulin effect
Memory Tip

Epi is fastest, glucagon is most important for sustained response, cortisol provides permissive support.

Glucose Counterregulation in Hypoglycemia

When blood glucose drops, hormones activate in strict order to restore glucose:

  1. ↓ Insulin (stops glucose consumption, fastest response)
  2. ↑ Glucagon (MOST IMPORTANT; stimulates hepatic glucose production)
  3. ↑ Epinephrine (glycogenolysis, lipolysis; also causes warning symptoms: tremor, sweating, palpitations)
  4. ↑ Cortisol + GH (gluconeogenesis, delayed but sustained)
Which hormone is most important for counterregulating hypoglycemia?
Glucagon is the single most important counterregulatory hormone. It directly stimulates hepatic glucose output. Epinephrine provides warning symptoms and glycogenolysis but is secondary to glucagon.
Key Concept

Epinephrine causes warning symptoms (tremor, anxiety, palpitations, sweating) of hypoglycemia. Without epi → hypoglycemia unawareness (patient unaware until severe cognitive dysfunction).

Type 1 Diabetes: Hypoglycemia Unawareness

Problem: After ~5 years of T1DM, glucagon counterregulation becomes blunted. Patients lose early epinephrine-mediated warning symptoms → hypoglycemia unawareness → severe hypoglycemia, seizures, DKA from overcorrection.

Management:

  • Strict hypoglycemia avoidance — avoid low BG episodes to preserve remaining glucagon response
  • Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) to detect early lows
  • Adjust insulin: fewer injections, use long-acting agents less aggressively
  • Glucagon emergency kit for family
Common Mistake

Overly tight glycemic control in T1DM patients with hypoglycemia unawareness → more hypoglycemic episodes → worsens unawareness. Safer to run slightly higher BG.

Diabetic Sick Day: Management Pearls

Acute illness (infection, surgery, trauma) → stress hyperglycemia even in well-controlled T1DM.

  • NEVER stop insulin — it's needed more, not less, during illness
  • Increase monitoring: Check BG q2-4h, ketones if BG >250
  • Check ketones: If BG >250 + nausea/vomiting → check urine or serum ketones (DKA risk)
  • Hydrate: Free water or sugar-free fluids; IV fluids if severe illness
  • Adjust insulin: May need 10-20% more during acute illness

Metabolic Effects of Counter-Regulatory Hormones

  • Insulin (fed state): Glucose uptake, glycogen storage, lipogenesis
  • Glucagon (fasting/stress): Glycogenolysis + gluconeogenesis
  • Cortisol + GH: Antagonize insulin (anti-insulin hormones)
  • Epinephrine: ↑ glucose + FFA availability

LO5: Immune-Endocrine Axis & Sepsis Adrenal Insufficiency

Bidirectional Immune-Endocrine Axis

Inflammation signals HPA activation: IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α produced during infection/inflammation → stimulate hypothalamic CRH → ↑ ACTH → ↑ cortisol → anti-inflammatory feedback.

Key Loop

Infection/Inflammation → Cytokines → HPA ↑ Cortisol → Anti-inflammation (negative feedback loop)

Cortisol's Anti-Inflammatory Mechanisms

  • ↓ NF-κB: Master transcription factor for pro-inflammatory genes (blocked by cortisol)
  • ↓ COX-2, PLA2: Reduces prostaglandin/leukotriene synthesis (via annexin-1)
  • ↓ Cytokines: ↓ IL-1, IL-2, IL-6, TNF-α, IFN-γ (Th1 response suppressed)
  • ↑ Neutrophil demargination: Paradoxically, neutrophils released from vessel walls → peripheral blood neutrophilia (but dysfunction)
  • ↓ Lymphocytes, eosinophils: Lymphocyte apoptosis, eosinopenia
  • ↓ Mast cells: Reduced local inflammation
Cortisol causes lymphopenia, so why is it anti-inflammatory?
Cortisol reduces adaptive (Th1/Th2) immune response but increases innate immunity (neutrophil demargination). The ↓ IL-2, TNF-α far outweighs the lymphopenia in terms of reducing inflammatory damage. This is adaptive for acute infection but problematic in chronic excess (immunosuppression).

Classic Stress Leukogram

Result of cortisol + catecholamine excess during acute stress:

  • Neutrophilia: Demargination + ↓ apoptosis
  • Lymphopenia: Apoptosis + redistribution to tissues
  • Eosinopenia: Sequestration in tissues (cortisol effect)
  • Monocytopenia: Redistribution

Sepsis: Relative Adrenal Insufficiency (CIRCI)

Critical-Illness Related Corticosteroid Insufficiency: Some septic patients cannot produce adequate cortisol relative to their illness severity → refractory hypotension despite vasopressors.

Diagnosis: Hemodynamic instability (MAP <65 despite fluids + vasopressors) + inadequate cortisol response to ACTH stimulation (< 9 mcg/dL rise in cortisol or absolute level <18-20 mcg/dL).

Treatment: Hydrocortisone 200 mg IV daily (often as 50 mg q6h) → restores vasopressor responsiveness, improves hemodynamics.

Key Trial Findings

CORTICUS & ADRENAL trials: Low-dose hydrocortisone in septic shock improves hemodynamics and reduces vasopressor requirements. In non-responders to ACTH (e.g., baseline cortisol >34 mcg/dL), steroids don't help and may increase mortality (don't treat responders).

Excess Cortisol/Steroids: Immunosuppression Complications

Paradox: While acute cortisol is adaptive, excess → immunosuppression → opportunistic infections.

  • PCP (Pneumocystis jirovecii): Risk if CD4 <200 in HIV or prolonged steroids
  • Cryptococcus: CNS meningitis in immunocompromised
  • CMV: Disseminated disease in severe immunosuppression
  • TB reactivation: Increased risk with chronic steroids
  • Candida: Mucosal + systemic infections
Clinical Pearl

Patient on chronic steroids presenting with fever + hypoxia + PCP-like presentation? Consider PCP prophylaxis if CD4 equivalent low or steroid dose high.

Waterhouse-Friderichsen Syndrome

Meningococcal (Neisseria meningitidis) sepsis → disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) → bilateral adrenal hemorrhage → acute adrenal insufficiency + septic shock.

  • Fulminant meningococcemia: petechial rash → shock
  • Adrenal hemorrhage visible at autopsy/imaging
  • High mortality if not recognized early (sepsis + adrenal crisis)

LO6: Integrative Cases & Clinical Scenarios

Case 1: Surgical Stress in Addison's Patient

Scenario: Patient with known Addison's disease scheduled for elective surgery. Takes replacement hydrocortisone 20 mg daily.

Problem: Surgical stress exceeds baseline cortisol needs. Without stress dosing → intraoperative hypotension, cardiovascular collapse, adrenal crisis.

Management:

  • Stress-dose steroids: Hydrocortisone 50-100 mg IV q6-8h perioperatively
  • Continue stress dosing for 24-48 hours postop, then taper back to maintenance
  • Maintain IV access; monitor vitals closely
Critical Pearl

Any patient with adrenal insufficiency facing major stress (surgery, sepsis, severe illness) requires stress-dose steroids to prevent adrenal crisis.

Case 2: Stress Hyperglycemia in ICU/Trauma

Scenario: Post-trauma ICU patient with no prior diabetes now has BG 250-400 mg/dL. Insulin requirements surge.

Mechanism: Catecholamines + cortisol + glucagon → hepatic glucose overproduction; stress hyperglycemia universal in critical illness.

Historic vs. Modern Management:

  • Old approach (Van den Berghe): Tight control (target 80-110 mg/dL) with intensive insulin
  • NICE-SUGAR trial: Tight control ↑ mortality (hypoglycemia, increased infections). New target: 140-180 mg/dL
  • Lesson: Avoid excessive insulin in ICU. Stress hyperglycemia is adaptive; treat cautiously
NICE-SUGAR Key Finding

Tight glucose control (80-110) in ICU → increased mortality. Target 140-180 is safer. Stress hyperglycemia serves a purpose.

Case 3: Non-Thyroidal Illness (NTIS) in ICU

Scenario: Critically ill patient: ↓ T3, ↑ rT3, ↓ TSH. Concern for thyroid dysfunction.

Mechanism: Stress hormones (cortisol, TNF-α) → ↓ D1 (converts T4 → T3), ↑ D3 (converts T4 → rT3, inactive). This is adaptive, not pathologic.

Management: DO NOT TREAT. Don't give thyroid hormone. It's a normal stress response. Hormone levels normalize as patient recovers.

Common Pitfall

Seeing ↓ T3/↑ rT3/↓ TSH in ICU and treating with levothyroxine → worsens outcomes. It's sick euthyroid, not hypothyroidism. Watch and wait for recovery.

Case 4: Psychological Stress & Depression

Scenario: Depressed patient: chronic HPA activation → persistent cortisol elevation.

Mechanism: Depressed mood → ↑ CRH → sustained cortisol. Chronic cortisol → hippocampal atrophy (↓ memory, mood regulation) → worsens depression (vicious cycle).

Lab findings: Dexamethasone suppression test (DST) may be abnormal (non-suppression of cortisol). But abnormal DST ≠ Cushing's in depression — clinical features distinguish.

Management: Treat depression (SSRIs, psychotherapy) → normalizes HPA axis → cortisol normalizes.

Case 5: Anorexia Nervosa

Scenario: Young female with severe restrictive eating: elevated cortisol, amenorrhea, hypothermia.

Hormonal findings:

  • ↑ Cortisol: Functional hypercortisolism from chronic starvation stress
  • ↓ GnRH → ↓ LH/FSH: Amenorrhea (hypothalamic hypogonadism)
  • ↓ T3, ↑ rT3: Non-thyroidal illness (euthyroid sick syndrome)
  • ↑ Ghrelin: Hunger hormone (paradoxically high but overridden by psychological restriction)

Management: Nutritional rehabilitation. Hormones normalize with weight restoration.

Case 6: Pheochromocytoma Crisis Intraoperatively

Scenario: Patient undergoing pheo resection. Intraoperatively, BP surges to 240/140, HR 140, SVT. Surgeon squeezed tumor releasing catecholamines.

Immediate management:

  • IV phentolamine (alpha-blocker) immediately — dilates vessels, drops BP acutely
  • Only THEN use esmolol IV (beta-blocker) if tachycardic
  • Never give beta-blocker alone — unopposed alpha → worse hypertension, MI
  • Once tumor removed: catecholamine levels drop → maintain fluid support (dilated vessels)
Intraoperative Pheo Rule

IV Phentolamine immediately for hypertensive crisis. No beta-blockers first.

Case 7: Ectopic ACTH (Small Cell Lung Cancer)

Scenario: 60-year-old smoker, hoarseness, weight loss. Labs: hypokalemia (K 2.8), metabolic alkalosis, hyperglycemia, ↑↑ cortisol (100+ ng/dL), ↑↑ ACTH.

Diagnosis: Ectopic ACTH syndrome (usually SCLC). Rapid Cushing's presentation (not gradual).

Distinguishing features from pituitary Cushing's:

  • Severe hypokalemia: Ectopic ACTH causes extreme cortisol → mineralocorticoid effect → Na+ reabsorption, K+ loss
  • Hyperpigmentation: ↑↑ ACTH stimulates melanocytes (MSH co-secreted)
  • NO fat redistribution: Onset too acute; no time for central obesity/buffalo hump
  • Rapid clinical decline: Weight loss, weakness, infections

Management:

  • Treat underlying malignancy: Chemotherapy if possible
  • Urgent steroid inhibition: Ketoconazole (inhibits 11β-hydroxylase) or metyrapone (inhibits 11β-hydroxylase) to lower cortisol acutely
  • Correct hypokalemia (K replacement, K-sparing diuretics)
  • Control hyperglycemia (insulin)

Practice Questions

Q1: A 45-year-old man with pheochromocytoma is scheduled for surgical resection. Which of the following is the CORRECT sequence of preoperative medications?

Answer: B & D are both correct. Alpha-blocker FIRST (phentolamine acutely or phenoxybenzamine for chronic prep), THEN beta-blocker. Beta-blocker monotherapy or first is contraindicated (unopposed alpha → hypertensive crisis).

Q2: PNMT (phenylethanolamine N-methyltransferase) catalyzes the final step of epinephrine synthesis (NE → Epi). Why is PNMT activity highest in the adrenal medulla?

Answer: B PNMT requires cortisol as a cofactor. The adrenal medulla has high local cortisol concentration from the adjacent cortex → high PNMT activity → abundant epinephrine production. This is why cortical disease can affect medullary hormone production.

Q3: A 30-year-old woman presents with episodic palpitations, sweating, and headaches. Elevated plasma free metanephrines confirmed. CT abdomen is normal. What is the NEXT appropriate test?

Answer: C MIBG (metaiodobenzylguanidine) scan detects extra-adrenal pheos and ectopic tumors that imaging misses. It's the next step after normal CT/MRI. VMA is less sensitive than plasma free metanephrines (outdated for diagnosis). Genetic testing comes after tumor localization.

Q4: The first counterregulatory response to hypoglycemia is:

Answer: B The FIRST response is decreased insulin (removes suppression of glucose production). Glucagon follows and is the MOST IMPORTANT counterregulatory hormone. Epinephrine comes 3rd (warning symptoms). Cortisol is 4th (delayed).

Q5: Which hormone is MOST IMPORTANT for counteracting hypoglycemia?

Answer: B Glucagon is the MOST IMPORTANT counterregulatory hormone. It directly stimulates hepatic glucose production. In Type 1 diabetes, glucagon response becomes blunted → hypoglycemia unawareness.

Q6: A 58-year-old ICU patient on mechanical ventilation has T3=40 (low), rT3=35 (high), TSH=1.2 (normal). What is the most appropriate management?

Answer: C Non-thyroidal illness (NTIS/euthyroid sick syndrome) is an adaptive stress response: ↓ D1, ↑ D3 → ↓ T3, ↑ rT3, normal TSH. Treatment with thyroid hormone worsens outcomes. Wait for recovery.

Q7: An ICU patient post-trauma has persistent hyperglycemia (250-350 mg/dL) despite insulin infusion. The previous approach of tight glucose control (80-110 mg/dL) is now avoided because:

Answer: B NICE-SUGAR trial (2009) showed intensive glucose control (80-110) in ICU → increased mortality from hypoglycemia and infections. Target now 140-180 mg/dL. Stress hyperglycemia is adaptive and shouldn't be overcorrected.

Q8: Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome is caused by which organism?

Answer: C Neisseria meningitidis (meningococcus) causes fulminant meningococcemia → DIC → bilateral adrenal hemorrhage → acute adrenal insufficiency. Presents with petechial rash, shock, high mortality if not recognized.

Q9: A septic patient with refractory hypotension (MAP 62 on norepinephrine 20 mcg/min + 6L fluids) has cortisol 18 mcg/dL and inadequate response to ACTH stimulation test. What is the most appropriate next step?

Answer: B CIRCI (Critical-Illness Related Corticosteroid Insufficiency): inadequate cortisol relative to illness severity → refractory hypotension. Hydrocortisone 200 mg/day IV restores vasopressor responsiveness. CORTICUS/ADRENAL trials support this approach.

Q10: Tyrosine hydroxylase catalyzes which step in catecholamine synthesis?

Answer: B Tyrosine hydroxylase catalyzes Tyrosine → L-DOPA and is the RATE-LIMITING step of catecholamine synthesis. It requires tetrahydrofolate (BH4) as cofactor.

Q11: VMA (vanillylmandelic acid) is the:

Answer: C VMA is the final urinary metabolite of catecholamines after breakdown by MAO and COMT. However, plasma free metanephrines have higher sensitivity/specificity for pheo diagnosis than VMA.

Q12: A 28-year-old with Type 1 diabetes has recurrent severe hypoglycemia with minimal warning symptoms. Glucose is found at 35 mg/dL during sleep. What is the BEST management strategy?

Answer: B T1DM with hypoglycemia unawareness = blunted glucagon response. Intensive insulin worsens hypoglycemia frequency. Strict hypoglycemia avoidance preserves remaining counterregulatory response. Higher BG targets, CGM, and glucagon kit are safer.

Q13: Cortisol's anti-inflammatory mechanism includes which of the following? (Select all that apply)

Answer: A, C, E Cortisol ↓ NF-κB (transcription factor for pro-inflammatory genes), ↓ COX-2/PLA2 (prostaglandins), and ↑ neutrophil demargination. It does NOT ↑ mast cells or cytokines; those are ↓.

Q14: Classic stress leukogram includes all of the following EXCEPT:

Answer: C Stress leukogram: neutrophilia (demargination), lymphopenia, eosinopenia (NOT eosinophilia), monocytopenia. All are cortisol/catecholamine effects from acute stress.

Q15: A 40-year-old woman with known Addison's disease is in the OR for abdominal surgery. Standard maintenance hydrocortisone is 20 mg daily. What is the appropriate perioperative steroid dosing?

Answer: B Surgical stress requires stress-dose steroids: hydrocortisone 50-100 mg IV q6-8h. Maintain stress dosing 24-48h postop, then taper back to 20 mg daily. Failure to stress-dose → adrenal crisis, cardiovascular collapse.

Q16: An ectopic ACTH-secreting small cell lung cancer presents with cortisol 95 ng/dL, ACTH 180 pg/mL (markedly elevated), K 2.8, metabolic alkalosis, and BP 165/95. Which of the following DISTINGUISHES this from pituitary Cushing's?

Answer: B Ectopic ACTH is fulminant: extreme cortisol → severe hypokalemia (mineralocorticoid effect), NO time for fat redistribution, hyperpigmentation (high ACTH → MSH). Pituitary Cushing's is gradual (central obesity, buffalo hump develop over months).

Q17: Which of the following statements about cortisol's permissive role in acute stress is TRUE?

Answer: C Cortisol is permissive, not primary. It ↑ adrenergic receptor expression. Without cortisol (Addison's), high catecholamines are ineffective → cardiovascular collapse despite catecholamine presence.

Q18: A 35-year-old with severe depression has an abnormal 1-mg dexamethasone suppression test (cortisol 2.1 ng/dL at 8 AM). What is the MOST appropriate next step?

Answer: B Abnormal DST in depression is NOT diagnostic of Cushing's. Depression causes mild HPA activation. Distinguish with clinical features (no striae, HTN, myopathy) and confirmatory tests (UFC, late-night salivary cortisol). Treat depression; reassess HPA after mood improves.

Lecture 14: Diagnosis & Treatment of Thyroid Disorders

Subject: Pharmacology | Focus: Thyroid & Calcium Treatment

LO1: Hypothyroidism Treatment — Levothyroxine

▼

Levothyroxine (LT4, Synthroid)

  • Synthetic T4; preferred over T3 (liothyronine) for replacement
  • Why T4 not T3: T4 has longer half-life (~7 days vs 1 day), more stable levels, peripherally converted to T3 as needed; T3 peaks and troughs
  • Dosing: Weight-based ~1.6 μg/kg/day; start low in elderly and cardiac patients, then titrate
  • Critical: Take on EMPTY STOMACH (30–60 min before food) — food, calcium, iron, aluminum antacids, PPIs decrease absorption
  • Monitoring:
    • TSH (in primary hypothyroidism)
    • Free T4 (in secondary hypothyroidism — TSH unreliable)
    • Steady state achieved in 4–6 weeks → recheck TSH 4–8 weeks after dose change
  • Pregnancy: Dose needs ↑ ~25–30% immediately (↑ TBG, ↑ T4 clearance, fetal demand)

Liothyronine (LT3)

  • Short-acting; used in: myxedema coma (IV), thyroid cancer monitoring (withdrawal), some patients who feel better on T4+T3 combo

Drug Interactions

  • Warfarin: ↑ clotting factor metabolism → ↑ INR — may need ↓ warfarin dose when starting T4
  • Digoxin: ↑ digoxin requirements

Myxedema Coma — Treatment

  • IV levothyroxine (200–400 μg bolus) + IV hydrocortisone (concurrent adrenal insufficiency common) + supportive (warming, ventilation)

Subclinical Hypothyroidism — When to Treat?

  • Treat if: TSH >10, OR symptomatic, OR pregnant, OR cardiovascular risk factors
Levothyroxine must be taken on empty stomach; food, calcium, iron, and PPIs all reduce absorption. Monitor TSH in primary hypo; free T4 in secondary hypo. Steady state = 4–6 weeks.
Many students forget that T3 peaks/troughs (less stable), while T4 is preferred because of its longer half-life and gradual release. Also: PPIs decrease absorption — important in patients on both drugs.
• T4 = TIME-RELEASE (long half-life, stable); T3 = TURBO (fast, short, emergencies)
• Empty stomach 30–60 min before food
• Pregnancy: ↑ dose 25–30% immediately
• Monitor TSH (primary); free T4 (secondary)
"T4 = TIME-RELEASE (long half-life, stable); T3 = TURBO (fast, short, used in emergencies)"

LO2: Hyperthyroidism Treatment — Antithyroid Drugs

▼

Thionamides Overview

Block thyroid hormone synthesis at TPO level. Two main agents with different profiles:

Propylthiouracil (PTU)

  • Mechanism: Blocks TPO (organification + coupling) AND peripheral T4→T3 conversion (5'-deiodinase) — unique advantage
  • Dosing: 3x/day (shorter half-life)
  • Preferred in:
    • Thyroid storm (FIRST-LINE due to peripheral conversion block)
    • 1st trimester pregnancy (crosses placenta LESS than methimazole)
  • Side effects:
    • Agranulocytosis (rare, ~0.5% — fever + sore throat → STOP drug + check WBC)
    • Hepatotoxicity (BLACK BOX — rare but severe)
    • Rash, arthralgias
    • ANCA-vasculitis (PTU-specific)

Methimazole (MMI, Tapazole)

  • Mechanism: Blocks TPO only (NOT peripheral conversion)
  • Dosing: Once daily (longer half-life) → better compliance
  • Preferred: Over PTU in most non-pregnant patients (less hepatotoxicity)
  • TERATOGENIC: Aplasia cutis (scalp defect), choanal atresia, esophageal atresia, omphalocele → AVOID in 1st trimester
  • Pregnancy switch: MMI → PTU in 1st trimester; then PTU → MMI after 1st trimester (PTU more hepatotoxic long-term)
  • Side effects: Agranulocytosis (same class warning), rash, urticaria, arthralgias

Shared Warnings & Management

  • Agranulocytosis: Warn ALL patients to report fever/sore throat immediately; check CBC; ANC <500 → permanently stop drug
  • Both drugs: Used to render patient euthyroid BEFORE surgery or radioiodine; can cross placenta → neonatal hypothyroidism risk (use lowest effective dose)
Feature PTU Methimazole
Blocks peripheral conversion YES ✓ (unique) NO
Dosing frequency 3x/day Once daily
1st trimester preference YES ✓ NO — teratogenic
Hepatotoxicity risk Higher (black box) Lower
Thyroid storm PREFERRED No
PTU is preferred in 1st trimester pregnancy AND thyroid storm (because it blocks peripheral T4→T3 conversion). Methimazole is teratogenic (aplasia cutis, choanal atresia). Both cause agranulocytosis — counsel patients to report fever/sore throat immediately.
Common mistake: Using methimazole in 1st trimester (it's teratogenic!). Also: Forgetting that PTU has TWO mechanisms (synthesis + peripheral conversion) — this is why it's superior in thyroid storm. Another trap: Continuing drug after agranulocytosis develops — must stop immediately.
• PTU = 1st trimester + Thyroid Storm + Peripheral conversion block + ANCA-vasculitis
• Methimazole = Daily dosing, less hepatotoxic, teratogenic (avoid 1st trim)
• Both: agranulocytosis risk — fever/sore throat = STOP + CBC
• Switch MMI→PTU→MMI in pregnancy
"PTU = Pregnancy (1st trim), Thyroid storm, ANCA-vasculitis, blocks pEripheral conversion"

LO3: Hyperthyroidism — RAI, Surgery, Beta-Blockers

▼

Radioiodine (¹³¹I)

  • Mechanism: Follicular cells take up ¹³¹I via NIS → emits beta radiation → destroys thyroid tissue
  • Indications: Graves' disease (first-line option in US), toxic MNG, toxic adenoma
  • Absolute contraindications:
    • PREGNANCY (causes fetal hypothyroidism/cretinism)
    • Breastfeeding
    • Active ophthalmopathy (can WORSEN — use antithyroid drugs or surgery instead)
    • Large goiter with compressive symptoms
  • Pre-RAI prep: Must be euthyroid BEFORE RAI (antithyroid drugs → stop 3–5 days before RAI → restart after to bridge)
  • Expected outcome: Hypothyroidism in most (treated with levothyroxine long-term)
  • Graves' ophthalmopathy: RAI can worsen (immune response-related); cover with glucocorticoids if mild; severe → prefer surgery
  • Iodine avoidance: Must be avoided 4–6 weeks before (IV contrast, iodine-containing foods) — competes with ¹³¹I

Thyroidectomy

  • Indications:
    • Large goiter with compressive symptoms (dysphagia, tracheal compression)
    • Failed medical therapy
    • Malignancy suspected/confirmed
    • Pregnancy (if needed — 2nd trimester safest)
    • Severe ophthalmopathy
    • Patient preference
    • Children
  • Types: Total thyroidectomy (most common), hemithyroidectomy (lobectomy + isthmus)
  • Complications:
    • Recurrent laryngeal nerve injury (hoarseness — unilateral; bilateral → respiratory failure, emergent intubation)
    • Hypoparathyroidism (hypocalcemia — check Ca post-op, usually transient)
    • Bleeding/hematoma (can compress airway — surgical emergency)
    • Hypothyroidism (expected → levothyroxine)
  • Pre-operative: Must be euthyroid; Lugol's iodine (SSKI) given 10–14 days pre-op → reduces vascularity + hormone secretion (Wolff-Chaikoff effect)

Beta-Blockers (Propranolol, Atenolol, Metoprolol)

  • Mechanism: Block SYMPTOMS (palpitations, tremor, anxiety, heat intolerance) — do NOT treat the thyroid
  • Propranolol advantage: Also blocks peripheral T4→T3 conversion at high doses
  • When used: In ALL patients while awaiting definitive therapy and in thyroid storm
  • Drug choice: Non-selective (propranolol) contraindicated in asthma → use selective (atenolol)
RAI is first-line for Graves' in the US. CONTRAINDICATED in pregnancy (causes fetal cretinism) and active ophthalmopathy (can worsen). Surgery preferred for compressive goiter, malignancy, or severe ophthalmopathy. Beta-blockers treat symptoms only, not the disease.
Trap: Giving RAI without making patient euthyroid first — causes thyroid storm risk. Also: Forgetting that Graves' ophthalmopathy can WORSEN with RAI (immune response). Another: Prescribing asthmatic patients non-selective beta-blockers (propranolol) — use atenolol instead. And: Iodine must be avoided 4–6 weeks before RAI (competes with ¹³¹I).
• RAI contraindicated: pregnancy, active ophthalmopathy, large goiter (compressive)
• Euthyroid BEFORE RAI; hypothyroidism expected outcome
• Surgery: compressive goiter, malignancy, severe ophthalmopathy, pregnancy (2nd trim)
• Pre-op: Lugol's iodine 10–14 days before (Wolff-Chaikoff effect)
• Beta-blockers: symptom management only; propranolol also ↓ T4→T3 conversion
"For Graves: ABC = Antithyroid drugs, Beta-blockers, ¹³¹I/C (RAI or Chop it out = surgery)"

LO4: Thyroid Storm — Recognition and Management

▼

Overview

  • Definition: Life-threatening hyperthyroidism; medical emergency
  • Mortality: ~10–30%

Precipitants

  • Infection/sepsis (MOST COMMON)
  • Surgery, trauma, iodine load (contrast, amiodarone)
  • MI, labor/delivery, radioiodine
  • Non-compliance with antithyroid drugs

Diagnosis (Clinical)

  • No single lab diagnostic; clinical diagnosis based on Burch-Wartofsky Point Scale (BWPS)
  • Temperature, CNS effects, GI dysfunction, HR, heart failure, precipitant → score >45 = thyroid storm
  • Labs: ↑↑ T4/T3, ↓↓ TSH, hyperglycemia, ↑ LFTs, leukocytosis

Features of Thyroid Storm

  • High fever (>40°C), tachycardia (HR >140), A-fib
  • Hypertension → cardiovascular collapse
  • Agitation, psychosis, delirium
  • Nausea/vomiting/diarrhea, jaundice

Treatment (SEQUENTIAL — CRITICAL ORDER)

  1. PTU (loading 500–1000 mg, then 250 mg q4–6h): Blocks synthesis AND peripheral conversion
  2. Propranolol (IV or PO): Blocks symptoms + peripheral conversion (high dose)
  3. Iodine (Lugol's or SSKI, 1–2h AFTER PTU): Blocks hormone release (Wolff-Chaikoff); MUST give PTU first (otherwise iodine = substrate for synthesis)
  4. Glucocorticoids (dexamethasone or hydrocortisone): Blocks peripheral T4→T3 conversion, treats relative adrenal insufficiency
  5. Supportive: Cooling (acetaminophen PREFERRED over aspirin), fluids, O₂, treat precipitant

Critical Drug Interaction

  • Aspirin CONTRAINDICATED in thyroid storm — displaces T4 from TBG → ↑ free T4 → WORSENS crisis
  • Use acetaminophen for fever instead
Thyroid storm is a medical emergency. Treatment is sequential: PTU FIRST (blocks both synthesis and peripheral conversion), then propranolol, then iodine (1–2h after PTU), then steroids. Aspirin is contraindicated. Know the Burch-Wartofsky Point Scale for diagnosis.
CRITICAL: Iodine AFTER PTU, not before. If iodine given first, it becomes substrate for synthesis → WORSENS. Also: Using aspirin for fever (WRONG — use acetaminophen). Another trap: Forgetting that PTU has two mechanisms (synthesis + peripheral conversion) — this is why it's first-line in thyroid storm, not methimazole.
• Thyroid storm = medical emergency (10–30% mortality)
• Treatment order: PTU (synthesis + conversion) → Propranolol → Iodine (1–2h after) → Steroids
• Iodine AFTER PTU (critical!)
• Aspirin CONTRAINDICATED (displaces T4 from TBG)
• Infection is most common precipitant
"BIGG treatment = Block synthesis (PTU), Inhibit release (iodine — after PTU!), Give beta-blocker, Glucocorticoids"

LO5: Thyroid Cancer Treatment

▼

Papillary Thyroid Carcinoma (PTC)

  • Surgery: Total thyroidectomy (for tumors >1 cm or high-risk features); hemithyroidectomy may suffice for microcarcinoma <1 cm, low risk
  • Radioiodine (¹³¹I): For remnant ablation and to treat metastases (follicular cells have NIS); NOT effective for MTC or anaplastic
  • TSH suppression therapy: Give supraphysiologic LT4 to suppress TSH (→ ↓ TSH-driven tumor growth); target TSH <0.1 mIU/L in high-risk; side effects: A-fib, osteoporosis
  • Surveillance: Thyroglobulin (Tg) as tumor marker (if total thyroidectomy → undetectable Tg; rising Tg = recurrence); neck ultrasound; whole-body RAI scan
  • Prognosis: Excellent (10-year survival >95%)

Follicular Thyroid Carcinoma (FTC)

  • Treatment: Total thyroidectomy + RAI ablation
  • Surveillance: Thyroglobulin + ultrasound
  • Metastases: Blood-borne mets (lung, bone) treated with RAI if iodine-avid

Medullary Thyroid Carcinoma (MTC)

  • Surgery: Total thyroidectomy + central neck dissection (lymph node-heavy spread)
  • RAI ineffective: C cells have NO NIS → does NOT respond to RAI
  • Tumor markers:
    • Calcitonin (primary — follow pre/post-op and for recurrence)
    • CEA (secondary)
  • Genetics (CRITICAL): ALL MTC patients → RET mutation testing (germline) → if positive, test family members → prophylactic thyroidectomy for RET+ carriers
  • MEN2 association: Screen for pheochromocytoma BEFORE thyroidectomy (must treat pheo first!)
  • Advanced/metastatic: Kinase inhibitors: vandetanib (RET + VEGFR inhibitor), cabozantinib

Anaplastic Thyroid Carcinoma

  • Approach: Palliative intent usually; aggressive multimodal therapy
  • Targeted therapy: BRAF V600E+ → dabrafenib + trametinib (FDA approved)
  • Prognosis: Poor (median survival ~5 months)

Thyroid Nodule Management (Bethesda Classification)

  • Bethesda I (non-diagnostic): Repeat FNA
  • Bethesda II (benign): Follow with ultrasound
  • Bethesda III/IV (indeterminate/follicular neoplasm): Molecular testing (ThyroSeq, Afirma) or lobectomy
  • Bethesda V/VI (suspicious/malignant): Surgery
PTC and FTC respond to RAI (have NIS). MTC does NOT respond to RAI — use RET kinase inhibitors instead. All MTC patients need RET testing (germline); positive → prophylactic thyroidectomy for family. In MTC, monitor calcitonin (not thyroglobulin). Screen for pheo BEFORE thyroidectomy in MEN2.
Trap: Giving RAI to MTC patient (won't work — C cells lack NIS). Another: Forgetting RET genetic testing in MTC (ALL patients need it). Also: Not screening for pheochromocytoma BEFORE MTC surgery (can cause intraop crisis). Another: Using thyroglobulin as tumor marker in MTC (wrong — use calcitonin).
• PTC/FTC: respond to RAI, follow Tg, surveillance ultrasound, good prognosis
• MTC: NO RAI, RET testing (all patients!), calcitonin marker, prophylactic thyroidectomy for RET+ family
• MEN2: Screen for pheo BEFORE thyroidectomy
• Anaplastic: poor prognosis, dabrafenib+trametinib if BRAF V600E+
• TSH suppression therapy in PTC: target TSH <0.1 (risks: A-fib, osteoporosis)
"PTC and FTC love RAI; MTC = RET + calcitonin + NO RAI; Anaplastic = Run (poor prognosis)"

LO6: Calcium Disorder Treatment — Hypo and Hypercalcemia

▼

Hypocalcemia Treatment

  • Acute (symptomatic — tetany, seizures):
    • IV calcium gluconate (preferred over calcium chloride — less tissue damage if extravasation)
    • If refractory → check/correct Mg first (hypomagnesemia BLOCKS PTH action)
  • Chronic:
    • Oral calcium carbonate (with food → needs acid for absorption) or calcium citrate (without food, better in achlorhydria/PPIs)
    • + Calcitriol (active vitamin D = 1,25-OH₂ D₃) if hypoparathyroid
  • Hypoparathyroidism: Calcium + calcitriol ± recombinant PTH (natpara) for chronic management
  • DiGeorge: Calcitriol + calcium; may eventually get parathyroid auto-transplant
  • Hypomagnesemia-induced: Treat Mg FIRST (PTH won't work without adequate Mg)

Hypercalcemia Treatment

  • First-line (all patients):
    • IV normal saline (0.9% NaCl) hydration → ↑ renal Ca²⁺ excretion
    • NO furosemide anymore (dehydrates → worsens, not proven to help)
  • Bisphosphonates (zoledronate IV, pamidronate):
    • Inhibit osteoclasts → ↓ bone resorption → ↓ Ca
    • Use for malignancy-associated hypercalcemia
    • Takes 2–4 days for full effect
    • Nephrotoxic (check Cr first)
  • Calcitonin (salmon):
    • Fast-acting (hours), short-lived (tachyphylaxis in 48–72h)
    • Use for acute severe hypercalcemia while awaiting bisphosphonate effect
  • Denosumab (RANK-L inhibitor): For bisphosphonate-refractory malignant hypercalcemia
  • Glucocorticoids: For vitamin D toxicity, sarcoidosis, lymphoma (↓ 1α-hydroxylase activity in macrophages)
  • Cinacalcet (calcimimetic): For 2° HPT in CKD; mimics Ca at CaSR → ↓ PTH; also for inoperable parathyroid carcinoma
  • Definitive: Parathyroidectomy for primary HPT (if indicated); treat underlying malignancy

Calcium Correction for Albumin

  • Corrected Ca = measured Ca + 0.8 × (4 - albumin)
  • Alternative: Check ionized Ca directly

PTHrP (PTH-Related Protein)

  • From malignancy: Squamous cell carcinoma lung, breast, renal cell, bladder
  • Mechanism: Binds PTH receptor → ↑ Ca²⁺, ↓ phosphate, ↑ ALP
  • Labs (distinguishes from 1° HPT):
    • ↑ Ca, ↓ phosphate, ↑↑ PTHrP
    • LOW PTH (vs. HIGH in 1° HPT)
    • LOW 1,25-OH D (malignancy suppresses 1α-hydroxylase)
Hypercalcemia treatment: IV saline first (increases renal excretion), then bisphosphonates (takes 2–4 days). Calcitonin used for acute severe cases (but tachyphylaxis develops). For hypocalcemia: IV calcium gluconate acutely; check/treat hypomagnesemia first (PTH won't work without Mg). Corrected calcium accounts for albumin.
Trap: Using furosemide for hypercalcemia (outdated — it dehydrates and doesn't help; use saline instead). Another: Forgetting to check Mg in hypocalcemic patients — hypomagnesemia blocks PTH action, so treating Ca alone won't work. Also: Giving calcium carbonate without food (needs acid for absorption — use calcium citrate without food instead). And: Not correcting calcium for low albumin.
• Hypercalcemia: IV NS (first-line), bisphosphonates (takes 2–4 days), calcitonin (acute, tachyphylaxis)
• NO furosemide (outdated)
• Hypocalcemia acute: IV calcium gluconate; check/treat Mg first
• Calcium carbonate = with food (needs acid); citrate = without food
• Hypomagnesemia blocks PTH — treat Mg FIRST
• Correct Ca for albumin or use ionized Ca
"Hypercalcemia treatment = FINS = Fluids IV, IV bisphosphonates, No furosemide, Steroids (if sarcoid/lymphoma)"

Practice MCQs: Thyroid & Calcium Treatment

▼
1. A 45-year-old woman with primary hypothyroidism is started on levothyroxine 50 μg daily. She returns 4 weeks later complaining of persistent fatigue. Her TSH is still elevated at 8 mIU/L. Which of the following is the most likely explanation for her inadequate response?
✓ CORRECT: B) Steady state is not achieved at 4 weeks; recheck at 8 weeks after dose adjustment
Levothyroxine has a half-life of ~7 days. Steady state is achieved in 4–6 weeks, NOT at 4 weeks. You should recheck TSH 4–8 weeks after any dose change. At 4 weeks, the patient is still approaching steady state, so rechecking at 8 weeks is more reliable. Also, assess adherence and absorption (take on empty stomach, 30–60 min before food; avoid calcium, iron, PPIs at same time).
✗ INCORRECT: A) She needs a higher dose; TSH recheck at 4 weeks is appropriate
4 weeks is too early; steady state takes 4–6 weeks. Wait until 8 weeks for more reliable TSH level before adjusting dose.
2. A 32-year-old woman in her first trimester of pregnancy presents with Graves' hyperthyroidism. Which antithyroid drug should be started?
✓ CORRECT: B) Propylthiouracil (PTU), because it blocks peripheral T4→T3 conversion and crosses placenta less
In 1st trimester pregnancy, PTU is preferred over methimazole. Methimazole is teratogenic (aplasia cutis, choanal atresia, esophageal atresia, omphalocele). PTU crosses the placenta less and also blocks peripheral conversion, making it superior. After 1st trimester, switch to methimazole (which has lower hepatotoxicity for long-term use).
3. A 28-year-old man presents to the ED in thyroid storm with HR 160, fever 41°C, and severe agitation. He was diagnosed with Graves' disease 2 months ago but has been non-compliant with medications. What is the correct order of drug administration?
✓ CORRECT: B) PTU → propranolol → Lugol's iodine (1–2h after PTU) → steroids
Thyroid storm treatment is sequential and order-dependent. PTU FIRST (blocks both synthesis and peripheral conversion). Then propranolol (blocks symptoms + adds conversion block at high dose). Then iodine 1–2h AFTER PTU (blocks hormone release via Wolff-Chaikoff effect; if given before PTU, iodine becomes substrate for synthesis, WORSENING the crisis). Finally steroids. Critical: Use acetaminophen for fever, NOT aspirin (aspirin displaces T4 from TBG, worsening crisis).
4. A 52-year-old woman with Graves' disease is being evaluated for radioiodine therapy. She mentions she has had increasing eye discomfort and lid retraction over the past month. What is the most appropriate next step?
✓ CORRECT: C) Recommend thyroidectomy rather than RAI (due to active ophthalmopathy)
Active/moderate-to-severe Graves' ophthalmopathy is a relative (some say absolute) contraindication to RAI because RAI can WORSEN ophthalmopathy via immune response. In this scenario, thyroidectomy is preferred. If mild ophthalmopathy, RAI can be considered with concurrent glucocorticoid coverage. Surgery avoids the immune-mediated worsening of eye disease.
5. A 24-year-old man with medullary thyroid carcinoma (MTC) is being prepared for total thyroidectomy. Before surgery, which additional screen is ESSENTIAL?
✓ CORRECT: C) Plasma metanephrines or 24-h urine catecholamines for pheochromocytoma
MTC is part of MEN2 syndrome. BEFORE thyroidectomy, you MUST screen for pheochromocytoma (present in ~50% of MEN2B patients). If a pheo is present and you don't know, induction of anesthesia and surgical manipulation can trigger massive catecholamine release → hypertensive crisis, MI, arrhythmias. Always screen for pheo FIRST, then treat it first if found. Also: All MTC patients need RET genetic testing (germline mutation) and family screening.
6. A 68-year-old woman with chronic kidney disease is hospitalized for acute symptomatic hypocalcemia (Ca 6.8 mg/dL, ionized Ca low). She has tetany and positive Chvostek sign. What is your first management priority?
✓ CORRECT: B) Give IV calcium gluconate; check serum magnesium level
Acute symptomatic hypocalcemia (tetany, seizures) requires IMMEDIATE IV calcium gluconate. CRITICAL: Check magnesium level. If the patient has hypomagnesemia (common in CKD), PTH secretion and action are blocked — calcium supplementation alone WON'T work. Treat hypomagnesemia FIRST (IV magnesium) before or simultaneously with calcium. Then add calcitriol (active vitamin D) for chronic management. Calcium gluconate is preferred over chloride (less tissue damage if extravasation).
7. A 62-year-old man with squamous cell lung cancer develops hypercalcemia (Ca 12.8 mg/dL, albumin low at 2.8). He is asymptomatic. Labs show: PTH low, PTHrP elevated, phosphate low, 1,25-OH D low. What is the appropriate first treatment step?
✓ CORRECT: B) IV normal saline (0.9% NaCl) to enhance renal calcium excretion
First-line treatment for ANY hypercalcemia is IV normal saline hydration. This increases glomerular filtration and renal calcium excretion. Bisphosphonates and calcitonin are adjunctive (bisphosphonates take 2–4 days). Furosemide is OUTDATED and contraindicated (dehydrates patient, worsens hypercalcemia). Once hydrated, bisphosphonates can be started. This is PTHrP-mediated hypercalcemia from lung cancer. Corrected calcium = 12.8 + 0.8 × (4 – 2.8) = 13.76 mg/dL (still elevated despite low albumin).
8. A 35-year-old woman with papillary thyroid carcinoma (T4, N1b, M0) undergoes total thyroidectomy and RAI ablation. She is started on TSH suppression therapy with high-dose levothyroxine (target TSH <0.1 mIU/L). Which of the following complications is she at HIGHEST risk for during long-term TSH suppression?
✓ CORRECT: B) Atrial fibrillation and osteoporosis
TSH suppression therapy (supraphysiologic thyroid hormone) increases risk of atrial fibrillation (from chronic thyroid hormone excess) and osteoporosis (high thyroid hormone decreases bone density, especially in postmenopausal women). TSH must be suppressed to <0.1 mIU/L in high-risk PTC to prevent TSH-driven tumor growth, but long-term side effects must be monitored. Regular cardiovascular and bone density screening is recommended.
9. A 48-year-old woman is taking levothyroxine 75 μg daily for hypothyroidism. She develops GI symptoms and is started on a proton pump inhibitor for GERD. Her TSH rises to 7.2 mIU/L at follow-up. What is the most likely explanation?
✓ CORRECT: B) PPIs decrease levothyroxine absorption by raising gastric pH
Levothyroxine absorption requires acidic gastric pH. PPIs suppress gastric acid → ↓ levothyroxine dissolution and absorption → ↓ serum T4 → ↑ TSH. Solution: Take levothyroxine at least 30–60 minutes before PPI, or separate by 4+ hours. Other agents that reduce absorption: calcium, iron, aluminum antacids. Since PPI was just started, it's the most likely culprit.
10. A 19-year-old man with newly diagnosed medullary thyroid carcinoma is RET mutation positive. Genetic testing identifies him as part of an MEN2A family. What is the recommendation for his asymptomatic siblings who carry the RET mutation?
✓ CORRECT: B) Prophylactic (preventive) thyroidectomy because they carry RET mutation
RET-positive family members in MEN2A/MEN2B have near 100% penetrance for MTC development. Prophylactic thyroidectomy is recommended in childhood/adolescence (timing depends on RET genotype and penetrance), BEFORE malignancy develops. This is one of the few accepted uses of prophylactic surgery in genetic cancer syndromes. Screening for pheochromocytoma BEFORE thyroidectomy is mandatory (to avoid intraop crisis). RAI won't work in MTC (C cells lack NIS). PTU doesn't prevent MTC development.

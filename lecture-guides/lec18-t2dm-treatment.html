1 Lifestyle & Stepwise Approach to T2DM
▾
🎯 Tested Concepts
Stepwise approach; HbA1c targets; treatment algorithm; lifestyle foundation; MNT + exercise + weight loss
⚠️ Common Traps
Ignoring lifestyle (always FIRST); wrong HbA1c target (not universal <7%); skipping patient factors in drug selection
⭐ High Yield
5-10% wt loss → ↓ HbA1c; bariatric surgery remission 50-70%; 150 min/week aerobic exercise

T2DM Management: The Foundation — Lifestyle First

  • Lifestyle modification is FOUNDATIONAL for all stages — diet, exercise, weight loss, smoking cessation
  • Medical Nutrition Therapy (MNT): reduce refined carbohydrates, moderate caloric restriction for weight loss, Mediterranean/DASH patterns
  • Physical activity: 150 min/week moderate-intensity aerobic + resistance training → ↑ GLUT4 translocation (insulin-independent glucose uptake)
  • Weight loss: 5-10% body weight → meaningful ↓ HbA1c, BP, lipids; 10-15% loss → even greater benefit
  • Bariatric surgery: 50-70% T2DM remission (via GLP-1 effect, caloric restriction, metabolic remodeling)

HbA1c Targets (Individualized)

  • <7% for most non-pregnant adults (standard target)
  • <6.5% if achievable without hypoglycemia (young, new DM, no CV disease)
  • <8% for elderly, limited life expectancy, frequent hypoglycemia, extensive comorbidities

2023 ADA Treatment Algorithm (Stepwise)

  1. Step 1: Lifestyle + Metformin (first-line if no contraindications)
  2. Step 2: Add second agent based on PATIENT-SPECIFIC FACTORS (not just HbA1c):
    • • Established ASCVD or high CV risk → GLP-1 agonist or SGLT2 inhibitor (proven CV benefit)
    • • Heart failure → SGLT2 inhibitor (↓ HF hospitalizations)
    • • CKD → SGLT2 inhibitor (renal protection), GLP-1 agonist
    • • Need for weight loss → GLP-1 agonist or SGLT2 inhibitor
    • • Cost concerns → sulfonylurea or thiazolidinedione
    • • Need to avoid hypoglycemia → DPP-4, GLP-1, SGLT2
  3. Step 3: Progress to triple therapy or insulin if not at goal
Mnemonic
"Treat T2DM with LEGAL drugs: Lifestyle, Exercise, GLP-1/SGLT2 (for ASCVD/HF/CKD), Agents (metformin first), Last resort = insulin"
2 Metformin: Mechanism, Benefits, Contraindications
▾
🎯 Tested Concepts
AMPK activation; ↓ gluconeogenesis; NO hypoglycemia; eGFR cutoffs; lactic acidosis; B12 deficiency; contrast hold
⚠️ Common Traps
Hold for contrast (48h after if stable); check B12 annually; contraindicated eGFR <30 (caution 30-45); lactic acidosis with renal/liver/hypoxia
⭐ High Yield
First-line drug; ↓ CVD events (UKPDS); no weight gain; safe in renal insufficiency eGFR ≥30; AMPK ↓ gluconeogenesis

Mechanism: AMPK Activation

  • Activates AMP-kinase (AMPK) → metabolic master switch
  • PRIMARY: ↓ hepatic gluconeogenesis (↓ PEPCK, ↓ G6Pase) — MAIN glucose-lowering effect
  • ↑ peripheral insulin sensitivity (↑ muscle GLUT4 expression)
  • ↓ intestinal glucose absorption
  • Modulates gut microbiome (emerging evidence)
  • Mild weight loss or weight neutral

Benefits

  • NO hypoglycemia (no insulin release)
  • ↓ cardiovascular events (UKPDS benefit)
  • Weight-neutral or modest weight loss
  • ↓ LDL, inexpensive

Side Effects

  • GI: nausea, diarrhea, metallic taste (most common; take with food to reduce)
  • Lactic acidosis: rare but serious → ↑ risk in renal insufficiency (accumulation), liver failure, CHF, hypoxia, contrast administration
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency: chronic use → ↓ absorption → check B12 annually

Contraindications & Drug Holds

  • eGFR <30: contraindicated; use cautiously if eGFR 30-45 (reduce dose)
  • IV iodinated contrast: HOLD metformin at time of procedure → restart 48h after if renal function stable (lactic acidosis risk from contrast-induced nephropathy)
  • Hepatic failure, CHF, respiratory failure (hypoxia → lactic acidosis risk)
Mnemonic
"Metformin = Main drug; blocks Gluconeogenesis; No hypoglycemia; B12 deficiency risk; Beware renal/contrast"
3 Sulfonylureas, Meglitinides, & Thiazolidinediones
▾
🎯 Tested Concepts
SU K⁺-ATP mechanism; hypoglycemia + wt gain; glyburide renal issues; TZD PPAR-γ; HF contraindication; fracture risk
⚠️ Common Traps
SU causes hypoglycemia (NOT with metformin alone); glyburide unsafe in renal failure; TZD CONTRAINDICATED NYHA III-IV HF; TZD ↑ fractures (women)
⭐ High Yield
SU drug of choice for MODY3 (HNF1α); glyburide > glipizide > glimepiride (hypoglycemia risk); TZD no hypoglycemia; PPAR-γ ↑ adiponectin

SULFONYLUREAS: Glyburide, Glipizide, Glimepiride

  • Mechanism: block K⁺-ATP channels on β cells → depolarization → ↑ Ca²⁺ → insulin secretion REGARDLESS of glucose level (insulin secretagogue)
  • Receptor: SUR1 (sulfonylurea receptor 1) — part of K⁺-ATP channel complex
  • Effect: ↓ HbA1c 1-2%; stimulate insulin secretion
  • Benefits: inexpensive, effective, decades of use
  • Side effects: HYPOGLYCEMIA (most important) — insulin secretion even if glucose is low; weight gain (↑ insulin → anabolic)
  • Key differences:
    • • Glyburide: longest acting → most hypoglycemia; active metabolites accumulate in renal failure → AVOID
    • • Glipizide: shorter acting, safer in renal failure
    • • Glimepiride: once daily, less hypoglycemia risk
  • Drug of choice for MODY3 (HNF1α mutation): very sensitive to sulfonylureas
Mnemonic (SU)
"SUs = Secret insulin (secretagogue); cause hypoGlycemia and weight Gain"

MEGLITINIDES: Repaglinide, Nateglinide

  • Mechanism: same as SU (block K-ATP channels) but bind DIFFERENT site; faster onset, shorter duration
  • Dosing: taken WITH each meal (skip if meal skipped → ↓ hypoglycemia risk)
  • Less hypoglycemia than SU; useful in irregular meal patterns; can use in renal failure (repaglinide)
  • Side effects: hypoglycemia (less than SU), weight gain

THIAZOLIDINEDIONES: Pioglitazone, Rosiglitazone

  • Mechanism: PPAR-γ (peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma) agonist → nuclear receptor → ↑ adiponectin, ↑ insulin-sensitive genes → ↑ insulin sensitivity in adipose + muscle + liver
  • Redistributes fat from visceral → subcutaneous (safer metabolically)
  • Benefits: NO hypoglycemia, ↑ HDL, pioglitazone ↓ TG, durable effect
  • Side effects:
    • • Fluid retention → edema, ↑ risk HF exacerbation — CONTRAINDICATED in NYHA Class III-IV HF
    • • Weight gain (fat redistribution + fluid)
    • • Osteoporosis (↑ fracture risk — especially women; activates osteoclasts in bone)
    • • Bladder cancer risk (pioglitazone — FDA warning; rosiglitazone removed temporarily due to CV concerns)
Mnemonic (TZD)
"TZDs = Treat insulin resistance (PPAR-γ) but beware Heart Failure and Heavy bones (fractures)"
4 GLP-1 Receptor Agonists & DPP-4 Inhibitors
▾
🎯 Tested Concepts
GLP-1 RA glucose-dependent; weight loss; CV benefit (liraglutide, semaglutide); tirzepatide dual GLP-1/GIP; DPP-4 NO hypoglycemia, weight neutral
⚠️ Common Traps
GLP-1 MTC CI if personal/family hx; saxagliptin ↑ HF; DPP-4 neutral CV outcome; GLP-1 GI side effects (transient)
⭐ High Yield
GLP-1 = greatest weight loss agent; CVOT proven (LEADER, SUSTAIN-6, REWIND); DPP-4 = oral, safe, weak; tirzepatide > GLP-1 alone (dual mechanism)

GLP-1 RECEPTOR AGONISTS (GLP-1 RAs)

Drugs: liraglutide, semaglutide, dulaglutide, exenatide, tirzepatide (dual GLP-1/GIP)

  • Mechanism: activate GLP-1 receptor (Gs-coupled) → ↑ insulin secretion (GLUCOSE-DEPENDENT) → ↓ glucagon, ↓ gastric emptying (↓ postprandial glucose), ↑ satiety (→ weight loss), CNS appetite suppression
  • KEY: NO hypoglycemia when glucose is low (glucose-dependent mechanism)
  • Major benefits: WEIGHT LOSS (2-15 kg), CV benefit (liraglutide LEADER, semaglutide SUSTAIN-6 → ↓ MACE), renal protection (semaglutide FLOW trial)
  • Route: SC injection (daily or weekly); oral semaglutide (Rybelsus) — limited absorption, requires specific administration
  • Side effects: GI (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea — most common; why patients lose weight; improves with time), pancreatitis (rare), medullary thyroid cancer (rodent data only; CI in personal/family hx MTC or MEN2A/2B), kidney injury (dehydration from GI)
  • Tirzepatide (Mounjaro): dual GLP-1 + GIP agonist → superior weight loss (20-25% body weight in obesity trials)
Mnemonic (GLP-1 RA)
"GLP-1 agonists = Great Losers of Pounds (weight loss); GLUCOSE-DEPENDENT → no hypoglycemia"

DPP-4 INHIBITORS ("Gliptins")

Drugs: sitagliptin, saxagliptin, linagliptin, alogliptin

  • Mechanism: inhibit DPP-4 (dipeptidyl peptidase-4) enzyme → prevent degradation of endogenous GLP-1 and GIP → ↑ GLP-1/GIP levels (2-3-fold) → glucose-dependent insulin secretion + ↓ glucagon
  • Weaker than GLP-1 agonists (raises GLP-1 levels vs direct receptor activation)
  • Benefits: NO hypoglycemia, weight neutral, oral, well-tolerated, can use in CKD (linagliptin - hepatic elimination, no dose adjustment; others need reduction)
  • Side effects: nasopharyngitis (mild), UTIs, pancreatitis (rare, controversial), joint pain (arthralgia — FDA warning)
  • Saxagliptin: possible ↑ HF hospitalizations (SAVOR-TIMI trial) — avoid in HF
  • CV outcomes: neutral (DPP-4 do NOT reduce CV events unlike GLP-1 or SGLT2)
Mnemonic (DPP-4)
"DPP-4 inhibitors = Dipeptidyl Protease Prevented (gliptins); Neutral weight, No hypoglycemia, Nasal symptoms"
5 SGLT2 Inhibitors & Other Agents
▾
🎯 Tested Concepts
SGLT2 glucosuria mechanism; euglycemic DKA; genital yeast (most common side effect); HF benefit (non-diabetics); CKD protection; hold peri-op
⚠️ Common Traps
Euglycemic DKA (perioperative, low carb, sepsis); genital infections (yeast most common, not UTI); Fournier's gangrene (rare); canagliflozin ↑ amputation; hold 3-7 days before surgery
⭐ High Yield
SGLT2 CVOT proven (EMPA-REG, CANVAS, DECLARE); HF benefit even non-diabetics (EMPEROR, DAPA-HF); CKD benefit (CREDENCE, DAPA-CKD); lowest mortality benefit

SGLT2 INHIBITORS ("Gliflozins")

Drugs: empagliflozin, dapagliflozin, canagliflozin, ertugliflozin

  • Mechanism: inhibit SGLT2 (sodium-glucose cotransporter 2) in proximal convoluted tubule → block glucose reabsorption → glucosuria (+ natriuresis, osmotic diuresis)
  • Lower glucose by removing ~60-100g glucose/day in urine
  • Major cardiovascular/renal benefits (FDA approved indications BEYOND T2DM):
    • • ↓ MACE (CV death/MI/stroke) in established ASCVD
    • • ↓ Hospitalization for heart failure (EMPA-REG OUTCOME, DAPA-HF — even in non-diabetics)
    • • ↓ Progression of CKD (canagliflozin CREDENCE, dapagliflozin DAPA-CKD)
    • • Mechanism of HF/CKD benefit: ↓ preload/afterload (natriuresis), ↓ RAAS, ↑ ketones (cardiac fuel), anti-fibrotic
  • Benefits: weight loss (~2-3 kg), ↓ BP (2-4 mmHg), NO hypoglycemia, cardiorenal benefits
  • Side effects:
    • • Genital mycotic infections (most common) — yeast (women), balanitis (men) — from glucosuria sugar-rich environment
    • • UTIs (mild ↑)
    • • Euglycemic DKA (perioperative, low carb diet, sepsis) — insulin requirements ↑, glucosuria continues → ketosis without high glucose
    • • Volume depletion / hypotension (elderly, diuretics)
    • • Fournier's gangrene (necrotizing fasciitis perineum — rare, serious FDA warning)
    • • Canagliflozin: ↑ fracture risk, possible amputation risk (lower extremity)
    • • Lower extremity amputations: canagliflozin (CANVAS trial); possibly drug-class effect
  • Hold SGLT2: 3-7 days before surgery (euglycemic DKA risk perioperatively); hold in contrast procedures, acute illness
  • Dose adjustment CKD: empagliflozin/dapagliflozin for CKD benefit to eGFR ≥20; less glucose-lowering at low eGFR
Mnemonic (SGLT2)
"SGLT2 = Spills Glucose Like a Toilet (glucosuria); Saves hearts and kidneys; Sexy complications (genital yeast)"

ALPHA-GLUCOSIDASE INHIBITORS: Acarbose, Miglitol

  • Mechanism: inhibit intestinal α-glucosidase → ↓ starch/disaccharide digestion → ↓ postprandial glucose
  • Mainly postprandial glucose lowering
  • Side effects: flatulence, GI bloating (undigested carbs fermented) — poorly tolerated
  • NO hypoglycemia; weight neutral
  • IMPORTANT: if hypoglycemia occurs while on alpha-glucosidase inhibitor, treat with GLUCOSE (not sucrose) — sucrose requires α-glucosidase for absorption (blocked!)

Other Agents (Brief Overview)

  • Pramlintide: amylin analog; see Lec 17 (T1DM adjunct); also approved for T2DM on insulin
  • Colesevelam: bile acid sequestrant; modest glucose lowering; combined diabetes + hypercholesterolemia; GI side effects
  • Bromocriptine: dopamine D2 agonist; modest HbA1c lowering; mechanism unclear; add-on therapy
6 Insulin in T2DM, Hypoglycemia Risk, & CV Drug Selection
▾
🎯 Tested Concepts
When to start insulin (high HbA1c, oral failure, acute illness); basal-bolus regimens; hypoglycemia risk by drug; CVOT outcomes; special populations
⚠️ Common Traps
Hypoglycemia HIGH: insulin/SU/meglitinides; NO: metformin/TZD/GLP-1/DPP-4/SGLT2; CV benefit ONLY GLP-1 & SGLT2 (NOT DPP-4); saxagliptin HF risk
⭐ High Yield
CVOT proven agents (GLP-1/SGLT2); HF = SGLT2 only; CKD = SGLT2 > GLP-1; avoid hypoglycemia in elderly; MACE reduction = GLP-1 & SGLT2

When to Start Insulin in T2DM

  • HbA1c very high (>10%), glucose >300, symptomatic hyperglycemia → start insulin promptly
  • Failure of multiple oral agents
  • Acute illness, surgery, hospitalization
  • Pregnancy (insulin preferred if oral agents fail)

Insulin Regimens in T2DM

  • Start: basal insulin (glargine or degludec) once daily; typically 10 units or 0.2 units/kg at bedtime; titrate 2 units every 3 days to fasting glucose target
  • Intensify: add prandial insulin (basal-plus → basal-bolus) for postprandial control
  • Premixed insulins (70/30, 75/25): fixed intermediate + rapid; less flexible; ↑ hypoglycemia risk

Hypoglycemia Risk by Drug Class

  • HIGH risk: insulin, sulfonylureas, meglitinides
  • NO/LOW risk: metformin, TZDs, GLP-1 agonists, DPP-4 inhibitors, SGLT2 inhibitors, alpha-glucosidase inhibitors

Cardiovascular Outcomes Trials (CVOTs) — KEY for Step 1

  • GLP-1 agonists with CV benefit: liraglutide (LEADER), semaglutide (SUSTAIN-6), dulaglutide (REWIND), albiglutide (HARMONY) → ↓ MACE
  • SGLT2 with CV benefit: empagliflozin (EMPA-REG), canagliflozin (CANVAS), dapagliflozin (DECLARE)
  • SGLT2 with HF benefit: empagliflozin (EMPEROR-Reduced), dapagliflozin (DAPA-HF) — even in non-diabetics
  • SGLT2 with CKD benefit: canagliflozin (CREDENCE), dapagliflozin (DAPA-CKD)
  • DPP-4: neutral CV (saxagliptin slight ↑ HF hospitalizations SAVOR-TIMI — avoid in HF)

T2DM Special Populations

  • CKD: SGLT2 preferred; GLP-1 second; avoid metformin if eGFR <30; dose-adjust most drugs
  • Heart failure: SGLT2 first-line; avoid TZDs, saxagliptin
  • Obesity: GLP-1 agonists (semaglutide, tirzepatide) → greatest weight loss
  • Elderly: avoid hypoglycemia; target HbA1c <8%; prefer DPP-4 or GLP-1 (no hypoglycemia)
  • Pregnancy: insulin preferred; metformin and glyburide used but not preferred (cross placenta)
Mnemonic
"For CV disease in T2DM: GOAL = GLP-1 Or SGLT2 Are the Lead; For HF = SGLT2 always; For CKD = SGLT2 first, GLP-1 second"

T2DM Treatment Algorithm: Mind Map

Central: T2DM → Lifestyle + Metformin → Add-on (SU/TZD/GLP-1/DPP-4/SGLT2) → Insulin

MCQ Bank: 18 USMLE-Style Questions

1. A 58-year-old man with type 2 diabetes is started on metformin for glucose control. Before prescribing, you check his renal function. Which eGFR cutoff would be an absolute contraindication to metformin use?
✓ CORRECT: C) eGFR <30
Why: Metformin is contraindicated at eGFR <30 due to lactic acidosis risk (metformin accumulates in renal insufficiency). eGFR 30-45 requires caution and dose reduction. HY Trap: Students often forget eGFR thresholds; remember: hold for contrast; check B12 annually.
2. A patient with T2DM is scheduled for cardiac angiography with IV iodinated contrast. He is on metformin 1000 mg BID. What is the correct management?
✓ CORRECT: C) Hold at procedure; restart 48h after if renal stable
Why: IV iodinated contrast → contrast-induced nephropathy → ↑ metformin accumulation → lactic acidosis risk. Hold metformin at time of contrast; restart 48h after if renal function stable. HY: This is HIGH YIELD for Step 1 exams.
3. A 62-year-old woman with T2DM is on glipizide 10 mg BID. She calls complaining of tremor, sweating, and confusion after missing breakfast. What is the most likely diagnosis and what is the drug-mechanism explanation?
✓ CORRECT: B) Hypoglycemia; K-ATP channel blockade
Why: Sulfonylureas (glyburide, glipizide) block K⁺-ATP channels → depolarization → insulin secretion REGARDLESS of glucose level. Unlike GLP-1 agonists (glucose-dependent), SU cause hypoglycemia even at low glucose. HY Trap: Most important SU side effect is HYPOGLYCEMIA.
4. A 55-year-old man with T2DM and known NYHA Class II heart failure is being considered for additional glucose-lowering therapy. Which drug is CONTRAINDICATED?
✓ CORRECT: C) TZD — contraindicated NYHA III-IV HF
Why: TZDs → fluid retention, edema → worsens HF. Contraindicated in NYHA Class III-IV (severe HF); caution in Class II. SGLT2 inhibitors are BENEFICIAL for HF (even non-diabetics). GLP-1 and DPP-4 are neutral/safe in HF. HY Trap: TZD + HF = danger; SGLT2 + HF = benefit.
5. A 72-year-old woman with T2DM is started on liraglutide (GLP-1 agonist). She experiences mild nausea and vomiting in the first 2 weeks. What counseling is most appropriate?
✓ CORRECT: C) Transient GI effects; improve over time
Why: GLP-1 RAs → slowed gastric emptying + satiety → nausea, vomiting, diarrhea. These are TRANSIENT (improve in 1-4 weeks) and are the mechanism for weight loss. Not a contraindication; counsel patience and reassurance. HY: GI side effects ≠ pancreatitis (which is rare).
6. A 48-year-old man with T2DM and established ASCVD (prior MI) requires additional glucose-lowering therapy. According to 2023 ADA guidelines, which drug CLASS is recommended as FIRST add-on based on CV outcomes?
✓ CORRECT: C) GLP-1 agonist or SGLT2 inhibitor
Why: CVOTs prove GLP-1 RAs (liraglutide LEADER, semaglutide SUSTAIN-6) and SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin EMPA-REG, canagliflozin CANVAS) ↓ MACE. This is now ADA standard: CV disease → GLP-1 or SGLT2 FIRST (not SU, DPP-4, or TZD). HY: CVOT knowledge = must-know for board exams.
7. A 64-year-old man with T2DM and CKD (eGFR 25 mL/min) requires drug selection. Which agent offers BOTH glucose lowering AND proven renal protection (↓ CKD progression)?
✓ CORRECT: C) SGLT2 inhibitor
Why: SGLT2 inhibitors (especially dapagliflozin, canagliflozin) have proven CKD benefit independent of glucose lowering (DAPA-CKD, CREDENCE trials → ↓ CKD progression, ↓ ESRD, ↓ CV death). GLP-1 has CV benefit but weaker CKD evidence. SGLT2 is FIRST-LINE for CKD. HY: SGLT2 for HF/CKD; GLP-1 for CV disease/obesity.
8. A 51-year-old woman with T2DM is on canagliflozin 100 mg daily. She presents with genital itching and vaginal yeast infection. What is the mechanism?
✓ CORRECT: C) Glucosuria → glucose-rich environment
Why: SGLT2 inhibitors → block glucose reabsorption → 60-100g glucose/day in urine → creates osmotic environment favoring yeast overgrowth (balanitis in men, vulvovaginal candidiasis in women). This is the MOST COMMON SGLT2 side effect (not serious, but bothersome). Counsel good hygiene. HY Trap: "Sexy complications" (genital yeast) is HIGH YIELD.
9. A 45-year-old man with T2DM is undergoing elective knee surgery. He is on dapagliflozin (SGLT2 inhibitor) and metformin. Pre-op labs show glucose 185, eGFR 42. Which medication should be HELD 3-7 days before surgery?
✓ CORRECT: B) Hold dapagliflozin
Why: SGLT2 inhibitors → euglycemic DKA risk during surgery/illness. Mechanism: ↑ insulin requirements (stress), glucosuria continues → ketosis WITHOUT high glucose (euglycemic). Hold 3-7 days perioperatively. Resume after stable oral intake. Metformin can continue (eGFR 42 is safe, though hold if acute renal injury). HY: Euglycemic DKA with SGLT2 is a MUST-KNOW trap.
10. A 70-year-old man with T2DM, history of medullary thyroid cancer (MTC), and CKD Stage 3b (eGFR 35) requires glucose-lowering therapy. Which agent is CONTRAINDICATED?
✓ CORRECT: C) GLP-1 agonist
Why: GLP-1 RAs are CONTRAINDICATED in patients with personal or family history of medullary thyroid cancer (MTC) or MEN2A/2EN2B (based on rodent data showing C-cell hyperplasia). Always screen for hx MTC before prescribing GLP-1. SGLT2, DPP-4, metformin, SU are safe. HY Trap: GLP-1 + MTC hx = absolute CI.
11. A 55-year-old patient with T2DM is on metformin monotherapy with HbA1c 8.5%. He has established ASCVD and BMI 32. Which agent would provide the GREATEST weight loss as add-on therapy?
✓ CORRECT: D) GLP-1 agonist/tirzepatide
Why: GLP-1 RAs cause 2-15 kg weight loss (gastric emptying ↓, satiety ↑, CNS appetite suppression). Tirzepatide (dual GLP-1/GIP) → 20-25% body weight loss in obesity trials (superior to GLP-1 alone). This is the GREATEST weight loss among all glucose-lowering agents. SGLT2 → 2-3 kg. SU/TZD → weight gain. HY: Obesity + CV disease → GLP-1/tirzepatide preferred.
12. A patient with T2DM is on acarbose (alpha-glucosidase inhibitor) and develops symptomatic hypoglycemia (tremor, sweating, confusion). What is the CORRECT treatment?
✓ CORRECT: C) Pure glucose
Why: Acarbose blocks α-glucosidase → prevents sucrose/disaccharide digestion. If hypoglycemia occurs, treat with PURE GLUCOSE (monosaccharide, doesn't require glucosidase for absorption), NOT table sugar (sucrose requires glucosidase → blocked by acarbose). HY Trap: This is a classic board exam question testing mechanism understanding.
13. A 60-year-old woman with T2DM and BMI 28 is on metformin + saxagliptin. She develops dyspnea and lower extremity edema over 3 months. Echocardiogram shows reduced EF (35%). What is the likely culprit?
✓ CORRECT: B) Saxagliptin (SAVOR-TIMI)
Why: SAVOR-TIMI trial: saxagliptin (DPP-4 inhibitor) → slight ↑ HF hospitalizations. While other DPP-4s (sitagliptin, linagliptin) appear neutral, saxagliptin should be AVOIDED in HF. Management: discontinue saxagliptin; consider SGLT2 inhibitor (proven HF benefit). HY Trap: DPP-4 generally neutral, but saxagliptin is an exception (↑ HF).
14. A 66-year-old woman with T2DM and eGFR 28 requires medication adjustment. Which statement about renal dosing is CORRECT?
✓ CORRECT: D) All correct
Why: (A) Metformin CI at eGFR <30 ✓ (B) Linagliptin = hepatic elimination = no renal adjustment ✓ (C) Sitagliptin = renal elimination = needs dose reduction ✓ (E is incorrect: dapagliflozin can be used down to eGFR ≥20 for CKD benefit, though glucose-lowering effect ↓). HY: Know specific renal dosing for each drug class — board exams test this detail.
15. A 52-year-old man with T2DM has HbA1c 7.8% on metformin alone. He wants to avoid insulin. Which regimen follows 2023 ADA guidelines BEST if he has no CV disease, HF, or CKD?
✓ CORRECT: D) GLP-1 or SGLT2 reasonable
Why: 2023 ADA guidelines: in absence of comorbidities, GLP-1 agonist or SGLT2 inhibitor are REASONABLE first add-ons due to weight loss, CV benefit, and safety. SU/TZD/DPP-4 are alternatives but less preferred. Guidelines shifted toward GLP-1/SGLT2 as preferred. HY: Memorize the stepwise approach: metformin → GLP-1 or SGLT2 (preferred) → or DPP-4/SU (alternatives).
16. A 78-year-old patient with T2DM, dementia, and multiple comorbidities is hospitalized. Current HbA1c 6.8%. What is the most appropriate HbA1c target for this patient?
✓ CORRECT: C) <8%
Why: HbA1c targets are INDIVIDUALIZED: <7% standard, <6.5% if young/new DM/no CV disease, <8% if elderly/limited life expectancy/frequent hypoglycemia risk/extensive comorbidities. Tight control (<6.5%) in elderly with dementia → ↑ hypoglycemia risk (confusion/injury) with minimal benefit. Target <8% is appropriate. HY: Individualized targets = must-know concept.
17. A 51-year-old man with newly diagnosed T2DM (HbA1c 9.2%) and normal weight is started on metformin. His glucose remains high. Which add-on should be considered FIRST to minimize hypoglycemia risk?
✓ CORRECT: C) GLP-1, SGLT2, or DPP-4
Why: To minimize hypoglycemia while adding therapy, choose agents that DO NOT cause hypoglycemia: GLP-1 (glucose-dependent), SGLT2 (insulin-independent), DPP-4 (glucose-dependent). Avoid SU/meglitinides (insulin secretagogues) and early insulin. HY: Hypoglycemia-free agents are increasingly preferred in modern diabetes management.
18. A 55-year-old man with T2DM and CKD (eGFR 28) develops medullary thyroid cancer (MTC) on screening imaging. He is currently on linagliptin and metformin. Which statement is CORRECT regarding future therapy?
✓ CORRECT: B) GLP-1 CI in personal hx MTC
Why: GLP-1 RAs are CONTRAINDICATED in patients with personal or family history of MTC or MEN2A/2B (based on rodent C-cell hyperplasia data). Even if CV benefit exists, the CI is absolute. Other agents (SGLT2, DPP-4, metformin, SU) can continue. HY Trap: This is a HIGH YIELD concept tested frequently on boards.
